Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every piece of information astronomers have about the universe beyond Earth has arrived as light. No astronomer has ever touched a star, sampled the atmosphere of another planet with their hands, or stood on a distant moon. Everything is known from light. Understanding light is therefore not a side topic in astronomy. It is the foundation of the entire science.

Chapter 5 gave you the tools to think about astronomical distances and scales — light-years, parsecs, lookback time, and scientific notation. Chapter 6 now asks what is actually traveling across those distances to reach our telescopes and detectors. The answer is light. More precisely, it is electromagnetic radiation in all its forms: radio waves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.

For most of human history, astronomers could only observe the narrow slice of the electromagnetic spectrum that human eyes detect. Over the past two centuries, physics revealed that visible light is only a small portion of a vast family of waves. Each region of this spectrum carries different information and reveals different physical processes. A complete picture of any astronomical object — a star, a galaxy, a nebula, the universe itself — requires observations across many wavelengths.

This chapter introduces electromagnetic waves and the full spectrum. It explains wavelength, frequency, energy, and what each region of the spectrum reveals. It also introduces the observatories, both on the ground and in space, that allow astronomers to observe the universe in all forms of light.

Opening Question

You can see a candle flame from 50 meters away — but you cannot feel its warmth at that distance. Your eyes detect visible light; your skin detects infrared. The flame produces both, but your senses respond to different regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. Astronomers face the same challenge at cosmic scale: the same star, galaxy, or nebula looks completely different depending on which form of light you observe. Why? Because different physical processes produce different kinds of light. By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly why a galaxy looks different in radio, infrared, visible, X-ray, and gamma-ray light — and why each view tells a different story.

Section 6.1

Light as Information

When you look at the night sky, what you are actually doing is receiving information. Light has traveled from the Moon, from planets, from stars hundreds of light-years away, and from galaxies billions of light-years distant. Every photon that reaches your eye or a telescope's detector has carried a message: where something is, how hot it is, what it is made of, how fast it is moving, and in some cases, what happened to it billions of years ago.

This is the central fact of observational astronomy: light is the primary messenger of the universe. Astronomers cannot travel to the objects they study. They cannot send probes to distant stars or sample the gas inside a nebula. What they can do — and what they do with extraordinary precision — is analyze the light those objects emit, reflect, or absorb.

For most of human history, "light" meant only the visible light that human eyes can detect. But in the last two centuries, physicists and astronomers discovered that visible light is only a small portion of a much broader phenomenon: the electromagnetic spectrum. Visible light, radio waves, infrared radiation, ultraviolet light, X-rays, and gamma rays are all forms of electromagnetic radiation. They differ in wavelength and energy, but they are all, fundamentally, light.

Each region of the electromagnetic spectrum reveals different kinds of information. Cool gas glows in radio waves. Warm dust glows in infrared. Hot stellar surfaces shine in visible and ultraviolet light. Extremely hot gas — superheated by black holes, neutron stars, or supernova explosions — blazes in X-rays and gamma rays. An astronomer who studies only visible light is reading only one sentence in a much longer document.

Key Idea

Astronomers cannot visit the objects they study. Everything they know about distant objects — composition, temperature, motion, distance — comes from analyzing light. This makes understanding the properties of light essential to everything else in astronomy.

Section 6.2

Waves and Wavelength

Light is a wave. More precisely, it is an electromagnetic wave — a wave made of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that travel together through space. Unlike a wave on a rope or a sound wave in air, an electromagnetic wave does not need a medium. It can travel through the vacuum of space.

To understand light, it helps to understand wave properties in general. A wave is a disturbance that repeats in space and in time. The distance from one crest to the next is the wavelength, usually represented by the Greek letter lambda (λ). Wavelength is measured in meters, although for light waves, the relevant units are often much smaller: nanometers (one billionth of a meter, or 10⁻⁹ m) for visible and ultraviolet light, and centimeters or meters for radio waves.

The amplitude of a wave is the height of its crests above the undisturbed baseline. Amplitude corresponds to the intensity or brightness of light. A brighter light source emits electromagnetic waves with greater amplitude.

Wavelength is the most important wave property for understanding the electromagnetic spectrum. Waves with different wavelengths behave differently, carry different amounts of energy, and interact with matter in different ways. The wavelength of a light wave determines what kind of light it is — whether it is radio, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray, or gamma ray.

Wave Properties: Summary

Wavelength (λ) — Distance from crest to crest. Determines the type of electromagnetic radiation.

Amplitude — Height of crests. Determines the intensity (brightness) of the radiation.

Longer wavelength — More spread-out cycles; lower frequency; lower energy per photon.

Shorter wavelength — More tightly packed cycles; higher frequency; higher energy per photon.

Section 6.3

Frequency and Energy

Wavelength and frequency are related. Frequency is the number of wave cycles that pass a fixed point per second. Its unit is the hertz (Hz), where one hertz means one cycle per second.

All electromagnetic waves travel through a vacuum at the same speed: the speed of light, approximately 300,000 kilometers per second (written as c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s). Because all electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed, there is a fixed relationship between wavelength and frequency:

The Wave Equation

speed = wavelength × frequency

c = λ × f

where c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s, λ = wavelength in meters, f = frequency in hertz (Hz).

Because c is constant, λ and f are inversely proportional: when wavelength increases, frequency decreases, and vice versa.

Energy is also tied to frequency. Electromagnetic waves carry energy, and the energy of a single photon — a discrete packet of electromagnetic radiation — is proportional to its frequency. Higher-frequency (shorter-wavelength) photons carry more energy. Lower-frequency (longer-wavelength) photons carry less energy.

This relationship has profound consequences for astronomy. Radio waves, with very long wavelengths and very low frequencies, carry very little energy per photon and pass through most materials easily. Gamma rays, with extremely short wavelengths and extremely high frequencies, carry enormous energy per photon and can penetrate solid matter and ionize atoms.

Detecting gamma rays from a galaxy tells you that something violent and energetic is happening — perhaps a collapsing stellar core or matter falling into a black hole. Detecting radio waves from a gas cloud tells you about cooler, less energetic processes. The energy of light reveals the energy of the process that produced it.

PropertyRelationship
Longer wavelengthLower frequency, lower energy per photon
Shorter wavelengthHigher frequency, higher energy per photon
All forms of lightTravel at the same speed in a vacuum (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)
Example 6.3.1
Using the wave equation to find frequency
  • ProblemA radio wave has a wavelength of 3.0 m. What is its frequency? (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)
  • ThinkUse c = λ × f. Solve for f by dividing both sides by λ: f = c ÷ λ.
  • Solve f = c ÷ λ = (3 × 10⁸ m/s) ÷ (3.0 m) = 1 × 10⁸ Hz = 100 MHz
  • CheckRadio stations broadcast in the MHz range. 100 MHz is FM radio frequency. Reasonable. ✓
Answer: f = 1 × 10⁸ Hz = 100 MHz
Example 6.3.2
Using the wave equation to find wavelength
  • ProblemVisible green light has a frequency of approximately 6 × 10¹⁴ Hz. What is its wavelength in nanometers?
  • ThinkUse c = λ × f. Solve for λ: λ = c ÷ f. Then convert from meters to nanometers (1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m).
  • Solve λ = c ÷ f = (3 × 10⁸ m/s) ÷ (6 × 10¹⁴ Hz) = 5 × 10⁻⁷ m Convert: 5 × 10⁻⁷ m × (1 nm / 10⁻⁹ m) = 500 nm
  • CheckGreen light is approximately 520 nm — 500 nm is a reasonable estimate. ✓
Answer: λ ≈ 500 nm (green visible light)
Student Warning — Faster vs. More Energetic

All electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed (c) in a vacuum. A gamma ray and a radio wave both travel at 3 × 10⁸ m/s. What differs is energy, not speed. High-frequency light is more energetic per photon, not faster. Do not confuse energy and speed.

Section 6.4

Visible Light

The portion of the electromagnetic spectrum that human eyes can detect is called visible light. It is a narrow band of wavelengths, roughly from 400 to 700 nanometers. Within that range, different wavelengths correspond to different colors.

ColorApproximate Wavelength
Violet~400 nm
Blue~450 nm
Green~520 nm
Yellow~580 nm
Orange~610 nm
Red~700 nm

White light contains all visible wavelengths mixed together. When white light passes through a prism or raindrops, the different wavelengths bend by slightly different amounts — a phenomenon called dispersion — and the colors separate into a rainbow. This spreading of light into its component wavelengths is the basic principle behind spectroscopy, the science of analyzing light that is central to almost all of modern astronomy.

The visible range is narrow compared to the full electromagnetic spectrum, but it is the region where the Sun emits most of its light. Human eyes evolved under sunlight, so they respond to wavelengths the Sun produces most abundantly. Other stars emit peak radiation at different wavelengths depending on their temperatures, which is why some stars appear distinctly blue or red when you observe them in the night sky.

Most ground-based observatories were built to study visible light. However, Earth's atmosphere limits what can be observed from the ground. Water vapor absorbs much infrared radiation. Oxygen and ozone absorb ultraviolet. X-rays and gamma rays are almost entirely blocked by the atmosphere. To observe those parts of the spectrum, astronomers must send telescopes into space.

Key Connection — Chapter 7

When astronomers spread visible light from a star into its component wavelengths — a spectrum — they can identify which elements are present, how hot the star is, and how fast it is moving toward or away from Earth. This technique, spectroscopy, is the subject of Chapter 7. The dispersion of visible light is where it begins.

Section 6.5

Radio and Microwave Astronomy

At the long-wavelength, low-frequency end of the electromagnetic spectrum are radio waves, with wavelengths ranging from millimeters to kilometers. Radio waves pass through Earth's atmosphere with minimal absorption, which means they can be observed from the ground — an important advantage over many other parts of the spectrum.

Radio astronomy was born accidentally in the 1930s when engineer Karl Jansky discovered that the center of the Milky Way emits radio waves. Today, radio telescopes are among the largest and most powerful instruments in astronomy. The Very Large Array (VLA) in New Mexico uses 27 large radio dishes working together. The Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) in Chile detects millimeter-wavelength radio waves from cold gas and dust in star-forming regions.

What Radio Waves Reveal in Astronomy

PhenomenonWhy Radio?
Cold hydrogen gasHydrogen emits a characteristic radio signal at 21 cm wavelength. Radio maps trace the distribution of gas in the Milky Way and other galaxies.
PulsarsRapidly spinning neutron stars emit beams of radio waves that sweep across Earth at precise intervals, like a cosmic lighthouse.
Active galactic nucleiSupermassive black holes in some galaxy centers produce powerful jets that emit intense radio waves visible across billions of light-years.
Cosmic microwave backgroundThe faint afterglow of the Big Bang is detected as microwave radiation — a short-wavelength form of radio waves — and reveals the early structure of the universe.

Section 6.6

Infrared Astronomy

Infrared radiation lies between visible red light and microwave/radio waves, with wavelengths roughly from 700 nanometers to 1 millimeter. Objects that are warm but not hot enough to glow visibly still emit infrared light. Your body, at about 37°C, glows in infrared. A pan on a stove glows in infrared before it glows in visible light. The same principle applies to astronomical objects.

Seeing through dust. The Milky Way is filled with clouds of tiny solid particles called interstellar dust. This dust scatters and absorbs visible light, blocking our view of stars and objects behind it. Infrared radiation has longer wavelengths that can pass through dust more easily. Infrared telescopes can peer into the center of the Milky Way, into the interiors of star-forming clouds, and through dusty regions that are invisible optically.

Cool and cold objects. Planets, brown dwarfs, cold gas clouds, and cool stars all emit primarily in infrared. The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), launched in 2021, is primarily an infrared observatory. It can detect light from some of the first galaxies to form after the Big Bang — light that has been redshifted from its original ultraviolet and visible wavelengths into the infrared by the expansion of the universe over billions of years (redshift is explained in detail in Chapters 7 and 22).

Star formation. Stars form inside dense clouds of gas and dust that are opaque to visible light. The protostars inside glow in infrared, and infrared telescopes can image the earliest stages of stellar birth.

Why Infrared Requires Space — or High Altitude

Earth's atmosphere absorbs much of the infrared spectrum, primarily because water vapor absorbs infrared radiation. This is why many infrared observations require space telescopes or observatories at very high altitude in dry locations (like the Atacama Desert in Chile, at over 5,000 m elevation). Less water vapor in the atmosphere means more infrared passes through.

Section 6.7

Ultraviolet Astronomy

Ultraviolet (UV) radiation lies just beyond the violet end of the visible spectrum, with wavelengths from about 10 to 400 nanometers. It carries more energy per photon than visible light, enough to break chemical bonds and ionize atoms.

Most ultraviolet radiation from the Sun is absorbed by Earth's ozone layer before reaching the surface — which is fortunate for life on Earth, since intense UV radiation damages DNA. For astronomers, this absorption means that UV observations must be made from space.

Hot stars. Very hot, massive, young stars (spectral classes O and B) emit most of their energy in the ultraviolet. UV telescopes can identify regions of intense, recent star formation — places where massive young stars are ionizing the surrounding gas.

Hot gas. Gas heated to tens of thousands of degrees glows in UV. The chromosphere and corona of the Sun, which are far hotter than the visible surface, are studied in ultraviolet.

Active galaxies. Galaxies with active, accreting supermassive black holes at their centers can be bright in ultraviolet. Supernovae — the explosive deaths of massive stars — also produce intense UV flashes. The Hubble Space Telescope has UV capabilities that make it a powerful tool for studying hot stars and energetic phenomena.

Section 6.8

X-Ray and Gamma-Ray Astronomy

At the shortest-wavelength, highest-energy end of the spectrum are X-rays and gamma rays. These are the most energetic forms of electromagnetic radiation, and they reveal the most violent, extreme environments in the universe.

X-rays (wavelengths roughly from 0.01 to 10 nm) are emitted by gas heated to millions of degrees. This kind of extreme heating occurs when gas falls toward a neutron star or a black hole, forming a swirling accretion disk heated to millions of degrees. Many neutron star and black hole systems were first discovered because of their X-ray emission. Clusters of galaxies contain superheated gas at millions of degrees filling the space between galaxies, visible only in X-rays. The Sun's corona also emits X-rays, as do the expanding shells of supernovae.

Because Earth's atmosphere absorbs X-rays, X-ray astronomy requires space telescopes. Major X-ray observatories have included NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory (launched in 1999) and the European Space Agency's XMM-Newton.

Gamma rays (wavelengths shorter than about 0.01 nm) are the most energetic form of electromagnetic radiation. They are produced by gamma-ray bursts — among the most energetic events in the universe, thought to result from collapsing massive stars or merging neutron stars. Rapidly spinning pulsars, nuclear reactions in stellar explosions, and supermassive black holes in active galactic nuclei also produce gamma rays. The Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope maps the gamma-ray sky and has revealed enormous gamma-ray bubbles extending above and below the galactic center of the Milky Way.

Example 6.8.1

  • CheckThis reasoning is consistent with how X-ray sources are actually interpreted in astronomy. ✓

Section 6.9

The Electromagnetic Spectrum as a Tool

No single region of the spectrum gives a complete picture of any astronomical object or system. A spiral galaxy looks very different in visible light (stars), infrared (warm dust and cool stars), radio (cold hydrogen gas), and X-ray (any central black hole activity). Combining observations across multiple wavelengths — called multi-wavelength astronomy — gives astronomers a much richer and more accurate understanding of what is happening.

RegionWavelength RangeTypical Sources in Astronomy
Radio> 1 mmCold gas, pulsars, active nuclei, cosmic microwave background
Microwave~1 mm to 1 cmCold molecular gas, cosmic microwave background
Infrared~700 nm to 1 mmWarm dust, cool stars, star-forming regions, distant galaxies
Visible~400 to 700 nmStars, reflected starlight, galaxies, planetary surfaces
Ultraviolet~10 to 400 nmHot young stars, solar corona, active galaxies, supernovae
X-ray~0.01 to 10 nmAccreting black holes, neutron stars, hot cluster gas, supernova remnants
Gamma ray< 0.01 nmGamma-ray bursts, pulsars, nuclear reactions, active nuclei

Consider the Sun as an example of multi-wavelength astronomy. In visible light, the Sun appears as a smooth, bright yellow-white disk with occasional dark sunspots. In ultraviolet, the solar corona lights up with loops and arches of magnetically active hot gas. In X-rays, the corona blazes brilliantly. In radio, astronomers trace emission from solar flares. Each wavelength reveals a different layer and different physical process. Together, they paint a complete picture of how the Sun works.

The same multi-wavelength strategy applies to galaxies, nebulae, star-forming regions, clusters of galaxies, and the entire universe. The history of 20th- and 21st-century astronomy is in large part a history of discovering new phenomena by opening new wavelength windows — often finding things that no one expected.

Example 6.9.1
Multi-wavelength analysis of a spiral galaxy
  • ProblemA spiral galaxy is imaged in visible, infrared, radio, and X-ray. What feature would be most prominent in each image?
  • VisibleBright spiral arms with blue (young) stars and yellow-white (older) stars in the central bulge. Dark dust lanes. Overall shape of the galaxy clearly visible.
  • InfraredWarm dust glows brightly, tracing star-forming regions. Dust lanes that appear dark in visible light now glow. Cool red stars in the bulge are prominent.
  • RadioCold hydrogen gas traced in 21-cm emission, mapping the full extent of the disk — often larger than what is visible optically. Any radio jets from an active nucleus stand out.
  • X-rayIf the galaxy has an active central black hole, its accretion disk blazes in X-rays. Hot gas blown out by supernovae glows faintly. Individual X-ray binary systems (neutron stars, black holes with companion stars) appear as point sources.
Answer: Each wavelength reveals a different component — stars, dust, gas, or energetic compact objects — that together give the complete picture.

Section 6.10

Why Space Observatories Matter

Earth's atmosphere is essential for life but is a significant obstacle for astronomy. Different parts of the electromagnetic spectrum encounter the atmosphere very differently.

Atmospheric EffectWavelengths AffectedCause
Transmitted (window open)Most visible, most radioAtmosphere relatively transparent in these ranges
Partially blockedNear-infraredWater vapor absorption, varies with humidity and altitude
Largely blockedMost infraredWater vapor and carbon dioxide absorption
Almost completely blockedMost ultravioletOzone and oxygen absorption in upper atmosphere
Almost completely blockedX-ray and gamma rayAbsorption by nitrogen, oxygen, and other atmospheric molecules

Because of this selective transmission, ground-based observatories are largely limited to visible and radio astronomy. To observe the universe in ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma ray, or much of the infrared, astronomers must send telescopes into space.

Major Space Observatories

ObservatoryPrimary WavelengthKey Contributions
Hubble Space TelescopeVisible, UV, near-IRSharp imaging above atmosphere; UV observations of hot stars; deep-field observations of distant galaxies
Chandra X-ray ObservatoryX-rayAccreting black holes, neutron stars, galaxy cluster gas, supernova remnants in unprecedented detail
Spitzer Space TelescopeInfraredStar-forming regions, planetary disks, cool stars, distant galaxies (2003–2020)
James Webb Space TelescopeNear- and mid-infraredEarliest galaxies in the universe, star-forming clouds, exoplanet atmospheres; launched 2021
Fermi Gamma-ray Space TelescopeGamma rayGamma-ray bursts, pulsars, active nuclei, mysterious Fermi bubbles in the Milky Way
A Pattern in the History of Astronomy

Every time a new wavelength window has been opened — radio in the 1930s, X-ray in the 1960s, gamma ray in the 1970s, infrared with JWST most recently — astronomers have discovered phenomena that were completely unknown before. New wavelengths do not just show familiar objects more clearly; they reveal entirely new classes of objects and processes. This suggests that the universe is still full of phenomena waiting to be discovered whenever new observational tools are developed.

  • Sec 6.4Visible light spans roughly 400–700 nm. Different wavelengths within this range correspond to different colors, from violet (shorter) to red (longer). Dispersion separates white light into its colors. The visible range is the primary window for ground-based observing.
  • Sec 6.5Radio waves are the longest-wavelength, lowest-energy form of electromagnetic radiation. They can be observed from Earth's surface. Radio astronomy reveals cold hydrogen gas, pulsars, active galactic nuclei, and the cosmic microwave background.
  • Sec 6.6Infrared radiation reveals warm dust, cool stars, and star-forming regions hidden inside dusty clouds. Because Earth's atmosphere absorbs much infrared, space telescopes like JWST or high-altitude dry-site observatories are often required.
  • Sec 6.7Ultraviolet radiation reveals hot young stars, the solar corona, and active galaxies. Earth's ozone layer absorbs nearly all UV from outside Earth, requiring space telescopes for UV astronomy.
  • Sec 6.8X-rays reveal gas heated to millions of degrees near neutron stars, black holes, and in galaxy clusters. Gamma rays — the most energetic form — reveal gamma-ray bursts, pulsars, and active nuclei. Both require space telescopes because Earth's atmosphere is opaque at these wavelengths.
  • Sec 6.9No single wavelength gives a complete picture. Multi-wavelength astronomy combines observations across the spectrum. Each region reveals different physical processes — together they provide the most accurate and complete understanding of any astronomical object.
  • Sec 6.10Earth's atmosphere is largely transparent only to visible light and radio waves. To observe at UV, X-ray, gamma-ray, and much of the infrared wavelengths, astronomers use space observatories. Each new wavelength window opened has led to discoveries of previously unknown phenomena.

Key Terms Review

electromagnetic radiation
The family of waves made of oscillating electric and magnetic fields, including radio, infrared, visible, UV, X-ray, and gamma ray. All travel at the speed of light in a vacuum.
wavelength (λ)
The distance from one wave crest to the next. Determines what kind of electromagnetic radiation a wave is. Usually measured in meters or nanometers (nm).
frequency (f)
The number of wave cycles passing a fixed point per second, measured in hertz (Hz). Inversely related to wavelength.
speed of light (c)
The speed at which all electromagnetic radiation travels in a vacuum: approximately 3 × 10⁸ m/s (300,000 km/s).
photon
A discrete packet of electromagnetic energy. Higher-frequency light consists of higher-energy photons.
visible light
The narrow range of the electromagnetic spectrum (approximately 400–700 nm) detectable by human eyes.
radio waves
The longest-wavelength, lowest-energy form of electromagnetic radiation. Can be observed from Earth's surface through the radio window.
infrared radiation
Electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths longer than visible light, emitted by warm objects. Useful for observing through dust and cool or distant objects.
ultraviolet (UV)
Electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths shorter than visible light, emitted by hot stars and energetic sources. Mostly absorbed by Earth's ozone layer.
X-rays
High-energy electromagnetic radiation emitted by gas heated to millions of degrees, neutron stars, and black hole accretion disks. Absorbed by Earth's atmosphere.
gamma rays
The shortest-wavelength, highest-energy electromagnetic radiation, produced by the most energetic events in the universe.
electromagnetic spectrum
The complete range of electromagnetic radiation arranged by wavelength or frequency, from radio waves to gamma rays.
multi-wavelength astronomy
Observing the same object or region in multiple parts of the electromagnetic spectrum to obtain a more complete and accurate understanding.
atmospheric window
A range of wavelengths that passes through Earth's atmosphere with relatively little absorption. The main windows are visible and radio.
accretion disk
A disk of gas spiraling inward toward a compact object such as a neutron star or black hole, heated to high temperatures that produce X-ray emission.
dispersion
The separation of white light into its component wavelengths (colors) when it passes through a prism or refracting medium. The basis of spectroscopy.

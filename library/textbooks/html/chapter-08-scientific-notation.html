Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Numbers in mathematics and everyday arithmetic are usually manageable. You might add grocery prices, measure a room, or calculate a tip, and the numbers stay within a comfortable range. But science, engineering, medicine, and technology regularly deal with numbers that are far outside that range, numbers so large or so small that writing them in ordinary form becomes impractical, confusing, and error-prone.

The distance from Earth to the nearest star outside our solar system is approximately 40,000,000,000,000 kilometers. A single human red blood cell is approximately 0.000008 meters in diameter. The number of atoms in a tablespoon of water is approximately 500,000,000,000,000,000,000,000. Writing these numbers in full is tedious at best and dangerously easy to misread at worst. A single missing zero changes the meaning entirely.

Scientific notation solves this problem. It is a compact, precise, internationally used system for writing very large and very small numbers using powers of ten. It does not change the value of a number. It changes the way the number is written so that it becomes easier to read, compare, compute with, and communicate.

This chapter teaches scientific notation from the ground up. You will begin by reviewing powers of ten, then learn to convert numbers into and out of scientific notation, then practice multiplying and dividing in scientific notation, and finally connect these skills to real scientific and technical data. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to read the language of scale that scientists, engineers, and mathematicians use every day.

Opening Question

A newspaper headline reads: "Scientists estimate the observable universe contains approximately 200,000,000,000 galaxies." A biology textbook states: "A typical bacterium is approximately 0.000001 meters long."

Both of these numbers are difficult to read at a glance. How many zeros are in the first number? How many decimal places is the second? Is there a better way to write them? There is. By the time you finish Chapter 8, you will be able to write both numbers in a form that is precise, compact, and easy to work with.

Why This Chapter Matters

Scientific notation is not only a topic for science classes. It appears in astronomy, when describing distances between planets, stars, and galaxies; in chemistry, when describing the size of atoms and molecules; in biology, when describing bacteria, viruses, and cell dimensions; in medicine, when describing dosages and concentrations; in engineering, when working with tolerances and material properties; in computing, when describing file sizes and processor speeds; and in finance, when working with national debt figures and large-scale economic data.

Scientific notation also connects directly to the exponent rules you studied in Chapter 7, to decimal place value from Chapter 5, and to the estimation skills you have been building throughout this textbook. Section 8.1

Why Scientific Notation Is Needed

The number system you use every day is built on base ten. Place value in its ordinary written form, however, becomes clumsy when the numbers grow very large or very small. Consider the following examples from actual science.

QuantityStandard Form
Distance to Proxima Centauri (km)40,000,000,000,000
Mass of Earth (kg)5,970,000,000,000,000,000,000,000
Diameter of a hydrogen atom (m)0.0000000001
Mass of a proton (kg)0.000000000000000000000000001673

Each of these numbers is technically writable in standard form. But try reading the mass of a proton aloud. Try copying it from one line to another without losing track of the zeros. Try comparing it to a similar number to see which is larger. The difficulty is not with the mathematics. The difficulty is with the notation itself.

Scientific notation addresses this by using the structure of powers of ten to compress the writing of any number into a short, readable, precise form. A number written in scientific notation always takes this form:

\[ a \times 10^n \]

where \(a\) is a decimal number satisfying \(1 \leq a < 10\), and \(n\) is an integer (positive, negative, or zero). The coefficient \(a\) captures the significant digits. The power of ten \(10^n\) captures the scale.

QuantityScientific Notation
Distance to Proxima Centauri (km)\(4 \times 10^{13}\)
Mass of Earth (kg)\(5.97 \times 10^{24}\)
Diameter of a hydrogen atom (m)\(1 \times 10^{-10}\)
Mass of a proton (kg)\(1.673 \times 10^{-27}\)
Student Note — The Form Is Strict

The form \(a \times 10^n\) is not optional. A number like \(23 \times 10^4\) is not in proper scientific notation, even though it uses a power of ten. The coefficient \(a\) must be at least 1 and less than 10. You must adjust the coefficient until it is in the correct range.

Section 8.2

Powers of Ten

A power of ten is ten raised to an integer exponent. You studied exponents in Chapter 7. Here you apply that knowledge specifically to base ten, which is especially clean because our entire number system is built around it.

\[ 10^1 = 10 \qquad 10^2 = 100 \qquad 10^3 = 1{,}000 \qquad 10^4 = 10{,}000 \]
\[ 10^{-1} = 0.1 \qquad 10^{-2} = 0.01 \qquad 10^{-3} = 0.001 \]
\[ 10^0 = 1 \]

Each increase by one in the exponent multiplies the value by ten, producing a new zero in standard form. Each decrease by one in the exponent divides the value by ten, moving the significant digit one more place to the right of the decimal point. The zero exponent fits the pattern exactly: moving from \(10^1 = 10\) down to \(10^0 = 1\) is a single division by ten.

\(10^6\)1,000,000one million
\(10^3\)1,000one thousand
\(10^0\)1one
\(10^{-3}\)0.001one thousandth
\(10^{-6}\)0.000001one millionth
\(10^{-9}\)0.000000001one billionth

Section 8.3

Positive Powers of Ten

Positive powers of ten represent numbers greater than or equal to one. The larger the exponent, the larger the number. When a number is written as \(a \times 10^n\) with a positive \(n\), the number is at least ten.

Multiplying any number by \(10^n\) (where \(n\) is a positive integer) moves the decimal point \(n\) places to the right. The exponent tells you the order of magnitude, the approximate power of ten that describes the scale of the number.

Example 8.3.1
Evaluating a positive power of ten
  • ProblemEvaluate \(10^7\) in standard form.
  • ThinkIn standard form, a power of ten is 1 followed by that many zeros.
  • Solve10^7 = 10,000,000
  • CheckCount the zeros. There are seven zeros after the 1. This matches the exponent.
Answer: \(10^7 = 10{,}000{,}000\)
Example 8.3.2
Multiplying by a positive power of ten
  • ProblemCompute \(2.8 \times 10^4\).
  • ThinkMultiplying by \(10^4\) moves the decimal point four places to the right.
  • Solve2.8 × 10^4 → 28,000
  • Check\(2.8 \times 10{,}000 = 28{,}000\). Correct.
Answer: \(28{,}000\)

Section 8.4

Negative Powers of Ten

Negative powers of ten represent numbers between zero and one. The more negative the exponent, the smaller the number. Multiplying any number by \(10^{-n}\) (where \(n\) is a positive integer) moves the decimal point \(n\) places to the left.

Critical Distinction

A negative exponent does not mean the number is negative. It means the number is small, specifically a fraction of one. The number \(6.02 \times 10^{-3} = 0.00602\) is a small positive number. The negative sign belongs to the exponent on the ten, not to the value itself.

Example 8.4.1
Evaluating a negative power of ten
  • ProblemEvaluate \(10^{-5}\) in standard form.
  • ThinkA negative power of ten means \(\frac{1}{10^5}\). The significant digit 1 sits five places to the right of the decimal point.
  • Solve10^{-5} = 1/100,000 = 0.00001
  • CheckCount: the 1 is in the fifth decimal place. Correct.
Answer: \(10^{-5} = 0.00001\)
Example 8.4.2
Multiplying by a negative power of ten
  • ProblemCompute \(4.5 \times 10^{-4}\).
  • ThinkMultiplying by \(10^{-4}\) moves the decimal point four places to the left.
  • Solve4.5 × 10^{-4} → 0.00045
  • Check\(4.5 \div 10{,}000 = 0.00045\). Correct.
Answer: \(0.00045\)

Section 8.5

Standard Form and Scientific Notation

Every number can be written in two forms. Standard form is the familiar decimal number with all digits in place, such as 93,000,000 or 0.000052. Scientific notation is a compact form using a coefficient between 1 and 10 multiplied by a power of ten, such as \(9.3 \times 10^7\) or \(5.2 \times 10^{-5}\). Both forms represent the same value. Scientific notation neither changes the number nor rounds it, unless rounding is explicitly done.

Three Conditions for Proper Scientific Notation

1. The coefficient is a decimal number satisfying \(1 \leq a < 10\).

2. The coefficient is multiplied by a power of ten.

3. The exponent is an integer (positive, negative, or zero).

ExpressionProper?Reason if Not
\(3.0 \times 10^8\)Yes—
\(7.41 \times 10^5\)Yes—
\(23 \times 10^4\)NoCoefficient 23 is not less than 10
\(0.6 \times 10^3\)NoCoefficient 0.6 is not at least 1
\(5 \times 3^4\)NoBase is not 10
Order of Magnitude

The power of ten in scientific notation is called the order of magnitude. It gives you an immediate sense of the size of the number. A number with \(10^6\) is in the millions. A number with \(10^{-9}\) is in the billionths. Reading the exponent at a glance is one of the main practical advantages of scientific notation.

Section 8.6

Converting Standard Numbers to Scientific Notation

Five-Step Conversion Procedure

Step 1. Locate the decimal point. (If none is written, it sits at the right end of the number.)

Step 2. Move the decimal point left or right until you have a number between 1 and 10.

Step 3. Count how many places you moved. This count becomes the exponent.

Step 4. Determine the sign: moved left → positive exponent; moved right → negative exponent.

Step 5. Write in the form \(a \times 10^n\).

Original NumberDecimal MovedExponent Sign
Greater than or equal to 10LeftPositive
Between 0 and 1RightNegative
Example 8.6.1
Large number to scientific notation
  • ProblemWrite 47,300,000 in scientific notation.
  • ThinkThe decimal point is at the right end. Move it left until the coefficient is between 1 and 10.
  • Solve47,300,000. → 4.73 Decimal moved 7 places left → exponent is +7
  • Check\(4.73 \times 10^7 = 4.73 \times 10{,}000{,}000 = 47{,}300{,}000\). Correct.
Answer: \(4.73 \times 10^7\)
Example 8.6.2
Small number to scientific notation
  • ProblemWrite 0.000059 in scientific notation.
  • ThinkThe number is between 0 and 1. Move the decimal point right until the coefficient is between 1 and 10.
  • Solve0.000059 → 5.9 Decimal moved 5 places right → exponent is -5
  • Check\(5.9 \times 10^{-5} = 5.9 \times 0.00001 = 0.000059\). Correct.
Answer: \(5.9 \times 10^{-5}\)
Example 8.6.3
Correcting improper scientific notation
  • ProblemThe number \(52 \times 10^3\) is not in proper form. Rewrite it correctly.
  • ThinkThe coefficient 52 is greater than 10. Move its decimal one place left to get 5.2. To keep the value the same, increase the exponent by 1.
  • Solve52 × 10^3 = 5.2 × 10^1 × 10^3 = 5.2 × 10^4
  • Check\(5.2 \times 10^4 = 52{,}000\) and \(52 \times 10^3 = 52{,}000\). Same value. Correct.
Answer: \(5.2 \times 10^4\)
Adjusting Coefficients — Keep the Value Constant

When you adjust an improper coefficient, always adjust the exponent in the opposite direction to keep the value the same. Moving the decimal left (making the coefficient smaller) increases the exponent. Moving the decimal right (making the coefficient larger) decreases the exponent.

Section 8.7

Converting Scientific Notation to Standard Form

Converting from scientific notation back to standard form means expanding the number by following the instruction of the exponent. A positive exponent tells you to move the decimal point that many places to the right, making the number larger. A negative exponent tells you to move the decimal point that many places to the left, making the number smaller. Fill in zeros as needed when you run out of digits.

Example 8.7.1
Positive exponent to standard form
  • ProblemWrite \(3.15 \times 10^6\) in standard form.
  • ThinkExponent is 6: move the decimal point in 3.15 six places to the right.
  • Solve3.15 → 31.5 → 315 → 3150 → 31500 → 315000 → 3,150,000
  • Check3,150,000 is in the millions, which matches \(10^6\). Correct.
Answer: \(3{,}150{,}000\)
Example 8.7.2
Negative exponent to standard form
  • ProblemWrite \(8.4 \times 10^{-7}\) in standard form.
  • ThinkExponent is \(-7\): move the decimal point in 8.4 seven places to the left.
  • Solve8.4 → 0.84 → 0.084 → 0.0084 → 0.00084 → 0.000084 → 0.0000084 → 0.00000084
  • CheckThe first significant digit (8) sits seven places to the right of the decimal point. Correct.
Answer: \(0.00000084\)
Common Directional Error

Students sometimes confuse the direction of decimal movement. Remember: positive exponent → move right → number gets bigger. Negative exponent → move left → number gets smaller. If you end up with a tiny number after applying a positive exponent, recheck the direction.

Section 8.8

Multiplying Scientific Notation

Because scientific notation is built on powers of ten, and because you know the product rule for exponents from Chapter 7, multiplication in scientific notation is clean and structured. Recall:

\[ 10^m \times 10^n = 10^{m+n} \]
Multiplication Procedure

Step 1. Multiply the two coefficients together.

Step 2. Add the exponents of the powers of ten.

Step 3. Adjust if the new coefficient is not between 1 and 10.

(a × 10ᵐ) × (b × 10ⁿ) = (a × b) × 10ᵐ⁺ⁿ

Example 8.8.1
Basic multiplication in scientific notation
  • ProblemCompute \((3 \times 10^4) \times (2 \times 10^5)\).
  • ThinkMultiply the coefficients; add the exponents.
  • Solve(3 × 2) × 10^(4+5) = 6 × 10^9
  • Check\(30{,}000 \times 200{,}000 = 6{,}000{,}000{,}000 = 6 \times 10^9\). Correct.
Answer: \(6 \times 10^9\)
Example 8.8.2
Adjusting after multiplication
  • ProblemCompute \((4 \times 10^3) \times (5 \times 10^6)\).
  • ThinkPreliminary: \((4 \times 5) \times 10^{3+6} = 20 \times 10^9\). Coefficient 20 is too large. Adjust.
  • Solve20 × 10^9 = 2 × 10^1 × 10^9 = 2 × 10^10
  • Check\(4{,}000 \times 5{,}000{,}000 = 20{,}000{,}000{,}000 = 2 \times 10^{10}\). Correct.
Answer: \(2 \times 10^{10}\)
Example 8.8.3
Multiplication with negative exponents
  • ProblemCompute \((6 \times 10^{-3}) \times (3 \times 10^{-4})\).
  • ThinkCoefficients: \(6 \times 3 = 18\). Exponents: \(-3 + (-4) = -7\). Adjust 18.
  • Solve18 × 10^{-7} = 1.8 × 10^1 × 10^{-7} = 1.8 × 10^{-6}
  • Check\(0.006 \times 0.0001 = 0.0000006 = 6 \times 10^{-7}\). With more precise coefficients: \(1.8 \times 10^{-6} = 0.0000018\). Correct.
Answer: \(1.8 \times 10^{-6}\)

Section 8.9

Dividing Scientific Notation

Division in scientific notation follows the same logic as multiplication but uses the quotient rule for exponents from Chapter 7:

\[ \frac{10^m}{10^n} = 10^{m-n} \]
Division Procedure

Step 1. Divide the two coefficients.

Step 2. Subtract the exponent of the denominator from the exponent of the numerator.

Step 3. Adjust if the coefficient is not between 1 and 10.

(a × 10ᵐ) ÷ (b × 10ⁿ) = (a ÷ b) × 10ᵐ⁻ⁿ

Example 8.9.1
Basic division in scientific notation
  • ProblemCompute \(\dfrac{8 \times 10^9}{2 \times 10^3}\).
  • ThinkDivide coefficients: \(8 \div 2 = 4\). Subtract exponents: \(9 - 3 = 6\).
  • Solve4 × 10^6
  • Check\(8{,}000{,}000{,}000 \div 2{,}000 = 4{,}000{,}000 = 4 \times 10^6\). Correct.
Answer: \(4 \times 10^6\)
Example 8.9.2
Division yielding a negative exponent
  • ProblemCompute \(\dfrac{9 \times 10^2}{3 \times 10^7}\).
  • ThinkDivide coefficients: \(9 \div 3 = 3\). Subtract exponents: \(2 - 7 = -5\).
  • Solve3 × 10^{-5}
  • Check\(900 \div 30{,}000{,}000 = 0.00003 = 3 \times 10^{-5}\). Correct.
Answer: \(3 \times 10^{-5}\)
Example 8.9.3
Division requiring coefficient adjustment
  • ProblemCompute \(\dfrac{5 \times 10^8}{4 \times 10^3}\).
  • ThinkDivide coefficients: \(5 \div 4 = 1.25\). Subtract exponents: \(8 - 3 = 5\). Coefficient 1.25 is already in range. No adjustment needed.
  • Solve1.25 × 10^5
  • Check\(500{,}000{,}000 \div 4{,}000 = 125{,}000 = 1.25 \times 10^5\). Correct.
Answer: \(1.25 \times 10^5\)
Subtract the Exponents — Do Not Divide Them

When dividing in scientific notation, the exponents are subtracted, not divided. Students occasionally try to divide the exponents just as they divide the coefficients. Keep the operations separate: divide the coefficients and subtract the exponents.

Section 8.10

Multiplying and Dividing Scientific Notation Together

Some calculations require both multiplication and division in the same expression. This is common in physics formulas, chemistry calculations, and engineering unit conversions. The approach is the same: handle the coefficients with arithmetic and handle the powers of ten with exponent rules, then combine and adjust.

Example 8.10.1
Combined multiplication and division
  • ProblemCompute \(\dfrac{(6 \times 10^5) \times (4 \times 10^3)}{8 \times 10^4}\).
  • ThinkMultiply the numerator first, then divide by the denominator.
  • Solve Step 1 — Numerator: (6 × 4) × 10^(5+3) = 24 × 10^8 Step 2 — Divide: (24 ÷ 8) × 10^(8-4) = 3 × 10^4
  • Check\(600{,}000 \times 4{,}000 = 2{,}400{,}000{,}000\). Divided by \(80{,}000\): \(= 30{,}000 = 3 \times 10^4\). Correct.
Answer: \(3 \times 10^4\)
Example 8.10.2
Combined operations with negative exponents
  • ProblemCompute \(\dfrac{(2 \times 10^{-3}) \times (6 \times 10^5)}{4 \times 10^{-2}}\).
  • Solve Step 1 — Numerator: (2 × 6) × 10^(-3+5) = 12 × 10^2 = 1.2 × 10^3 Step 2 — Divide: (1.2 ÷ 4) × 10^(3-(-2)) = 0.3 × 10^5 Step 3 — Adjust: 0.3 × 10^5 = 3 × 10^{-1} × 10^5 = 3 × 10^4
Answer: \(3 \times 10^4\)
Subtracting a Negative Exponent

Remember that subtracting a negative exponent changes the sign: \(3 - (-2) = 3 + 2 = 5\). This step causes many errors in multi-part problems. Write it out carefully.

Section 8.11

Estimating with Scientific Notation

In scientific and engineering work, an exact answer is not always necessary or even possible. Scientific notation is excellent for quick estimation because the exponent immediately tells you the order of magnitude. The simplest form of estimation is to round the coefficients and add or subtract the exponents.

Comparing two numbers in scientific notation is also straightforward: compare the exponents first. If the exponents are different, the number with the larger exponent is larger, regardless of the coefficients. If the exponents are equal, compare the coefficients.

Example 8.11.1
Estimating a product
  • ProblemEstimate \((2.9 \times 10^8) \times (6.1 \times 10^5)\) to one significant figure.
  • ThinkRound 2.9 to 3 and round 6.1 to 6. Multiply rounded coefficients and add exponents.
  • Solve(3 × 10^8) × (6 × 10^5) = 18 × 10^13 ≈ 2 × 10^14
Answer: approximately \(2 \times 10^{14}\)   (exact: \(1.769 \times 10^{14}\))
Example 8.11.2
Comparing two measurements
  • ProblemA bacterium is \(2.3 \times 10^{-6}\) meters long. A virus is \(8.0 \times 10^{-9}\) meters long. Approximately how many times larger is the bacterium?
  • Solve 2.3 × 10^{-6} / 8.0 × 10^{-9} ≈ (2 / 8) × 10^{-6-(-9)} = 0.25 × 10^3 = 2.5 × 10^2
Answer: The bacterium is approximately 250 times larger than the virus.

Section 8.12

Scientific Notation on Calculators

Most calculators cannot display the full multiplication sign and raised exponent of scientific notation on a single screen. Instead, they use the letter E (sometimes EE, exp, or e) to stand for "times ten to the power of." On a calculator, the display 3.7E8 means \(3.7 \times 10^8\), and 5.2E-4 means \(5.2 \times 10^{-4}\).

Calculator DisplayScientific NotationStandard Form
4.5E7\(4.5 \times 10^7\)45,000,000

6.674E-11\(6.674 \times 10^{-11}\)0.00000000006674
9.461E15\(9.461 \times 10^{15}\)9,461,000,000,000,000

Entering Scientific Notation on a Calculator

The general method: (1) type the coefficient; (2) press the EE, E, or EXP key, not the multiplication key and not the letter E for variables; (3) type the exponent. For a negative exponent, press the negative key, not the subtraction key, before typing the exponent digits.

Negative Key vs. Subtraction Key

On many calculators, there is a difference between the subtraction key and the negative (change sign) key. The subtraction key is a binary operation between two numbers. The negative key makes a number negative. For entering negative exponents in scientific notation, you need the negative key, not the subtraction key. Using the wrong key is a common input error that produces a completely different calculation.

Section 8.14

Preparing for Science and Engineering Applications

Scientific notation is a foundational tool for every quantitative field of study. In this chapter you have learned why very large and very small numbers need a compact notation, how powers of ten encode size and scale, how to convert numbers in both directions, how to multiply and divide using exponent rules, how to estimate using orders of magnitude, how calculators display and accept scientific notation, and how to read and compare real scientific data.

These skills connect directly to several future areas of study. In Algebra I and Algebra II, you will work with scientific notation inside equations, use it to interpret function values, and apply it in formulas. The exponent rules from Chapter 7 and Chapter 8 will reappear regularly in polynomial expressions and exponential functions.

In MGU science courses, scientific notation will be used from the first page. Chemistry calculations involving Avogadro's number, physics problems involving gravitational constants and electrical charges, biology measurements of cells and organelles, and astronomy calculations involving distances and timescales all rely on your ability to read, write, and compute in scientific notation fluently.

One of the most useful things scientific notation gives you is a sense of scale, an intuitive feel for how large or small the numbers you encounter actually are. When you know that a human hair is roughly \(7 \times 10^{-5}\) meters wide and a virus is roughly \(1 \times 10^{-8}\) meters wide, you can immediately see that the hair is several thousand times wider than the virus. You do not need to compute the exact ratio to have a meaningful understanding of the difference. Mathematics at every level is partly about building this kind of number sense, and scientific notation is one of the most direct tools we have for developing it.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1Scientific notation expresses any number as \(a \times 10^n\) where \(1 \leq a < 10\) and \(n\) is an integer. It solves the problem of writing and reading very large and very small numbers in ordinary decimal form.
  • Sec 8.2A power of ten is \(10^n\) for an integer \(n\). Positive powers produce large numbers; negative powers produce fractions of one. \(10^0 = 1\). Each step up in exponent multiplies by ten; each step down divides by ten.
  • Sec 8.3Positive powers of ten produce numbers greater than or equal to one. Multiplying by \(10^n\) (positive \(n\)) moves the decimal point \(n\) places to the right. The exponent signals the order of magnitude.
  • Sec 8.4Negative powers of ten produce numbers between zero and one. Multiplying by \(10^{-n}\) moves the decimal point \(n\) places to the left. A negative exponent does not make the number negative.
  • Sec 8.5A number is in proper scientific notation when its coefficient satisfies \(1 \leq a < 10\) and is multiplied by a power of ten with an integer exponent. The order of magnitude, the exponent, tells you the scale immediately.
  • Sec 8.6To convert from standard form: move the decimal point until the coefficient is between 1 and 10, count the moves, and assign the exponent. Moved left → positive exponent. Moved right → negative exponent.
  • Sec 8.7To convert from scientific notation to standard form: move the decimal point in the direction and number of places given by the exponent. Positive exponent → right (bigger). Negative exponent → left (smaller).
  • Sec 8.8To multiply: multiply the coefficients and add the exponents. Adjust the coefficient if needed. The product rule \(10^m \times 10^n = 10^{m+n}\) is the key.
  • Sec 8.9To divide: divide the coefficients and subtract the exponents. Do not divide the exponents. The quotient rule \(10^m / 10^n = 10^{m-n}\) governs the powers of ten.
  • Sec 8.10Combined operations apply multiplication and division in sequence. Handle the numerator first, then divide. Take special care when subtracting a negative exponent.
  • Sec 8.11Estimation rounds coefficients and reads the order of magnitude from the exponent. Comparing two numbers: compare exponents first. If exponents differ, the larger exponent gives the larger number.
  • Sec 8.12Calculators display scientific notation as E notation (e.g., 3.7E8 = \(3.7 \times 10^8\)). Use the EE or EXP key to enter it. Use the negative key, not the subtraction key, for negative exponents.
  • Sec 8.13Scientific notation appears in astronomy, chemistry, biology, computing, and finance. Reading, comparing, and computing with real data are the practical payoff for mastering this notation.
  • Sec 8.14The skills of this chapter carry forward into Algebra I, chemistry, physics, biology, and engineering. Scientific notation is the language of scale used across all quantitative disciplines.

Key Terms Review

scientific notation
The form \(a \times 10^n\) where \(1 \leq a < 10\) and \(n\) is an integer. A compact, precise way to write very large or very small numbers.
standard form
An ordinary decimal number written without exponents, such as 4,500,000 or 0.000032.
coefficient
In scientific notation, the number between 1 (inclusive) and 10 (exclusive) that is multiplied by the power of ten.
power of ten
A number expressed as \(10^n\) for some integer \(n\). The building block of scientific notation.
positive exponent
An exponent greater than zero. In scientific notation, indicates a number greater than or equal to the coefficient.
negative exponent
An exponent less than zero. In scientific notation, indicates a number smaller than the coefficient. Does not make the number negative.
order of magnitude
The power of ten that describes the scale of a number. The exponent in scientific notation. Used to compare sizes quickly.
decimal point movement
The process of moving the decimal point when converting between standard form and scientific notation, or when multiplying or dividing by powers of ten.
E notation
The way calculators display scientific notation, using the letter E instead of \(\times 10\). For example, 3.7E8 means \(3.7 \times 10^8\).
estimation
An approximate calculation using rounded values. In scientific notation, often means rounding coefficients and reading the order of magnitude directly from the exponent.

Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter is the formal bridge from arithmetic into algebraic thinking. Every arithmetic operation you have practiced so far — whether adding fractions, simplifying decimals, applying percent change, or evaluating exponents — used specific numbers in specific situations. Algebra begins when you ask a different kind of question: what if the number is not yet known? What if the quantity changes depending on the situation? What if you want to write a rule that works for every number at once?

The answer to all three questions is the same tool: a variable.

Chapter 9 introduces variables as meaningful mathematical objects, not as mysterious symbols invented to make mathematics harder. It shows how letters represent unknown numbers, changing quantities, and general patterns. It explains how to build algebraic expressions from words and situations, how to name the parts of an expression, how to simplify expressions by combining like terms, and how to evaluate expressions by substituting specific values. It ends by showing how familiar arithmetic sentences can be rewritten as algebraic expressions, which is the first step toward writing and solving equations.

By the end of this chapter, you will see that algebra is not a new subject disconnected from arithmetic. It is arithmetic made general.

Opening Question

A plumber charges a flat fee of $75 for showing up, plus $40 for each hour worked. If you knew the plumber worked exactly three hours, you could compute the cost directly: 75 + 40 × 3 = 75 + 120 = $195. But what if you do not know yet how many hours the plumber will work? What if you want to write a single expression that gives the correct cost for any number of hours? The expression 75 + 40h does exactly that. When h = 3, it gives $195. When h = 5, it gives $275. One expression, written once, works for every possible number of hours. That is the power of a variable.

Why This Chapter Matters

Algebra I depends entirely on the ideas in this chapter. Before you can solve equations, you must understand what an expression is. Before you can simplify equations, you must know how to combine like terms. Before you can graph a linear relationship, you must understand how a variable represents a changing quantity.

Beyond Algebra I, variables appear in science and engineering formulas such as d = rt and F = ma; in financial calculations such as interest formulas and budget models; in computer programming, where variables hold values that change as a program runs; in statistics, where variables represent measured quantities in data sets; and in every future MGU mathematics course beyond this textbook.

This chapter does not ask you to solve equations yet. It asks you to understand what expressions are, where they come from, how to read them, how to simplify them, and how to evaluate them. These are the prerequisites for everything that follows.

Section 9.1

What a Variable Is

In arithmetic, you work with specific numbers. You might add 12 and 7, multiply 3.5 by 4, or divide 18 by 6. The numbers are always given. The task is to compute. In algebra, some quantities are not given. They might be unknown, might change depending on the situation, or might stand for any number at all. Mathematicians represent these quantities with letters. A letter used to represent a number is called a variable.

The word "variable" comes from the Latin word for "changing," and that name fits well. A variable is a placeholder that can take on different values. It is not a fixed amount. It is a symbol that represents a number we do not yet know, a number that changes, or a number we want to keep general.

Any letter can be a variable. In algebra, the letters x, y, n, and t are especially common. In geometry formulas, l, w, and h are used for length, width, and height. In science, d, r, and t represent distance, rate, and time. Letters are chosen to be memorable, but any letter works mathematically.

Two Types of Mathematical Expressions

Arithmetic expression — contains only numbers and operation symbols, with no variables. Examples: 4 + 3, 12 × 5, 7² + 3.

Algebraic expression — contains at least one variable, possibly combined with numbers and operation symbols. Examples: x + 5, 3n, 2a − 7, 5x² + 3x − 9.

Neither type has an equal sign. An expression is a phrase, not a sentence. A sentence with an equal sign is called an equation.

Student Tip — What a Variable Is Not

A variable is not a label or a unit abbreviation. If a problem involves meters, you might choose m as a convenient letter, but m still represents a numerical value such as 3 or 7.5 or 100. In the expression 5m, the letter stands for a number. Think of a variable as an empty box that holds a number. The box has a label on the outside (the letter), and a number can be placed inside.

Example 9.1.1
Identifying Arithmetic Expressions, Algebraic Expressions, and Equations
  • ProblemState whether each of the following is an arithmetic expression, an algebraic expression, or neither: (a) 4 + 9   (b) 3x − 7   (c) n/5   (d) "The sum of a number and twelve"   (e) 6 = 6
  • ThinkAsk: does it contain a variable? Does it have an equal sign? Is it a phrase (expression) or a sentence (equation)?
  • Solve (a) 4 + 9 → Contains only numbers. Arithmetic expression. (b) 3x − 7 → Contains variable x. Algebraic expression. (c) n/5 → Contains variable n. Algebraic expression. (d) Verbal → A phrase in words; translates to n + 12. (e) 6 = 6 → Has an equal sign. This is an equation, not an expression.

Section 9.2

Variables as Unknown Numbers

One of the oldest uses of a variable is to represent a number that is not yet known. Imagine a situation where someone is thinking of a number but has not told you what it is. You might call that hidden number n, or x, or any letter you choose, and then write mathematical statements about it. This is not magic. It is organized reasoning.

Consider this: you know that a number added to 8 gives 15. You can write n + 8 = 15. Here, n represents the unknown number. When a variable represents an unknown number, it has one specific value, even if you do not yet know what that value is. Your goal, when solving such a problem, is to find that specific value. This is what you will do in Chapter 10 when you solve equations.

Implied Multiplication

In algebra, when a number and a variable are written directly next to each other without any symbol between them, multiplication is implied. The expression 6x means 6 × x. The multiplication sign is dropped because it can be confused with the variable x. Always remember: a number directly beside a letter means multiply.

Example 9.2.1
Writing Expressions for Unknown Numbers
  • ProblemWrite an algebraic expression or equation for each: (a) A number decreased by 11. (b) Five more than a number equals 19. (c) A number multiplied by 6 equals 42. (d) A number divided by 3 — expression only.
  • ThinkIdentify the operation described, the known numbers, and place the variable where the unknown belongs.
  • Solve (a) "Decreased by" = subtraction. Expression: x − 11 (b) "Five more than" + "equals 19" Equation: x + 5 = 19 (c) "Multiplied by 6" + "equals 42" Equation: 6x = 42 (d) "Divided by 3" — expression. Expression: x/3
  • CheckRead each answer back in words to confirm the translation matches the original phrase.
Answer: (a) x − 11   (b) x + 5 = 19   (c) 6x = 42   (d) x/3

Section 9.3

Variables as Changing Quantities

The second major use of a variable is to represent a quantity that changes from one situation to the next. Unlike an unknown number, which has one specific hidden value, a changing quantity can take on many different values depending on the input or the circumstances.

Return to the plumber example. The expression 75 + 40h contains h, which is not a mystery. There is no single "correct" value for h. The value of h depends on how many hours the plumber actually works. If the job takes 2 hours, h = 2. If it takes 6 hours, h = 6. The variable h represents a changing input, and the expression describes how the total cost changes as h changes. This idea is the foundation of functions, which are central to Algebra I.

60tt = hours traveledDistance changes with tSpeed of 60 mph
2.5nn = notebooks boughtTotal cost changes with nPrice $2.50 each
Example 9.3.1
Building and Evaluating a Changing-Quantity Expression
  • ProblemA parking garage charges $4.00 for the first hour and $2.50 for each additional hour. Write an expression for the total cost for h additional hours, then evaluate for h = 0, h = 2, and h = 5.
  • ThinkThe $4.00 is fixed. The $2.50 per additional hour depends on h. Total cost = fixed charge + variable charge.
  • Solve Total cost = 4 + 2.5h h = 0: 4 + 2.5(0) = 4 + 0 = $4.00 h = 2: 4 + 2.5(2) = 4 + 5 = $9.00 h = 5: 4 + 2.5(5) = 4 + 12.50 = $16.50
  • CheckZero additional hours means only the base $4.00. Each additional hour adds $2.50 exactly. The pattern is consistent and reasonable.
Expression: 4 + 2.5h. Values: $4.00, $9.00, $16.50.

Section 9.4

Variables in Patterns

A third use of a variable is to describe a general pattern — a rule that holds for all numbers, or for every member of a sequence. This is perhaps the most powerful use of variables, because a single expression can describe infinitely many situations at once. Consider the even numbers: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, and so on. Instead of listing them all, you can describe them with the expression 2n, where n is any positive integer. When n = 1, you get 2. When n = 100, you get 200. The variable n lets you talk about all even numbers at once.

Example 9.4.1
Writing a Pattern Rule with a Variable
  • ProblemThe table shows a number pattern. Write an expression using the variable n, where n is the position number.

    Position: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5   |   Value: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25
  • ThinkThe value increases by 5 each time n increases by 1. When n = 1, value = 5. When n = 2, value = 10. The value is always 5 times the position number.
  • SolveExpression: 5n
  • Checkn = 1: 5(1) = 5 ✓    n = 3: 5(3) = 15 ✓    n = 5: 5(5) = 25 ✓
Answer: 5n
Example 9.4.2
Extending a Pattern Using an Expression
  • ProblemUsing the expression 5n from Example 9.4.1, find the value at position 12 and position 50.
  • Solve Position 12: 5(12) = 60 Position 50: 5(50) = 250
At position 12, the value is 60. At position 50, the value is 250.
Tip — Testing a Pattern Rule

When you write a pattern rule with a variable, always test it using at least two or three values from the table. If the expression gives the correct output for each input you test, it is a good candidate for the pattern rule.

Section 9.5

Writing Expressions from Words

One of the most important skills in algebra is translating between English and mathematics. Words describe situations; expressions describe them mathematically. Learning to move fluently between these two forms is essential not only for algebra, but for solving word problems in science, finance, and everyday life.

OperationCommon Verbal Phrases
Additionsum, plus, added to, increased by, more than, total, combined, greater than
Subtractiondifference, minus, subtracted from, decreased by, less than, reduced by, fewer than, take away
Multiplicationproduct, times, multiplied by, of, twice, double, triple, groups of
Divisionquotient, divided by, per, for each, ratio of, split equally
Student Warning — Order Reversal in Subtraction

"Five less than a number" means n − 5, not 5 − n. "A number subtracted from 10" means 10 − n, not n − 10. The phrase "less than" reverses the expected order. You are taking 5 away from the number, starting with the number. Always re-read your translation after writing it.

Example 9.5.1
Translating Single and Multi-Operation Phrases
  • ProblemWrite an algebraic expression for each: (a) The sum of a number and 8. (b) Twelve less than a number. (c) Four times a number, decreased by 3. (d) The quotient of a number and 7. (e) Five more than three times a number.
  • ThinkIdentify the operations, identify the numbers, and place them in the correct order. Watch carefully for reversal phrases.
  • Solve (a) "Sum" = addition. n + 8 (b) "Less than" reverses order. n − 12 (c) "Four times" then subtract. 4n − 3 (d) "Quotient" = division. n/7 (e) "Three times" then add 5. 3n + 5
  • CheckRead each expression back in words to verify it matches the original phrase.
Answers: (a) n + 8   (b) n − 12   (c) 4n − 3   (d) n/7   (e) 3n + 5
Example 9.5.2
Translating Expressions into Words
  • ProblemWrite a verbal phrase for each: (a) x + 4   (b) 2x − 9   (c) x/6 + 1
  • Solve (a) x + 4 → A number increased by four. (b) 2x − 9 → Twice a number, decreased by nine. (c) x/6 + 1 → A number divided by six, increased by one.
Multiple correct translations exist for each expression, as long as they describe the same operations in the correct order.

Section 9.6

Terms, Coefficients, and Constants

An algebraic expression is built from smaller parts called terms. Understanding the parts of an expression is like understanding the structure of a sentence: it lets you read, interpret, and manipulate expressions correctly.

A term is a single number, a single variable, or a product of numbers and variables that is separated from other terms by addition or subtraction signs. The minus sign in front of a term belongs to that term. When you list the terms of an expression, include the sign as part of the term.

Four Key Parts of Any Expression

Term — a single quantity connected to others by + or −. In 5x² − 3x + 7, the terms are 5x², −3x, and 7.

Coefficient — the numerical factor of a variable term. In −3x, the coefficient is −3. In x, the coefficient is 1 (implied). In −x, the coefficient is −1.

Constant — a term with no variable. Its value never changes. In 5x² − 3x + 7, the constant is 7.

Factor — one of the quantities multiplied within a product. In 5x, the factors are 5 and x.

Example 9.6.1
Identifying Parts of an Expression
  • ProblemFor the expression 8x − 4y + x − 3, identify: (a) the number of terms, (b) each term and its coefficient, (c) any constant terms.
  • ThinkLook for the addition and subtraction signs that separate terms. Each sign belongs to the term that follows it.
  • Solve Terms: 8x, −4y, x, −3 (four terms total) Coefficients: 8x → coefficient is 8 −4y → coefficient is −4 x → coefficient is 1 (implied) −3 → constant term, no coefficient Constant term: −3
  • CheckReassemble: 8x + (−4y) + x + (−3) = 8x − 4y + x − 3. Matches the original. ✓
Four terms. Coefficients: 8, −4, 1 (for x), none for constant. Constant term: −3.
Student Warning — The Implied Coefficient of 1

Students often forget that a term with no written coefficient has an implied coefficient of 1. The term x means 1x. The term −x means −1x. This matters when combining like terms. For example: 5x − x = 5x − 1x = 4x. Students who forget the implied 1 sometimes write 5x − x = 5, which is wrong.

Section 9.7

Like Terms

Not all terms in an expression can be combined. You can only add or subtract terms that are like terms. Like terms are terms that have exactly the same variable part — the same variables raised to the same powers. The coefficients do not need to match. Only the variable parts matter.

Why can you only combine like terms? Think about what the terms represent. The term 3x means "3 groups of whatever x is." The term 7x means "7 groups of whatever x is." Combining them gives "10 groups of whatever x is," which is 10x. But 3x and 7x² represent fundamentally different things. You cannot combine them any more than you can add 3 apples and 7 tables. They are not the same kind of quantity.

Like TermsUnlike TermsReason Unlike
3x and 7x3x and 3x²Different exponents
5x² and −2x²5x and 5yDifferent variables
4xy and 9xy4xy and 4xDifferent variable parts
6 and −117 and 7xOne constant, one variable term
x and −xx² and x³Different powers
Tip — Think of Variable Parts as Units

Think of variable parts as units. Just as inches and square inches are different units (you cannot add 5 inches + 3 square inches), x and x² are different "units." You can add inches to inches and square inches to square inches, but not to each other.

Section 9.8

Combining Like Terms

Once you can identify like terms, you can simplify expressions by combining like terms. Combining like terms means adding or subtracting the coefficients of like terms while keeping the variable part unchanged. The rule comes from the distributive property working in reverse: 3x + 7x = (3 + 7)x = 10x. The variable is factored out and the coefficients are added.

Simplifying an expression means writing it in its most compact form by combining all like terms until no two remaining terms are like each other.

Procedure for Combining Like Terms

3x + 7x = 10x    Add coefficients; keep variable part.

9a² − 4a² = 5a²    Subtract coefficients; keep variable part.

5y − y = 4y    Note: y = 1y, so 5y − 1y = 4y.

Steps: (1) Identify like-term groups. (2) Add or subtract coefficients within each group. (3) Write the simplified expression. (4) Confirm no further combining is possible.

Example 9.8.1
Combining Like Terms — Single Variable
  • ProblemSimplify 7x + 3 − 2x + 8.
  • ThinkThe x-terms are 7x and −2x. The constants are 3 and 8. Group and combine each type separately.
  • Solve 7x + 3 − 2x + 8 = (7x − 2x) + (3 + 8) = 5x + 11
  • CheckLet x = 2. Original: 14 + 3 − 4 + 8 = 21. Simplified: 10 + 11 = 21. ✓
Answer: 5x + 11
Example 9.8.2
Combining Like Terms — Two Variables
  • ProblemSimplify 4a − 3b + 2a + 7b − 5.
  • ThinkGroup the a-terms, the b-terms, and the constants separately.
  • Solve a-terms: 4a + 2a = 6a b-terms: −3b + 7b = 4b Constants: −5 (only one; nothing to combine) Result: 6a + 4b − 5
  • CheckThree unlike term groups remain. No further simplification is possible.
Answer: 6a + 4b − 5
Example 9.8.3
An Expression That Is Already Simplified
  • ProblemSimplify 5x² + 3x − 7.
  • Think5x² has x². 3x has x to the first power. −7 is a constant. All three are unlike.
  • SolveNo terms can be combined. The expression is already in simplest form.
Answer: 5x² + 3x − 7 (already simplified)
Student Warning — Exponents Do Not Change When You Add

A common mistake is to add the exponents when combining like terms. For example: 3x + 5x ≠ 8x². When you add like terms, you add only the coefficients. The variable part stays exactly the same. The correct answer is 8x. Exponents change only under multiplication rules (Chapter 7), never under addition or subtraction.

Section 9.9

Evaluating Expressions

To evaluate an algebraic expression means to find its numerical value by replacing each variable with a specific number and then computing the result using order of operations. This is called substitution: you substitute (replace) each variable with a given value.

The process: (1) Write the expression. (2) Replace each variable with the given value, using parentheses around substituted values to avoid sign errors. (3) Simplify using order of operations — grouping symbols first, then exponents, then multiplication and division left to right, then addition and subtraction left to right. (4) State the result.

Example 9.9.1
Evaluating a Simple Expression
  • ProblemEvaluate 3x + 7 when x = 4.
  • Solve 3x + 7 = 3(4) + 7 = 12 + 7 = 19
  • Check3 × 4 = 12; 12 + 7 = 19. Order of operations followed (multiplication before addition). ✓
Answer: 19
Example 9.9.2
Evaluating with a Negative Value
  • ProblemEvaluate 5x − 2 when x = −3.
  • ThinkReplace x with (−3). Always use parentheses to keep the negative sign attached and visible.
  • Solve 5x − 2 = 5(−3) − 2 = −15 − 2 = −17
  • Check5 × (−3) = −15; −15 − 2 = −17. Signs handled correctly. ✓
Answer: −17
Example 9.9.3
Evaluating with Exponents
  • ProblemEvaluate x² + 3x − 4 when x = 2.
  • ThinkReplace x with (2) everywhere. Apply order of operations: exponents before multiplication, multiplication before addition and subtraction.
  • Solve x² + 3x − 4 = (2)² + 3(2) − 4 = 4 + 6 − 4 = 6
  • Check2² = 4; 3(2) = 6; 4 + 6 − 4 = 6. ✓
Answer: 6
Example 9.9.4
Evaluating with Two Variables
  • ProblemEvaluate 2a + 3b − 1 when a = 5 and b = −2.
  • Solve 2a + 3b − 1 = 2(5) + 3(−2) − 1 = 10 + (−6) − 1 = 10 − 6 − 1 = 3
  • CheckEach multiplication was done first. Additions and subtractions then left to right. Result: 3. ✓
Answer: 3
Student Warning — Parentheses with Negative Substitution

When you substitute a negative value for a variable, always use parentheses around it. For example, if x = −4 and you need to compute x², write (−4)² = 16, not −4² = −16. Without parentheses, −4² is parsed as −(4²) = −16. With parentheses, (−4)² = (−4)(−4) = +16. These are different values, and omitting the parentheses is one of the most common errors in early algebra.

Section 9.10

Substitution

Substitution is the general name for replacing a variable with a value and computing the result. It applies not only to single expressions, but to formulas, geometric rules, patterns, and tables of values. When a formula gives a rule, substitution is how you apply that rule to a specific situation. Consider the formula for the area of a rectangle: A = lw. If l = 9 and w = 4, substitution gives A = (9)(4) = 36 square units.

Example 9.10.1
Substitution in the Distance Formula
  • ProblemThe distance formula is d = rt, where d = distance, r = rate (speed), t = time. Find the distance if r = 55 mph and t = 3 hours.
  • Solved = rt = (55)(3) = 165
  • Check55 miles each hour for 3 hours = 165 miles. Reasonable. ✓
Answer: 165 miles.
Example 9.10.2
Substitution in a Table of Values
  • ProblemComplete the table for the expression 3n + 2 using n = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4.
  • Solve n = 0: 3(0) + 2 = 2 n = 1: 3(1) + 2 = 5 n = 2: 3(2) + 2 = 8 n = 3: 3(3) + 2 = 11 n = 4: 3(4) + 2 = 14
  • CheckEach value increases by 3, which matches the coefficient 3 in front of n. The pattern is consistent. ✓
Values: 2, 5, 8, 11, 14.

Section 9.11

Expressions in Real Situations

Algebraic expressions are not classroom abstractions. They come from, and return to, real situations. Every formula, pricing rule, recipe scale, science equation, and financial model is an algebraic expression at its core. When you write an expression from a real situation, ask yourself three questions: What is changing or unknown? (That is your variable — define it clearly with its units.) What is fixed? (That is your constant or fixed coefficient.) What operation connects them? (That is your expression structure.) Always define your variable before writing the expression. A vague variable produces a vague answer.

Example 9.11.1
A Cell Phone Plan
  • ProblemA cell phone plan charges $30 per month for unlimited texting and $0.10 per minute for phone calls. Define a variable, write an expression, evaluate for 120 minutes, and interpret the result.
  • DefineLet m = the number of minutes of phone calls made in one month.
  • Solve Total cost = 30 + 0.10m When m = 120: 30 + 0.10(120) = 30 + 12 = $42
  • InterpretIn a month with 120 minutes of phone calls, the total cost of the plan would be $42.
  • Check$0.10 × 120 = $12 for calls. Plus $30 base fee. Total $42. Reasonable. ✓
Expression: 30 + 0.10m.    Cost for 120 minutes: $42.
Example 9.11.2
A Geometry Situation
  • ProblemA fence is being built around a rectangular garden. The length is fixed at 18 feet. The width is not yet decided. Write an expression for the total fencing needed, then evaluate for a width of 7 feet.
  • DefineLet w = the width of the garden in feet. Perimeter formula: P = 2l + 2w, where l = 18.
  • Solve P = 2(18) + 2w = 36 + 2w When w = 7: P = 36 + 2(7) = 36 + 14 = 50 feet
Expression: 36 + 2w.    For width of 7 feet: 50 feet of fencing needed.

Section 9.12

Common Mistakes with Variables

As students move from arithmetic into algebraic thinking, certain mistakes appear repeatedly. This section identifies them clearly, explains why they happen, and shows how to avoid them. Recognizing these mistakes in your own work, and in sample student work, is one of the fastest ways to improve your algebra skills.

Mistake 1 — Treating a Variable as a Label, Not a Number
"In 5m, the m stands for meters." Correct: m represents a numerical value. If m = 3, then 5m = 5(3) = 15. Variables are numbers, not unit abbreviations.
Mistake 2 — Forgetting the Implied Coefficient of 1
5x − x = 5 (treating x as having no coefficient) Correct: x = 1x, so 5x − x = 5x − 1x = 4x. The coefficient 1 is always present even when not written.
Mistake 3 — Adding Exponents When Combining Like Terms
3x + 5x = 8x² Correct: 3x + 5x = 8x. Add only the coefficients; the variable part stays the same. Exponents change under multiplication, not addition.
Mistake 4 — Combining Unlike Terms
4x + 3 = 7x Correct: 4x + 3 cannot be simplified. 4x and 3 are unlike terms. A variable term and a constant cannot be combined by addition.
Mistake 5 — Omitting Parentheses When Substituting a Negative Value
Evaluate x² at x = −4: x² = −4² = −16 Correct: (−4)² = (−4)(−4) = +16. Without parentheses, −4² is parsed as −(4²) = −16, which is a different value.
Mistake 6 — Reversing the Order in Subtraction Phrases
"Eight less than a number" → 8 − n Correct: n − 8. "Less than" reverses the expected order. You start with the number and subtract 8 from it.
Mistake 7 — Dropping a Negative Sign When Rearranging Terms
Rewriting 5 − 3x as 3x + 5 Correct: 5 − 3x = −3x + 5. The negative sign belongs to the term 3x. Moving a term without its sign changes the expression's value.

Section 9.13

From Arithmetic Sentences to Algebraic Expressions

This final section brings together all the ideas from the chapter. It shows you how to look at familiar arithmetic sentences, patterns, and rules and rewrite them using variables. This is the last step before Chapter 10, where you will write and solve equations.

Arithmetic sentences are specific. Algebraic expressions are general. When you move from one to the other, you are moving from "this one case" to "all cases of this type." In each case, the algebraic expression captures the general rule. The specific number is replaced by a variable. The relationship stays the same.

Arithmetic (Specific)Algebra (General)
3 × 5 = 15 — one multiplication fact3n — any number multiplied by 3
Perimeter of a square with side 6: 4 × 6 = 24Perimeter of any square with side s: 4s
Earn $12/hr × 8 hrs = $96Earn $12/hr × h hours = 12h
Example 9.13.1
Generalizing a Savings Pattern
  • ProblemA student saves $15 every week. (a) Write arithmetic expressions for total savings after 1, 2, 3, and 4 weeks. (b) Identify the pattern. (c) Write a general algebraic expression for total savings after w weeks.
  • Solve (a) Week 1: 15 × 1 = 15 Week 2: 15 × 2 = 30 Week 3: 15 × 3 = 45 Week 4: 15 × 4 = 60 (b) The pattern multiplies 15 by the number of weeks. (c) Let w = the number of weeks. Total savings = 15w
  • CheckFor w = 3: 15(3) = 45. Matches the arithmetic computation. ✓
Answer: 15w
Example 9.13.2
Generalizing a Geometry Relationship
  • ProblemThe area of a triangle is half the base times the height. (a) Compute the area when base = 8 and height = 5. (b) Write the general algebraic formula using variables b and h. (c) Use the formula to find the area when b = 12 and h = 7.
  • Solve (a) Area = (1/2) × 8 × 5 = 20 square units (b) A = (1/2)bh (c) A = (1/2)(12)(7) = 84/2 = 42 square units
  • Check12 × 7 = 84; half of 84 = 42. Correct. ✓
Formula: A = (1/2)bh.    Area for b = 12, h = 7: 42 square units.

Chapter 9 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 9.1A variable is a letter that represents a number — unknown, changing, or general. An algebraic expression contains at least one variable. It is a phrase, not a sentence; it has no equal sign. Variables are not labels; they stand for actual numerical values.
  • Sec 9.2When a variable represents an unknown number, it has one specific value you are trying to find. Writing a number directly beside a letter means multiplication. This is the starting point of equation writing in Chapter 10.
  • Sec 9.3When a variable represents a changing quantity, it can take on many different values. Different inputs produce different outputs. The expression describes the rule; substitution applies it. This is the foundation of functions in Algebra I.
  • Sec 9.4Variables in patterns describe general rules that hold for every member of a sequence. A single expression can capture infinitely many cases. Always test a pattern rule against at least two or three values before accepting it.
  • Sec 9.5Translating between words and symbols requires knowing signal words for all four operations. Subtraction and division are not commutative — order matters. "Less than" and "subtracted from" reverse the expected order. Always re-read a translation after writing it.
  • Sec 9.6An expression is built from terms separated by + and − signs. Each term has a coefficient (numerical factor) and a variable part. A constant term has no variable. The implied coefficient of x is 1; of −x it is −1.
  • Sec 9.7Like terms have exactly the same variable part, including the same exponents. Only like terms can be combined. The coefficient plays no role in deciding likeness — only the variable part matters.
  • Sec 9.8To combine like terms, add or subtract the coefficients and keep the variable part unchanged. An expression is simplified when no two remaining terms are like each other. Exponents do not change when adding; only when multiplying.
  • Sec 9.9Evaluating an expression means substituting a specific value and simplifying with the order of operations. Always use parentheses when substituting negative values, especially before exponents. Without parentheses, sign errors are almost inevitable.
  • Sec 9.10Substitution applies to formulas, tables, and any rule expressed algebraically. Filling a table of values by substituting each input is the first step toward graphing relationships on the coordinate plane in Chapter 12.
  • Sec 9.11Real-world expressions come from situations with fixed and varying quantities. Always define your variable with its units, write the expression, evaluate for given values, and interpret the results in context with a full sentence.
  • Sec 9.12Seven recurring errors: treating variables as labels; ignoring the implied coefficient of 1; adding exponents when combining like terms; combining unlike terms; omitting parentheses on negative substitution; reversing subtraction order; dropping negative signs when rearranging.
  • Sec 9.13Arithmetic sentences are specific; algebraic expressions are general. Replacing a specific number with a variable generalizes a rule so it works for every case. This generalization is the core idea behind formulas, equations, and functions.

Key Terms Review

variable
A letter used to represent a number that is unknown, changing, or general. Variables are numbers, not labels.
algebraic expression
A mathematical phrase containing at least one variable, possibly combined with numbers and operation symbols, but no equal sign.
arithmetic expression
A mathematical phrase containing only numbers and operation symbols, with no variables.
term
A single number, a single variable, or a product of numbers and variables in an expression. Terms are separated by addition or subtraction signs.
coefficient
The numerical factor of a variable term. The coefficient of 5x is 5. The coefficient of x is 1 (implied). The coefficient of −x is −1.
constant
A term with no variable. Its value does not depend on any input and does not change.
like terms
Terms that have exactly the same variable part, including identical exponents. Only like terms may be combined by addition or subtraction.
unlike terms
Terms with different variable parts. They cannot be combined by addition or subtraction and must remain separate in a simplified expression.
combining like terms
Adding or subtracting the coefficients of like terms while keeping the variable part unchanged. The process by which expressions are simplified.
simplifying
Writing an expression in its most compact equivalent form by combining all like terms. A simplified expression has no two remaining terms that are like each other.
evaluate
To find the numerical value of an expression by substituting specific values for variables and applying order of operations.
substitution
Replacing a variable with a specific numerical value. Negative values must always be enclosed in parentheses before applying any operations.
factor
One of the quantities multiplied to form a product. In the term 5x, the factors are 5 and x.
verbal expression
A phrase in words that describes a mathematical relationship or operation. Verbal expressions can be translated into algebraic expressions.
pattern rule
An algebraic expression that describes the output for every position in a number sequence. Always verify a pattern rule by testing it against known values.
algebraic model
An expression or formula that uses a defined variable to represent a real-world rule. Always define variables with units before writing the model.

Chapter 3 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 2 gave you tools for understanding the structure of whole numbers. You learned to factor, to find common ground between numbers, and to identify the building blocks hidden inside larger values. Chapter 3 uses that foundation to enter one of the most important and most misunderstood areas of arithmetic: fractions.

Fractions appear everywhere. They describe portions of a recipe, doses of medication, lengths in carpentry, rates in science, probabilities in statistics, and slopes in algebra. Despite their ubiquity, fractions are the single topic where student confidence most commonly breaks down. The reason is almost always the same: students were taught procedures without understanding, and when the procedures blur together in memory, there is nothing left to fall back on.

This chapter will not ask you to simply memorize rules. It will build each idea from the ground up, show you why the rule works, and then give you practice applying it. By the end of this chapter, you will understand what a fraction is, how to represent fractions in multiple ways, how to simplify them, and how to perform all four operations with them. Every idea here will carry directly into Chapter 4 on mixed numbers, Chapter 5 on decimals, Chapter 6 on ratios and percents, and the algebra chapters that follow.

Take your time in this chapter. Fractions reward careful thinking.

Opening Question

A recipe calls for 3/4 of a cup of flour. You want to make half of the recipe. How much flour do you need? While you are working in the kitchen, you also realize you have 1/3 of a cup of sugar remaining but need 5/6 of a cup. How much more sugar do you need to buy?

These two questions require multiplying a fraction by a whole number and subtracting fractions with different denominators. By the end of this chapter, both will be straightforward.

Why This Chapter Matters

Fractions are not a detour from "real math." They are the foundation of nearly everything that follows in this textbook and in later mathematics. Ratios are comparisons written as fractions. A student who is fluent and confident with fractions enters Algebra I prepared. A student who is not will struggle at every turn, not because algebra is harder, but because fractions keep appearing inside every algebraic procedure.

Section 3.1

What a Fraction Means

A fraction is a way of expressing a quantity that is part of a whole, a comparison between two quantities, or the result of a division. The word fraction comes from a Latin word meaning "to break." A fraction describes something that has been broken into equal parts.

When we write 3/4, we are communicating something precise: a whole has been divided into 4 equal parts, and we are considering 3 of them. But fractions are richer than just "parts of a pizza." They have at least five interpretations that you will use throughout this textbook.

Interpretation 1: Part of a Whole

This is the most familiar interpretation. Imagine a rectangle divided into 4 equal sections. If 3 sections are shaded, the shaded region represents 3/4 of the whole rectangle. The key word here is equal. If the 4 sections are not equal in size, the fraction 3/4 does not describe the shaded area accurately. Equal parts are the foundation of every fraction.

Diagram

Draw a rectangle divided into 4 equal vertical sections. Shade 3 of them. Label the shaded region "3/4." The unshaded section represents the remaining 1/4.

Interpretation 2: Part of a Set

A fraction can describe a portion of a group of objects, not just a portion of a single shape. If a bag contains 8 marbles and 3 of them are red, the fraction of marbles that are red is 3/8. Here, the "whole" is the set of 8 marbles, and 3 is the count of the particular type we are interested in.

Interpretation 3: Division

The fraction 3/4 means exactly 3 ÷ 4. The fraction bar is a division symbol. A fraction and a division expression are two ways of writing the same thing: 3/4 = 3 ÷ 4 = 0.75. This interpretation will be developed fully in Section 3.3.

Interpretation 4: A Number on the Number Line

A fraction is a number, and like every number, it has a specific location on the number line. The number 3/4 sits three-fourths of the way between 0 and 1. This will be explored fully in Section 3.4.

Interpretation 5: A Ratio

A fraction can express a comparison between two quantities. If a class has 12 boys and 16 girls, the ratio of boys to total students is 12/28, which simplifies to 3/7. Ratios are studied in depth in Chapter 6, but recognizing that fractions and ratios share the same notation prepares you for that work.

Why Multiple Interpretations Matter

Students who think fractions only mean "part of a pizza" become confused when fractions appear in division, on number lines, or in algebraic expressions. Understanding all five interpretations gives you flexibility. When one interpretation does not fit a context, another one will.

Section 3.2

Numerators and Denominators

The Two Parts of a Fraction

Denominator — the bottom number. It names the type of piece by indicating how many equal parts the whole has been divided into.

Numerator — the top number. It counts how many of those parts are being considered.

In the fraction 5/8, the denominator is 8 (the whole has been divided into 8 equal parts) and the numerator is 5 (we are counting 5 of those parts). Although the numerator is written on top, the denominator should be understood first. The denominator establishes what kind of pieces you are working with. Once you know the pieces are eighths, the numerator tells you how many eighths you have. Think of it this way: you would not say "I have 5 ... of something" before knowing what the something is. The denominator names the unit; the numerator counts it.

Unit Fractions

A unit fraction is a fraction with a numerator of 1, such as 1/2, 1/3, 1/8, and 1/100. Every fraction can be understood as a multiple of the corresponding unit fraction: 3/4 = 3 × (1/4). This means "three copies of one-fourth." This interpretation is especially helpful when multiplying fractions by whole numbers.

TypeConditionExamplesValue
Proper fractionNumerator < Denominator1/2, 3/5, 7/10Between 0 and 1
Improper fractionNumerator ≥ Denominator5/3, 8/8, 11/4Greater than or equal to 1
Unit fractionNumerator = 11/2, 1/7, 1/100Between 0 and 1
Note on Improper Fractions

The word "improper" does not mean wrong or forbidden. Improper fractions are perfectly valid and often more useful than mixed numbers when performing operations. The fraction 11/4 is as legitimate as the fraction 3/4. Chapter 4 will examine the relationship between improper fractions and mixed numbers in full detail.

When Numerator Equals Denominator

Any nonzero number divided by itself equals 1: 4/4 = 1, 9/9 = 1, 100/100 = 1. This fact is central to creating equivalent fractions in Section 3.5.

Section 3.3

Fractions as Division

The Core Definition

The expression a/b means a ÷ b. This is not a metaphor or approximation. It is a definition. A fraction is a division expression written in a convenient form.

6/2 = 6 ÷ 2 = 3    3/5 = 3 ÷ 5 = 0.6    7/4 = 7 ÷ 4 = 1.75

The division interpretation explains several things that otherwise seem arbitrary. It explains why any number over itself equals 1: n/n = n ÷ n = 1. It explains why any number over 1 equals itself: n/1 = n ÷ 1 = n. It explains how to convert fractions to decimals (divide numerator by denominator), and it explains why fractions appear in algebraic formulas.

Sharing Scenarios

The division interpretation is the most natural one for sharing situations. If you have 3 granola bars to share equally among 4 people, each person receives 3 ÷ 4 = 3/4 of a granola bar. Dividing 3 things equally into 4 groups gives each group three one-fourth pieces.

Diagram

Draw 3 rectangles side by side, representing 3 granola bars. Divide each rectangle into 4 equal sections. Shade one section from each bar and group them together. Each person's share consists of three sections each of size 1/4, which totals 3/4.

Example 3.3.1
Writing a division expression as a fraction
  • ProblemWrite each as a fraction and evaluate if possible: (a) 5 ÷ 8, (b) 12 ÷ 4, (c) 1 ÷ 3
  • Solve a. 5 ÷ 8 = 5/8 (does not simplify to a whole number) b. 12 ÷ 4 = 12/4 = 3 (simplifies to a whole number) c. 1 ÷ 3 = 1/3 (a unit fraction)
  • CheckFor (b): 4 × 3 = 12. ✓
Answer: 5/8, 3, and 1/3
Student Warning — Direction of Division

Students sometimes write 5 ÷ 8 as 8/5, reversing the numerator and denominator. Remember: the dividend (the number being divided) goes on top. 5 ÷ 8 = 5/8, not 8/5. Always place the number being divided in the numerator.

Section 3.4

Fractions on the Number Line

Every fraction has a unique location on the number line. Proper fractions live between 0 and 1. Improper fractions live at 1 or beyond. To place 3/4 on the number line: locate 0 and 1, divide the segment into 4 equal parts (denominator is 4), count 3 parts from 0 (numerator is 3), and mark the point.

Diagram — Proper Fraction on the Number Line

Draw a number line from 0 to 1. Mark four equally spaced tick marks at 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, and 4/4. Label each mark. Place a bold filled circle at 3/4.

An improper fraction like 7/4 belongs between 1 and 2 on the number line. Since 4/4 = 1 and 8/4 = 2, divide the segment from 1 to 2 into 4 equal parts and count 3 parts past 1, because 7/4 = 4/4 + 3/4 = 1 + 3/4.

Diagram — Improper Fraction on the Number Line

Draw a number line from 0 to 2. Mark eighths from 0 to 2. Label 4/4 = 1 and place a bold filled circle at 7/4, which falls three-fourths of the way between 1 and 2.

Why the Number Line Matters

First, it confirms that fractions are real numbers with specific positions. Second, it makes comparison immediate: the number further right is larger, so 3/4 > 1/2. Third, it connects fractions to decimals: 1/2 and 0.5 mark the exact same point on the number line. They are different names for the same location.

Example 3.4.1
Placing and comparing fractions on the number line
  • ProblemPlace 2/5 and 3/5 on a number line and state which is larger.
  • ThinkBoth fractions have denominator 5, so divide the segment from 0 to 1 into 5 equal parts.
  • Solve Number line: 0 — 1/5 — 2/5 — 3/5 — 4/5 — 1 ● ● 2/5 3/5
  • Check3/5 is to the right of 2/5, so 3/5 > 2/5. Also: 3 > 2 with the same denominator confirms this. ✓
Answer: 3/5 is larger.

Section 3.5

Equivalent Fractions

Two fractions are equivalent if they represent the same quantity, even though they are written with different numerators and denominators. The fractions 1/2, 2/4, 3/6, 4/8, and 50/100 all mark the same point on the number line: the midpoint between 0 and 1.

Diagram — Equivalent Fractions

Draw a stack of three identical rectangles. Divide the first into 2 equal parts and shade 1. Divide the second into 4 equal parts and shade 2. Divide the third into 6 equal parts and shade 3. The shaded region is the same size in all three rectangles, showing that 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6.

Creating Equivalent Fractions

To make a larger equivalent fraction: multiply both numerator and denominator by the same nonzero whole number.

1/2 = (1×3)/(2×3) = 3/6

To make a smaller equivalent fraction: divide both numerator and denominator by the same nonzero whole number.

6/10 = (6÷2)/(10÷2) = 3/5

Both operations multiply the fraction by a form of 1 (such as 3/3 or 2/2), which leaves the value unchanged.

Checking for Equivalence — Cross Multiplication

To check whether 3/4 and 9/12 are equivalent, multiply across: 3 × 12 = 36 and 4 × 9 = 36. The cross products are equal, so the fractions are equivalent.

Example 3.5.1
Writing equivalent fractions
  • ProblemWrite three fractions equivalent to 2/5.
  • Solve 2/5 = (2×2)/(5×2) = 4/10 2/5 = (2×3)/(5×3) = 6/15 2/5 = (2×10)/(5×10) = 20/50
  • CheckCross-multiply each pair with 2/5: 2×10=20 and 5×4=20 ✓; 2×15=30 and 5×6=30 ✓
Answer: 4/10, 6/15, and 20/50 are all equivalent to 2/5.
Example 3.5.2
Building a fraction with a specific denominator
  • ProblemWrite a fraction equivalent to 3/8 with a denominator of 40.
  • ThinkWhat must you multiply 8 by to get 40? The answer is 5. Multiply both numerator and denominator by 5.
  • Solve3/8 = (3×5)/(8×5) = 15/40
  • CheckCross-multiply: 3 × 40 = 120 and 8 × 15 = 120. ✓
Answer: 15/40

Section 3.6

Reducing Fractions

Reducing a fraction means writing it as an equivalent fraction with the smallest possible numerator and denominator. A fraction in this form is in lowest terms or simplest form. A fraction is in lowest terms when the GCF of its numerator and denominator is 1.

The most efficient method: divide both numerator and denominator by their Greatest Common Factor in one step. If you do not immediately see the GCF, divide by any common factor and repeat until no common factors remain.

Example 3.6.1
Reducing to lowest terms — GCF method
  • ProblemReduce 48/72 to lowest terms.
  • ThinkFind the GCF of 48 and 72 using prime factorization from Chapter 2.
  • Solve 48 = 2⁴ × 3 72 = 2³ × 3² GCF(48, 72) = 2³ × 3 = 24 48/72 = (48÷24)/(72÷24) = 2/3
  • CheckGCF(2, 3) = 1. No further reduction possible. ✓
Answer: 48/72 = 2/3
Example 3.6.2
Reducing with large numbers
  • ProblemReduce 105/140 to lowest terms.
  • Solve 105 = 3 × 5 × 7 140 = 2² × 5 × 7 GCF(105, 140) = 5 × 7 = 35 105/140 = (105÷35)/(140÷35) = 3/4
  • CheckGCF(3, 4) = 1. Fully reduced. ✓
Answer: 105/140 = 3/4
Student Warning

A fraction is not fully reduced just because you divided by some common factor. You must keep dividing until the only common factor remaining is 1. After reducing, verify your result by checking whether the GCF of the new numerator and denominator is 1.

Section 3.7

Cancellation and Why It Works

Cancellation is a shortcut for reducing fractions during multiplication. Instead of multiplying first and then reducing the result, you reduce before multiplying by dividing any numerator and any denominator by a common factor they share. The same answer is reached with smaller intermediate numbers.

Cancellation works because the commutative and associative properties of multiplication allow you to rearrange factors in the numerator and denominator, and dividing paired factors by their GCF is equivalent to multiplying by a form of 1.

Rules of Cancellation

You may cancel a numerator with a denominator when they share a common factor.

You may cancel across different fractions in a multiplication problem.

You may not cancel a numerator with another numerator, or a denominator with another denominator.

You may not cancel in addition or subtraction — only in multiplication.

Example 3.7.1
Cancellation before multiplying
  • ProblemCompute (5/12) × (8/15) using cancellation.
  • Think5 and 15 share a factor of 5: 5÷5=1, 15÷5=3. Then 8 and 12 share a factor of 4: 8÷4=2, 12÷4=3.
  • Solve (5̶/1̶2̶) × (8̶/1̶5̶) → (1/3) × (2/3) = 2/9 ÷5 ÷4 ÷4 ÷5
  • CheckWithout cancellation: (5×8)/(12×15) = 40/180. GCF(40,180)=20. 40/180 = 2/9. ✓
Answer: (5/12) × (8/15) = 2/9
Student Warning — Cancellation in Addition

Cancellation only works in multiplication. You cannot cancel in 2/3 + 1/2 by removing the 2's. In addition and subtraction, every number stays where it is until you have a common denominator. Incorrect cancellation in addition is one of the most frequent and damaging errors in fraction work.

Section 3.8

Division by Zero

One of the most important rules in all of mathematics is this: you cannot divide by zero. The denominator of any fraction can never be zero.

To understand why, recall what division means. If 6/3 = 2, it is because 3 × 2 = 6. Division and multiplication are inverse operations. Every division problem corresponds to a multiplication problem. Now ask: what would 6/0 mean? It would require finding a number k such that 0 × k = 6. But 0 × k = 0 for every possible value of k. No such k exists. The expression 6/0 has no answer. We say it is undefined.

The case 0/0 is different but equally problematic. Here, 0 × k = 0 is true for every value of k, so any number would satisfy the condition. Because the result is not unique, 0/0 is called indeterminate. Both cases are unusable for our purposes.

ExpressionValueReason
0/50Zero divided by any nonzero number is zero.
5/15Any number divided by 1 is itself.
n/n (n≠0)1Any nonzero number divided by itself is 1.
5/0UndefinedNo number times 0 gives 5.
0/0IndeterminateEvery number times 0 gives 0; the answer is not unique.
Looking Ahead to Algebra

In algebra, you will often work with fractions that have variables in the denominator. Whenever you see a variable in a denominator, one of your first responsibilities is to note that the variable cannot equal zero. This habit begins here.

Section 3.9

Adding Fractions

You can only add fractions directly when they have the same denominator. This is not an arbitrary rule. It reflects what fractions mean. Adding 2/7 + 3/7 means: 2 sevenths plus 3 sevenths equals 5 sevenths. You add the numerators and keep the denominator. This is exactly like adding units in any context: 2 apples + 3 apples = 5 apples. What you cannot do is add 2/7 + 3/5 directly, any more than you can add 2 sevenths and 3 fifths without first converting them to a common unit.

Adding Fractions — Like Denominators

Add the numerators. Keep the denominator. Reduce the result if possible.

a/c + b/c = (a+b)/c

Adding Fractions — Unlike Denominators (Step by Step)

1. Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD) = LCM of the denominators.

2. Rewrite each fraction as an equivalent fraction with the LCD.

3. Add the numerators. Keep the LCD as denominator.

4. Reduce the result to lowest terms.

Example 3.9.1
Adding fractions with like denominators
  • ProblemAdd 3/10 + 4/10.
  • Solve3/10 + 4/10 = (3+4)/10 = 7/10
  • CheckGCF(7, 10) = 1. Already in lowest terms. ✓
Answer: 7/10
Example 3.9.2
Adding fractions with unlike denominators
  • ProblemAdd 1/4 + 2/6.
  • Step 1Find the LCD. LCM(4, 6) = 12.
  • Step 2 1/4 = (1×3)/(4×3) = 3/12 2/6 = (2×2)/(6×2) = 4/12
  • Step 33/12 + 4/12 = 7/12
  • Check1/4 = 0.25, 2/6 ≈ 0.333. Sum ≈ 0.583. And 7/12 ≈ 0.583. ✓
Answer: 1/4 + 2/6 = 7/12
Example 3.9.3
Adding three fractions with different denominators
  • ProblemAdd 1/3 + 1/4 + 1/6.
  • Step 1LCM(3, 4, 6) = 12.
  • Step 21/3 = 4/12, 1/4 = 3/12, 1/6 = 2/12
  • Step 34/12 + 3/12 + 2/12 = 9/12
  • Step 4GCF(9, 12) = 3 → 9/12 = 3/4
Answer: 1/3 + 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/4
Student Warning — The Most Common Fraction Error

Students sometimes add both numerators and denominators: 1/3 + 1/4 = 2/7. This is incorrect. The denominator must become a common denominator, not a sum. The correct answer is 7/12 ≈ 0.583, which is larger than either addend, as a sum should be. The result 2/7 ≈ 0.286 is smaller than 1/3, which is impossible for a sum of two positive fractions.

Section 3.10

Subtracting Fractions

Subtraction of fractions follows the same logic as addition. You need a common denominator. Once the fractions share a denominator, subtract the numerators and keep the denominator.

Subtracting Fractions

Find a common denominator, convert, subtract numerators, keep the denominator, reduce.

a/c - b/c = (a-b)/c

Example 3.10.1
Subtracting fractions with like denominators
  • ProblemSubtract 7/9 − 2/9.
  • Solve7/9 - 2/9 = (7-2)/9 = 5/9
  • CheckGCF(5, 9) = 1. In lowest terms. ✓
Answer: 5/9
Example 3.10.2
Subtracting fractions with unlike denominators
  • ProblemSubtract 5/6 − 3/8.
  • Step 1LCM(6, 8) = 24.
  • Step 25/6 = 20/24, 3/8 = 9/24
  • Step 320/24 - 9/24 = 11/24
  • Check0.833 − 0.375 = 0.458. And 11/24 ≈ 0.458. ✓
Answer: 5/6 − 3/8 = 11/24
Example 3.10.3
Subtracting with a result that requires reducing
  • ProblemSubtract 7/10 − 2/15.
  • Step 110 = 2×5, 15 = 3×5. LCM = 2×3×5 = 30.
  • Step 27/10 = 21/30, 2/15 = 4/30
  • Step 321/30 - 4/30 = 17/30
  • Check17 is prime and does not divide 30. GCF = 1. In lowest terms. ✓
Answer: 7/10 − 2/15 = 17/30
Tip — Check Your Conversions

After finding a common denominator, double-check your equivalent fractions before subtracting. A small error in converting leads to a wrong answer even if the subtraction itself is correct. Write out the multiplication clearly: 7/10 = (7×3)/(10×3) = 21/30.

Section 3.11

Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying Fractions

Multiply numerators together. Multiply denominators together. Reduce the result.

(a/b) × (c/d) = (a×c)/(b×d)

No common denominator is needed. Apply cancellation before multiplying to keep numbers small.

Multiplication with fractions means "of." The expression (2/3) × (3/4) means "two-thirds of three-fourths." The result will be less than three-fourths, because you are taking only part of it. This surprises many students: multiplying two fractions less than 1 gives a product smaller than either factor. One-half of one-half is one-fourth. Take half of something, then half of that. You end up with a quarter of the original.

Diagram — Why 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4

Draw a square. Shade the left half to represent 1/2. Now shade the lower portion of the shaded half to represent 1/2 of that half. The doubly-shaded region is one-fourth of the whole square, confirming (1/2) × (1/2) = 1/4.

Example 3.11.1
Multiplying fractions with cancellation
  • ProblemMultiply (3/4) × (8/9).
  • Think3 and 9 share a factor of 3. Then 8 and 4 share a factor of 4.
  • Solve 3 and 9: divide both by 3 → 1 and 3 8 and 4: divide both by 4 → 2 and 1 (1/1) × (2/3) = 2/3
  • Check(3×8)/(4×9) = 24/36. GCF(24,36)=12. 24/36 = 2/3. ✓
Answer: (3/4) × (8/9) = 2/3
Example 3.11.2
Fraction of a whole number — real-world application
  • ProblemA class has 30 students. Three-fifths of them passed the first test. How many students passed?
  • RepresentFind (3/5) × 30. Write 30 as 30/1.
  • Solve (3/5) × (30/1) Cancel: 30 and 5 share factor 5 → 6 and 1 = (3/1) × (6/1) = 18
  • CheckOne-fifth of 30 is 6. Three-fifths is 3 × 6 = 18. ✓
  • Explain18 students passed the test.
Answer: 18 students

Section 3.12

Dividing Fractions

Division asks: how many times does one quantity fit into another? How many 2's fit in 8? The answer is 4. How many one-fourths fit in three-fourths? The answer is 3. The procedure for dividing fractions is called Keep-Change-Flip (KCF): keep the first fraction, change the division to multiplication, and flip the second fraction (write its reciprocal).

Keep-Change-Flip

(a/b) ÷ (c/d) = (a/b) × (d/c)

Keep the first fraction. Change ÷ to ×. Flip the second fraction.

This works because multiplying by d/c is the inverse of multiplying by c/d. A number and its reciprocal always multiply to 1.

Example 3.12.1
Dividing a fraction by a fraction
  • ProblemDivide (3/4) ÷ (2/5).
  • ThinkKeep 3/4, change ÷ to ×, flip 2/5 to 5/2.
  • Solve(3/4) × (5/2) = 15/8
  • CheckVerify: (15/8) × (2/5) = 30/40 = 3/4. ✓
Answer: (3/4) ÷ (2/5) = 15/8
Example 3.12.2
Dividing a whole number by a fraction
  • ProblemHow many 1/3-cup servings are in 4 cups?
  • Represent4 ÷ (1/3). Write 4 as 4/1.
  • Solve(4/1) × (3/1) = 12
  • Check12 servings of 1/3 cup = 12 × (1/3) = 4 cups. ✓
Answer: 12 servings
Example 3.12.3
Dividing a fraction by a whole number
  • ProblemDivide (5/6) ÷ 3.
  • ThinkWrite 3 as 3/1. Flip to get 1/3.
  • Solve(5/6) × (1/3) = 5/18
  • Check(5/18) × 3 = 15/18 = 5/6. ✓
Answer: (5/6) ÷ 3 = 5/18
Student Warning — Flip the Right Fraction

Students sometimes flip the first fraction instead of the divisor. Always flip only the second fraction, the one you are dividing by. In (3/5) ÷ (2/7), flip 2/7 to get 7/2, not the other way around.

Section 3.13

Reciprocals

What a Reciprocal Is

The reciprocal of a fraction is the fraction with its numerator and denominator swapped. The reciprocal of a/b is b/a.

Defining property: a fraction and its reciprocal always multiply to 1.

(3/4) × (4/3) = 12/12 = 1

NumberWritten AsReciprocalVerify
3/43/44/3(3/4) × (4/3) = 1
5 (whole number)5/11/55 × (1/5) = 1
1/7 (unit fraction)1/77/1 = 7(1/7) × 7 = 1
00/11/0 = undefinedZero has no reciprocal

Every division problem can be rewritten as multiplication by the reciprocal. This is the deeper reason why Keep-Change-Flip works: a ÷ b = a × (1/b), and (a/b) ÷ (c/d) = (a/b) × (d/c). The reciprocal "undoes" the original fraction.

Example 3.13.1

  • ProblemFind the reciprocal of: 7/9, 6, 1/4, and 5/1.
  • Solve Reciprocal of 7/9 = 9/7 Reciprocal of 6 = 1/6 (since 6 = 6/1) Reciprocal of 1/4 = 4 (since 1/4 → 4/1 = 4) Reciprocal of 5/1 = 1/5
  • Check(7/9) × (9/7) = 1. 6 × (1/6) = 1. (1/4) × 4 = 1. (5/1) × (1/5) = 1. ✓
Answer: 9/7, 1/6, 4, 1/5

Section 3.14

Signs of Fractions

A fraction can be positive, negative, or zero, just like any other number. The sign of a fraction follows the same rules for dividing signed numbers that you learned in Chapter 1.

DivisionSignExample
Positive ÷ PositivePositive3/4 > 0
Negative ÷ NegativePositive(−3)/(−4) = 3/4 > 0
Negative ÷ PositiveNegative(−3)/4 < 0
Positive ÷ NegativeNegative3/(−4) < 0
Three Equivalent Sign Positions

For any fraction, the negative sign can appear in three equivalent positions:

−(3/4) = (−3)/4 = 3/(−4)

All three represent negative three-fourths. The standard convention is to write the negative sign in front of the entire fraction: −(3/4).

Convention Tip

Placing the negative sign in the denominator is unusual in practice and can cause confusion. Prefer −(3/4) or (−3)/4 over 3/(−4).

Section 3.15

Common Fraction Mistakes

Reviewing common errors is as important as practicing procedures. Recognizing an error pattern is the first step to correcting it permanently.

Mistake 1 — Adding Numerators and Denominators Separately
1/3 + 1/4 = 2/7

The denominator is not a quantity being added. Find the LCD, convert both fractions, then add numerators only. Correct answer: 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12.

Mistake 2 — Canceling in Addition or Subtraction
2/3 + 1/2 → cancel the 2 → 1/3 + 1/2

Cancellation only applies in multiplication. In addition and subtraction, every number stays until you have a common denominator. Changing 2/3 to 1/3 changes its value entirely.

Mistake 3 — Forgetting to Reduce the Final Answer
Leaving 6/8 as a final answer

GCF(6, 8) = 2. The correct final answer is 3/4. A fraction is not fully simplified until the GCF of numerator and denominator equals 1.

Mistake 4 — Flipping the Wrong Fraction in Division
(3/5) ÷ (2/7) → (5/3) × (2/7)

Flip only the divisor, the second fraction. The first fraction stays unchanged. Correct: (3/5) × (7/2) = 21/10.

Mistake 5 — Assuming a Larger Denominator Means a Larger Fraction
"3/8 is bigger than 3/5 because 8 is bigger than 5."

When numerators are equal, the larger denominator means smaller pieces, giving a smaller fraction. 3/5 > 3/8 because fifths are larger pieces than eighths. Draw both on a number line to confirm.

Diagram — Why 3/5 > 3/8

Draw two equal rectangles side by side. Divide the first into 5 equal parts and shade 3. Divide the second into 8 equal parts and shade 3. The shaded region in the first rectangle is visibly larger, confirming 3/5 > 3/8.

Mistake 6 — Using the Product of Denominators Instead of the LCD
For 1/4 + 1/6: using denominator 4×6 = 24 instead of LCD = 12

Using the product of denominators works but gives larger numbers. Using the LCD (LCM of the denominators) keeps numbers smaller and reduces the amount of simplification at the end.

Mistake 7 — Treating Improper Fractions as Errors
"11/4 is wrong. It should be a mixed number."

Improper fractions are perfectly valid. In multiplication and division, it is often easier to work with improper fractions than mixed numbers. Converting prematurely can create confusion.

Mistake 8 — Dropping the Negative Sign
Treating −3/4 as if it were 3/4 midway through a calculation

−3/4 and 3/4 are different numbers separated by a distance of 3/2 on the number line. Track your signs carefully through every step. A dropped negative sign changes the answer entirely.

Chapter 3 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 3.1A fraction has five interpretations: part of a whole, part of a set, division, a point on the number line, and a ratio. Flexibility with all five supports every future use of fractions.
  • Sec 3.2The denominator names the type of piece; the numerator counts the pieces. Proper fractions are less than 1. Improper fractions are at least 1. Unit fractions have numerator 1.
  • Sec 3.3The fraction bar is a division symbol. Every fraction is also a division expression. The dividend goes in the numerator.
  • Sec 3.4Every fraction has a specific location on the number line. The position confirms its size and connects it to its decimal equivalent.
  • Sec 3.5Equivalent fractions represent the same value with different numerators and denominators. They are created by multiplying or dividing both parts by the same nonzero number — a form of 1.
  • Sec 3.6Reducing means dividing both numerator and denominator by the GCF. A fraction is in lowest terms when the GCF of its parts is 1.
  • Sec 3.7Cancellation reduces fractions before multiplying. It is valid only in multiplication, never in addition or subtraction.
  • Sec 3.8Division by zero is undefined. The denominator of any fraction must be nonzero. Zero in the numerator gives a value of zero (as long as the denominator is nonzero).
  • Sec 3.9Adding fractions requires a common denominator. Add numerators, keep the denominator. Use the LCD when denominators differ.
  • Sec 3.10Subtracting fractions follows the same process as addition: common denominator, subtract numerators, reduce.
  • Sec 3.11Multiplying fractions requires no common denominator. Multiply numerators, multiply denominators, reduce. Cancellation applies before multiplying.
  • Sec 3.12Dividing fractions uses Keep-Change-Flip: multiply the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second fraction.
  • Sec 3.13The reciprocal of a fraction is its numerator and denominator swapped. A fraction and its reciprocal multiply to 1. Zero has no reciprocal.
  • Sec 3.14Fractions follow the same sign rules as signed numbers from Chapter 1. The negative sign can appear in three equivalent positions.
  • Sec 3.15Eight recurring errors: adding denominators, canceling in addition, not fully reducing, flipping the wrong fraction, confusing denominator size with fraction size, using products instead of LCD, dismissing improper fractions, and dropping negative signs.

Key Terms Review

fraction
A number representing part of a whole, a division, a ratio, or a point on the number line. Written as a/b where b ≠ 0.
numerator
The top number of a fraction. It counts how many of the equal parts are being considered.
denominator
The bottom number of a fraction. It names the size of each part by stating how many equal parts the whole is divided into.
unit fraction
A fraction with a numerator of 1, such as 1/3 or 1/8. Every fraction is a multiple of a unit fraction.
proper fraction
A fraction in which the numerator is less than the denominator. Its value is between 0 and 1.
improper fraction
A fraction in which the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator. Its value is 1 or greater. It is a valid and useful form.
equivalent fractions
Fractions that represent the same value despite having different numerators and denominators. Created by multiplying or dividing both parts by the same nonzero number.
lowest terms
A fraction is in lowest terms (simplest form) when the GCF of its numerator and denominator is 1. No further reduction is possible.
common denominator
A shared denominator used to add or subtract fractions with different denominators.
LCD
Least Common Denominator. The LCM of two or more denominators. The smallest common denominator available for addition or subtraction.
reciprocal
The multiplicative inverse of a fraction, formed by swapping numerator and denominator. A fraction and its reciprocal always multiply to 1.
undefined
A mathematical expression that has no meaningful value. Division by zero is undefined because no number times 0 equals a nonzero number.
cancellation
A method of reducing fractions during multiplication by dividing a numerator and a denominator by a common factor before multiplying. Valid only in multiplication.

Bridge Note: Chapter 3 and the Road Ahead
Chapter 4: Mixed Numbers — and Beyond

Chapter 3 gave you the full toolkit for working with fractions: what they mean, how to simplify them, and how to add, subtract, multiply, and divide them. These skills do not pause for the rest of this textbook. They are in constant use from this point forward.

Chapter 4 extends fractions into mixed numbers, which combine a whole number and a fraction into a single value. You will convert between improper fractions and mixed numbers, and you will perform all four operations with mixed numbers using exactly the fraction skills you practiced here.

Chapter 5 will show you that decimals are fractions in disguise. Every terminating decimal is a fraction with a power of ten in the denominator. Moving fluently between fractions and decimals depends on the equivalent fraction work from this chapter.

Chapter 6 on ratios and percents will treat percent as a fraction with denominator 100, and proportions as pairs of equivalent fractions set equal to each other.

In the algebra chapters, you will encounter rational expressions — fractions with variables in the numerator or denominator. Adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing rational expressions follows exactly the same logic you applied to numerical fractions here. Every step you practiced has an algebraic counterpart waiting ahead. Take the time now to be fluent.

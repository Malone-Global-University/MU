Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Mathematics is not only calculation. It is also communication, and one of the most powerful ways mathematics communicates is through pictures. A graph turns a list of numbers into a shape. It turns a pattern into a line. It turns a relationship between two quantities into something a person can see, trace, and interpret at a glance.

Chapter 12 introduces the coordinate plane, the standard mathematical system for locating and graphing points in two dimensions. Students will learn how the plane is organized, how to name and plot locations using ordered pairs, how to recognize patterns in tables, and how to represent those patterns visually. By the end of this chapter, students will understand that graphing is not decoration added to mathematics. It is a method of reasoning, a tool for finding relationships, and a foundation for the linear equations and functions that come next in Algebra I.

Opening Question

A weather station records the temperature every hour throughout a single day. At midnight the temperature is 48 degrees. By noon it has risen to 74 degrees. By midnight again it has dropped back to 51 degrees. The data can be written in a table. But what if someone wants to see how the temperature changed, when it rose fastest, and when it began to fall? A table of numbers does not answer those questions easily. A graph does. How do mathematicians turn a list of numbers into a picture, and what does that picture reveal that the numbers alone do not?

Why This Chapter Matters

Every graph in science, business, economics, medicine, and engineering rests on the same foundation: the coordinate plane. Students who understand ordered pairs, axes, and plotting will be prepared to read stock charts, understand scientific data, navigate digital maps, program computers, and study linear equations in Algebra I. More immediately, the coordinate plane ties together the arithmetic and pre-algebra skills from earlier chapters. Negative numbers locate points to the left or below zero. Fractions and decimals appear as precise coordinates. Patterns connect arithmetic sequences to the beginning of algebraic functions. This chapter is the visual language of mathematics. Learning it here means that when it reappears in algebra, geometry, physics, and statistics, students will recognize it as familiar ground.

Section 12.1

Why Graphs Matter

Numbers carry information, but they do not always reveal it. A list of values tells you what happened. A graph often shows you how it happened, why it happened, and what is likely to happen next.

Consider two situations. In the first, a student earns $8 per hour working at a store. In one hour she earns $8. In two hours she earns $16. In three hours she earns $24. In four hours she earns $32. In the second, the same numbers are graphed, placing hours along one direction and earnings along the other. Each pair of values becomes a point. When all the points are marked, they fall in a perfectly straight line rising from left to right.

The table answers a specific question: how much for four hours? The graph reveals something deeper: the earnings grow at a constant, unchanging rate. The relationship is linear. That straight line communicates a whole relationship in one image.

Why Visual Representation Is Powerful

Graphs do several things that tables and equations do not do as easily. They show trends: a rising line means growth, a falling line means decrease, a flat line means no change. They show speed of change: a steep line means fast change, a shallow line means slow change. They show comparisons: two lines on the same graph reveal which quantity is larger, where they are equal, and when one overtakes the other. They also reveal patterns instantly, often before a student can write an algebraic equation.

From Data to Graph

The process of making a graph always follows the same logic: collect pairs of related values, place them on a plane, and mark each pair as a point. To do this consistently and correctly, mathematicians use a structured system called the coordinate plane, which this chapter develops section by section.

Section 12.2

The Coordinate Plane

The coordinate plane is a flat surface divided into a grid by two perpendicular number lines. The word "coordinate" means a value that locates a position. The word "plane" means a flat, two-dimensional surface that extends in all directions without ending.

The coordinate plane makes it possible to describe any point on that surface using exactly two numbers. Those two numbers work together: one locates the point horizontally, and one locates it vertically. Together they form a unique address for every location on the plane.

How the Plane Is Built

The coordinate plane is constructed from two number lines placed at right angles to each other, crossing at the zero mark of each line. This crossing point is called the origin. The horizontal number line runs left and right. Positive values go to the right, negative values go to the left. The vertical number line runs up and down. Positive values go upward, negative values go downward. Together these two lines divide the plane into four regions called quadrants, discussed in Section 12.6.

y | Q II | Q I | (neg x, pos y) | (pos x, pos y) | ———————————————————+—————————————————— x | (neg x, neg y) | (pos x, neg y) | Q III | Q IV |
Why Two Dimensions

A single number line locates positions in one direction only. The coordinate plane uses two directions, so it can represent two quantities at once — making it possible to graph a relationship between two things, such as time and earnings, hours and temperature, or input and output in a function.

Student Note — The Cartesian Plane

The coordinate plane is sometimes called the Cartesian plane, named after the French mathematician René Descartes, who developed the coordinate system in the seventeenth century. You will see both names used in future mathematics courses.

Section 12.3

The x-Axis and y-Axis

The two number lines that form the coordinate plane each have a name. The x-axis is the horizontal number line. It runs left and right across the plane. Positive x-values are to the right of the origin, and negative x-values are to the left. The y-axis is the vertical number line. It runs up and down through the plane. Positive y-values are above the origin, and negative y-values are below. The two axes are perpendicular — they cross at exactly a right angle — and they meet at the origin, which carries coordinates (0, 0).

What the Axes Represent

In a blank coordinate plane, the axes are simply reference lines for location. When a student graphs a real-world relationship, the axes are usually labeled to show what each direction represents. For example, a graph of time versus distance might label the x-axis "Hours" and the y-axis "Miles." A graph of temperature over a day might label the x-axis "Hour of Day" and the y-axis "Temperature (°F)." In abstract mathematics problems, the axes are simply called the x-axis and y-axis.

Scale on the Axes

The axes are marked with evenly spaced numbers, just like a ruler. The spacing between marks can vary depending on the data being graphed. If values range from 0 to 100, numbering by tens is a reasonable scale. If values range from 0 to 10, numbering by ones is clearer. The scale must remain consistent: if one tick mark represents two units, every tick mark on that axis must represent two units.

Student Warning — Scale Consistency

Always keep the scale consistent on each axis. If one tick mark represents two units, every tick mark on that axis must represent two units. An inconsistent scale distorts the graph and produces misleading pictures.

Section 12.4

Ordered Pairs

A location on the coordinate plane is named by an ordered pair. An ordered pair is written as two numbers separated by a comma and enclosed in parentheses: (x, y). The first number is the x-coordinate, describing horizontal position measured from the origin. The second number is the y-coordinate, describing vertical position. The order matters — the x-coordinate always comes first, the y-coordinate always comes second.

Ordered PairHorizontal MovementVertical Movement
(4, 3)4 units right3 units up
(−2, 5)2 units left5 units up
(3, −4)3 units right4 units down
(−1, −6)1 unit left6 units down
(0, 0)No movementNo movement (origin)
(6, 0)6 units rightNo movement (on x-axis)
(0, −3)No movement (on y-axis)3 units down
Student Warning — Order Matters

The pair (4, 3) and the pair (3, 4) name two different points. Swapping the coordinates changes the location. The x-coordinate always comes first. A very common mistake is reading or writing coordinates in the wrong order.

Example 12.4.1
Identifying coordinates from descriptions
  • ProblemName the ordered pair for each location: (a) 5 units right, 2 units up; (b) 3 units left, 4 units down; (c) 6 units right, on the x-axis; (d) 7 units below the origin, on the y-axis.
  • ThinkMatch the direction to the sign of each coordinate. Right is positive x. Left is negative x. Up is positive y. Down is negative y. On the x-axis means y = 0. On the y-axis means x = 0.
  • Solve (a) (5, 2) (b) (−3, −4) (c) (6, 0) — on the x-axis, so y = 0 (d) (0, −7) — on the y-axis, so x = 0
  • CheckIn (a), both values are positive, matching "right" and "up." In (b), both are negative, matching "left" and "down." In (c), y is zero, confirming the x-axis. In (d), x is zero, confirming the y-axis.
Answer: (a) (5, 2), (b) (−3, −4), (c) (6, 0), (d) (0, −7)

Section 12.5

Plotting Points

Plotting a point means marking its location on the coordinate plane. The process follows a simple, consistent sequence. Step 1: start at the origin (0, 0). Step 2: move horizontally by the x-coordinate, right if positive, left if negative, and not at all if zero. Step 3: from that position, move vertically by the y-coordinate, up if positive, down if negative, and not at all if zero. Step 4: mark the point with a dot and label it if required.

Think of the coordinate plane as a city grid. The x-axis is a street running east and west. The y-axis is an avenue running north and south. The origin is the intersection of Main Street and Central Avenue. An ordered pair gives directions: go three blocks east, then two blocks north. There is exactly one location that matches those directions, and marking it on the map is plotting the point.

Example 12.5.1
Plotting a set of points
  • ProblemPlot and label each point: A = (3, 4), B = (−2, 1), C = (0, −5), D = (−4, −3), E = (6, 0).
  • ThinkApply the four plotting steps to each point. Check the sign of each coordinate before moving.
  • Solve A (3, 4): 3 right, 4 up — upper right area B (−2, 1): 2 left, 1 up — upper left area C (0, −5): no move, 5 down — on the y-axis, below origin D (−4, −3): 4 left, 3 down — lower left area E (6, 0): 6 right, no move — on the x-axis, right of origin
  • CheckA is in the upper right. B is in the upper left. C is on the y-axis below origin. D is in the lower left. E is on the x-axis to the right.
Answer: All five points are placed at their correct coordinates. C and E lie on the axes.
Example 12.5.2
Reading coordinates from a graph
  • ProblemA coordinate plane has four labeled points. Find the ordered pair for each: P is 2 units right and 5 units above origin; Q is directly on the x-axis, 3 units left; R is 4 units below origin on the y-axis; S is 1 unit left and 7 units below origin.
  • ThinkRight is positive x. Left is negative x. Up is positive y. Down is negative y. On an axis means the other coordinate is zero.
  • Solve P: (2, 5) Q: (−3, 0) R: (0, −4) S: (−1, −7)
  • CheckEach pair matches the described direction and distance.
Answer: P is (2, 5), Q is (−3, 0), R is (0, −4), S is (−1, −7).

Points on the Axes

Points that lie on the x-axis or y-axis do not belong to any quadrant. They sit on the boundary between quadrants. The origin itself is the meeting point of all four quadrants but belongs to none of them.

  • CheckEach result matches the sign pattern for that quadrant or axis.

Section 12.7

Coordinates and Direction

The sign of each coordinate carries directional meaning. The x-coordinate tells how far a point is from the y-axis and in which horizontal direction. A positive x-coordinate means the point is to the right of the y-axis. A negative x-coordinate means the point is to the left. An x-coordinate of zero means the point is on the y-axis itself.

The y-coordinate tells how far a point is from the x-axis and in which vertical direction. A positive y-coordinate means the point is above the x-axis. A negative y-coordinate means the point is below. A y-coordinate of zero means the point is on the x-axis.

The absolute value of each coordinate is the distance from the axis. The point (5, −3) is 5 units to the right of the y-axis and 3 units below the x-axis. This connects the absolute value concept from Chapter 1 directly to coordinate geometry: distance is always positive, even when the direction is negative.

Reflecting Points Across Axes

When the sign of one coordinate is changed, the point moves to the opposite side of that axis while staying the same distance from it. The point (3, 4) and the point (−3, 4) are reflections of each other across the y-axis. The point (3, 4) and the point (3, −4) are reflections across the x-axis. The point (3, 4) and (−3, −4) are reflections through the origin.

Example 12.7.1
Describing coordinates using direction language
  • ProblemDescribe the location of each point using direction words and distance from each axis: (a) (4, −6), (b) (−1, 3), (c) (−5, −2).
  • ThinkUse the x-coordinate for horizontal location relative to the y-axis. Use the y-coordinate for vertical location relative to the x-axis.
  • Solve (a) (4, −6): 4 right of y-axis, 6 below x-axis → Quadrant IV (b) (−1, 3): 1 left of y-axis, 3 above x-axis → Quadrant II (c) (−5, −2): 5 left of y-axis, 2 below x-axis → Quadrant III
  • CheckThe quadrant assignments match the sign patterns from Section 12.6.
Answer: (a) 4 right, 6 below, Quadrant IV. (b) 1 left, 3 above, Quadrant II. (c) 5 left, 2 below, Quadrant III.

Section 12.8

Tables of Values

Before graphing a pattern or relationship, it is helpful to organize the data in a table of values. A table of values is a structured list of input-output pairs. Each row of the table holds one ordered pair that can be plotted on the coordinate plane.

In a relationship between two quantities, the quantity that is freely chosen is called the input, usually represented by x and placed on the horizontal axis. The quantity that results from the input is called the output, usually represented by y and placed on the vertical axis.

A table of values typically has two columns. The left column holds input values, labeled x or with the name of the first quantity. The right column holds output values, labeled y or with the name of the second quantity. Each row is one pair of related values.

Section 12.9

Simple Linear Patterns

A pattern is a sequence of values that changes according to a consistent rule. A linear pattern is a pattern in which the output changes by the same amount each time the input increases by one. The values grow or shrink at a constant rate, and when graphed, a linear pattern produces a straight line.

To check whether a pattern is linear, look at the differences between consecutive output values. If those differences are all equal, the pattern is linear.

Example 12.9.1
Identifying a linear pattern
  • ProblemDetermine whether each table shows a linear pattern.

    Table A: x = 0,1,2,3,4 and y = 3,6,9,12,15.
    Table B: x = 0,1,2,3,4 and y = 1,2,4,8,16.
  • ThinkFind the differences between consecutive y-values in each table.
  • Solve Table A differences: 6−3=3, 9−6=3, 12−9=3, 15−12=3 All differences equal 3. → Linear pattern. Table B differences: 2−1=1, 4−2=2, 8−4=4, 16−8=8 Differences are 1, 2, 4, 8. Not constant. → Not linear.
  • CheckTable A has constant differences. Table B has differences that double each time, indicating exponential growth.
Answer: Table A is linear. Table B is not linear.
Example 12.9.2

  • ProblemA candle is 20 cm tall when first lit and burns down 2 cm each hour. Complete the table and identify the pattern.
  • ThinkThe candle loses 2 centimeters per hour. Subtract 2 from each previous height.
  • Solve

    020

    Differences: 18−20=−2, 16−18=−2, 14−16=−2, 12−14=−2 Constant difference of −2.

Key Idea — Why Constant Differences Mean a Straight Line

When a pattern adds the same amount to the output each time the input increases by one, the graph produces a straight line. The constant difference is related to the steepness of that line, a concept Algebra I formalizes as slope. A negative constant difference means the line slopes downward from left to right.

Section 12.10

Pattern Rules

A pattern rule is a statement or formula that describes how to find any output value from a given input value. To write a pattern rule from a table of values, ask two questions. First: by how much does y change each time x increases by 1? This is the constant difference, sometimes called the rate of change. Second: what is the y-value when x is 0? This is the starting value.

If the y-value starts at b when x is 0, and increases by m for each unit increase in x, the rule is:

Pattern Rule (Linear)

y = mx + b

where m = the constant rate of change (how much y changes per unit increase in x), and b = the starting value (the value of y when x = 0). This formula will be recognized in Algebra I as the slope-intercept form of a linear equation.

Example 12.10.1
Writing a pattern rule from a table
  • ProblemWrite the pattern rule for the table: x = 0,1,2,3,4 and y = 5,8,11,14,17.
  • ThinkFind the constant difference and identify the y-value when x is 0.
  • Solve Constant difference: 8 − 5 = 3. Rate of change m = 3. Starting value: when x = 0, y = 5. So b = 5. Pattern rule: y = 3x + 5
  • CheckAt x = 2: y = 3(2) + 5 = 11. Table shows 11. ✓ At x = 4: y = 3(4) + 5 = 17. Table shows 17. ✓
Answer: y = 3x + 5
Example 12.10.2
Writing a rule for a decreasing pattern
  • ProblemWrite the pattern rule for the table: x = 0,1,2,3,4 and y = 20,17,14,11,8.
  • ThinkFind the constant difference and starting value.
  • Solve Constant difference: 17 − 20 = −3. Rate of change m = −3. Starting value: when x = 0, y = 20. So b = 20. Pattern rule: y = −3x + 20
  • CheckAt x = 3: y = −3(3) + 20 = −9 + 20 = 11. Table shows 11. ✓
Answer: y = −3x + 20
Example 12.10.3
Using a rule to find unknown values
  • ProblemUsing the rule y = 3x + 5 from Example 12.10.1, find the output when x = 10 and when x = 50.

  • CheckFrom x = 4 (y = 17) to x = 10 is 6 more steps: 17 + 6(3) = 17 + 18 = 35. Confirmed.

Section 12.11

Graphing Patterns

Once a table of values is complete, the next step is to plot the ordered pairs on a coordinate plane and observe the shape they form. For a linear pattern, the plotted points will fall along a straight line. Connecting them confirms the pattern and makes the relationship visible. The steps are: build or review the table of values, set up a coordinate plane with an appropriate scale, plot each ordered pair, observe whether the points fall in a line, and if the pattern is linear, connect the points with a straight line and extend it beyond the plotted points.

What the Graph Reveals

A line rising from left to right shows a positive rate of change. A line falling from left to right shows a negative rate of change. A steep line means the output changes quickly. A shallow line means the change is slow. The point where the line crosses the y-axis (when x is 0) is the starting value of the pattern.

Section 12.12

Reading Meaning from Graphs

Plotting a graph is only the first step. Equally important is reading a graph, interpreting what it shows, and drawing conclusions from its shape. The y-intercept is the point where the graph crosses the y-axis. It shows the output when the input is zero and often represents a starting value in context: beginning temperature, initial cost, or battery level at hour zero. The x-intercept is the point where the graph crosses the x-axis. It shows the input when the output is zero and may represent the moment something runs out, reaches zero, or breaks even.

The direction of the line shows whether the relationship is increasing or decreasing. The steepness shows how fast. Equal spacing between points confirms a linear relationship. Points that drift away from a line suggest a non-linear one.

Example 12.12.1
Reading information from a graph
  • ProblemA line passes through (0, 20) and (5, 45). Answer: (a) starting value, (b) rate of change, (c) output when x = 3, (d) when does output reach 50?
  • ThinkIdentify the y-intercept and compute the rate of change from the two given points. Then write the rule and use it.
  • Solve (a) y-intercept: (0, 20). Starting value = 20. (b) Change in y: 45 − 20 = 25. Change in x: 5 − 0 = 5. Rate of change: 25 ÷ 5 = 5 per unit. Rule: y = 5x + 20. (c) x = 3: y = 5(3) + 20 = 15 + 20 = 35. (d) Set y = 50: 50 = 5x + 20 → 30 = 5x → x = 6.
  • CheckAt x = 5: y = 5(5) + 20 = 45. Matches the given point. ✓
Answer: (a) 20, (b) 5 per unit, (c) 35, (d) when x = 6.
Example 12.12.2
Comparing two patterns on one graph
  • ProblemStudent A starts with $10 and saves $20 per week. Student B starts with $70 and saves $5 per week. At what week are their savings equal?
  • ThinkWrite rules for each student, build combined tables, and find where the values match.
  • Solve Student A: y = 20x + 10 Student B: y = 5x + 70 Week 0: A = $10, B = $70 Week 1: A = $30, B = $75 Week 2: A = $50, B = $80 Week 3: A = $70, B = $85 Week 4: A = $90, B = $90 ← Equal at week 4
  • CheckA: 20(4) + 10 = 90. B: 5(4) + 70 = 90. Both equal $90 at week 4. ✓
Answer: Their savings are equal at week 4 ($90 each). On the graph, the two lines intersect at (4, 90).

Section 12.13

Common Coordinate Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Reversing the Coordinates
The point 3 right, 5 up written as (5, 3)

Correction: The x-coordinate always comes first. "How far horizontally?" goes first. A point 3 right and 5 up is (3, 5), not (5, 3).

Mistake 2 — Misreading the Sign of a Coordinate
Plotting (−3, 4) at (3, 4) by forgetting to move left

Correction: Before plotting any point, check the sign of each coordinate. Negative x means move left. Negative y means move down.

Mistake 3 — Inconsistent Scale on an Axis
Y-axis marks at 0, 5, 10, 20, 30 at equal spacing

Correction: Use equal spacing for all marks on each axis. An inconsistent scale distorts a straight-line relationship and makes a linear pattern appear curved.

Mistake 4 — Mixing Up Table Columns
Placing x-values in the right column and y-values in the left column

Correction: Always label columns clearly. The input (x) belongs in the left column. The output (y) belongs in the right column.

Mistake 5 — Skipping the Check Step
Proceeding to graph without verifying table values against the rule

Correction: After building the table, verify at least two rows by substituting back into the rule. One arithmetic error in the table will produce a point that does not align with the others.

Mistake 6 — Connecting Non-Linear Points with a Straight Line
Drawing a straight line through points that are not collinear

Correction: Check constant differences in the table before connecting points. If the differences are not constant, the pattern is not linear and the points should not be connected with a straight line.

Mistake 7 — Confusing Which Axis Is Which
Treating the vertical axis as the x-axis because the graph is not labeled

Correction: The horizontal axis is always the x-axis and runs left-right. The vertical axis is always the y-axis and runs up-down. Confirm labels before plotting.

Section 12.14

Preparing for Linear Equations

Chapter 12 has introduced the tools and language that Algebra I will use constantly. Students in this chapter have learned to locate any point on the coordinate plane using a signed ordered pair, read coordinates from a graph and plot them on a grid, build tables of values by applying a rule, recognize linear patterns by identifying constant differences, write a pattern rule in the form y = mx + b, and graph a linear pattern and read information from it.

All of these skills are pre-algebraic foundations for the study of linear equations.

What Algebra I Will Add

In Algebra I, students will formalize what has been explored here. The pattern rule y = mx + b will be named the slope-intercept form of a linear equation. The constant difference m will be named the slope. The starting value b will be named the y-intercept. Students will solve for equations given two points, graph equations without a table, and work with systems of two equations simultaneously. Every one of these algebra skills begins with the coordinate plane and ordered pairs.

A Preview of Slope

In Algebra I, the constant difference in a table becomes formalized as slope:

slope = (change in y) ÷ (change in x)

A positive slope means the line rises. A negative slope means the line falls. A slope of zero means the line is horizontal. Students will compute slope from two points, from a table, and from an equation. The constant differences practiced in this chapter are already the concept of slope — just not yet named.

Chapter 12 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1Graphs communicate trends, rates of change, and comparisons that tables alone do not reveal. They turn lists of numbers into visual relationships.
  • Sec 12.2The coordinate plane is a flat surface defined by two perpendicular number lines meeting at the origin (0, 0). Every point can be named by two coordinates.
  • Sec 12.3The x-axis is horizontal and the y-axis is vertical. They divide the plane into four regions. Scale must be consistent on each axis.
  • Sec 12.4An ordered pair (x, y) names a point's location. The x-coordinate comes first and describes horizontal position. The y-coordinate comes second and describes vertical position. Order matters.
  • Sec 12.5To plot a point: start at the origin, move horizontally by the x-coordinate, then vertically by the y-coordinate, then mark and label the point.
  • Sec 12.6Quadrant I (upper right): both positive. Quadrant II (upper left): negative x, positive y. Quadrant III (lower left): both negative. Quadrant IV (lower right): positive x, negative y. Points on axes belong to no quadrant.
  • Sec 12.7The sign of each coordinate encodes direction. Positive x is right, negative x is left. Positive y is up, negative y is down. Absolute value gives distance from the axis.
  • Sec 12.8A table of values organizes input-output pairs. The left column holds inputs (x), the right holds outputs (y). Build tables by choosing x-values and calculating the corresponding y-values from the rule.
  • Sec 12.9A linear pattern has a constant difference between consecutive output values. Constant differences produce straight-line graphs. A negative constant difference means the line decreases.
  • Sec 12.10A pattern rule has the form y = mx + b, where m is the constant rate of change and b is the y-value when x = 0. Once written, the rule can find any output for any input.
  • Sec 12.11To graph a pattern: build the table, plot the ordered pairs, and connect with a straight line if the pattern is linear. A rising line shows a positive rate of change. A falling line shows a negative rate of change.
  • Sec 12.12Reading a graph: the y-intercept is the starting value (where the line crosses the y-axis). The x-intercept shows when the output reaches zero. Two lines on one graph can be compared to find where they are equal.
  • Sec 12.13Seven common mistakes: reversed coordinates, sign errors, inconsistent scale, mixed-up table columns, skipping the check step, connecting non-linear points with a line, and confusing which axis is which.
  • Sec 12.14The pattern rule y = mx + b is the slope-intercept form of a linear equation. The constant difference m is the slope. All coordinate plane skills carry directly into Algebra I linear equation work.

Key Terms Review

coordinate plane
A flat surface defined by two perpendicular number lines. Every point is located by two coordinates.
x-axis
The horizontal number line on the coordinate plane. Positive values are to the right of the origin. Negative values are to the left.
y-axis
The vertical number line on the coordinate plane. Positive values are above the origin. Negative values are below.
origin
The point where the x-axis and y-axis intersect. Its coordinates are (0, 0). Every other point is measured from here.
ordered pair
Two numbers in the form (x, y) that name the location of a point. The x-coordinate always comes first.
x-coordinate
The first number in an ordered pair. Describes the horizontal position of the point relative to the y-axis.
y-coordinate
The second number in an ordered pair. Describes the vertical position of the point relative to the x-axis.
quadrant
One of the four regions of the coordinate plane. Numbered I through IV counterclockwise from the upper right.
plot
To mark a point on the coordinate plane at the location described by an ordered pair.
table of values
An organized list of input-output pairs used to build coordinates for graphing. Inputs in the left column, outputs in the right.
pattern
A sequence of values that changes according to a consistent rule.
linear pattern
A pattern in which the output changes by the same constant amount each time the input increases by one. Produces a straight line when graphed.
pattern rule
A formula that describes how to calculate any output from a given input. Written as y = mx + b for linear patterns.
input
The independent value in a relationship. Usually represented by x and placed on the horizontal axis.
output
The dependent value in a relationship. Usually represented by y and placed on the vertical axis.
graph
A visual representation of a set of ordered pairs or a mathematical relationship on the coordinate plane.

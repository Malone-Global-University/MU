Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces proportional reasoning, one of the most important mathematical ideas connecting arithmetic to algebra, science, finance, and everyday decision-making. Students who understand ratios, rates, proportions, and percents are equipped to read a nutrition label, compare prices, calculate a discount, interpret a map, analyze data, and manage money. These are not separate tricks to memorize. They are related expressions of a single idea: that quantities can be compared and scaled in consistent, predictable ways.

By the end of this chapter, students should not only be able to compute with ratios and percents. They should be able to reason proportionally — asking whether two quantities are in balance, whether a change is fair, and whether an answer makes sense in context.

Opening Question

A store sells a 12-ounce bag of trail mix for $3.60 and a 20-ounce bag for $5.80. Which bag is the better value? Before you can answer that question confidently, you need to know how to compare quantities that are not the same size. You need proportional reasoning. This chapter will give you the tools to answer questions like this one, and many others like it.

Why This Chapter Matters

Proportional reasoning appears in nearly every area of applied mathematics. Recipes scale up and down using ratios. Maps use scales that are proportions. Science experiments involve rates. Shopping decisions involve unit prices. Tax, discount, and interest are all forms of percent. Every time you ask "how much of one thing per one unit of another," you are thinking proportionally.

In algebra, proportional reasoning becomes even more important. Linear equations, slope, direct variation, and dimensional analysis are all built on the ideas introduced here. This chapter is not a detour from algebra preparation. It is one of the most direct paths toward it.

Section 6.1

What Ratios Compare

A ratio is a comparison of two quantities by division. It answers the question: how much of one thing is there compared to another? When you say there are 3 red marbles and 5 blue marbles in a bag, you can express this comparison in three equivalent forms.

FormExampleRead as
Using the word to3 to 5"three to five"
Using a colon3 : 5"three to five"
Using a fraction3/5"three to five"

All three forms mean the same thing. The fraction form is especially useful for computation. The two quantities in a ratio are called terms. Order matters: a ratio of 3 to 5 is not the same as 5 to 3. When both quantities share the same unit, the units cancel in the simplified form. When they have different units, keep the units.

Like fractions, ratios can be simplified by dividing both terms by their greatest common factor (GCF).

Example 6.1.1
Simplifying a Ratio
  • ProblemA bag contains 15 red candies and 10 green candies. Write the ratio of red to green in simplest form.
  • ThinkThe ratio is 15/10. Find the GCF of 15 and 10.
  • SolveGCF(15, 10) = 5 15/10 = (15 ÷ 5)/(10 ÷ 5) = 3/2
  • Check3 and 2 share no common factor other than 1. The ratio is fully simplified.
Answer: The ratio of red to green is 3 to 2.
Student Warning

A ratio of 3 to 2 does not mean that 3/2 of something exists. It means for every 3 of the first quantity, there are 2 of the second. The fraction form is a notation choice, not a claim about parts of a whole, unless the ratio is specifically written as part-to-whole (see Section 6.2).

Practice 6.1Ratios and Simplification

  7. Error Analysis: A student writes the ratio of 10 boys to 15 girls as 15/10. What mistake did the student make?
Section 6.2

Part-to-Part and Part-to-Whole Ratios

A part-to-part ratio compares one group to another group within the same whole. If a class has 14 girls and 11 boys, the ratio of girls to boys is 14 to 11. Neither group includes the other.

A part-to-whole ratio compares one group to the entire total. If a class has 14 girls out of 25 students, the ratio of girls to total students is 14 to 25. This form connects directly to fractions, decimals, and percents. Read each problem carefully to determine which type you need.

Example 6.2.1
Part-to-Part vs. Part-to-Whole
  • ProblemA fruit bowl has 6 apples, 4 bananas, and 2 oranges. Write (a) the ratio of apples to bananas, and (b) the ratio of apples to all pieces of fruit.
  • Think(a) Apples to bananas is part-to-part. (b) Apples to all fruit is part-to-whole. Total = 6 + 4 + 2 = 12.
  • Solvea) 6/4 = 3/2 (part-to-part) b) 6/12 = 1/2 (part-to-whole)
  • CheckIn (b), 1/2 means apples are half of all the fruit. Since 6 is half of 12, this is correct.
Answer: (a) 3 to 2    (b) 1 to 2
Student Tip

When a problem asks "what fraction of the total," you need a part-to-whole ratio. This is the form that connects most directly to percents and probability.

  6. Error Analysis: A student is asked for the part-to-whole ratio of boys in a class of 20 where 8 are boys. The student writes 8/12. Identify and correct the error.
Section 6.3

Equivalent Ratios

Two ratios are equivalent if they represent the same comparison at different scales. To create an equivalent ratio, multiply or divide both terms by the same nonzero number. To test equivalence, simplify both ratios to lowest terms and compare, or use cross multiplication: if a/b = c/d, then a × d = b × c.

Example 6.3.1
Testing Whether Two Ratios Are Equivalent
  • ProblemAre the ratios 4 to 6 and 10 to 15 equivalent?
  • Method 1Simplify both: 4/6 = 2/3 10/15 = 2/3 ✓ Both equal 2/3.
  • Method 2Cross multiply: 4 × 15 = 60 6 × 10 = 60 ✓ Cross products are equal.
Answer: Yes. 4 to 6 and 10 to 15 are equivalent ratios.
Example 6.3.2
Creating an Equivalent Ratio
  • ProblemA recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of water. How many cups of flour are needed for 12 cups of water?
  • ThinkRatio is 2/3. Find the multiplier: 3 × ? = 12, so multiply both terms by 4.
  • Solve2/3 = (2 × 4)/(3 × 4) = 8/12
  • Check8/12 = 2/3. Correct.
Answer: 8 cups of flour are needed for 12 cups of water.
Practice 6.3Equivalent Ratios

  2. Determine whether each pair is equivalent: (a) 3 to 5 and 9 to 15   (b) 4 to 7 and 8 to 15   (c) 6 : 10 and 9 : 15

Section 6.4

Ratio Tables

A ratio table is an organized display of equivalent ratios. It has at least two rows, one for each quantity. Each column holds one equivalent pair. Every column must reflect the same ratio relationship. To build a table, start with the original ratio and multiply both quantities by 1, 2, 3, 4, and so on. To find missing values, locate the multiplier that connects two known entries and apply it to the missing quantity.

Example 6.4.1
Building a Ratio Table
  • ProblemA car uses 3 gallons of fuel for every 90 miles. Complete a ratio table for 1 through 5 gallons.
  • ThinkFind the unit rate: 90 ÷ 3 = 30 miles per gallon. Then multiply.
  • Solve
    Gallons12345
    Miles306090120150
  • CheckEach miles value divided by its gallons value equals 30. The ratio is consistent.
Answer: Unit rate is 30 miles per gallon. Table shown above.
Example 6.4.2
Using a Ratio Table to Find a Missing Value
  • ProblemA store packs 5 notebooks per bag. How many bags are needed for 35 notebooks?
  • SolveMultiplier: 35 ÷ 5 = 7 Bags: 1 × 7 = 7 bags
  • Check7 × 5 = 35. Correct.
Answer: 7 bags are needed.

Section 6.5

Rates and Unit Rates

A rate is a ratio that compares two quantities with different units. The word "per" is the key signal — it means "for each one" and indicates division. A unit rate is a rate in which the second quantity is exactly 1. Unit rates make it easy to compare, estimate, and scale because everything is expressed "per one unit." To find a unit rate, divide the first quantity by the second.

Unit Rate Formula

Unit rate = first quantity ÷ (1 unit of second quantity)

Examples: 60 miles per hour, $4.50 per pound, 24 heartbeats per minute.

Example 6.5.1
Finding a Unit Rate
  • ProblemA car travels 240 miles in 4 hours. Find the unit rate in miles per hour.
  • Solve240 ÷ 4 = 60 miles per hour
  • Check60 × 4 = 240. Correct.
Answer: 60 miles per hour.
Example 6.5.2
Comparing Unit Rates
  • ProblemRunner A completes 5 miles in 40 minutes. Runner B completes 8 miles in 72 minutes. Who runs faster?
  • SolveRunner A: 5 ÷ 40 = 0.125 miles per minute Runner B: 8 ÷ 72 ≈ 0.111 miles per minute
  • CheckBoth rates are less than 1 mile per minute, which is reasonable since neither runner is sprinting at track-record pace.
Answer: Runner A is faster at 0.125 miles per minute versus 0.111.
Student Tip

Always label unit rates with both units. Writing "60" is incomplete. Writing "60 miles per hour" is correct. Units tell you what is being compared and prevent confusion later.

Section 6.6

Unit Price

Unit price is a specific, practical kind of unit rate. It tells you the cost per single unit of a product and is the most reliable way to compare the value of products sold in different quantities. Stores often sell the same product in multiple sizes. Always calculate unit price rather than assuming the larger size is always cheaper.

Unit Price Formula

Unit price = Total price ÷ Number of units

The result is a price per single unit — per ounce, per count, per liter, etc.

Example 6.6.1
Calculating and Comparing Unit Prices
  • ProblemA 12-oz bag of trail mix costs $3.60. A 20-oz bag costs $5.80. Which is the better value?
  • Solve12-oz: $3.60 ÷ 12 = $0.30 per oz 20-oz: $5.80 ÷ 20 = $0.29 per oz
  • CheckThe 20-oz bag costs less per ounce even though it costs more in total.
Answer: The 20-ounce bag is the better value at $0.29 per ounce.
Student Warning

"Better value" means lower unit price — not lower total price. A product with a higher sticker price can still be the better deal if it contains significantly more quantity. Always calculate; never assume.

Section 6.7

Unit Multipliers

A unit multiplier is a fraction equal to 1 used to convert a measurement from one unit to another. Because multiplying by 1 does not change a quantity's value, unit multipliers let you change the unit without changing what the measurement means. Every conversion fact yields two unit multipliers. Since 12 inches = 1 foot, both 12 in/1 ft and 1 ft/12 in equal 1. Place the unit you want to cancel in the denominator so it divides out. This technique is called dimensional analysis in science courses.

Steps for Using a Unit Multiplier

Step 1. Write the starting measurement as a fraction over 1.

Step 2. Multiply by the unit multiplier with the unwanted unit in the denominator.

Step 3. Cancel the unwanted unit. Simplify the numbers. Write the answer with the new unit.

Example 6.7.1
Converting Inches to Feet
  • ProblemConvert 84 inches to feet.
  • Think12 in = 1 ft. Cancel inches by placing them in the denominator.
  • Solve84 in × (1 ft / 12 in) = 84/12 ft = 7 ft
  • Check7 × 12 = 84. Correct.
Answer: 84 inches = 7 feet.
Example 6.7.2
Two-Step Unit Conversion
  • ProblemConvert 3 hours to seconds.
  • ThinkUse two multipliers in sequence: hours → minutes, then minutes → seconds.
  • Solve3 hr × (60 min / 1 hr) × (60 sec / 1 min) = 3 × 60 × 60 sec = 10,800 sec
  • Check3 × 60 = 180 min. 180 × 60 = 10,800 sec. Correct.
Answer: 3 hours = 10,800 seconds.
Student Tip

If you set up a unit multiplier and the units do not cancel, you have used the wrong orientation. Flip the fraction and try again.

Section 6.8

Proportions

A proportion is a statement that two ratios are equal: a/b = c/d. When two ratios form a proportion, the quantities are in balance. If you scale one ratio up by a factor, the other must scale by the same factor. Proportions appear in recipes, map scales, enlarged photographs, tax rates, and scientific measurements — anywhere a consistent relationship must be maintained.

Cross Products

In the proportion a/b = c/d, the cross products are a × d and b × c.

If the cross products are equal, the proportion is true.

If the cross products are unequal, the proportion is false.

Example 6.8.1
Testing a True Proportion
  • ProblemIs 3/4 = 9/12 a true proportion?
  • Solve3 × 12 = 36 4 × 9 = 36 ✓ Cross products are equal.
Answer: Yes. 3/4 = 9/12 is a true proportion.
Example 6.8.2
Identifying a False Proportion
  • ProblemIs 5/8 = 15/25 a true proportion?
  • Solve5 × 25 = 125 8 × 15 = 120 ✗ 125 ≠ 120.
Answer: No. 5/8 = 15/25 is not a true proportion.
Practice 6.8Testing Proportions
  1. What is a proportion?
  2. How do cross products tell you whether a proportion is true or false?
  3. Determine whether each is a true proportion: (a) 2/5 = 6/15   (b) 4/7 = 12/22   (c) 9/6 = 12/8   (d) 3/11 = 9/33

Section 6.9

Solving Proportions

When one term in a proportion is unknown, solve for it using cross multiplication. Write the proportion, cross multiply, set the products equal, and divide to isolate the variable. This connects directly to solving one-step equations in Chapter 10. When setting up a proportion from a word problem, verify that both ratios compare the same quantities in the same order.

Example 6.9.1
Solving a Basic Proportion
  • ProblemSolve for x: 3/5 = x/20
  • Solve3 × 20 = 5 × x 60 = 5x x = 60 ÷ 5 = 12
  • Check3/5 = 12/20. Cross products: 3 × 20 = 60, 5 × 12 = 60. Equal. ✓
Answer: x = 12
Example 6.9.2
Proportion from a Word Problem
  • ProblemIf 4 pounds of apples cost $5.20, how much would 10 pounds cost?
  • ThinkCost to pounds = cost to pounds. Same order on both sides.
  • Solve$5.20 / 4 lb = x / 10 lb 5.20 × 10 = 4 × x 52 = 4x x = 52 ÷ 4 = $13.00
  • Check$5.20 ÷ 4 = $1.30/lb. $1.30 × 10 = $13.00. Correct.
Answer: 10 pounds of apples cost $13.00.
Example 6.9.3
Map Scale Proportion
  • ProblemA map scale is 1 inch : 40 miles. Two cities are 3.5 inches apart on the map. What is the actual distance?
  • Solve1 in / 40 mi = 3.5 in / x mi 1 × x = 40 × 3.5 x = 140 miles
  • Check3.5 × 40 = 140. Correct.
Answer: The actual distance between the cities is 140 miles.
Student Warning

When setting up a proportion from a word problem, always verify that both ratios compare the same quantities in the same order. A proportion with flipped terms will produce a wrong answer that may still look plausible at first glance.

Section 6.10

Percent as Per Hundred

The word percent comes from the Latin per centum, meaning "per hundred." A percent is a ratio that always compares a quantity to 100. So 45% simply means 45/100, and 7% means 7/100. Percent is a way of standardizing comparison by fixing the whole at 100. Because percent is always out of 100, it connects directly to decimals and fractions through reliable conversion rules.

ConversionMethodExamples
Percent → DecimalDivide by 100 (move decimal two places left)45% = 0.45   7% = 0.07   125% = 1.25
Decimal → PercentMultiply by 100 (move decimal two places right)0.38 = 38%   0.06 = 6%   1.5 = 150%
Percent → FractionWrite over 100 and simplify60% = 3/5   25% = 1/4
Fraction → PercentDivide numerator by denominator, then × 1003/4 = 75%   2/5 = 40%

Percents are not limited to the range 0 to 100. A percent greater than 100% means more than the whole. A price increase of 150% means the new price is 2.5 times the original. A percent less than 1% is also valid: 0.5% = 0.005 as a decimal.

Example 6.10.1
Converting Percent to Decimal and Fraction
  • ProblemConvert 35% to a decimal and a fraction in simplest form.
  • SolveDecimal: 35% = 35 ÷ 100 = 0.35 Fraction: 35/100 = 7/20
  • Check7 ÷ 20 = 0.35. Consistent.
Answer: 35% = 0.35 = 7/20
Example 6.10.2
Converting a Fraction to a Percent
  • ProblemConvert 3/8 to a percent.
  • Solve3 ÷ 8 = 0.375 0.375 × 100 = 37.5%
Answer: 3/8 = 37.5%

Section 6.11

Percent and Percentage Problems

Every percent problem involves three quantities: the base (the whole), the rate (the percent as a decimal), and the percentage (the part). All three types of percent problems use the same formula — only the unknown changes.

The Percent Formula

Percentage = Rate × Base    (P = r × B)

Type 1 — Find the part: P = r × B   (given rate and base)

Type 2 — Find the rate: r = P ÷ B   (given part and base; convert result to %)

Type 3 — Find the base: B = P ÷ r   (given part and rate)

Example 6.11.1
Type 1: Finding the Percentage
  • ProblemA backpack is priced at $65. Sales tax is 8%. How much is the tax?
  • SolveP = 0.08 × 65 = $5.20
  • Check10% of $65 is $6.50. 8% should be slightly less. $5.20 is reasonable.
Answer: The sales tax is $5.20.
Example 6.11.2
Type 2: Finding the Rate
  • ProblemA student answered 42 out of 56 questions correctly. What percent is that?
  • Solver = 42 ÷ 56 = 0.75 = 75%
  • Check0.75 × 56 = 42. Correct.
Answer: The student answered 75% correctly.
Example 6.11.3
Type 3: Finding the Base
  • ProblemA tip of $9 is 15% of the restaurant bill. What was the total bill?
  • SolveB = 9 ÷ 0.15 = $60
  • Check0.15 × 60 = 9. Correct.
Answer: The total restaurant bill was $60.
Student Tip

Identify the missing value before writing the equation. If you are given the rate and base, find the part. If given part and whole, find the rate. If given part and rate, find the base.

Section 6.12

Percent Increase and Decrease

When a quantity changes, percent change expresses that change as a percent of the original. This captures how significant the change is relative to where the quantity started. A raise of $1,000 means different things at a salary of $10,000 versus $100,000. In both cases the original value — not the new value — appears in the denominator.

Percent Change Formulas

Percent Increase = (New − Original) / Original × 100

Percent Decrease = (Original − New) / Original × 100

Finding a new value directly:

New value after increase = Original × (1 + r)

New value after decrease = Original × (1 − r)

Where r is the percent written as a decimal.

Example 6.12.1
Calculating Percent Increase
  • ProblemA store raised the price of a book from $12.00 to $15.00. What is the percent increase?
  • SolveIncrease = 15 − 12 = 3 Percent Increase = 3/12 × 100 = 25%
  • Check25% of $12 = $3. $12 + $3 = $15. Correct.
Answer: The price increased by 25%.
Example 6.12.2
Calculating Percent Decrease
  • ProblemA jacket originally priced at $80 is now on sale for $60. What is the percent decrease?
  • SolveDecrease = 80 − 60 = 20 Percent Decrease = 20/80 × 100 = 25%
Answer: The price decreased by 25%.
Example 6.12.3
Finding a Sale Price Directly
  • ProblemA television costs $450 and is marked down 30%. What is the sale price?
  • SolveSale Price = 450 × (1 − 0.30) = 450 × 0.70 = $315
  • Check30% of 450 = 135. 450 − 135 = $315. Correct.
Answer: The sale price is $315.
Student Warning

Always divide by the original value when calculating percent change. Dividing by the new value gives a different and incorrect result. This is the most common error in percent change problems.

Section 6.13

Simple Interest

When money is borrowed or deposited in a bank, the amount paid or earned for using that money over time is called interest. Simple interest is calculated on the original amount alone, making it the most straightforward interest model. It applies to many savings accounts, short-term loans, and basic financial instruments.

Simple Interest Formula

I = P × r × t

I = interest earned or owed

P = principal (the original amount)

r = annual interest rate written as a decimal

t = time in years (convert months to years by dividing by 12)

Total amount after interest: A = P + I

Example 6.13.1
Finding Simple Interest and Total Amount
  • ProblemA student deposits $500 in a savings account that pays 4% simple annual interest. How much interest is earned after 3 years? What is the total balance?
  • SolveP = 500, r = 0.04, t = 3 I = 500 × 0.04 × 3 = $60 A = 500 + 60 = $560
  • Check$500 × 0.04 = $20 per year. $20 × 3 = $60. Correct.
Answer: Interest earned: $60. Total balance: $560.
Example 6.13.2
Time Given in Months
  • ProblemA loan of $1,200 is borrowed at 6% annual simple interest for 8 months. How much interest is owed?
  • ThinkConvert 8 months to years: t = 8/12 = 2/3
  • SolveI = 1200 × 0.06 × (2/3) = 1200 × 0.04 = $48
  • Check$1,200 × 0.06 = $72/year. For 2/3 year: $72 × 2/3 = $48. Correct.
Answer: The interest owed is $48.
Example 6.13.3
Solving for Principal
  • ProblemAn account earned $90 in simple interest at 5% per year over 3 years. What was the principal?
  • SolveP = I ÷ (r × t) = 90 ÷ (0.05 × 3) = 90 ÷ 0.15 = $600
  • Check600 × 0.05 × 3 = 90. Correct.
Answer: The principal was $600.
Student Tip — Simple vs. Compound Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal. In later mathematics, you will encounter compound interest, where interest is calculated on both the principal and the accumulated interest. Compound interest grows faster, which is good for savings but more expensive for loans.

Section 6.14

Real-Life Applications of Proportional Reasoning

The ideas in this chapter do not exist in separate boxes. Ratios, rates, proportions, percents, and interest all express the same underlying idea: how quantities compare and how they scale. This section brings these tools together in realistic situations. The problem-solving method applies throughout: Read → Represent → Solve → Check → Explain.

Example 6.14.1
Shopping Application
  • ProblemA store is selling a $90 coat at 30% off. Sales tax is 7.5%. What is the final price?
  • RepresentFind the discount → sale price → apply tax → final price.
  • SolveDiscount = 0.30 × 90 = $27 Sale price = 90 − 27 = $63 Tax = 0.075 × 63 = $4.73 Final price = 63 + 4.73 = $67.73
  • CheckA 30% discount on $90 gives $63. A small tax raises it to $67.73. Reasonable.
Answer: The final cost of the coat is approximately $67.73.
Example 6.14.2
Science Application
  • ProblemA solution contains 15 g of salt in 200 mL of water. A larger batch uses 500 mL. How many grams of salt maintain the same concentration?
  • Represent15 g / 200 mL = x g / 500 mL
  • Solve15 × 500 = 200 × x 7,500 = 200x x = 37.5 g
  • Check37.5/500 = 0.075 and 15/200 = 0.075. Same concentration.
Answer: 37.5 grams of salt are needed for 500 mL of water.
Example 6.14.3
Recipe Scaling Application
  • ProblemA cookie recipe calls for 1.5 cups of sugar for 36 cookies. How much sugar is needed for 60 cookies?
  • Solve1.5 / 36 = x / 60 1.5 × 60 = 36 × x 90 = 36x x = 2.5 cups
Answer: 2.5 cups of sugar are needed for 60 cookies.
Example 6.14.4
Budget Application
  • ProblemA person earns $3,200 per month and budgets 28% for rent and 15% for food. How many dollars go to each category? What percent remains?
  • SolveRent: 0.28 × 3,200 = $896 Food: 0.15 × 3,200 = $480 Used: 28 + 15 = 43% Left: 57% → 0.57 × 3,200 = $1,824
Answer: Rent $896, food $480, $1,824 (57%) remains for other expenses.

Chapter 6 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1A ratio is a comparison of two quantities by division. It can be written in three forms: a to b, a : b, or a/b. Simplified by dividing both terms by their GCF. Order matters.
  • Sec 6.2Part-to-part ratios compare two groups. Part-to-whole ratios compare one group to the total. Part-to-whole ratios connect directly to fractions, decimals, and percents.
  • Sec 6.3Equivalent ratios represent the same comparison at different scales. Created by multiplying or dividing both terms by the same nonzero number. Tested by simplification or cross products.
  • Sec 6.4Ratio tables organize equivalent ratios in columns. Every column must reflect the same ratio. Tables make it easy to scale quantities and find missing values systematically.
  • Sec 6.5A rate compares two quantities with different units. A unit rate has a second quantity of 1 and is calculated by dividing the first quantity by the second. Always include both units in the label.
  • Sec 6.6Unit price is the cost per single unit of a product, calculated as total price divided by quantity. It is the most reliable tool for comparing value across different sizes and quantities.
  • Sec 6.7Unit multipliers are fractions equal to 1 that convert between units. Place the unwanted unit in the denominator so it cancels. Two-step conversions use two multipliers in sequence.
  • Sec 6.8A proportion states that two ratios are equal. Cross products test the truth of a proportion. Equal cross products confirm it; unequal cross products disprove it.
  • Sec 6.9Proportions with unknown terms are solved by cross multiplication. Both ratios must compare the same quantities in the same order. Check by substituting the answer back into the proportion.
  • Sec 6.10Percent means per hundred. Percents convert to decimals by dividing by 100 and to fractions by writing over 100 and simplifying. Percents above 100 mean more than the whole.
  • Sec 6.11The three percent problem types all use P = rB. Type 1 finds the part; Type 2 finds the rate; Type 3 finds the base. Identify the unknown before setting up the equation.
  • Sec 6.12Percent change measures increase or decrease as a percent of the original value. The original is always in the denominator. New values are found using Original × (1 ± r).
  • Sec 6.13Simple interest uses I = Prt. Time in months must be divided by 12 to convert to years. Total amount is A = P + I. The formula can be rearranged to solve for P, r, or t.
  • Sec 6.14Proportional reasoning unifies ratios, rates, percents, and interest as a single way of thinking about how quantities compare and scale. It appears in science, finance, cooking, and everyday decision-making.

Key Terms Review

ratio
A comparison of two quantities by division. Written as a to b, a : b, or a/b. Order of terms matters.
equivalent ratios
Ratios that represent the same comparison after simplification. Created by multiplying or dividing both terms by the same nonzero number.
part-to-part ratio
A ratio comparing one group to another group within the same whole. Neither group includes the other.
part-to-whole ratio
A ratio comparing one group to the total of all groups. Connects directly to fractions, decimals, and percents.
rate
A ratio comparing two quantities with different units. Must always include both units in its label.
unit rate
A rate with a second quantity of 1. Calculated by dividing the first quantity by the second.
unit price
The cost per single unit of measurement. Calculated as total price divided by number of units.
unit multiplier
A fraction equal to 1 used to convert between units. The unwanted unit goes in the denominator to cancel.
proportion
An equation stating that two ratios are equal: a/b = c/d. Solved using cross multiplication.
cross products
In a/b = c/d, the products a × d and b × c. Equal cross products confirm a true proportion.
percent
A ratio comparing a quantity to 100. Written with the % symbol. Equivalent to n/100 as a fraction.
base
The whole quantity in a percent problem. The denominator in a part-to-whole comparison.
percentage
The part of the whole in a percent problem. Found using P = rB.
percent increase
The ratio of the amount of increase to the original value, expressed as a percent. Original is the denominator.
percent decrease
The ratio of the amount of decrease to the original value, expressed as a percent. Original is the denominator.
simple interest
Interest computed only on the original principal using I = Prt. Does not compound over time.
principal
The original amount of money borrowed or deposited. The P in the simple interest formula.
interest rate
The percent charged or earned per year. Converted to a decimal before use in formulas.
proportional reasoning
The ability to think about how quantities compare and scale. The unifying idea behind ratios, rates, proportions, percents, and interest.
dimensional analysis
The scientific name for using unit multipliers to convert between units of measurement.

Bridge Note: Looking Ahead
Connecting Chapter 6 to Algebra I and Beyond

The work of this chapter does not end at the checkpoint. The ideas here grow into some of the most important structures in mathematics.

Proportional relationships become linear equations in algebra. When two quantities are always in a constant ratio, their relationship can be written as y = kx, where k is the unit rate. This is the simplest linear function and is the starting point for studying slope, direct variation, and graphing in Algebra I.

Percent problems become equation-solving problems. Every percent problem in this chapter used P = rB, which is a multiplication equation. As equations grow more complex, the same structure continues to appear.

Dimensional analysis using unit multipliers is the foundation of scientific measurement in chemistry, physics, and engineering. The same technique of canceling units through fraction multiplication will be essential in every science course you take.

Simple interest is the entry point to the study of exponential functions. Compound interest, population growth and decay, and radioactive half-life all develop from the basic question this chapter introduced: how does a quantity change over time at a consistent rate?

Proportional reasoning itself is not a chapter topic. It is a way of thinking. Students who develop this habit — asking "how do these quantities relate?" and "what stays the same when I scale?" — will find later mathematics more accessible, more connected, and more meaningful.

Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 5 extended differentiation to the full family of functions from precalculus: trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic. In each case, the function was given explicitly — the output was written as a clear formula in terms of the input. You were given \( f(x) \), and you differentiated it.

But many of the most important mathematical relationships in science, engineering, economics, and geometry are not written that way. A circle, a sphere, a cooling object, a filling tank, an expanding ripple, or an economic equilibrium may be described by an equation relating two or more variables — without solving cleanly for one in terms of another.

This chapter introduces implicit differentiation, a method that extends the chain rule to equations where two variables are related but not explicitly solved. It then introduces related rates, one of the most powerful and practical early applications of derivatives: the analysis of quantities that change together over time.

By the end of this chapter, you should understand how to differentiate relationships that are not in explicit form, how to find slopes and tangent lines on implicit curves, how to connect changing quantities through their rates, and how to read, set up, and solve related rate problems in geometry, motion, science, and engineering.

Opening Question

A ladder 10 feet long leans against a vertical wall. The base of the ladder is being pulled away from the wall at a rate of 2 feet per second. How fast is the top of the ladder sliding down the wall at the moment when the base is 6 feet from the wall?

This is not a differentiation problem in the usual sense — there is no single function \( f(x) \) to differentiate. Instead, there is a geometric relationship between the height of the top of the ladder and the distance of the base from the wall. Both are changing. Calculus lets you find how fast one changes given how fast the other changes. This is a related rates problem, and this chapter builds everything you need to solve it.

Why This Chapter Matters

The ideas in this chapter appear throughout applied mathematics. A satellite's position, velocity, and orbital path are described by equations relating multiple changing variables. The volume, pressure, and temperature of a gas are related by equations that must be differentiated implicitly when any of them changes over time. In economics, the demand for a product depends on its price, and the price depends on production levels — quantities that change together. In machine learning and optimization, gradient computations often involve implicit relationships between parameters, predictions, and loss functions.

Calculus without implicit differentiation is limited to functions written explicitly as \( y = f(x) \). With implicit differentiation, the full power of derivatives extends to relationships described in any form.

Section 6.1

Explicit and Implicit Relationships

What Does Explicit Mean?

When a function is written explicitly, one variable is isolated on one side of the equation and defined as a clear formula in terms of the other. Every function you have differentiated so far in this course has been explicit:

Examples of Explicit Functions

y = x³ − 4x + 1

f(x) = sin(2x) · eˣ

g(x) = (x² + 3) / (x − 1)

In each case, \( y \) or \( f(x) \) is on the left. The right side is a formula involving only \( x \).

What Does Implicit Mean?

A relationship is implicit when both variables appear together in an equation without one being solved in terms of the other. The equation defines a relationship between \( x \) and \( y \), but does not spell out \( y \) as a formula in \( x \).

Implicit EquationName / DescriptionCan be solved explicitly?
\( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \)Circle, radius 5Only as two separate halves: \( y = \pm\sqrt{25-x^2} \)
\( x^3 + y^3 = 6xy \)Folium of DescartesNo clean explicit formula exists
\( \sin(xy) = x + y \)Transcendental curveNot possible explicitly
\( x^2 - 3xy + y^2 = 7 \)Conic sectionPossible but messy
Why Not Always Solve Explicitly?

Consider \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \). Solving gives \( y = \pm\sqrt{25-x^2} \) — two functions, not one. The circle is not a single function. Keeping the equation in implicit form and differentiating implicitly handles the entire curve with one unified calculation.

Graph Instruction

Draw the circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \) centered at the origin with radius 5. Mark the point \( (3, 4) \) on the upper half and \( (3, -4) \) on the lower half. Label both. Note that neither half alone is the whole circle, but both points satisfy the same implicit equation.

Section 6.2

Why Implicit Differentiation Is Needed

Suppose you want the slope of \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \) at \( (3, 4) \). You could solve for \( y = \sqrt{25-x^2} \) and differentiate to get \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{25-x^2}} \), then substitute \( x = 3 \) to get \( -3/4 \). This works — for the upper semicircle only. For \( (3,-4) \) you would need a second calculation using the lower semicircle formula.

For equations where \( y \) cannot be solved explicitly at all — like \( x^3 + y^3 = 6xy \) or \( \sin(xy) = x + y \) — there is no choice. Implicit differentiation handles every point on every such curve through one systematic method.

The Chain Rule Is the Key

Implicit differentiation is not a new rule. It is the chain rule, applied carefully to a variable that depends on another. When you differentiate \( y^2 \) with respect to \( x \), you treat \( y \) as a function of \( x \):

\( \frac{d}{dx}[y^2] = 2y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \)

This is the central idea. Once you see why this step is necessary, the rest follows naturally.

Section 6.3

Differentiating Both Sides

The Five-Step Method for Implicit Differentiation

1. Start with an equation relating \( x \) and \( y \).

2. Differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \( x \).

3. Apply the chain rule to every term involving \( y \) — attach \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) as a factor.

4. Collect all terms with \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) on one side.

5. Factor and solve for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \).

Example 6.3.1
Differentiating expressions involving \( y \) with respect to \( x \)
  • ProblemFind \( \frac{d}{dx} \) of each: (a) \( y^3 \)   (b) \( 5y \)   (c) \( y^2 - 3y \)   (d) \( \sin(y) \)
  • ThinkEach involves \( y \), which depends on \( x \). The chain rule requires multiplying by the derivative of the inner function \( y \) with respect to \( x \).
  • Calculate (a) d/dx [y³] = 3y² · dy/dx (b) d/dx [5y] = 5 · dy/dx (c) d/dx [y² − 3y] = 2y · dy/dx − 3 · dy/dx = (2y − 3) dy/dx (d) d/dx [sin(y)] = cos(y) · dy/dx
  • InterpretEvery expression in \( y \) differentiated with respect to \( x \) picks up a factor of \( \frac{dy}{dx} \). This is the chain rule at work — \( y \) is the inner function, and its derivative with respect to \( x \) is \( \frac{dy}{dx} \).
Section 6.4

Treating \( y \) as a Function of \( x \)

When you differentiate \( y^2 \) with respect to \( x \), you are differentiating the composition \( [y(x)]^2 \). By the chain rule: \( \frac{d}{dx}\left[(y(x))^2\right] = 2 \cdot y(x) \cdot y'(x) = 2y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \). You are not assuming \( y \) has an explicit formula. You are only assuming that \( y \) is a function of \( x \) — which is guaranteed by the equation relating them, at least locally near a given point.

This assumption is supported by the implicit function theorem: under mild conditions, an implicit equation \( F(x, y) = 0 \) defines \( y \) as a function of \( x \) near any point where the partial derivatives are not both zero — even if we cannot write an explicit formula.

Notation Reminder

\( \frac{dy}{dx} \) and \( y' \) both mean the same thing: the derivative of \( y \) with respect to \( x \). In implicit differentiation, \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) appears naturally in the calculation and is the preferred form because it reminds you that \( y \) is changing with respect to \( x \).

Section 6.5

Finding Slopes of Implicit Curves

Example 6.5.1
Implicit differentiation — circle
  • ProblemFind \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) for the circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \).
  • ThinkDifferentiate both sides with respect to \( x \). The term \( x^2 \) differentiates normally. The term \( y^2 \) requires the chain rule.
  • Calculate d/dx [x² + y²] = d/dx [25] 2x + 2y · dy/dx = 0 2y · dy/dx = −2x dy/dx = −x / y
  • CheckAt \( (3, 4) \): slope = \( -3/4 \). At \( (3, -4) \): slope = \( -3/(-4) = 3/4 \). One formula handles both points.
  • InterpretThe slope of the circle at any point \( (x, y) \) is \( -x/y \). At the top and bottom (\( x = 0 \)), the slope is zero — horizontal tangents. At the far sides (\( y = 0 \)), the slope is undefined — vertical tangents.
Answer: \( \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-x}{y} \)
Example 6.5.2
Implicit differentiation — folium of Descartes
  • ProblemFind \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) for \( x^3 + y^3 = 6xy \).
  • ThinkThis curve has no clean explicit formula for \( y \). The right side \( 6xy \) is a product of \( x \) and \( y \), so the product rule applies there.
  • Calculate Left side: d/dx [x³ + y³] = 3x² + 3y² · dy/dx Right side: d/dx [6xy] = 6(x · dy/dx + y · 1) = 6x dy/dx + 6y Setting equal: 3x² + 3y² dy/dx = 6x dy/dx + 6y Collect: 3y² dy/dx − 6x dy/dx = 6y − 3x² Factor: (3y² − 6x) dy/dx = 6y − 3x² Solve: dy/dx = (2y − x²) / (y² − 2x)
  • CheckThe formula requires \( y^2 \neq 2x \). Where this denominator is zero, the tangent line is vertical.
  • InterpretEven without an explicit formula for \( y \), implicit differentiation gives a usable slope expression for any point on the curve.
Answer: \( \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2y - x^2}{y^2 - 2x} \)
Example 6.5.3
Implicit differentiation — trigonometric equation
  • ProblemFind \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) for \( \sin(x + y) = y^2 \cos x \).
  • ThinkThe left side uses chain rule for \( \sin(x+y) \). The right side requires the product rule on \( y^2 \cos x \).
  • Calculate Left: cos(x+y) · (1 + dy/dx) Right: 2y dy/dx · cos x + y²(−sin x) Expand left: cos(x+y) + cos(x+y) dy/dx = 2y cos x · dy/dx − y² sin x Collect: [cos(x+y) − 2y cos x] dy/dx = −y² sin x − cos(x+y) dy/dx = (−y² sin x − cos(x+y)) / (cos(x+y) − 2y cos x)
  • InterpretThe result is complex in appearance but was obtained by systematic application of the chain rule and product rule — no new technique was required.
Answer: \( \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-y^2\sin x - \cos(x+y)}{\cos(x+y) - 2y\cos x} \)

Section 6.6

Tangent Lines to Implicit Curves

Once you have \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) from implicit differentiation, finding the equation of a tangent line follows the same three steps as in Chapter 3: find \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) in terms of \( x \) and \( y \), evaluate it at the given point, then apply point-slope form \( y - y_0 = m(x - x_0) \).

Example 6.6.1
Tangent line to a circle at a given point
  • ProblemFind the equation of the tangent line to \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \) at the point \( (3, 4) \).
  • ThinkFrom Example 6.5.1, \( \frac{dy}{dx} = -x/y \). Evaluate at \( (3, 4) \) to get the slope.
  • Calculate m = −3/4 y − 4 = −(3/4)(x − 3) y = −(3/4)x + 9/4 + 4 = −(3/4)x + 25/4
  • CheckAt \( x = 3 \): \( y = -9/4 + 25/4 = 16/4 = 4 \). ✓ The radius to \( (3,4) \) has slope \( 4/3 \). Product of slopes: \( (4/3)(-3/4) = -1 \). ✓ Tangent is perpendicular to radius.
  • InterpretThe tangent to a circle at any point is perpendicular to the radius at that point. Implicit differentiation confirms this geometric fact algebraically.
Answer: \( y = -\frac{3}{4}x + \frac{25}{4} \)
Example 6.6.2
Tangent line to a mixed implicit curve
  • ProblemFind the slope of the tangent line to \( x^2 y + y^3 = 10 \) at the point \( (1, 2) \).
  • ThinkThe term \( x^2 y \) requires the product rule because both \( x^2 \) and \( y \) depend on \( x \). The term \( y^3 \) requires the chain rule.
  • Calculate d/dx [x²y + y³] = d/dx [10] (2xy + x² dy/dx) + 3y² dy/dx = 0 2xy + dy/dx(x² + 3y²) = 0 dy/dx = −2xy / (x² + 3y²) At (1, 2): dy/dx = −2(1)(2) / (1 + 12) = −4/13
  • CheckVerify \( (1,2) \) is on the curve: \( (1)^2(2) + (2)^3 = 2 + 8 = 10 \). ✓
  • InterpretAt \( (1, 2) \), the curve is decreasing with slope \( -4/13 \). Tangent line: \( y - 2 = -\frac{4}{13}(x-1) \).
Answer: slope \( = -\frac{4}{13} \)
Section 6.7

Derivatives of Inverse Relationships

Implicit differentiation provides an elegant path to derivatives of inverse functions. If \( y = f^{-1}(x) \), then \( f(y) = x \). Differentiating both sides with respect to \( x \):

Inverse Function Derivative Rule

If \( y = f^{-1}(x) \), differentiate \( f(y) = x \):

f'(y) · dy/dx = 1

dy/dx = 1 / f'(y)

In other words: \( \frac{d}{dx}[f^{-1}(x)] = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(x))} \)

Example 6.7.1
Deriving \( \frac{d}{dx}[\arcsin x] \)
  • ProblemFind \( \frac{d}{dx}[\arcsin x] \) using implicit differentiation.
  • ThinkLet \( y = \arcsin x \), so \( \sin y = x \). Differentiate both sides with respect to \( x \).
  • Calculate cos y · dy/dx = 1 dy/dx = 1 / cos y Use sin²y + cos²y = 1: cos y = √(1 − sin²y) = √(1 − x²) dy/dx = 1 / √(1 − x²), −1 < x < 1
  • CheckThe derivative is always positive: arcsine is increasing. The slope grows larger as \( x \to \pm 1 \), consistent with how the arcsine curve rises sharply near its endpoints.
  • InterpretImplicit differentiation derives the arcsine formula cleanly, without needing an explicit formula for the inverse function.
Answer: \( \displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}[\arcsin x] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \)
Example 6.7.2
Deriving \( \frac{d}{dx}[\arctan x] \)
  • ProblemFind \( \frac{d}{dx}[\arctan x] \) using implicit differentiation.
  • ThinkLet \( y = \arctan x \), so \( \tan y = x \).
  • Calculate sec²y · dy/dx = 1 dy/dx = 1 / sec²y = cos²y Use sec²y = 1 + tan²y = 1 + x²: dy/dx = 1 / (1 + x²)
  • InterpretAlways positive, always between 0 and 1. As \( x \to \pm\infty \), the slope approaches zero — consistent with arctangent leveling off toward \( \pm\pi/2 \).
Answer: \( \displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}[\arctan x] = \frac{1}{1 + x^2} \)

Section 6.8

What Related Rates Measure

A related rate is the rate of change of one quantity that is connected to the rate of change of another quantity through a known equation. Suppose two variables \( A \) and \( r \) are related by \( A = \pi r^2 \) — the area of a circle. Both can change over time. If the radius is increasing at a known rate, calculus lets you find how fast the area is increasing. Differentiate both sides of \( A = \pi r^2 \) with respect to time \( t \):

The Related Rate for a Circle

\( A = \pi r^2 \)

\( \frac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi r \cdot \frac{dr}{dt} \)

This says: the rate at which area changes equals \( 2\pi r \) times the rate at which the radius changes. If you know \( r \) and \( \frac{dr}{dt} \) at a moment, you can compute \( \frac{dA}{dt} \) at that moment.

Notation for Rates of Change

NotationMeaningTypical Units
\( \frac{dx}{dt} \)Rate of change of \( x \) with respect to timem/s, ft/min, …
\( \frac{dV}{dt} \)Rate of change of volumem³/min, L/s, …
\( \frac{dA}{dt} \)Rate of change of areacm²/s, ft²/hr, …
\( \frac{dr}{dt} \)Rate of change of radiuscm/s, m/min, …
\( \frac{dh}{dt} \)Rate of change of heightm/min, ft/s, …
Section 6.9

Setting Up Related Rate Problems

Five-Step Method for Related Rates

Step 1. Draw and label. Sketch the situation. Label all changing quantities as variables. Do not assign numerical values to changing quantities yet.

Step 2. Identify the given rate and the unknown rate. Write down what you know and what you need.

Step 3. Write the relationship equation. Use geometry, physics, or a formula connecting the variables whose rates appear.

Step 4. Differentiate both sides with respect to \( t \). Apply the chain rule. Every variable picks up a rate factor.

Step 5. Substitute and solve. Plug in values at the specific moment. Solve for the unknown rate. Include units.

Critical Warning — Never Substitute Before Differentiating

If you substitute the specific numerical value of a changing variable before differentiating, you turn it into a constant. Differentiating a constant gives zero — which is wrong. The equation before differentiation is a general relationship. After differentiation, it is a rate equation. Only then do you substitute the values at the specific moment in question.

Example 6.9.1
Expanding circle — area changing
  • ProblemThe radius of a circle increases at 3 cm/s. How fast is the area increasing when the radius is 7 cm?
  • Given\( \frac{dr}{dt} = 3 \) cm/s. Find \( \frac{dA}{dt} \) when \( r = 7 \) cm.
  • Relate\( A = \pi r^2 \)
  • Differentiate dA/dt = 2πr · dr/dt
  • Substitute dA/dt = 2π(7)(3) = 42π ≈ 131.9 cm²/s
  • InterpretWhen the radius is 7 cm and growing at 3 cm/s, the area is growing at approximately 132 cm²/s. The unit cm²/s confirms this is an area rate.
Answer: \( \frac{dA}{dt} = 42\pi \approx 131.9 \) cm²/s
Section 6.10

Geometry-Based Related Rates

Example 6.10.1
The sliding ladder
  • ProblemA 10-foot ladder leans against a vertical wall. The base slides away at 2 ft/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the base is 6 feet from the wall?
  • LabelLet \( x \) = horizontal distance of base from wall, \( y \) = height of top on wall. Both change with time. Hypotenuse = 10.
  • Given\( \frac{dx}{dt} = 2 \) ft/s. Find \( \frac{dy}{dt} \) when \( x = 6 \) ft.
  • Relate\( x^2 + y^2 = 100 \)
  • Differentiate 2x dx/dt + 2y dy/dt = 0
  • Find yWhen \( x = 6 \): \( y = \sqrt{100 - 36} = \sqrt{64} = 8 \) ft.
  • Substitute 2(6)(2) + 2(8) dy/dt = 0 24 + 16 dy/dt = 0 dy/dt = −24/16 = −1.5 ft/s
  • InterpretThe negative sign means the top is sliding down at 1.5 ft/s. This is physically correct: as the base moves out, the top falls.
Answer: \( \frac{dy}{dt} = -1.5 \) ft/s (top slides down at 1.5 ft/s)
Example 6.10.2
Water draining from a conical tank
  • ProblemWater drains from an inverted conical tank (vertex at bottom, height 5 m, base radius 2 m) at 2 m³/min. How fast is the water level dropping when the water is 3 m deep?
  • Given\( \frac{dV}{dt} = -2 \) m³/min. Find \( \frac{dh}{dt} \) when \( h = 3 \) m.
  • RelateUse similar triangles to relate \( r \) and \( h \): full dimensions give \( r/h = 2/5 \), so \( r = 2h/5 \). Substitute into volume formula:
  • Equation V = (1/3)π r² h = (1/3)π (2h/5)² h = 4πh³/75
  • Differentiate dV/dt = (4π/75) · 3h² · dh/dt = (4πh²/25) dh/dt
  • Substitute −2 = (4π · 9 / 25) dh/dt = (36π/25) dh/dt dh/dt = −50 / (36π) = −25/(18π) ≈ −0.442 m/min
  • InterpretThe water level drops at approximately 0.44 m/min when the depth is 3 m. The negative sign confirms the level is falling.
Answer: \( \frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{-25}{18\pi} \approx -0.442 \) m/min

Example 6.10.3
Expanding ripple on a pond
  • ProblemThe radius of a circular ripple increases at 4 m/s. How fast is the enclosed area increasing when the radius is 10 m?
  • Calculate A = πr² → dA/dt = 2πr · dr/dt = 2π(10)(4) = 80π ≈ 251.3 m²/s
  • InterpretThe area grows at approximately 251 m²/s — far faster than the 4 m/s radius growth, because area scales as the square of the radius.
Answer: \( \frac{dA}{dt} = 80\pi \approx 251.3 \) m²/s
Section 6.11

Motion-Based Related Rates

Example 6.11.1
Two cars moving at a right angle
  • ProblemCar A moves north at 60 mph, Car B moves east at 80 mph. Both start from the same intersection. How fast is the distance between them increasing after 1 hour?
  • Label\( y \) = northward distance of A, \( x \) = eastward distance of B, \( z \) = distance between them.
  • At t = 1\( y = 60 \), \( x = 80 \), \( z = \sqrt{60^2 + 80^2} = \sqrt{10000} = 100 \) miles.
  • Relate\( z^2 = x^2 + y^2 \)
  • Differentiate z dz/dt = x dx/dt + y dy/dt 100 · dz/dt = 80(80) + 60(60) = 6400 + 3600 = 10000 dz/dt = 100 mph
  • InterpretAfter 1 hour, the cars are separating at exactly 100 mph — a 3-4-5 triangle scaled by 20, giving a clean result.
Answer: \( \frac{dz}{dt} = 100 \) mph
Example 6.11.2
Observer tracking a moving car
  • ProblemA person stands 30 m from a straight road. A car passes at 20 m/s. How fast is the distance from the person to the car increasing 2 seconds after the car passes the closest point?
  • Label\( x \) = distance of car from closest point on road, \( z \) = distance from person to car. Fixed perpendicular distance = 30 m.
  • At t = 2 s\( x = 40 \) m, \( z = \sqrt{40^2 + 900} = \sqrt{2500} = 50 \) m.
  • Calculate z² = x² + 900 → 2z dz/dt = 2x dx/dt 50 dz/dt = 40 · 20 = 800 dz/dt = 16 m/s
  • Interpret16 m/s — less than the car's 20 m/s, because part of the car's motion is across the field of view rather than purely away from the observer.
Answer: \( \frac{dz}{dt} = 16 \) m/s
Section 6.12

Applied Related Rates in Science and Engineering

Example 6.12.1
Boyle's Law: pressure and volume
  • ProblemBoyle's Law: \( PV = k \) (constant temperature). When \( P = 50 \) lb/in² and \( V = 100 \) in³, the volume is increasing at 5 in³/min. How fast is the pressure changing?
  • Differentiate P dV/dt + V dP/dt = 0 50(5) + 100 dP/dt = 0 dP/dt = −2.5 lb/in²/min
  • InterpretPressure drops at 2.5 lb/in² per minute as the volume expands — exactly what Boyle's Law predicts: pressure and volume change in opposite directions at constant temperature.
Answer: \( \frac{dP}{dt} = -2.5 \) lb/in²/min
Example 6.12.2
Shadow length
  • ProblemA 2 m person walks away from a 6 m lamppost at 1.5 m/s. How fast is the shadow lengthening?
  • Label\( x \) = distance of person from lamppost, \( s \) = length of shadow. Tip of shadow is at \( x + s \) from the post.
  • Similar triangles 6 / (x + s) = 2 / s → 6s = 2x + 2s → 4s = 2x → s = x/2
  • Differentiate ds/dt = (1/2) dx/dt = (1/2)(1.5) = 0.75 m/s
  • InterpretThe shadow always grows at half the person's walking speed — at any distance from the post. The linear relationship \( s = x/2 \) means the rate is constant throughout the walk.
Answer: \( \frac{ds}{dt} = 0.75 \) m/s
Example 6.12.3
Filling a cylindrical tank
  • ProblemWater is pumped into a cylindrical tank (radius 3 m) at 5 m³/min. How fast is the water level rising?
  • Calculate V = π(9)h = 9πh → dV/dt = 9π dh/dt 5 = 9π dh/dt → dh/dt = 5/(9π) ≈ 0.177 m/min
  • InterpretThe level rises at about 0.177 m/min regardless of the current depth — the constant cross-section of a cylinder means the inflow rate produces a constant rise rate.
Answer: \( \frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{5}{9\pi} \approx 0.177 \) m/min

Section 6.13

Common Implicit and Related Rate Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting dy/dx on y-terms
d/dx [y³] = 3y²

Correct: \( \frac{d}{dx}[y^3] = 3y^2 \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \). The chain rule requires multiplying by the derivative of the inner function. Differentiating any expression in \( y \) with respect to \( x \) must include a \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) factor.

Mistake 2 — Substituting numbers before differentiating
r = 5, so V = (4/3)π(125) = constant; dV/dt = 0

Correct: Differentiate the general equation \( V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \) first to get \( \frac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \frac{dr}{dt} \). Only then substitute \( r = 5 \). Substituting first replaces a variable with a constant; constants have zero derivative and all rate information is destroyed.

Mistake 3 — Missing the product rule in implicit differentiation
d/dx [x²y] = 2xy'

Correct: \( \frac{d}{dx}[x^2y] = 2x \cdot y + x^2 \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \). The expression \( x^2y \) is a product of two functions of \( x \). The product rule applies: differentiate the first times the second, plus the first times the derivative of the second.

Mistake 4 — Assuming rates of related variables are equal
"The ladder is 10 feet, so dx/dt and dy/dt are the same."

Correct: The relationship \( x^2 + y^2 = 100 \) gives \( 2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \), so \( \frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{x}{y}\frac{dx}{dt} \). The rates depend on the current values of \( x \) and \( y \), which change over time. They are almost never equal.

Mistake 5 — Getting the sign wrong
dh/dt = +0.44 m/min when the tank is draining

Correct: A decreasing quantity has a negative rate. If water is draining, \( \frac{dV}{dt} < 0 \). If the water level is dropping, \( \frac{dh}{dt} < 0 \). Always check whether the sign of your answer matches the physical direction of change.

Mistake 6 — Using the wrong geometric formula
Using the sphere volume formula for a cone problem

Correct: Always identify the shape before writing the formula. Verify: Sphere \( V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \). Cone \( V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h \). Cylinder \( V = \pi r^2 h \). Using the wrong formula gives a wrong rate equation and a wrong answer.

Mistake 7 — Failing to use similar triangles to reduce variables
Differentiating V = (1/3)πr²h without eliminating r

Correct: In cone problems, there are two changing spatial variables, \( r \) and \( h \). You can only differentiate one equation with respect to \( t \). Use similar triangles to express \( r \) in terms of \( h \) before differentiating, reducing to a single variable.

Mistake 8 — Omitting units from the answer
dy/dt = −1.5

Correct: A rate without units is incomplete. State the units: \( -1.5 \) ft/s, or m/min, etc. Units confirm the meaning of the derivative and catch unit-conversion errors. If the length is in feet and time in seconds, the rate is in ft/s.

Section 6.14

Preparing for Derivative Applications

This chapter extended differentiation in two important directions. Implicit differentiation lets you find \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) for any curve or relationship — even when you cannot solve for \( y \) explicitly. Differentiate both sides with respect to \( x \), attach \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) to every \( y \)-term by the chain rule, and solve. Related rates use derivatives to analyze how quantities change together over time. Label clearly, write the relationship equation, differentiate with respect to \( t \), and substitute only after differentiating.

Both methods rest on the same foundation: the chain rule. Implicit differentiation is the chain rule applied to \( y \) as a function of \( x \). Related rates is the chain rule applied to all variables as functions of \( t \). You have now differentiated everything from polynomial functions to implicit curves to changing geometric quantities.

Chapter 6 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1An explicit function isolates \( y \) as a formula in \( x \). An implicit relationship gives an equation in both \( x \) and \( y \) without solving for either. Many important curves cannot be expressed explicitly, or require two separate explicit formulas.
  • Sec 6.2Implicit differentiation is needed when solving for \( y \) is impractical or impossible. The method works by differentiating both sides of an equation with respect to \( x \), treating \( y \) as a function of \( x \) and applying the chain rule.
  • Sec 6.3The five-step method: differentiate both sides → apply chain rule to \( y \)-terms (each picks up \( \frac{dy}{dx} \)) → collect \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) terms → factor → solve. The chain rule is the entire mechanism.
  • Sec 6.4Treating \( y \) as a function of \( x \) is justified by the implicit function theorem. The notation \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) (or \( y' \)) represents this derivative throughout implicit calculations.
  • Sec 6.5Implicit differentiation gives \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) as a formula in both \( x \) and \( y \). This formula gives the slope of the curve at any point where the denominator is nonzero. Examples include circles, the folium of Descartes, and trigonometric implicit equations.
  • Sec 6.6To find a tangent line: differentiate implicitly to get \( \frac{dy}{dx} \), evaluate at the given point, apply point-slope form. The tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at that point, as confirmed algebraically.
  • Sec 6.7If \( y = f^{-1}(x) \), then \( f(y) = x \). Differentiating gives \( f'(y) \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \), so \( \frac{dy}{dx} = 1/f'(y) \). This procedure derives all six inverse trigonometric derivative formulas.
  • Sec 6.8A related rate connects the rate of change of one quantity to the rate of another through a shared equation. Rates are written as derivatives with respect to time \( t \). Units of rates must be stated explicitly.
  • Sec 6.9The five-step method: draw and label → identify given and unknown rates → write the relationship equation → differentiate with respect to \( t \) → substitute and solve. Never substitute specific values of changing variables before differentiating.
  • Sec 6.10Geometry problems use area, volume, and the Pythagorean theorem. Cone problems require similar triangles to eliminate one variable before differentiating. The sign of the rate reflects the direction of change.
  • Sec 6.11Motion problems with two moving objects use the Pythagorean theorem to relate positions and the separating distance. The resulting rate of separation depends on both positions and both velocities at the moment in question.
  • Sec 6.12Physical applications include Boyle's Law, shadow length (via similar triangles), and tank filling (where constant cross-section gives constant rate). Units and context guide interpretation of every answer.
  • Sec 6.13Eight recurring errors: missing \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) on \( y \)-terms; substituting before differentiating; missing the product rule; assuming equal rates; sign errors; wrong formulas; failing to use similar triangles; omitting units.
  • Sec 6.14Both methods in this chapter rest on the chain rule. Chapter 7 uses the first and second derivatives to analyze graph behavior: increasing/decreasing intervals, extrema, concavity, and inflection points.

Key Terms Review

explicit function
A relationship written as \( y = f(x) \), where one variable is solved as a clear formula in terms of the other.
implicit relationship
An equation involving two or more variables where one is not solved explicitly in terms of the other. Examples: \( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \), \( x^3 + y^3 = 6xy \).
implicit differentiation
Differentiating both sides of an equation with respect to \( x \), treating \( y \) as a function of \( x \) and applying the chain rule to every \( y \)-term.
chain rule
The rule \( \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \). The engine behind implicit differentiation: \( y \) is the inner function, and every \( y \)-term picks up a \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) factor.
related rates
Using derivatives to connect the rates of change of two or more variables that are related by an equation. Both variables are functions of time \( t \).
implicit curve
A curve defined by an implicit equation such as \( x^2 + y^2 = r^2 \). Not necessarily the graph of a single function.
tangent line
A line touching a curve at a single point with slope equal to \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) at that point. Found using point-slope form after evaluating the derivative at the given point.
similar triangles
Triangles with the same angles and proportional sides. Used in cone and shadow problems to express one variable in terms of another before differentiating.

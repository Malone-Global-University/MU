Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces differential equations as the language of change. A differential equation is not simply a harder algebra equation — it is a different kind of mathematical statement entirely. Rather than asking "what is the value of \( x \)?", a differential equation asks "what function satisfies this relationship between a quantity and its rate of change?"

Differential equations arise naturally in almost every quantitative field. A biologist describing a growing bacterial colony, an engineer tracking the temperature of a cooling object, an economist modeling a savings account, and a physicist analyzing a falling object are all working with differential equations, whether they name them as such or not. The goal of this chapter is to make that connection explicit, to build careful reasoning about what differential equations mean, and to develop reliable techniques for solving the most accessible class: separable first-order differential equations.

Students who have worked through Chapters 9 through 12 have already encountered antiderivatives, initial value problems, and accumulation. This chapter extends and formalizes those ideas.

Opening Question

A scientist introduces a small number of bacteria into a nutrient-rich dish and measures the population every hour. The measurements suggest that the rate at which the population grows is proportional to the population itself — larger populations grow faster. No fixed formula for the population was given. Instead, only the rule governing how it changes is known. How do you find the population function? And once you have it, what does it tell you about the future behavior of the colony? This is the central kind of question a differential equation answers.

Section 13.1

What a Differential Equation Is

A differential equation is an equation that involves an unknown function and one or more of its derivatives. Rather than asking for a specific number, it asks for a function whose derivative relationship matches the equation.

To make this concrete, consider a simple example. Suppose you know that the rate at which a quantity \( y \) changes with respect to time \( t \) is always equal to the quantity itself:

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = y$$

This is a differential equation. It says: "find a function \( y(t) \) such that when you differentiate it, you get the function back." The function \( y = e^t \) satisfies this, and so does \( y = 2e^t \), and more generally \( y = Ce^t \) for any constant \( C \). This family of functions is the general solution.

The Order of a Differential Equation

The order of a differential equation is the highest derivative that appears. \( \frac{dy}{dt} = y \) is first-order; \( \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = -y \) is second-order. This chapter focuses on first-order equations.

EquationOrderWhat It Describes
\( \frac{dy}{dt} = ky \)FirstGrowth or decay proportional to current value
\( \frac{dy}{dt} = k(A - y) \)FirstRate of change proportional to distance from target
\( \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = -\omega^2 y \)SecondSimple harmonic oscillation (springs, pendulums)
\( \frac{dP}{dt} = rP\!\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) \)FirstLogistic population growth with carrying capacity
Read First, Compute Second

Before solving any differential equation, read it carefully and describe in words what it says. Developing this habit builds understanding and prevents many common errors. Ask: what does the rate depend on? Is it always growing, always decaying, or does behavior change at a certain value?

Section 13.2

Solutions as Functions

The solution to a differential equation is not a number — it is a function. This is the single most important conceptual shift in this chapter. When you solve \( 2x + 3 = 11 \), you find the number \( x = 4 \). When you solve \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 2t \), you find the function \( y(t) = t^2 + C \).

General vs. Particular Solutions

The general solution is a family of functions satisfying the equation, containing one or more arbitrary constants. For a first-order ODE, it typically contains one constant \( C \).

A particular solution is a single member of that family, selected by applying an initial condition \( y(t_0) = y_0 \).

Graph Instruction — Family of Solutions

Draw several curves of the form \( y = t^2 + C \) for \( C = -2,\, 0,\, 2,\, 4 \). Each curve is a parabola shifted vertically. Together they form the general solution to \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 2t \). No single curve is the complete answer; the entire family is. An initial condition pins down which parabola is the answer to a specific problem.

Section 13.3

Verifying Solutions

One of the most important and underused skills in differential equations is verification: checking whether a given function actually satisfies a given equation. Verification requires substituting the proposed function and its derivative into the equation and confirming that both sides are equal for all values of the variable — not just at one point.

Example 13.3.1
Verifying an Exponential Solution
  • ProblemVerify that \( y = 3e^{2t} \) is a solution to \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 2y \).
  • ThinkCompute \( \frac{dy}{dt} \) from the proposed function, then compute \( 2y \), and confirm they are equal.
  • Calculate $$\frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(3e^{2t}\right) = 6e^{2t}$$ $$2y = 2 \cdot 3e^{2t} = 6e^{2t}$$
  • CheckBoth expressions equal \( 6e^{2t} \) for all \( t \). ✓
Answer: \( y = 3e^{2t} \) is a solution to \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 2y \).
Example 13.3.2
A Function That Is Not a Solution
  • ProblemDetermine whether \( y = t^2 \) satisfies \( \frac{dy}{dt} = y \).
  • Calculate\( \frac{dy}{dt} = 2t \) and \( y = t^2 \).
  • CheckIs \( 2t = t^2 \) for all \( t \)? No — it holds only at \( t = 0 \) and \( t = 2 \), not as an identity.
Answer: \( y = t^2 \) is not a solution. It fails to satisfy the equation for all values of \( t \).
Example 13.3.3
Verifying a Trigonometric Solution
  • ProblemVerify that \( y = \sin(3t) \) satisfies \( \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = -9y \).
  • Calculate $$\frac{dy}{dt} = 3\cos(3t), \qquad \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = -9\sin(3t)$$ $$-9y = -9\sin(3t)$$
  • CheckBoth sides equal \( -9\sin(3t) \). ✓
Answer: \( y = \sin(3t) \) is a solution to this second-order equation.
Section 13.4

Slope Fields

When a differential equation cannot be solved analytically, or when you want a visual picture of all possible solutions before computing, a slope field (also called a direction field) is an invaluable tool. A first-order equation of the form \( \frac{dy}{dt} = f(t, y) \) specifies the slope of the solution curve at every point \( (t, y) \) in the plane. A slope field visualizes this by drawing a short segment at each grid point with the indicated slope.

Graph Instruction — Slope Field for \( \frac{dy}{dt} = y \)

Choose a grid with \( t \in \{-2,-1,0,1,2\} \) and \( y \in \{-2,-1,0,1,2\} \). At each point \( (t,y) \), draw a short segment with slope \( = y \). Along \( y = 0 \), all segments are horizontal. Along \( y = 1 \), all segments have slope 1. Along \( y = -1 \), all segments have slope \( -1 \). The resulting field shows exponential curves flowing upward above the axis and downward below it, consistent with the general solution \( y = Ce^t \).

Any solution curve must be tangent to the slope field at every point it passes through. Tracing a path through the field following the indicated slopes sketches a particular solution.

Equilibrium Solutions

An equilibrium solution is a constant function \( y = c \) where the slope field shows horizontal segments along the entire line — meaning \( f(t, c) = 0 \) for all \( t \). The logistic equation \( \frac{dP}{dt} = rP\!\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) \) has equilibria at \( P = 0 \) (unstable) and \( P = K \) (stable).

Example 13.4.1
Interpreting a Slope Field for \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 1 - y \)
  • SituationA slope field is drawn. At each point \( (t, y) \), the segment has slope \( 1 - y \). Along \( y = 1 \), all slopes are zero. Above \( y = 1 \), slopes are negative. Below \( y = 1 \), slopes are positive.
  • ThinkWhat does this tell us about solution behavior?
  • AnalyzeSolutions starting above \( y = 1 \) decrease toward it. Solutions starting below increase toward it. The equilibrium at \( y = 1 \) is stable — all nearby solutions are attracted to it.
  • VerifyThe general solution is \( y = 1 + Ce^{-t} \). As \( t \to \infty \), \( Ce^{-t} \to 0 \), so all solutions approach \( y = 1 \). ✓
The slope field reveals qualitative behavior — stable equilibrium at \( y = 1 \) — before any algebraic computation.
Section 13.5

Initial Conditions

The general solution to a first-order ODE contains one arbitrary constant \( C \). An initial condition specifies the value of the function at one particular input — written \( y(t_0) = y_0 \) — and selects a unique particular solution from the general family. Together, a differential equation and initial condition form an initial value problem (IVP):

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = f(t, y), \qquad y(t_0) = y_0$$
Example 13.5.1
Solving an Initial Value Problem
  • ProblemSolve \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 3t^2 \) with \( y(0) = 4 \).
  • ThinkIntegrate both sides to find the general solution, then apply the initial condition.
  • Calculate $$y = \int 3t^2\,dt = t^3 + C$$ Apply \( y(0) = 4 \): \( \quad 4 = 0 + C \implies C = 4 \)
  • CheckDifferentiate \( y = t^3 + 4 \): gives \( 3t^2 \). ✓ At \( t = 0 \): \( y = 4 \). ✓
Particular solution: \( y = t^3 + 4 \)
Example 13.5.2
Initial Condition in a Growth Model
  • ProblemA bacterial colony satisfies \( \frac{dP}{dt} = 0.5P \) with initial population \( P(0) = 200 \) (in thousands).
  • CalculateGeneral solution: \( P = Ce^{0.5t} \). Apply \( P(0) = 200 \): \( C = 200 \).
  • Result\( P(t) = 200e^{0.5t} \). After 2 hours: \( P(2) = 200e^1 \approx 543 \) thousand cells.
Particular solution: \( P(t) = 200e^{0.5t} \)

Section 13.6

Separable Differential Equations

The most important solution technique in this chapter is separation of variables. It applies to differential equations where the variables \( y \) and \( t \) can be arranged on opposite sides of the equation. A first-order ODE is separable if it can be written in the form:

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = g(t) \cdot h(y) \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy = g(t)\,dt$$
Separation of Variables — Procedure

1. Rearrange so all \( y \) terms and \( dy \) are on one side, all \( t \) terms and \( dt \) are on the other.

2. Integrate both sides.

3. Solve for \( y \) if possible.

4. Apply the initial condition if given to find \( C \).

5. Verify the result satisfies the original equation.

Example 13.6.1
Basic Separation of Variables
  • ProblemSolve \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 3y \).
  • Separate\( \dfrac{1}{y}\,dy = 3\,dt \)
  • Integrate $$\int \frac{1}{y}\,dy = \int 3\,dt \implies \ln|y| = 3t + C_1$$
  • SolveExponentiate: \( |y| = e^{C_1}e^{3t} \). Let \( C = \pm e^{C_1} \), so \( y = Ce^{3t} \).
  • Check\( \frac{dy}{dt} = 3Ce^{3t} = 3y \). ✓
General solution: \( y = Ce^{3t} \)
Example 13.6.2
Separable with Both Variables — Curves of Integration
  • ProblemSolve \( \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{t}{y} \).
  • Separate\( y\,dy = t\,dt \)
  • Integrate $$\frac{y^2}{2} = \frac{t^2}{2} + C_1 \implies y^2 = t^2 + C$$
  • CheckDifferentiate implicitly: \( 2y\,\frac{dy}{dt} = 2t \implies \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{t}{y} \). ✓
General solution: \( y^2 = t^2 + C \) (a family of hyperbolas)
Example 13.6.3
Separable with Initial Condition
  • ProblemSolve \( \frac{dy}{dx} = xy \) with \( y(0) = 5 \).
  • Separate\( \dfrac{1}{y}\,dy = x\,dx \)
  • Integrate $$\ln|y| = \frac{x^2}{2} + C_1 \implies y = Ce^{x^2/2}$$
  • Apply IC\( 5 = Ce^0 = C \), so \( C = 5 \).
  • Check\( \frac{dy}{dx} = 5 \cdot x \cdot e^{x^2/2} = x \cdot y \). ✓ At \( x = 0 \): \( y = 5 \). ✓
Particular solution: \( y = 5e^{x^2/2} \)
Not All First-Order Equations Are Separable

The equation \( \frac{dy}{dt} = y + t \) cannot be separated — the right side does not factor into a product of a function of \( t \) alone times a function of \( y \) alone. Forcing separation on a non-separable equation produces incorrect results. Always check whether the equation factors cleanly before proceeding.

Section 13.7

Exponential Growth Models

Exponential growth arises when the rate of change of a quantity is proportional to its current value. The differential equation and its solution are among the most important in all of applied mathematics:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = kP \quad (k > 0) \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad P(t) = P_0 e^{kt}$$

The doubling time — the time for the quantity to double — depends only on \( k \):

$$t_d = \frac{\ln 2}{k}$$
Example 13.7.1
Bacterial Population Growth
  • ProblemA colony starts at \( P_0 = 500 \) with growth constant \( k = 0.693 \) per hour. Find \( P(3) \) and the doubling time.
  • Calculate $$P(3) = 500e^{0.693 \cdot 3} = 500e^{2.079} \approx 500 \cdot 8 = 4000$$ $$t_d = \frac{\ln 2}{0.693} = \frac{0.693}{0.693} = 1 \text{ hour}$$
  • CheckAfter 1 hr: 1000. After 2 hr: 2000. After 3 hr: 4000. Three doublings from 500. ✓
  • InterpretThe population doubles every hour. After 3 hours it has doubled three times.
After 3 hours: approximately 4000 cells. Doubling time: 1 hour.
Example 13.7.2
Finding the Growth Constant from Data
  • ProblemAn investment starts at \$1000. After 5 years the balance is \$1349. Find the continuous growth rate and doubling time.
  • Represent\( 1349 = 1000e^{5k} \)
  • Solve $$e^{5k} = 1.349 \implies 5k = \ln(1.349) \approx 0.2993 \implies k \approx 0.0599 \text{ per year}$$ $$t_d = \frac{\ln 2}{0.0599} \approx 11.6 \text{ years}$$
  • InterpretThe account earns approximately 5.99% continuous annual rate. The balance doubles every 11.6 years.
\( k \approx 0.0599 \) per year; doubling time \( \approx 11.6 \) years.
Section 13.8

Exponential Decay Models

Exponential decay arises when the rate of change is negative and proportional to the current value:

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = -kQ \quad (k > 0) \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad Q(t) = Q_0 e^{-kt}$$

The half-life depends only on the decay constant:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{k}$$
Example 13.8.1
Radioactive Decay — Carbon-14 Dating
  • ProblemCarbon-14 has a half-life of 5730 years. A bone fragment contains 25% of its original carbon-14. How old is it?
  • Find \( k \) $$k = \frac{\ln 2}{5730} \approx 0.0001209 \text{ per year}$$
  • Solve for \( t \) $$0.25Q_0 = Q_0 e^{-kt} \implies \ln(0.25) = -kt \implies t = \frac{\ln 4}{k} \approx \frac{1.3863}{0.0001209} \approx 11{,}460 \text{ years}$$
  • CheckTwo half-lives: \( 2 \times 5730 = 11{,}460 \). 25% is exactly two halvings from 100%. ✓
  • InterpretThe fragment is approximately 11,460 years old.
Age ≈ 11,460 years (two half-lives)
Example 13.8.2
Drug Concentration in the Bloodstream
  • ProblemInitial concentration: 80 mg/L. After 4 hours: 60 mg/L. Find the concentration after 10 hours.
  • Find \( k \) $$60 = 80e^{-4k} \implies e^{-4k} = 0.75 \implies k = \frac{-\ln(0.75)}{4} \approx 0.0719 \text{ per hour}$$
  • Evaluate $$C(10) = 80e^{-0.0719 \cdot 10} \approx 80 \cdot 0.487 \approx 38.9 \text{ mg/L}$$
  • InterpretAfter 10 hours, approximately 38.9 mg/L remains. This model is used in pharmacokinetics to determine dosing intervals.
Concentration after 10 hours ≈ 38.9 mg/L
Section 13.9

Logistic Growth as an Introduction

Exponential growth assumes unlimited resources. The logistic growth model accounts for the real constraint that no population can grow without bound. As a population approaches the environment's capacity, growth slows and eventually stops.

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\!\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)$$

Here \( r \) is the intrinsic growth rate and \( K \) is the carrying capacity. When \( P \ll K \), the factor \( (1 - P/K) \approx 1 \) and growth is nearly exponential. As \( P \to K \), the factor approaches 0 and growth stops.

Graph Instruction — S-Shaped Logistic Curve

Draw an S-shaped (sigmoidal) curve beginning with slow growth near \( P = 0 \), steepening to maximum rate at \( P = K/2 \), then leveling off as \( P \to K \). Draw a dashed horizontal line at \( P = K \) (carrying capacity) and mark the inflection point at \( P = K/2 \) where the growth rate is maximum. Compare to an exponential curve that would continue rising without bound.

Example 13.9.1
Reading a Logistic Model
  • ProblemA fish population satisfies the logistic model with \( r = 0.4 \) and \( K = 5000 \). Current population: 1000 fish. What is the current growth rate?
  • Calculate $$\frac{dP}{dt} = 0.4 \cdot 1000 \cdot \left(1 - \frac{1000}{5000}\right) = 400 \cdot 0.8 = 320 \text{ fish per unit time}$$
  • CompareAt \( P = 4500 \): \( \frac{dP}{dt} = 0.4 \cdot 4500 \cdot (1 - 0.9) = 180 \) fish per unit time — much slower.
  • InterpretGrowth slows as the population approaches 5000. The rate at 4500 fish is barely more than half of the rate at 1000 fish.
Current growth rate: 320 fish per unit time

Section 13.10

Newton's Law of Cooling as an Introduction

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the rate of change of an object's temperature is proportional to the difference between its current temperature and the surrounding (ambient) temperature \( T_s \):

$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_s), \quad k > 0$$

The substitution \( u = T - T_s \) converts this into the exponential decay equation \( \frac{du}{dt} = -ku \), giving the general solution:

$$T(t) = T_s + (T_0 - T_s)e^{-kt}$$
Example 13.10.1
A Cooling Coffee Cup
  • ProblemCoffee starts at 90°C in a 20°C room. After 5 minutes: 74°C. Find \( k \) and the temperature after 15 minutes.
  • Model\( T(t) = 20 + 70e^{-kt} \)
  • Find \( k \) $$74 = 20 + 70e^{-5k} \implies 54 = 70e^{-5k} \implies k = \frac{\ln(70/54)}{5} \approx 0.0518 \text{ per minute}$$
  • Evaluate $$T(15) = 20 + 70e^{-0.0518 \cdot 15} \approx 20 + 70 \cdot 0.460 \approx 52.2°C$$
  • InterpretAfter 15 minutes the coffee is about 52°C. It approaches room temperature (20°C) asymptotically but never drops below it.
After 15 minutes: ≈ 52.2°C
Example 13.10.2
A Forensic Application — Time of Death
  • ProblemA body is found in a 20°C room. Current temperature: 30°C. One hour later: 28°C. Normal body temperature: 37°C. Estimate time of death.
  • Model\( T(t) = 20 + 17e^{-kt} \), where \( t = 0 \) is time of death.
  • Find \( k \) Let \( t_1 \) = time of discovery. From \( T(t_1) = 30 \): \( e^{-kt_1} = \frac{10}{17} \). From \( T(t_1+1) = 28 \): $$8 = 10e^{-k} \implies e^{-k} = 0.8 \implies k = -\ln(0.8) \approx 0.2231 \text{ per hour}$$
  • Find \( t_1 \) $$e^{-0.2231 t_1} = \frac{10}{17} \implies t_1 = \frac{\ln(17/10)}{0.2231} \approx \frac{0.5306}{0.2231} \approx 2.38 \text{ hours}$$
  • InterpretApproximately 2.38 hours elapsed between death and discovery. Note: Newton's Law is a simplified model; actual forensic estimates carry substantial uncertainty.
Time of death ≈ 2.38 hours before discovery
Section 13.11

Motion Models

Differential equations provide the natural language for motion. The relationships between position, velocity, and acceleration are rate relationships:

$$v(t) = s'(t) = \frac{ds}{dt}, \qquad a(t) = v'(t) = \frac{dv}{dt}$$

In free fall with air resistance proportional to velocity, Newton's second law gives:

$$m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - bv$$

Terminal velocity occurs when \( \frac{dv}{dt} = 0 \), meaning drag equals gravity: \( v_{\text{term}} = \frac{mg}{b} \).

Example 13.11.1
Terminal Velocity and Velocity Model
  • ProblemA car starts from rest. Its velocity satisfies \( \frac{dv}{dt} = 4 - 0.2v \), \( v(0) = 0 \). Find the terminal velocity and \( v(t) \).
  • Terminal velocity $$0 = 4 - 0.2v_{\text{term}} \implies v_{\text{term}} = 20 \text{ m/s}$$
  • Solve Separate: \( \dfrac{dv}{4 - 0.2v} = dt \). Integrate (substitution \( u = 4 - 0.2v \)): $$-5\ln|4 - 0.2v| = t + C_1 \implies v(t) = 20\!\left(1 - e^{-t/5}\right)$$
  • CheckAt \( t = 0 \): \( v = 0 \). ✓ As \( t \to \infty \): \( v \to 20 \). ✓ Differentiate: \( \frac{dv}{dt} = 4e^{-t/5} = 4 - 0.2v \). ✓
  • InterpretThe car accelerates from rest and asymptotically approaches 20 m/s. Acceleration decreases as velocity increases.
\( v(t) = 20\!\left(1 - e^{-t/5}\right) \); terminal velocity = 20 m/s
Section 13.12

Differential Equations in Science and Finance

Continuous Compounding

If money \( A(t) \) grows at continuous rate \( r \), then \( \frac{dA}{dt} = rA \), giving \( A(t) = A_0 e^{rt} \). This is the mathematical foundation of continuous compounding.

Example 13.12.1
Continuous Compound Interest
  • ProblemAn account begins with \$5000 at 6% continuous annual interest. Balance after 10 years?
  • Calculate $$A(10) = 5000e^{0.06 \cdot 10} = 5000e^{0.6} \approx 5000 \cdot 1.8221 \approx \$9{,}111$$
  • InterpretThe balance nearly doubles in 10 years due to continuous compounding.
Balance after 10 years ≈ \$9,111

Mixing Tank Problems

Example 13.12.2
Setting Up a Mixing Problem
  • SituationA 100-gallon tank holds 50 lbs of dissolved salt. Fresh water enters at 3 gal/min; the well-mixed solution exits at 3 gal/min. Let \( A(t) \) = pounds of salt at time \( t \).
  • ThinkRate of change = rate in − rate out. Fresh water brings no salt in. Salt flows out at the tank's current concentration times the flow rate.
  • Represent $$\frac{dA}{dt} = 0 - \frac{A}{100} \cdot 3 = -\frac{3A}{100}$$
  • Solve This is exponential decay: \( A(t) = 50e^{-0.03t} \).
  • InterpretThe salt washes out continuously. As \( t \to \infty \), \( A \to 0 \): the tank eventually holds pure water.
\( A(t) = 50e^{-0.03t} \) pounds of salt

Section 13.13

Common Differential Equation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting the Constant of Integration
Solve \( \frac{dy}{dt} = 2t \): answer is \( y = t^2 \) Correct: y = t² + C. Without the constant, only the single solution through the origin is captured. Always include +C and determine it from an initial condition if one is given.
Mistake 2 — Forcing Separation on a Non-Separable Equation
Separate \( \frac{dy}{dt} = y + t \) as \( \frac{dy}{y} = (1 + t/y)\,dt \) Correct: \( y + t \) does not factor into g(t)·h(y). This equation is not separable. First-order linear methods (integrating factor) are needed — covered in the Differential Equations course.
Mistake 3 — Applying Growth Formula with Wrong Sign
Exponential decay: \( Q(t) = Q_0 e^{kt} \) with \( k > 0 \) Correct: For decay, the exponent must be negative: Q(t) = Q₀e^(−kt) with k > 0. The positive sign gives growth; the negative sign gives decay.
Mistake 4 — Forgetting to Apply the Initial Condition
Report general solution \( y = Ce^{3t} \) when \( y(0) = 4 \) was given Correct: Apply y(0) = 4 to get C = 4, then report the particular solution y = 4e^(3t). An unapplied initial condition leaves the answer incomplete.
Mistake 5 — Confusing Decay Constant and Half-Life
"Half-life is 10 years, so k = 10 per year" Correct: k = ln 2 / t₁/₂ = ln 2 / 10 ≈ 0.0693 per year. The decay constant and the half-life have an inverse relationship — they are not equal.
Mistake 6 — Misreading Newton's Law of Cooling
\( \frac{dT}{dt} = -kT \) for cooling toward \( T_s = 25°C \) Correct: \( \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_s) \). The first equation drives T toward 0; the correct equation drives T toward the ambient temperature T_s. The difference matters enormously in applications.
Mistake 7 — Applying Exponential Growth Near Carrying Capacity
Using \( P(t) = P_0 e^{rt} \) when \( P \approx K \) Correct: Exponential growth approximates logistic growth only when P ≪ K. Near the carrying capacity, the logistic brake is strong and exponential growth dramatically overestimates the population.
Mistake 8 — Omitting Units on the Rate Constant
"The growth constant is k ≈ 0.0693" (no units given) Correct: Always state the units — k ≈ 0.0693 per hour, or 0.0693 year⁻¹, etc. A rate constant without units is meaningless and will produce wrong answers if units are mixed.
Mistake 9 — Skipping the Verification Step
Finding y and not checking whether it satisfies the original equation Correct: After finding a solution, differentiate it and substitute back into the equation to confirm both sides match identically. Algebraic slips during separation can produce functions that do not satisfy the equation.
Mistake 10 — Treating General and Particular Solutions as Interchangeable
"The solution is Ce^(3t)" when a specific initial condition was provided Correct: When an initial condition is given, the problem asks for a particular solution, not the general family. The general solution is mathematically complete but contextually incomplete until C is determined.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1A differential equation involves an unknown function and its derivatives. The order equals the highest derivative present. Reading the equation in plain language is the first step before any computation.
  • Sec 13.2Solutions are functions, not numbers. The general solution contains one arbitrary constant \( C \) and represents a family of curves. A particular solution is selected by an initial condition.
  • Sec 13.3Verification: substitute the proposed function and its derivative into the equation and confirm both sides are equal for all values of the variable, not just one point.
  • Sec 13.4A slope field draws the slope specified by \( \frac{dy}{dt} = f(t,y) \) at each grid point. It reveals qualitative behavior — equilibria, stability, long-term trends — without requiring an explicit formula.
  • Sec 13.5An initial condition \( y(t_0) = y_0 \) selects a unique particular solution from the general family. A differential equation together with an initial condition is called an initial value problem (IVP).
  • Sec 13.6A separable equation \( \frac{dy}{dt} = g(t) \cdot h(y) \) is solved by separating variables, integrating both sides, and solving for \( y \). Not all first-order equations are separable.
  • Sec 13.7Exponential growth: \( \frac{dP}{dt} = kP \) gives \( P(t) = P_0 e^{kt} \). Doubling time: \( t_d = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \). Applies to populations, investments, and any quantity that grows proportionally to its size.
  • Sec 13.8Exponential decay: \( \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kQ \) gives \( Q(t) = Q_0 e^{-kt} \). Half-life: \( t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \). Applies to radioactive decay, drug elimination, and cooling.
  • Sec 13.9The logistic equation \( \frac{dP}{dt} = rP(1 - P/K) \) models bounded growth toward a carrying capacity \( K \). Solutions follow an S-shaped curve. Equilibria at \( P = 0 \) (unstable) and \( P = K \) (stable).
  • Sec 13.10Newton's Law of Cooling: \( \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_s) \). The substitution \( u = T - T_s \) reduces this to exponential decay, giving \( T(t) = T_s + (T_0 - T_s)e^{-kt} \).
  • Sec 13.11Position, velocity, and acceleration satisfy differential equations. With air resistance, falling objects approach terminal velocity \( v_{\text{term}} = mg/b \) rather than accelerating indefinitely.
  • Sec 13.12Differential equations model continuous compounding, mixing tanks, epidemic spread, and chemical reactions. The rate-in minus rate-out structure recurs across many applications.
  • Sec 13.13Ten common mistakes: omitting \( +C \), forcing separation, sign errors on \( k \), ignoring initial conditions, confusing \( k \) with half-life, miswriting Newton's Law, using exponential near carrying capacity, omitting units, skipping verification, and conflating general and particular solutions.
  • Sec 13.14This chapter covers first-order separable equations. Ahead: integrating factors for linear equations, second-order equations with oscillatory solutions, systems of ODEs, numerical methods, and partial differential equations.

Key Terms Review

differential equation
An equation involving an unknown function and one or more of its derivatives.
ordinary differential equation
A differential equation involving a function of one variable and its derivatives with respect to that variable.
order
The order of a differential equation equals the order of the highest derivative that appears in it.
general solution
The family of all functions satisfying a differential equation, containing one or more arbitrary constants.
particular solution
A specific member of the general solution family, determined by applying an initial condition.
initial condition
A specified value of the solution function at a particular input: \( y(t_0) = y_0 \).
initial value problem
A differential equation together with one or more initial conditions, which determine a unique particular solution.
slope field
A visual representation showing the slope specified by a differential equation at each point in the plane. Also called a direction field.
equilibrium solution
A constant function \( y = c \) satisfying the differential equation, occurring where the rate of change is zero.
separable equation
A first-order ODE that can be written as \( \frac{dy}{dt} = g(t) \cdot h(y) \), enabling separation of variables.
separation of variables
A solution method: place all \( y \) terms on one side, all \( t \) terms on the other, then integrate both sides.
exponential growth
Growth governed by \( \frac{dP}{dt} = kP \) with \( k > 0 \), producing \( P(t) = P_0 e^{kt} \).
exponential decay
Decay governed by \( \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kQ \) with \( k > 0 \), producing \( Q(t) = Q_0 e^{-kt} \).
doubling time
The time for an exponentially growing quantity to double: \( t_d = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \).
half-life
The time for an exponentially decaying quantity to halve: \( t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \).
logistic growth
Growth governed by \( \frac{dP}{dt} = rP(1 - P/K) \), producing S-shaped curves that level off at the carrying capacity.
carrying capacity
The maximum sustainable population \( K \) in the logistic model. A stable equilibrium solution.
Newton's Law of Cooling
The rate of temperature change is proportional to the difference between the object's temperature and the ambient temperature: \( \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_s) \).
terminal velocity
The limiting speed reached when air resistance exactly balances gravity, giving zero net acceleration: \( v_{\text{term}} = mg/b \).
growth constant
The proportionality constant \( k > 0 \) in an exponential growth model, with units of (time)⁻¹.
decay constant
The proportionality constant \( k > 0 \) in an exponential decay model, related to half-life by \( k = \ln 2 / t_{1/2} \).
verification
Confirming a proposed solution by substituting it and its derivative into the original equation and checking both sides are equal for all values.

Growth and Decay Applications

  1. Population Growth. A city of 250,000 grows at continuous rate 1.8% per year. (a) Write the differential equation and solution. (b) Population after 10 years. (c) When does it reach 350,000?
  2. Bacterial Culture. A culture starts with 400 cells and doubles every 3 hours. (a) Find \( k \). (b) Cells after 9 hours. (c) When does population reach 10,000?
  3. Radioactive Decay. Iodine-131 has a half-life of 8 days. A patient receives 150 mg. (a) Find \( k \). (b) Amount remaining after 16 days. (c) When does the dose fall below 10 mg?
  4. Investment. Account starts at \$8000, earning 4.5% continuous annual interest. (a) Balance after 20 years. (b) Doubling time.
  5. Carbon-14 Dating. An artifact retains 60% of its original carbon-14. Half-life: 5730 years. Estimate the artifact's age.
  6. Newton's Law of Cooling. A metal rod at 200°C is placed in a 22°C room. After 10 minutes: 140°C. (a) Find \( k \). (b) Temperature after 30 minutes. (c) When does it reach 50°C?
  7. Drug Decay. \( \frac{dC}{dt} = -0.08C \), \( C(0) = 120 \) mg/L. (a) Half-life. (b) Concentration after 6 hours. (c) When does it drop below 20 mg/L?
  8. Logistic Population. A deer herd satisfies \( \frac{dP}{dt} = 0.6P(1 - P/800) \). Current population: 200. (a) Current growth rate. (b) Growth rate at population 400. (c) At what population is growth rate maximum? (d) Long-run behavior.

Modeling Problems

  1. Mixing Tank. A 500-gallon tank holds pure water. Brine (2 lbs salt/gallon) enters at 4 gal/min; well-mixed solution exits at 4 gal/min. (a) Why does tank volume stay constant? (b) Write \( \frac{dA}{dt} \). (c) Find the equilibrium salt amount and interpret it. (d) Write the particular solution with \( A(0) = 0 \).
  2. Spread of Information. A post begins spreading in a community of 20,000. The rate of new viewers is proportional to the number who haven't seen it. (a) Write the differential equation for \( N(t) \). (b) Identify the carrying capacity. (c) How does this differ from simple exponential growth?
  3. Terminal Velocity. A skydiver of mass 75 kg falls with drag coefficient \( b = 15 \) kg/s and \( g = 9.8 \) m/s². (a) Write the velocity differential equation. (b) Find terminal velocity. (c) Interpret physically.
  4. Continuous Reinvestment. A company's revenue satisfies \( \frac{dR}{dt} = 0.12R \), \( R(0) = 3.5 \) million dollars. (a) Find \( R(t) \). (b) Revenue after 5 years. (c) Doubling time. (d) What real-world factors might eventually prevent continued exponential growth?
  5. Forensics. A body is found in a 18°C room. At 9:00 PM: body temperature 31°C. At 10:00 PM: 29°C. Normal body temperature: 37°C. Estimate the time of death.

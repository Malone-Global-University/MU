Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Triangles are the most fundamental figures in all of geometry. Every polygon can be divided into triangles. Every proof about quadrilaterals, circles, and three-dimensional solids eventually relies on reasoning about triangles. Engineers use triangles because they are the most rigid shape. Architects use triangles to distribute force. Surveyors use triangles to measure land that cannot be walked across directly. Navigation systems, computer graphics, and structural design all depend on triangular relationships.

This chapter introduces triangles as geometric objects with precise definitions, classification systems, angle relationships, and congruence properties. Students will learn to classify triangles by side lengths and angle measures, to reason about the angles inside and outside a triangle, and to determine when two triangles are congruent through a small number of carefully chosen measurements.

The central concept of this chapter is congruence. Two triangles are congruent when they have exactly the same shape and exactly the same size — their corresponding sides are equal in length and their corresponding angles are equal in measure. Congruence is not a visual impression. It is a precise geometric relationship that must be established through reasoning, and this chapter teaches students to establish it through a set of congruence shortcuts: SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, and HL.

Opening Question

A construction engineer is building a bridge truss. She cuts two triangular steel frames and wants to be certain they are identical before welding them into place. She cannot put one on top of the other because they are large and heavy. What information about the triangles could she check to guarantee they are exactly the same shape and size — without placing one directly on top of the other? This question introduces one of the key ideas of the chapter: a small number of strategically chosen measurements can be enough to confirm that two triangles are congruent.

Defining and Classifying Triangles

Section 4.1

What Makes a Triangle

A triangle is a closed figure formed by connecting three non-collinear points with three line segments. The word "non-collinear" is essential: three points lying on the same line cannot form a triangle because the figure would collapse into a single segment with no interior.

The three points are called vertices (singular: vertex), and the three connecting segments are called sides. A triangle is named using the symbol △ followed by three capital letters, one for each vertex.

Triangle Notation

△ABC names a triangle with vertices A, B, and C.

Its three sides are AB, BC, and AC.

Its three angles are ∠A, ∠B, and ∠C (also written ∠BAC, ∠ABC, ∠BCA).

Side BC lies opposite ∠A; side AC lies opposite ∠B; side AB lies opposite ∠C.

Diagram Instruction

Draw three non-collinear points and label them A, B, and C. Connect them with three segments to form a triangle. Label each side with its two-letter name. Mark each interior angle at its vertex with a small arc. Draw a small arrow from each side to its opposite angle to show the opposite relationship.

Included Angles and Included Sides

The included angle between two sides is the angle at the vertex where those two sides meet. In △ABC, the included angle between AB and AC is ∠A. The included side between two angles is the segment connecting the vertices of those two angles. The included side between ∠A and ∠B is AB.

Why This Matters Now

The terms "included angle" and "included side" appear in SAS and ASA congruence shortcuts later in this chapter. Understanding them precisely now prevents the most common classification error in triangle congruence work.

Section 4.2

Classifying Triangles by Sides

ClassificationDefinitionDiagram Marking
ScaleneAll three sides have different lengths. No equal sides.No tick marks; label three distinct lengths such as 5, 7, 9.
IsoscelesAt least two sides have equal lengths. The equal sides are legs; the third is the base.Single tick marks on the two equal sides.

Isosceles Vocabulary

In an isosceles triangle, the two equal sides are called the legs. The third side is the base. The two angles at the ends of the base are the base angles. The angle between the two legs (at the top) is the vertex angle.

Example 4.2.1
Classifying by side lengths
  • Problem△PQR has side lengths PQ = 6, QR = 6, and PR = 9. Classify △PQR by its sides.
  • ThinkCompare the three side lengths: two are equal, one differs.
  • SolvePQ = QR = 6 and PR = 9. Exactly two sides are equal.
  • CheckBase: PR = 9. Legs: PQ and QR, each of length 6. The vertex angle is ∠Q.
Answer: △PQR is an isosceles triangle.

Section 4.3

Classifying Triangles by Angles

ClassificationDefinitionKey Fact
AcuteAll three angles measure less than 90°.No angle reaches or passes 90°.
RightExactly one angle measures exactly 90°. Marked with a small square.The side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse — the longest side.
ObtuseExactly one angle measures more than 90°.Only one obtuse angle is possible per triangle.
EquiangularAll three angles are equal: 60° each.Every equiangular triangle is also equilateral.
A Triangle Cannot Have Two Right or Two Obtuse Angles

Two right angles alone total 180°, leaving 0° for the third angle — impossible. Two obtuse angles would exceed 180° before the third angle is even counted. The Triangle Angle Sum Theorem (Section 4.4) proves why only one of each is possible.

Example 4.3.1
Classifying by angles and combining classifications
  • Problem(a) Angles 55°, 70°, 55°   (b) Angles 90°, 45°, 45°   (c) Angles 110°, 40°, 30°
  • ThinkCheck each angle against 90°. Then consider the sides to add a side classification.
  • Solve (a) All angles < 90° → acute. Two equal angles (55°) → isosceles. (b) One angle = 90° → right. Two equal angles (45°) → isosceles (right isosceles). (c) One angle > 90° (110°) → obtuse.
Answer: (a) Acute isosceles, (b) Right isosceles, (c) Obtuse triangle.

Section 4.4

Triangle Angle Sum

Triangle Angle Sum Theorem

The sum of the measures of the interior angles of any triangle is 180°.

m∠A + m∠B + m∠C = 180°

Proof — Triangle Angle Sum Theorem
Given: △ABC    Prove: m∠A + m∠B + m∠C = 180°
Plan: Draw a line through vertex B parallel to AC. Use alternate interior angles to substitute angle measures, then use the straight angle at B.
Diagram Instruction

Draw △ABC with A at the lower left, B at the top, and C at the lower right. Through B, draw a dashed horizontal line parallel to AC. Label the angle left of B (between the dashed line and side BA) as ∠1, the interior angle at B as ∠2, and the angle right of B (between BC and the dashed line) as ∠3.

StatementReason
Draw line ℓ through B, parallel to ACParallel Postulate: through a point not on a line, exactly one parallel exists
∠1 ≅ ∠AAlternate Interior Angles; AB is a transversal cutting ℓ ∥ AC
∠3 ≅ ∠CAlternate Interior Angles; BC is a transversal cutting ℓ ∥ AC
m∠1 + m∠2 + m∠3 = 180°Angles 1, 2, 3 form a straight angle at B
m∠A + m∠B + m∠C = 180°Substitution: replacing ∠1 with ∠A and ∠3 with ∠C

The three interior angles of the triangle are the same three angles that appear at vertex B once the parallel line is drawn. Alternate interior angles allow the substitution.

Example 4.4.1
Finding a missing angle
  • ProblemIn △ABC, m∠A = 48° and m∠B = 73°. Find m∠C.
  • Representm∠A + m∠B + m∠C = 180°
  • Solve 48° + 73° + m∠C = 180° 121° + m∠C = 180° m∠C = 59°
  • Check48° + 73° + 59° = 180° ✓
Answer: m∠C = 59°
Example 4.4.2
Using algebra with the angle sum
  • ProblemIn △DEF, m∠D = (2x + 10)°, m∠E = (3x)°, m∠F = (x + 14)°. Find all three angles.
  • Represent(2x + 10) + 3x + (x + 14) = 180
  • Solve 6x + 24 = 180 6x = 156 x = 26 m∠D = 2(26) + 10 = 62° m∠E = 3(26) = 78° m∠F = 26 + 14 = 40°
  • Check62° + 78° + 40° = 180° ✓
Answer: m∠D = 62°, m∠E = 78°, m∠F = 40°

Section 4.5

Exterior Angle Theorem

When one side of a triangle is extended beyond a vertex, the angle formed outside the triangle is called an exterior angle. The two interior angles that are not adjacent to the exterior angle are called the remote interior angles.

Diagram Instruction

Draw △ABC with A at the left, B at the top, and C at the right. Extend side BC beyond C to a point D. The exterior angle ∠ACD opens to the right. Shade or arc ∠ACD. Label ∠A and ∠B inside the triangle as the two remote interior angles. Mark the adjacent interior angle ∠ACB separately.

Exterior Angle Theorem

The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the measures of the two remote interior angles.

m∠exterior = m∠remote₁ + m∠remote₂

This follows from the Angle Sum Theorem: the interior angle and exterior angle at C are supplementary (sum to 180°), and the three interior angles also sum to 180°. Subtracting the shared interior angle at C from both equations yields the theorem.

Example 4.5.1
Finding an exterior angle using algebra
  • ProblemExterior angle at C measures (5x + 10)°. Remote interior angles are 3x° and 40°.
  • Represent(5x + 10) = 3x + 40
  • Solve 2x = 30 x = 15 Exterior angle = 5(15) + 10 = 85°
  • CheckRemote angles: 3(15) = 45° and 40°. Sum: 45° + 40° = 85° ✓
Answer: x = 15; exterior angle = 85°
Example 4.5.2
Finding a remote interior angle
  • ProblemAn exterior angle measures 130°. One remote interior angle measures 74°. Find the other.
  • Represent130° = 74° + m∠B
  • Solvem∠B = 130° − 74° = 56°
  • Check74° + 56° = 130° ✓
Answer: The other remote interior angle is 56°.

Congruent Figures and Correspondence

Section 4.6

Congruent Figures

Two figures are congruent (symbol: ≅) when they have exactly the same shape and exactly the same size. If one could be lifted and placed on top of the other — through rotations, reflections, and translations — it would match perfectly at every point.

For two triangles to be congruent, all six corresponding parts must match: three pairs of equal sides and three pairs of equal angles. Geometry does not rely on visual estimates. Congruence is established through measurement or proof.

Congruence Requires All Six Corresponding Parts

If △ABC ≅ △DEF, then all of the following hold:

AB = DE, BC = EF, AC = DF (three pairs of sides)

∠A ≅ ∠D, ∠B ≅ ∠E, ∠C ≅ ∠F (three pairs of angles)

Section 4.7

Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles

A congruence statement must be written so that corresponding vertices appear in matching positions. The order of letters is not optional — it encodes which parts match which.

Vertex Order Is Not Optional

△ABC ≅ △DEF and △ABC ≅ △EDF are different statements with different correspondences. Always identify explicitly which vertex of one triangle matches which vertex of the other before writing the statement.

CPCTC — Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles are Congruent

Once two triangles are proven congruent, all corresponding parts are automatically congruent. CPCTC is not a method for proving congruence — it is a conclusion drawn after congruence is established by a shortcut (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, or HL).

Example 4.7.1
Reading a congruence statement
  • ProblemGiven △GHK ≅ △MNP, identify all six pairs of congruent corresponding parts.
  • ThinkMatch vertices in order: G ↔ M, H ↔ N, K ↔ P.
  • Solve Sides: GH ≅ MN, HK ≅ NP, GK ≅ MP Angles: ∠G ≅ ∠M, ∠H ≅ ∠N, ∠K ≅ ∠P
Answer: Six pairs listed above — the vertex correspondence G↔M, H↔N, K↔P determines everything.
Example 4.7.2
Writing a congruence statement from given information
  • Problem△XYZ and △RST satisfy: ∠X ≅ ∠R, ∠Y ≅ ∠S, ∠Z ≅ ∠T, XY ≅ RS, YZ ≅ ST, XZ ≅ RT. Write the congruence statement.
  • ThinkMatch vertices: X ↔ R, Y ↔ S, Z ↔ T.
Answer: △XYZ ≅ △RST

Sections 4.8 – 4.12
The Five Congruence Shortcuts

Section 4.8

SSS Congruence

SSS — Side-Side-Side Postulate

If three sides of one triangle are congruent to three sides of another triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

No angle information is needed. Three fixed side lengths determine a triangle's shape completely.

Diagram Instruction

Draw △ABC and △DEF side by side. Mark AB and DE with one tick each. Mark BC and EF with two ticks each. Mark AC and DF with three ticks each. No angle marks are needed for SSS.

Example 4.8.1
SSS with numerical side lengths
  • Problem△PQR: PQ = 8, QR = 11, PR = 7. △XYZ: XY = 8, YZ = 11, XZ = 7. Are they congruent?
  • ThinkMatch corresponding sides: PQ↔XY, QR↔YZ, PR↔XZ.
  • SolvePQ = XY = 8, QR = YZ = 11, PR = XZ = 7. All three pairs match.
Answer: △PQR ≅ △XYZ by SSS.
Section 4.9

SAS Congruence

SAS — Side-Angle-Side Postulate

If two sides and the included angle of one triangle are congruent to two sides and the included angle of another, the triangles are congruent.

The angle must be the one formed between the two congruent sides — the included angle. SAS with a non-included angle is not valid (see SSA warning in Section 4.14).

Diagram Instruction

Draw △ABC and △DEF. In △ABC, mark AB with one tick, ∠A with an arc, and AC with two ticks — the arc-marked angle sits between the two tick-marked sides. In △DEF, mark DE with one tick, ∠D with an arc, and DF with two ticks in the same pattern.

The Reflexive Property in SAS Proofs

When two triangles share a side or an angle, that element is congruent to itself by the Reflexive Property of Congruence. This is one of the most frequently used justifications in triangle congruence proofs. Never overlook the shared element in a diagram.

Example 4.9.1
Identifying SAS — checking the included angle
  • ProblemJK ≅ MN, ∠K ≅ ∠N, KL ≅ NP. Is this SAS?
  • Think∠K is at vertex K, which is shared by JK and KL. So ∠K is the included angle between those two sides.
  • Solve∠N is the included angle between MN and NP in the second triangle. Both angles are included. ✓
Answer: △JKL ≅ △MNP by SAS.
Example 4.9.2
SAS with the Reflexive Property and vertical angles
  • ProblemPoint B lies on both diagonals of a figure. AB ≅ CB and DB ≅ EB. Show △ABD ≅ △CBE.
  • Diagram∠ABD and ∠CBE are vertical angles at B. Vertical angles are always congruent.
  • Solve AB ≅ CB (given), DB ≅ EB (given) ∠ABD ≅ ∠CBE (Vertical Angles Theorem) ∠ABD is included between AB and DB in △ABD. ∠CBE is included between CB and EB in △CBE.
Answer: △ABD ≅ △CBE by SAS.
Section 4.10

ASA Congruence

ASA — Angle-Side-Angle Postulate

If two angles and the included side of one triangle are congruent to two angles and the included side of another, the triangles are congruent.

The side must connect the two congruent angles — the included side. If ∠A and ∠B are the known angles, the included side is AB.

Diagram Instruction

Draw △ABC and △DEF. In △ABC, mark ∠A with one arc, AB with a tick, and ∠B with two arcs. The tick-marked side lies between the two arc-marked angles. Match this pattern in △DEF: ∠D one arc, DE tick, ∠E two arcs.

Example 4.10.1
ASA with a shared side
  • ProblemIn △WXY and △WXZ, ∠WXY ≅ ∠WXZ and ∠XWY ≅ ∠XWZ. WX is shared.
  • ThinkIn △WXY, the angles at W and X are known; the side between vertices W and X is WX. Same in △WXZ.
  • SolveWX ≅ WX by the Reflexive Property. Two angles and their included side match in both triangles.
Answer: △WXY ≅ △WXZ by ASA.
Section 4.11

AAS Congruence

AAS — Angle-Angle-Side Theorem

If two angles and a non-included side of one triangle are congruent to two angles and the corresponding non-included side of another, the triangles are congruent.

AAS can be derived from ASA: once two angles are known, the third angle is determined by the Angle Sum Theorem, making AAS equivalent in information to ASA.

ShortcutThe Known Side Is…Example
ASABetween the two known angles (included)∠A, AB, ∠B — side AB lies between ∠A and ∠B
AASAdjacent to only one of the known angles (non-included)∠A, ∠B, BC — side BC is adjacent to ∠B but not between ∠A and ∠B
Example 4.11.1
Distinguishing AAS from ASA
  • Problem△MNO and △PQR: ∠M ≅ ∠P, ∠N ≅ ∠Q, NO ≅ QR. Is this ASA or AAS?
  • ThinkIs NO between ∠M and ∠N? NO connects vertices N and O. It is adjacent to ∠N but not between ∠M and ∠N (that would be MN).
  • SolveThe side is non-included → AAS.
Answer: △MNO ≅ △PQR by AAS.
Section 4.12

HL Congruence for Right Triangles

HL — Hypotenuse-Leg Theorem (Right Triangles Only)

If the hypotenuse and one leg of a right triangle are congruent to the hypotenuse and one leg of another right triangle, the triangles are congruent.

Three requirements: (1) Both triangles must have a right angle. (2) The hypotenuses must be congruent. (3) One pair of legs must be congruent.

HL works because the Pythagorean Theorem determines the third side once the hypotenuse and one leg are fixed — eliminating the ambiguity that makes SSA invalid for non-right triangles.

Diagram Instruction

Draw two right triangles side by side. Mark the right angle in each with a small square at the right-angle vertex. Mark both hypotenuses with double tick marks. Mark one corresponding leg in each triangle with single tick marks.

Example 4.12.1
Applying HL
  • Problem△ABC and △DEF are right triangles with right angles at C and F. AB ≅ DE (hypotenuses) and AC ≅ DF (one pair of legs).
  • Check requirements Right angles at C and F: confirmed ✓ Hypotenuses: AB and DE (sides opposite the right angles) ≅ ✓ One pair of legs: AC ≅ DF ✓
Answer: △ABC ≅ △DEF by HL.

Congruence Shortcut Summary

SSS
Side-Side-Side
All three pairs of sides congruent. No angle information needed.
SAS
Side-Angle-Side
Two sides and their included angle. Angle must be between the two sides.
ASA
Angle-Side-Angle
Two angles and their included side. Side must be between the two angles.
AAS
Angle-Angle-Side
Two angles and a non-included side. Side is adjacent to one angle only.
HL
Hypotenuse-Leg
Right triangles only. Hypotenuses and one pair of legs congruent.
Invalid Shortcuts — These Do NOT Guarantee Congruence

SSA (Side-Side-Angle): Two sides and a non-included angle allow two different triangles in general. The only exception is the right-triangle case, which is covered by HL.

AAA (Angle-Angle-Angle): Matching all three angles guarantees triangles have the same shape (similarity) but not the same size. A small and a large equilateral triangle are similar but not congruent.

Section 4.13

Triangle Congruence Proofs

Every congruence proof follows the same planning process: read the given information, draw and mark the diagram, identify which additional congruent parts come from the structure of the diagram (vertical angles, shared sides, parallel lines), determine which shortcut applies, and then write the proof step by step. Each statement must be supported by a definition, postulate, theorem, or previously proven result.

Proof Planning Process

1. Read all given information carefully.

2. Draw or interpret the diagram.

3. Mark congruent parts with tick marks and arcs.

4. Identify extra congruent parts from the diagram: vertical angles, shared sides, parallel lines, right angles, midpoints, bisectors.

5. Choose the shortcut (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, HL).

6. Write each statement with its justification.

Proof 4.13.1 — SAS with Vertical Angles
Proving △ABD ≅ △CBE using SAS
Given: AB ≅ CB; DB ≅ EB (point B lies on both diagonals)
Prove: △ABD ≅ △CBE
Diagram Instruction

Draw two triangles sharing vertex B. Label △ABD with A upper-left and D lower-left, and △CBE with C upper-right and E lower-right. Mark AB ≅ CB with single ticks and DB ≅ EB with double ticks. The angles ABD and CBE at vertex B are vertical angles.

Plan: Two side pairs are given. The angles at B are vertical angles — congruent by the Vertical Angles Theorem. ∠ABD is the included angle between AB and DB in △ABD; ∠CBE is the included angle between CB and EB in △CBE. This gives SAS.

Proving △MRN ≅ △QRP using ASA

Proof 4.13.4 — Paragraph Proof
Proving △JKL ≅ △JML using SAS
Given: JL bisects ∠KJM; KJ ≅ MJ
Prove: △JKL ≅ △JML

Since JL bisects ∠KJM, by the definition of an angle bisector, ∠KJL ≅ ∠MJL. We are given that KJ ≅ MJ. By the Reflexive Property of Congruence, JL ≅ JL, because JL is a side shared by both triangles. We now have two pairs of congruent sides with their included angle: KJ and JL with ∠KJL in △JKL, and MJ and JL with ∠MJL in △JML. Therefore, by SAS, △JKL ≅ △JML.

Section 4.14

Common Triangle Congruence Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Writing the Congruence Statement in the Wrong Order
△ABC ≅ △EDF when the correspondence is A↔D, B↔E, C↔F

Correct: △ABC ≅ △DEF. Every pair of corresponding vertices must appear in the correct matching position. Establish the correspondence explicitly before writing the statement.

Mistake 2 — Confusing SSA with SAS
Claiming SAS when the angle is not between the two known sides

Correct: SAS requires the included angle — the one formed between the two congruent sides. If the angle is across from one of the sides rather than between them, you have SSA, which is not a valid shortcut in general. Draw the diagram and trace which vertex forms the angle.

Mistake 3 — Using AAA to Claim Congruence
△ABC ≅ △DEF because all three angle measures are equal

Correct: AAA establishes similarity (same shape), not congruence (same size). A triangle with angles 60°-60°-60° and sides of length 3 is not congruent to one with the same angles but sides of length 6. Congruence requires at least one known side.

Mistake 4 — Using CPCTC Before Proving Congruence
Stating "∠A ≅ ∠D by CPCTC" as step 2 before congruence is established

Correct: CPCTC may only be used after the triangles are proven congruent by SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, or HL. The congruence shortcut must come first.

Mistake 5 — Assuming Information Not Given or Marked
Using equal sides, right angles, or parallel lines that are not stated in the given or clearly marked in the diagram

Correct: Only use angle marks, tick marks, right-angle squares, and parallel arrows that are explicitly provided. A segment that looks equal in a sketch is not necessarily equal unless it is marked or given.

Mistake 6 — Forgetting the Reflexive Property
Concluding "not enough information" when two triangles share a side or angle

Correct: When two triangles share a side or angle, that element is congruent to itself by the Reflexive Property of Congruence. A shared side gives you one free congruent pair. Always check whether any segment or angle appears in both triangles.

Mistake 7 — Applying HL to Non-Right Triangles
Using HL when the diagram shows no confirmed right angle

Correct: HL applies only when both triangles contain right angles. The right angle must be established from the given information or a marked square in the diagram. Always confirm both triangles are right triangles before applying HL.

Chapter 4 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 4.1A triangle (△ABC) is a closed figure with three non-collinear vertices, three sides, and three interior angles. Each side lies opposite one angle. The included angle between two sides is at their shared vertex; the included side between two angles connects their vertices.
  • Sec 4.2Triangles are classified by sides as scalene (all different), isosceles (at least two equal — with legs, base, base angles, and vertex angle), or equilateral (all equal). Tick marks in diagrams indicate equal lengths.
  • Sec 4.3Triangles are classified by angles as acute (all < 90°), right (one = 90°), obtuse (one > 90°), or equiangular (all = 60°). A triangle cannot have two right or two obtuse angles.
  • Sec 4.4Triangle Angle Sum Theorem: m∠A + m∠B + m∠C = 180°. Proven using a parallel line through one vertex and alternate interior angles. Used algebraically to find missing angle measures.
  • Sec 4.5Exterior Angle Theorem: an exterior angle equals the sum of the two remote interior angles. Follows from the Angle Sum Theorem and the supplementary relationship at the exterior angle's vertex.
  • Sec 4.6Congruent figures (≅) have exactly the same shape and size. Two congruent triangles share six pairs of equal corresponding parts: three sides and three angles. Congruence must be established through reasoning, not visual estimation.
  • Sec 4.7Congruence statements list corresponding vertices in matching order. △ABC ≅ △DEF means A↔D, B↔E, C↔F. CPCTC (Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles are Congruent) is used after congruence is proven, not to establish it.
  • Sec 4.8SSS: three pairs of congruent sides guarantee congruence. Three fixed side lengths determine a unique triangle shape.
  • Sec 4.9SAS: two sides and their included angle. The angle must be between the two sides. Vertical angles and the Reflexive Property are common sources of the included angle in proofs.
  • Sec 4.10ASA: two angles and their included side. The side must connect the two angles' vertices. Shared sides qualify via the Reflexive Property.
  • Sec 4.11AAS: two angles and a non-included side. Valid because the third angle is determined once two angles are known. Distinguished from ASA by the position of the known side.
  • Sec 4.12HL: right triangles only. Congruent hypotenuses and one pair of congruent legs suffice. The Pythagorean Theorem eliminates the ambiguity that makes SSA invalid for non-right triangles.
  • Sec 4.13Proof planning: mark the diagram, identify shortcut, write statements with justifications. Common sources of extra congruent parts: Reflexive Property, Vertical Angles Theorem, Alternate Interior Angles, definitions of bisector and midpoint.
  • Sec 4.14Common errors: wrong congruence statement order; SSA vs. SAS confusion; AAA establishing similarity not congruence; applying CPCTC before congruence is proven; assuming unmarked information; forgetting the Reflexive Property; applying HL without confirmed right angles.

Key Terms Review

triangle
A closed three-sided polygon formed by three segments connecting three non-collinear points.
vertex (vertices)
The corner points of a triangle. Each vertex is shared by two sides and named by a capital letter.
included angle
The angle formed between two sides — located at the vertex where those two sides meet.
included side
The side that connects the vertices of two specific angles of a triangle.
scalene
A triangle with all three sides having different lengths.
isosceles
A triangle with at least two equal sides. The equal sides are legs; the third is the base.
equilateral
A triangle with all three sides equal. Always also equiangular (60° each).
acute triangle
A triangle in which all three interior angles measure less than 90°.
right triangle
A triangle containing exactly one 90° angle. The side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse.
obtuse triangle
A triangle containing exactly one angle greater than 90°.
hypotenuse
The side opposite the right angle in a right triangle — always the longest side.
leg (right triangle)
Either of the two sides that form the right angle in a right triangle.
Angle Sum Theorem
The sum of the interior angles of any triangle equals 180°.
exterior angle
An angle formed outside a triangle by extending one side beyond a vertex.
remote interior angles
The two interior angles of a triangle that are not adjacent to a given exterior angle.
congruent (≅)
Having exactly the same shape and size. All corresponding parts are equal.
correspondence
A pairing that matches each vertex, side, or angle of one triangle to exactly one part of another.
CPCTC
Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles are Congruent. Used after congruence is proven.
SSS
Side-Side-Side congruence: three pairs of congruent sides guarantee congruent triangles.
SAS
Side-Angle-Side congruence: two sides and their included angle.
ASA
Angle-Side-Angle congruence: two angles and their included side.
AAS
Angle-Angle-Side congruence: two angles and a non-included side.
HL
Hypotenuse-Leg congruence for right triangles: one pair of hypotenuses and one pair of legs.
Reflexive Property
Any figure is congruent to itself. Provides the "shared" congruent part when two triangles share a side or angle.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 5: Triangle Relationships and Inequalities

Chapter 4 established the tools for determining when two triangles are congruent. Chapter 5 looks inside a single triangle to discover its internal structure. You will study the perpendicular bisectors, angle bisectors, medians, and altitudes of triangles — each a special segment with a distinctive relationship to the triangle's sides and angles.

You will find that the perpendicular bisectors of the three sides all meet at a single point (the circumcenter), the angle bisectors meet at another (the incenter), the medians meet at the centroid, and the altitudes at the orthocenter. The congruence reasoning from Chapter 4 is the tool used to prove these relationships. Chapter 5 also introduces the Triangle Inequality Theorem — the rules governing which sets of three lengths can and cannot form a triangle, and which sides are longest when certain angles are largest. These ideas are the geometric foundation for Chapter 6 (Similarity) and Chapter 7 (The Pythagorean Theorem).

Chapter 3 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every building, bridge, road, and diagram is shaped by lines and the angles they create. Before geometry can reason about triangles, polygons, circles, or three-dimensional figures, it needs a reliable system for describing how lines relate to each other and what happens at every intersection.

Chapter 3 builds that system. It studies what happens when two lines meet, what angle pairs are formed, and what special relationships appear when lines are perpendicular or parallel. It shows that angle relationships are not random facts to memorize — they are predictable patterns produced by the structure of the lines themselves. Once a student understands those patterns, a wide range of geometric problems become solvable through reasoning and algebra.

This chapter prepares students directly for triangle angle relationships, polygon interior angles, coordinate geometry, and proof. The ideas introduced here will return in nearly every chapter that follows.

Opening Question

A contractor is building a wooden deck. She cuts two boards so that they cross each other, and she needs to know the angle between them at the crossing point. She measures one angle with a protractor and gets 127°. Her assistant asks: Can we figure out the other three angles without measuring them?

The answer is yes — if you understand how intersecting lines behave. By the end of this chapter, you will not only answer this question, but also solve problems involving parallel lines, transversals, roads, railroads, bridges, and geometric proofs.

Section 3.1

Intersecting Lines

When two lines cross each other at a single point, they are said to intersect, and the point where they cross is called the point of intersection. At that crossing point, four angles are formed. These four angles fit together around the point of intersection in a way that is completely predictable.

Understanding what happens at an intersection is the foundation for everything else in this chapter. Every angle relationship studied here — vertical angles, linear pairs, supplementary pairs, alternate interior angles, corresponding angles, and more — begins with understanding how lines meet.

Diagram Instruction 3.1

Draw two straight lines crossing each other at a single point. Label the four angles formed at the intersection as ∠1, ∠2, ∠3, and ∠4, going clockwise from the upper-left region. Label the intersection point P.

When two lines intersect at point P, the four angles around P together make a full rotation of 360°. Any two adjacent angles (angles that share a side at P) form a straight line together, so they add up to 180°. The two angles across from each other — the ones that do not share a side — are called vertical angles, and as we will see in Section 3.2, they are always equal in measure.

Student Tip

Every time two lines intersect, you get exactly four angles, organized into two pairs of vertical angles and two linear pairs (adjacent supplementary angles). If you know one angle, you can always find the other three.

Section 3.2

Vertical Angles

Vertical angles are the two pairs of opposite angles formed when two lines intersect. They are called vertical angles not because they point upward, but because they share a common vertex (the intersection point) and open in opposite directions.

In the diagram from Section 3.1, ∠1 and ∠3 are vertical angles. So are ∠2 and ∠4.

Vertical Angles Theorem

Vertical angles are congruent. If two lines intersect, the angles across from each other are always equal in measure:

m∠1 = m∠3    and    m∠2 = m∠4

Proof — Why Vertical Angles Are Congruent
Algebraic Justification
Consider: ∠1 and ∠2 form a straight line, so m∠1 + m∠2 = 180°
Also: ∠2 and ∠3 form a straight line, so m∠2 + m∠3 = 180°
Both equal 180°: m∠1 + m∠2 = m∠2 + m∠3
Subtract m∠2: m∠1 = m∠3 ✓

This is not a coincidence that depends on how you draw the lines. It is a consequence of how lines and angles work. Vertical angles are always congruent, no matter what the intersection looks like.

Example 3.2.1
Finding Vertical Angle Measures
  • ProblemTwo lines intersect, forming four angles. One angle measures 54°. Find the measures of the other three angles.
  • ThinkThe given angle and its vertical angle are congruent. The given angle and each adjacent angle form a linear pair summing to 180°.
  • DiagramDraw two intersecting lines. Label the 54° angle as ∠1. Label ∠2, ∠3, and ∠4 going clockwise.
  • Solve ∠3 is vertical to ∠1:   m∠3 = 54° ∠1 and ∠2 are a linear pair:   m∠2 = 180° − 54° = 126° ∠4 is vertical to ∠2:   m∠4 = 126°
  • CheckAll four angles sum to 360°: 54° + 126° + 54° + 126° = 360° ✓
Answer: The four angles are 54°, 126°, 54°, and 126°.
Section 3.3

Linear Pairs

A linear pair is a pair of adjacent angles whose non-shared sides form a straight line. In other words, a linear pair is two angles that sit side by side at an intersection point, together forming a straight angle of 180°.

Linear Pair Postulate

If two angles form a linear pair, then they are supplementary — their measures add up to 180°.

m∠1 + m∠2 = 180°

Important: Not every pair of supplementary angles forms a linear pair. Two angles can add to 180° without being adjacent. But every linear pair is supplementary.

Example 3.3.1
Using a Linear Pair
  • ProblemTwo angles form a linear pair. One angle measures (3x + 15)° and the other measures (2x + 5)°. Find x and the measure of each angle.
  • ThinkLinear pair angles are supplementary, so their measures sum to 180°. Set up an equation.
  • Represent(3x + 15) + (2x + 5) = 180
  • Solve 5x + 20 = 180 5x = 160 x = 32 m∠1 = 3(32) + 15 = 96 + 15 = 111° m∠2 = 2(32) + 5 = 64 + 5 = 69°
  • Check111° + 69° = 180° ✓
Answer: x = 32; the angles measure 111° and 69°.
Section 3.4

Complementary and Supplementary Angles

TermDefinitionEquation
Complementary anglesTwo angles whose measures add up to 90°. Each is the complement of the other.m∠A + m∠B = 90°
Supplementary anglesTwo angles whose measures add up to 180°. Each is the supplement of the other.m∠A + m∠B = 180°
Memory Tip

C comes before S in the alphabet, just as 90° comes before 180°. Complementary = 90°, Supplementary = 180°.

Example 3.4.1
Finding a Complement
  • ProblemAn angle measures 37°. What is its complement?
  • ThinkComplement means the two angles together make 90°.
  • Solve90° − 37° = 53°
Answer: The complement of 37° is 53°.
Example 3.4.2
Supplementary Angles with Algebra
  • ProblemTwo supplementary angles are in the ratio 2:3. Find both angles.
  • ThinkLet the angles be 2x and 3x. Supplementary angles sum to 180°.
  • Represent2x + 3x = 180
  • Solve 5x = 180  →  x = 36 m∠1 = 2(36) = 72°    m∠2 = 3(36) = 108°
  • Check72° + 108° = 180° ✓
Answer: The two supplementary angles are 72° and 108°.
Student Warning

Complementary and supplementary describe pairs of angles, not single angles. An angle by itself is not "complementary" — it is complementary to another specific angle. Students frequently use these words incorrectly by saying "this angle is complementary" without naming the second angle.

Section 3.5

Perpendicular Lines

Two lines are perpendicular if they intersect and form a right angle. A right angle measures exactly 90°. The notation ℓ ⊥ m means line ℓ is perpendicular to line m.

When two lines are perpendicular, all four angles formed at their intersection are right angles. This follows directly from the vertical angles theorem and the linear pair postulate: if one angle is 90°, its vertical angle is also 90°, and each adjacent angle is 180° − 90° = 90°.

Diagram Instruction 3.5

Draw two lines intersecting at a right angle. Mark the right angle with a small square at the intersection. This small square is the standard geometric symbol for a 90° angle.

Perpendicular Lines Theorem

If two lines are perpendicular, then they form four right angles at their point of intersection.

Example 3.5.1
Perpendicular Lines and Angle Equations
  • ProblemLine ℓ is perpendicular to line m. At their intersection, one angle is labeled (4x − 10)°. Find x.
  • ThinkSince the lines are perpendicular, every angle at the intersection is 90°.
  • Represent4x − 10 = 90
  • Solve 4x = 100  →  x = 25
  • Check4(25) − 10 = 100 − 10 = 90° ✓
Answer: x = 25.

Section 3.6

Parallel Lines

Two lines in the same plane are parallel if they never intersect, no matter how far they are extended in either direction. Parallel lines run alongside each other, always maintaining the same distance apart. The notation ℓ ∥ m means line ℓ is parallel to line m.

Parallel lines are among the most important geometric objects. Roads, railroad tracks, electrical wires, and architectural elements rely on parallelism. The properties of parallel lines generate a rich set of angle relationships that will occupy the rest of this chapter.

Diagram Instruction 3.6

Draw two horizontal lines that do not intersect. Label the top line ℓ and the bottom line m. Add arrowheads at both ends of each line to show they continue without end. Place a small matching arrow (tick mark on the lines themselves) on each line to indicate they are parallel — this is standard geometric notation.

Student Warning — Appearance vs. Proof

Two lines that appear parallel in a diagram are not necessarily parallel unless the diagram states it or it can be proven. In geometry, parallelism must be given, measured, or proven — never assumed from appearance alone.

Parallel Lines in Three Dimensions

Two lines that do not intersect are not automatically parallel if they are in three-dimensional space. In three dimensions, lines can be skew — non-intersecting but not in the same plane. Chapter 3 focuses entirely on lines in the same plane.

Section 3.7

Transversals

A transversal is a line that intersects two or more other lines at distinct points. When a transversal crosses two parallel (or non-parallel) lines, it creates a structured set of angle relationships at each intersection point.

Diagram Instruction 3.7 — Standard Eight-Angle Setup

Draw two parallel horizontal lines. Label the top line ℓ and the bottom line m. Draw a diagonal line crossing both. Label this diagonal line t (for transversal). Label the four angles at the top intersection as ∠1, ∠2, ∠3, ∠4 clockwise from the upper-left, and the four angles at the bottom intersection as ∠5, ∠6, ∠7, ∠8 clockwise from the upper-left.

RegionAngles (Standard Diagram)Description
Interior∠3, ∠4, ∠5, ∠6Between the two parallel lines
Exterior∠1, ∠2, ∠7, ∠8Outside the two parallel lines
Why This Setup Matters

This diagram of eight angles at two intersections is the foundation for Sections 3.8 through 3.10. Every angle pair type — corresponding, alternate interior, alternate exterior, and same-side interior — is defined by where the angles sit within this eight-angle structure.

Section 3.8

Corresponding Angles

When a transversal crosses two lines, corresponding angles are pairs of angles that sit in the same position at each intersection. One is at the top intersection and one is at the bottom, but both are in the same corner relative to the transversal and the line it crosses.

Corresponding PairPosition
∠1 and ∠5Upper-left at each intersection
∠2 and ∠6Upper-right at each intersection
∠3 and ∠7Lower-left at each intersection
∠4 and ∠8Lower-right at each intersection
Corresponding Angles Postulate

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then corresponding angles are congruent.

If ℓ ∥ m, then m∠1 = m∠5,   m∠2 = m∠6,   m∠3 = m∠7,   m∠4 = m∠8

Why does this make sense? If you imagine sliding line ℓ straight down to the position of line m (keeping it parallel the whole time), every angle at the top intersection slides down perfectly onto the corresponding angle at the bottom intersection. Parallel lines make the two intersections geometrically identical — just shifted in position.

Example 3.8.1
Corresponding Angles
  • ProblemTwo parallel lines are cut by a transversal. If m∠1 = 73°, find m∠5.
  • Think∠1 and ∠5 are corresponding angles. When lines are parallel, corresponding angles are congruent.
  • Solvem∠5 = m∠1 = 73°
Answer: m∠5 = 73°.
Example 3.8.2
Corresponding Angles with Algebra
  • ProblemTwo parallel lines are cut by a transversal. Corresponding angles are labeled (5x + 12)° and (7x − 8)°. Find x and the measure of each angle.
  • ThinkCorresponding angles are congruent, so set the expressions equal.
  • Represent5x + 12 = 7x − 8
  • Solve 12 + 8 = 7x − 5x 20 = 2x  →  x = 10 5(10) + 12 = 62°
  • Check7(10) − 8 = 62° ✓
Answer: x = 10; both corresponding angles measure 62°.
Section 3.9

Alternate Interior and Alternate Exterior Angles

Alternate interior angles are pairs of angles that lie between the two lines (in the interior) and on opposite sides of the transversal (alternate sides). In the standard diagram, ∠3 and ∠6 are alternate interior angles, and ∠4 and ∠5 are alternate interior angles.

Alternate Interior Angles Theorem

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then alternate interior angles are congruent.

If ℓ ∥ m, then m∠4 = m∠5   and   m∠3 = m∠6

Proof Sketch
Why Alternate Interior Angles Are Congruent
Step 1: ∠4 and ∠8 are corresponding angles → congruent (since ℓ ∥ m)
Step 2: ∠8 and ∠5 are vertical angles → congruent
Step 3: Therefore ∠4 ≅ ∠8 ≅ ∠5, so ∠4 ≅ ∠5

This chain of reasoning — corresponding angles, then vertical angles — shows how the theorems build on each other.

Alternate exterior angles are pairs of angles that lie outside the two lines (in the exterior) and on opposite sides of the transversal. In the standard diagram, ∠1 and ∠8 are alternate exterior angles, and ∠2 and ∠7 are alternate exterior angles.

Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then alternate exterior angles are congruent.

If ℓ ∥ m, then m∠1 = m∠8   and   m∠2 = m∠7

Memory Device

The word alternate tells you the two angles are on opposite sides of the transversal. The words interior and exterior tell you whether the angles are between the parallel lines or outside them.

Example 3.9.1
Alternate Interior Angles
  • ProblemTwo parallel lines are cut by a transversal. One alternate interior angle measures 118°. Find the measure of the other.
  • ThinkAlternate interior angles are congruent when lines are parallel.
  • Solvem∠ = 118°
Answer: The other alternate interior angle also measures 118°.
Example 3.9.2
Alternate Exterior Angles with Algebra
  • ProblemTwo parallel lines are cut by a transversal. Alternate exterior angles are labeled (6x − 5)° and (4x + 19)°. Find x and the angle measure.
  • ThinkAlternate exterior angles are congruent when lines are parallel.
  • Represent6x − 5 = 4x + 19
  • Solve 2x = 24  →  x = 12 6(12) − 5 = 72 − 5 = 67°
  • Check4(12) + 19 = 48 + 19 = 67° ✓
Answer: x = 12; both angles measure 67°.
Section 3.10

Same-Side Interior Angles

Same-side interior angles (also called co-interior angles or consecutive interior angles) are angle pairs that both lie between the two lines and on the same side of the transversal — rather than on opposite sides. In the standard diagram, ∠3 and ∠5 are same-side interior angles (both left of the transversal), and ∠4 and ∠6 are same-side interior angles (both right of the transversal).

Same-Side Interior Angles Theorem

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then same-side interior angles are supplementary — they add up to 180°.

If ℓ ∥ m, then m∠3 + m∠5 = 180°   and   m∠4 + m∠6 = 180°

Unlike corresponding or alternate angles, same-side interior angles are supplementary, not congruent. They open toward each other on the same side and together fill exactly 180° of space between the parallel lines.

Example 3.10.1
Same-Side Interior Angles
  • ProblemTwo parallel lines are cut by a transversal. One same-side interior angle measures 74°. Find the other.
  • ThinkSame-side interior angles are supplementary.
  • Solve180° − 74° = 106°
Answer: The other same-side interior angle measures 106°.
Example 3.10.2
Same-Side Interior Angles with Algebra
  • ProblemTwo parallel lines are cut by a transversal. Same-side interior angles are labeled (8x − 10)° and (3x + 25)°. Find x and both angle measures.
  • ThinkSame-side interior angles are supplementary.
  • Represent(8x − 10) + (3x + 25) = 180
  • Solve 11x + 15 = 180 11x = 165  →  x = 15 8(15) − 10 = 120 − 10 = 110° 3(15) + 25 = 45 + 25 = 70°
  • Check110° + 70° = 180° ✓
Answer: x = 15; the angles measure 110° and 70°.

Summary of Parallel Line Angle Relationships

Angle PairRelationship When Lines Are Parallel
Corresponding anglesCongruent (equal measure)
Alternate interior anglesCongruent (equal measure)
Alternate exterior anglesCongruent (equal measure)
Same-side interior anglesSupplementary (sum to 180°)
Vertical angles (at each intersection)Congruent (always, not just when parallel)

Key Insight

If you know any one of the eight angles formed by two parallel lines and a transversal, you can find all eight. Four of them share the given measure, and four share its supplement.

Section 3.11

Proving Lines Parallel

All of Sections 3.8 through 3.10 described what happens when lines are parallel. But geometry also needs to reason in the other direction: given angle information, can we conclude that lines must be parallel? The answer is yes. Each of the theorems from the previous sections has a converse. The converses are the tools for proving lines parallel.

Converse TheoremCondition That Proves Lines Parallel
Converse of Corresponding Angles PostulateCorresponding angles are congruent
Converse of Alternate Interior Angles TheoremAlternate interior angles are congruent
Converse of Alternate Exterior Angles TheoremAlternate exterior angles are congruent
Converse of Same-Side Interior Angles TheoremSame-side interior angles are supplementary
Direction of the Proof

In a proof, you cannot simply draw two lines that look parallel and call them parallel. You must have a reason — and these converses provide valid reasons based on angle information alone. Pay attention to which direction you are reasoning: are you starting with parallel lines (use the theorem) or trying to prove lines are parallel (use the converse)?

Example 3.11.1
Proving Lines Parallel
  • ProblemTwo lines are cut by a transversal. Alternate interior angles at the two intersections both measure 65°. Are the lines parallel? Justify your answer.
  • ThinkIf alternate interior angles are congruent, then the lines are parallel by the Converse of the Alternate Interior Angles Theorem.
  • SolveThe alternate interior angles are congruent (both 65°). The Converse of the Alternate Interior Angles Theorem applies.
Answer: Yes, the lines are parallel because their alternate interior angles are congruent.
Example 3.11.2
Two-Column Proof: Proving Lines Parallel
Given: Line t is a transversal cutting lines ℓ and m. ∠1 ≅ ∠7 (alternate exterior angles)
Prove: ℓ ∥ m
Plan: ∠1 and ∠7 are alternate exterior angles. If alternate exterior angles are congruent, the lines are parallel by the Converse of the Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem.
StatementReason
1. ∠1 ≅ ∠7Given
2. ∠1 and ∠7 are alternate exterior anglesDefinition of alternate exterior angles
3. ℓ ∥ mConverse of the Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem
Section 3.12

Algebra with Angle Relationships

The angle relationships from this chapter become especially powerful when combined with algebra. Many geometry problems give angle measures as expressions in terms of a variable, and the geometric relationship between the angles provides the equation needed to solve for that variable.

The method for these problems follows the same structure throughout: (1) identify the type of angle pair, (2) apply the appropriate relationship — congruent or supplementary, (3) write the equation, (4) solve for the variable, (5) find the actual angle measures, (6) check using the original relationship.

Example 3.12.1
Vertical Angles and Algebra
  • ProblemTwo lines intersect. One angle measures (7x + 14)° and its vertical angle measures (9x − 6)°. Find x and the angle measures.
  • ThinkVertical angles are congruent.
  • Represent7x + 14 = 9x − 6
  • Solve 14 + 6 = 9x − 7x  →  20 = 2x  →  x = 10 7(10) + 14 = 84°
  • Check9(10) − 6 = 84° ✓
Answer: x = 10; both vertical angles measure 84°.
Example 3.12.2
Linear Pair and Algebra
  • ProblemA linear pair of angles has measures (12x + 3)° and (6x − 9)°. Find x and each angle measure.
  • ThinkLinear pair angles are supplementary.
  • Represent(12x + 3) + (6x − 9) = 180
  • Solve 18x − 6 = 180  →  18x = 186  →  x = 31/3 12(31/3) + 3 = 124 + 3 = 127° 6(31/3) − 9 = 62 − 9 = 53°
  • Check127° + 53° = 180° ✓
Answer: x = 31/3; the angles measure 127° and 53°.
Always Check That Angle Measures Are Positive and Reasonable

Algebraic solutions in geometry must produce angle measures that make sense geometrically. A negative angle, an angle greater than 180° in a linear pair context, or a measure of 0° is a signal that an error has occurred. After solving for x, always substitute back and verify that each angle is positive and that the geometric relationship holds.

Section 3.13

Real-World Parallel Line Applications

The angle relationships of parallel lines appear throughout the built and natural world. The following examples show how geometric reasoning extends into practical situations.

Roads and streets: City streets that run in the same direction are often parallel. When a diagonal street (a transversal) crosses them, it creates the same pattern of angles at each intersection. This is why the angles at two parallel intersections seem to match when you look down a diagonal road.

Railroad tracks and crossings: Railroad rails are parallel lines. When a road or another track crosses them at a diagonal, alternate interior angles at the two crossings are congruent. Railroad engineers use these relationships to design crossing angles safely.

Carpentry and framing: When a builder cuts parallel boards with a diagonal cut, the angles of the cut are the same at each board. If the boards are parallel, corresponding angles are congruent, which means cuts can be made at the same angle setting on a miter saw.

Bridges and trusses: Truss bridges use diagonal support members crossing parallel horizontal and vertical members. The angle relationships between the diagonals and the parallel members must be calculated precisely so the forces are distributed correctly.

Example 3.13.1
Road Crossing Parallel Train Tracks
  • ProblemA straight road crosses two parallel train tracks. The angle between the road and the first track (measured from the upper-left) is 52°. What angle does the road make with the second track at the same position?
  • ThinkThe road is a transversal. The two train tracks are parallel lines. The angles at the same position at each intersection are corresponding angles.
  • Solve Corresponding angles are congruent: m∠ = 52°
Answer: The road makes a 52° angle with the second track at the upper-left position.
Example 3.13.2
Carpentry Application
  • ProblemA carpenter cuts two parallel boards with a saw blade. She sets the blade so the cut on the first board creates a 38° angle on the left side (upper-left of the cut). What angle does the same blade cut create on the right side (upper-right) on the second board?
  • ThinkThe left-side and right-side cuts at the same intersection are supplementary (linear pair). The corresponding angles at the two boards are congruent.
  • Solve Upper-right at first board (linear pair with 38°): 180° − 38° = 142° Upper-right at second board (corresponding): 142°
Answer: The cut creates a 142° angle on the right side of the blade on the second board.
Section 3.14

Common Angle Relationship Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Assuming Lines Are Parallel from a Diagram
"The lines look parallel, so these alternate interior angles must be congruent."

Correct practice: Confirm that the lines are parallel (given, marked with arrows, or proven), then apply the theorem. Parallelism is never assumed from appearance alone.

Mistake 2 — Confusing Angle Pair Types
"These angles are on opposite sides, so they must be corresponding angles."

Corresponding angles are at the same corner at different intersections. Alternate interior/exterior angles are on opposite sides of the transversal. Always locate the angles in the diagram before naming the pair type.

Mistake 3 — Applying Congruence to Same-Side Interior Angles
"Same-side interior angles are congruent when lines are parallel."

Correct: Same-side interior angles are supplementary (sum to 180°), not congruent. Only alternate interior, alternate exterior, and corresponding angles are congruent when lines are parallel.

Mistake 4 — Forgetting to Check Angle Measures Are Positive
Solving for x and reporting a negative or zero angle as a valid answer.

After solving for x, always substitute back to verify that each angle measure is positive, that linear pairs sum to 180°, and that the geometric relationship holds. A result like −1° or 0° signals an error in setup.

Mistake 5 — Treating All Supplementary Pairs as Linear Pairs
"Since these two angles are supplementary, they must be a linear pair."

Supplementary angles add to 180° but do not need to be adjacent. A linear pair is a specific type — supplementary AND adjacent AND sharing a side at an intersection. Not all supplementary angles are linear pairs.

Mistake 6 — Confusing the Direction of the Proof
"Parallel lines create congruent corresponding angles, so if I want to prove lines are parallel, I just say corresponding angles are congruent."

The theorem and its converse are different tools. To find angles from parallel lines, use the theorem directly. To prove lines are parallel from angle information, use the converse. The direction of reasoning must match the direction of the proof.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 3.1Intersecting lines create four angles organized into two pairs of vertical angles and two linear pairs. If one angle is known, all four can be found.
  • Sec 3.2Vertical Angles Theorem: Vertical angles are always congruent. The proof follows from the linear pair postulate applied twice at the same intersection.
  • Sec 3.3Linear Pair Postulate: Adjacent angles forming a straight line are supplementary (sum to 180°). Not every supplementary pair is a linear pair, but every linear pair is supplementary.
  • Sec 3.4Complementary angles sum to 90°. Supplementary angles sum to 180°. These pairs do not need to be adjacent, though they often are. Algebra produces equations to find unknown measures.
  • Sec 3.5Perpendicular lines intersect at 90° and form four right angles. The small square symbol marks a right angle in a diagram. All four angles at a perpendicular intersection are equal.
  • Sec 3.6Parallel lines in the same plane never intersect. Parallelism must be given or proven — never assumed. Skew lines are non-intersecting lines in three dimensions that are not parallel.
  • Sec 3.7A transversal crosses two or more lines at distinct points, creating eight angles (four at each intersection). Angles between the lines are interior; angles outside are exterior.
  • Sec 3.8Corresponding angles occupy the same corner at each intersection. Corresponding Angles Postulate: congruent when lines are parallel.
  • Sec 3.9Alternate interior angles lie between the parallel lines on opposite sides of the transversal. Alternate exterior angles lie outside on opposite sides. Both pairs are congruent when lines are parallel.
  • Sec 3.10Same-side interior angles lie between the parallel lines on the same side of the transversal. They are supplementary (sum to 180°) when lines are parallel — not congruent.
  • Sec 3.11To prove lines are parallel, use the converse of any parallel line theorem: congruent corresponding, alternate interior, or alternate exterior angles; or supplementary same-side interior angles each prove parallelism.
  • Sec 3.12Algebra combines naturally with angle relationships. Identify the pair type, apply congruence or supplementarity, write the equation, solve for x, find angle measures, and verify the geometric relationship.
  • Sec 3.13Parallel line angle relationships appear in road crossings, railroad tracks, carpentry, bridge design, architecture, and navigation. Corresponding and alternate angle relationships solve practical measurement problems.
  • Sec 3.14Six common errors: assuming parallelism from appearance; confusing pair types; applying congruence to same-side interior angles; ignoring invalid angle measures; treating supplementary as always linear; reversing theorem direction in proof.

Key Terms Review

alternate exterior angles
Angle pairs formed by a transversal crossing two lines, lying outside the two lines on opposite sides. Congruent when lines are parallel.
alternate interior angles
Angle pairs formed by a transversal crossing two lines, lying between the lines on opposite sides. Congruent when lines are parallel.
complementary angles
Two angles whose measures sum to 90°. Each is the complement of the other.
congruent angles
Angles having the same measure. Marked with matching arc marks in diagrams.
corresponding angles
Angle pairs formed by a transversal crossing two lines, sitting in the same corner at each intersection. Congruent when lines are parallel.
linear pair
Two adjacent angles whose non-shared sides form a straight line. Linear pair angles are always supplementary.
parallel lines
Two lines in the same plane that never intersect, always maintaining the same distance apart. Notation: ℓ ∥ m.
perpendicular lines
Two lines that intersect to form a right angle (90°). All four angles at the intersection are right angles. Notation: ℓ ⊥ m.
same-side interior angles
Angle pairs formed by a transversal, lying between the lines on the same side. Supplementary (sum to 180°) when lines are parallel. Also called co-interior angles.
supplementary angles
Two angles whose measures sum to 180°. Each is the supplement of the other.
transversal
A line that intersects two or more other lines at distinct points, creating angle pairs at each intersection.
vertical angles
The pair of opposite angles formed at the intersection of two lines. Vertical angles are always congruent — a consequence of the linear pair postulate.

Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter develops the measurement of two-dimensional space and the outer surfaces of three-dimensional objects. Students who have worked through triangles, similarity, circles, quadrilaterals, and polygons have built a strong foundation of shape relationships. Now those shapes become objects to measure.

Area is not a formula. Area is a quantity — the amount of flat space enclosed by a boundary. Before any formula can be trusted, a student must understand what the formula is counting. This chapter insists on that understanding at every step: what does each formula measure, where does it come from, when does it apply, and what happens when the shape is not one of the standard cases?

Surface area extends that thinking into three dimensions. When a three-dimensional object is unfolded into its faces, each face is a flat region with an area. Surface area is the total area of all those faces. It is the quantity that determines how much material is needed to wrap a box, paint a cylinder, or manufacture a cone-shaped container.

Opening Question

A contractor is finishing a triangular dormer window on a house. The window has a base of 4 feet and a height of 3 feet. Below the window, a semicircular decorative arch extends downward with a diameter of 4 feet. The contractor needs to order enough specialty glass to fill both the triangle and the arch, and enough trim to outline the full perimeter of the combined shape. How do you find the total glass area and the total trim length? By the end of this chapter, problems like this will be routine.

Why This Chapter Matters

Area and surface area appear throughout mathematics, science, and practical work. In construction and architecture, area determines how much flooring, roofing, wallboard, paint, or fabric is needed for a project. In physics, the cross-sectional area of a pipe or wire determines how much fluid or current it can carry. In engineering and manufacturing, surface area calculations determine material costs, thermal properties, drag forces, and structural needs. In biology and medicine, surface area governs how quickly substances are absorbed across membranes — the lungs, intestines, and blood vessels are enormous in surface area because of internal folding. This chapter is not an isolated collection of formulas. It is a systematic study of measurement in two dimensions and on the surfaces of three-dimensional objects.

Chapter 11 — Key Terms

Vocabulary

Area
The measure of the amount of two-dimensional space enclosed within a boundary. Always measured in square units.
Base
The side of a polygon or face of a solid used as the reference side for an area or volume formula. Height is always measured perpendicular to the base.
Height
The perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite vertex or side. Never a slant side.
Apothem
The perpendicular distance from the center of a regular polygon to the midpoint of one of its sides.
Perimeter
The total length of the boundary of a two-dimensional figure.
Circumference
The perimeter of a circle. Equal to 2πr or πd.
Sector
A region of a circle bounded by two radii and the arc between them. Shaped like a slice of pie.
Composite Figure
A figure made by combining two or more basic figures. Area is found by adding or subtracting the parts.
Net
A two-dimensional pattern that folds into a three-dimensional figure. Used to visualize and calculate surface area.
Lateral Face
A non-base face of a prism or pyramid.
Lateral Surface
The curved surface of a cylinder or cone, not including the base or bases.
Slant Height (ℓ)
The distance along a lateral face of a pyramid or cone from the base edge to the apex. Different from the vertical height h.
Surface Area
The total area of all the faces or surfaces of a three-dimensional figure.
π (pi)
The ratio of the circumference of any circle to its diameter. Approximately 3.14159. Pi is irrational — its decimal expansion never repeats.
Regular Polygon
A polygon that is both equilateral (all sides equal) and equiangular (all angles equal).

Section 11.1

What Area Measures

Imagine tiling a flat floor with square tiles, each one foot on a side. The number of tiles required to cover the floor exactly — with no gaps and no overlaps — is the area of the floor in square feet. Each tile represents one square foot of space.

This is what area measures. Area is not the boundary of a shape. Area is not the length of the longest side. Area is the two-dimensional space enclosed inside the boundary of a flat figure.

When we say a rectangle has an area of 24 square inches, we mean that 24 non-overlapping unit squares of side length one inch could be arranged to fill the rectangle completely.

Why Units of Area Are Squared

Length is measured in one-dimensional units: inches, feet, centimeters, meters. When we measure area, we are covering a two-dimensional region using squares. Each square has a length and a width, both measured in the same unit. A square that is 1 inch on each side has an area of 1 × 1 = 1 square inch, written 1 in². This is why area is always measured in squared units: in², ft², cm², m², and so on. An answer of "24" with no squared unit is incomplete.

Perimeter vs. Area

Perimeter measures the length of the boundary. Area measures the space enclosed by that boundary. These are different quantities with different units. A rectangle 6 ft × 4 ft has perimeter 20 ft and area 24 ft². A square 5 ft on each side has perimeter 20 ft and area 25 ft². Both have the same perimeter but different areas.

Student Warning

Do not confuse perimeter and area. Area problems often require you to identify a height, which is a perpendicular distance — never a slant side. Perimeter adds up the actual side lengths. Height is used for area only.

Exact vs. Approximate Values

Many area calculations involve π, square roots, or other irrational numbers. Leave answers in exact form unless the problem asks for a decimal approximation.

Exact: A = 9π cm²

Approximate: A ≈ 28.27 cm²

Section 11.2

Area of Rectangles and Parallelograms

A rectangle is a quadrilateral with four right angles. Its area is the product of its base and its height. Because a rectangle has right angles, the height is simply the side perpendicular to the base.

Area of a Rectangle

A = bh    where b is the base and h is the height.

Example 11.2.1
Area of a Rectangle
  • ProblemA rectangular room measures 14 feet by 11 feet. What is the area of the floor?
  • ThinkThe room is a rectangle. Use A = bh with b = 14 and h = 11.
  • SolveA = bh = 14 × 11 = 154 ft²
  • CheckReasonable. A room of this size covers about 154 square feet of flooring. ✓
Answer: 154 ft²

Parallelograms

A parallelogram has two pairs of parallel sides. Its slant sides are not perpendicular to the base, so the height of a parallelogram must be measured perpendicular to the base, not along the slant side. If you cut a right triangle off one end of a parallelogram and move it to the other end, you form a rectangle with the same base and height. Therefore the area of the parallelogram equals the area of that rectangle.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a parallelogram with a slanted left and right side. Draw a dashed perpendicular from the top side down to the base and label it h. Label the base b. Mark the slant sides clearly so the height is distinguished from the slant height.

Area of a Parallelogram

A = bh    where h is the perpendicular height, not the slant side.

Example 11.2.2
Area of a Parallelogram
  • ProblemA parallelogram has a base of 9 cm, a slant side of 7 cm, and a perpendicular height of 5 cm. Find the area.
  • ThinkUse A = bh. The slant side is not the height. Use the perpendicular height of 5 cm.
  • SolveA = bh = 9 × 5 = 45 cm²
  • CheckIf we mistakenly used the slant side: 9 × 7 = 63 cm². The slant side is always longer than the perpendicular height, so the correct area must be smaller. 45 < 63. ✓
Answer: 45 cm²
Student Warning — Slant Side vs. Height

Never use the slant side as the height of a parallelogram. The height is always the perpendicular distance between the two parallel sides.

Section 11.3

Area of Triangles

A triangle is half of a parallelogram. Take any parallelogram and draw one diagonal — you divide it into two congruent triangles. Each triangle has the same base and height as the parallelogram but half the area.

Area of a Triangle

A = ½bh    where h is the perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite vertex.

Diagram Instruction

Draw three triangles side by side: an acute triangle with the height drawn inside; an obtuse triangle with the height drawn outside as a dashed extension; and a right triangle where the height is one of the legs. In each case label b and h and mark the right angle at the foot of the height.

Example 11.3.1
Area of an Acute Triangle
  • ProblemA triangle has a base of 12 inches and a height of 7 inches. Find the area.
  • SolveA = ½ × 12 × 7 = ½ × 84 = 42 in²
Answer: 42 in²
Example 11.3.2
Solving for a Missing Dimension
  • ProblemA triangle has an area of 36 ft² and a base of 9 ft. Find the height.
  • ThinkUse A = ½bh and solve for h.
  • Solve 36 = ½(9)(h) 36 = 4.5h h = 36 ÷ 4.5 = 8 ft
  • Check½(9)(8) = ½(72) = 36 ✓
Answer: h = 8 ft
Tip — Obtuse Triangles

For obtuse triangles, you may need to extend the base line to draw the perpendicular height. The height falls outside the triangle, but the formula A = ½bh still applies correctly with that perpendicular distance.

Section 11.4

Area of Trapezoids and Kites

Trapezoids

A trapezoid has exactly one pair of parallel sides, called the bases and labeled b₁ and b₂. The height h is the perpendicular distance between the two parallel sides. The formula multiplies the average of the two bases by the height — the average base acts like the single base of a rectangle with the same height and the same area.

Area of a Trapezoid

A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h

Derivation: place two identical trapezoids together to form a parallelogram with base b₁ + b₂ and height h. Each trapezoid is half that parallelogram.

Example 11.4.1
Area of a Trapezoid
  • ProblemA trapezoid has bases of 8 cm and 14 cm, with a height of 5 cm. Find the area.
  • SolveA = ½(8 + 14)(5) = ½(22)(5) = ½(110) = 55 cm²
  • CheckAverage base = (8 + 14)/2 = 11 cm. Rectangle 11 × 5 = 55 cm². ✓
Answer: 55 cm²

Kites

A kite is a quadrilateral with two pairs of consecutive congruent sides. Its diagonals are perpendicular, and one diagonal bisects the other. The area uses the diagonal lengths.

Area of a Kite

A = ½d₁d₂    where d₁ and d₂ are the lengths of the two diagonals.

Example 11.4.2
Area of a Kite
  • ProblemA kite has diagonals of length 10 inches and 6 inches. Find the area.
  • SolveA = ½(10)(6) = 30 in²
Answer: 30 in²
Section 11.5

Area of Rhombi

A rhombus is a parallelogram with four congruent sides. Its diagonals are perpendicular bisectors of each other. The area can be found two ways: the parallelogram formula (base × height) when the perpendicular height is known, or the diagonal formula when both diagonals are given. A rhombus is a special kite, which is why the diagonal formula is the same.

Area of a Rhombus — Two Methods

Method 1 (base and height): A = bh

Method 2 (diagonals): A = ½d₁d₂

Use the diagonal formula when both diagonals are given. Use base-height when the perpendicular height is given.

Example 11.5.1
Finding a Diagonal from Area
  • ProblemA rhombus has an area of 120 ft² and one diagonal of 15 ft. Find the other diagonal.
  • Solve 120 = ½(15)(d₂) 120 = 7.5d₂ d₂ = 16 ft
Answer: The other diagonal is 16 ft
Section 11.6

Area of Regular Polygons

A regular polygon is both equilateral and equiangular. The apothem is the perpendicular distance from the center to the midpoint of any side — the shortest distance from the center to a side. By dividing the polygon into congruent triangles from the center, each with base s (one side) and height a (the apothem), the area of all n triangles gives the formula below.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a regular hexagon with its center marked. Draw one line segment from the center perpendicular to a side, meeting the midpoint of that side. Label this segment a (the apothem). Label one side s. Show the triangle formed by two radii to adjacent vertices and the side s.

Area of a Regular Polygon

A = ½Pa    where P is the perimeter and a is the apothem.

Derived by summing n triangles, each with area ½ · s · a. Since P = ns: total area = ½ · (ns) · a = ½Pa.

Example 11.6.1
Area of a Regular Hexagon
  • ProblemA regular hexagon has a side length of 8 m and an apothem of 4√3 m. Find the area.
  • ThinkPerimeter of regular hexagon with side 8 m: P = 6 × 8 = 48 m. Use A = ½Pa.
  • SolveA = ½(48)(4√3) = ½(192√3) = 96√3 m²
  • Check96√3 ≈ 96 × 1.732 ≈ 166.3 m². Reasonable for a hexagon with 8-meter sides. ✓
Answer: Exact area = 96√3 m² ≈ 166.3 m²
Example 11.6.2
Area of a Regular Pentagon
  • ProblemA regular pentagon has a perimeter of 35 ft and an apothem of 4.82 ft. Find the area to the nearest square foot.
  • SolveA = ½Pa = ½(35)(4.82) = ½(168.7) = 84.35 ft²
Answer: ≈ 84 ft²
Section 11.7

Circumference and Area of Circles

The ratio of the circumference of any circle to its diameter is always the same constant: π ≈ 3.14159. Pi is irrational — its decimal expansion never repeats. The circumference is the perimeter of a circle. The area formula comes from rearranging thin pie-slice wedges into a near-rectangle whose width approaches πr and whose height is r, giving area πr · r = πr².

Circle Formulas

Circumference: C = 2πr or C = πd

Area: A = πr²

Always use the radius in A = πr². If given the diameter, divide by 2 first.

Example 11.7.1
Circumference and Area of a Circle
  • ProblemA circle has a radius of 7 cm. Find the circumference and the area in exact form.
  • Solve C = 2π(7) = 14π cm A = π(7)² = 49π cm²
Answer: C = 14π cm; A = 49π cm²
Example 11.7.2
Finding the Radius from Area
  • ProblemA circle has an area of 100π ft². Find the radius and diameter.
  • Solve 100π = πr² r² = 100 r = 10 ft d = 2r = 20 ft
Answer: r = 10 ft; d = 20 ft
Student Warning — Diameter vs. Radius

The area formula uses the radius, not the diameter. If you use the diameter where the radius is required, you will get a result four times too large: π(2r)² = 4πr², not πr².

Section 11.8

Sectors of Circles

A sector of a circle is a region bounded by two radii and the arc between them — like a slice of pie. If the central angle is θ degrees, the sector is θ/360 of the full circle. This fraction applies equally to arc length and to area.

Sector Formulas

Arc length: ℓ = (θ/360) · 2πr

Sector area: A = (θ/360) · πr²

Example 11.8.1
Area and Arc Length of a Sector
  • ProblemA sector has a central angle of 120° and a radius of 9 inches. Find the area and arc length in exact form.
  • ThinkThe sector is 120/360 = 1/3 of the full circle.
  • Solve A = (1/3) · π(9)² = (1/3)(81π) = 27π in² ℓ = (1/3) · 2π(9) = (1/3)(18π) = 6π in
Answer: A = 27π in²; ℓ = 6π in
Example 11.8.2
Finding the Central Angle from Sector Area
  • ProblemA sector of a circle with radius 12 cm has an area of 48π cm². Find the central angle.
  • Solve 48π = (θ/360) · π(12)² 48π = (θ/360) · 144π 48 = 144θ/360 48 = 0.4θ θ = 120°
Answer: Central angle = 120°

Section 11.9

Composite Figures

A composite figure is a region made by combining, removing, or overlapping simpler figures. Real floors, rooms, windows, signs, pools, and architectural features are rarely perfect rectangles or circles. They are combinations of familiar shapes.

Strategy for Composite Figures

Step 1: Identify the basic shapes that make up the composite figure.

Step 2: Decide whether you are adding regions (shapes combined) or subtracting regions (shapes cut out).

Step 3: Calculate the area of each piece separately.

Step 4: Add or subtract as needed.

Step 5: Check that units are correct and the answer is reasonable.

Diagram Instruction

Draw three composite figures side by side: (1) an L-shaped region divisible into two rectangles, (2) a rectangle with a semicircle added on one end, and (3) a large rectangle with a circular hole cut from the interior. In each case, show the dividing lines or the shape being removed.

Example 11.9.1
L-Shaped Region
  • ProblemAn L-shaped room has an overall width of 20 ft and overall height of 15 ft, with a rectangular notch of 8 ft by 7 ft cut from the upper-right corner. Find the area.
  • ThinkLarge rectangle minus the missing corner.
  • Solve A₁ (large rectangle) = 20 × 15 = 300 ft² A₂ (missing corner) = 8 × 7 = 56 ft² A = 300 − 56 = 244 ft²
  • CheckSplit L into two separate rectangles: 20 × 8 = 160 and 12 × 7 = 84. Total = 244. ✓
Answer: 244 ft²
Example 11.9.2
Rectangle with Semicircle
  • ProblemA window is shaped like a rectangle 4 ft wide and 6 ft tall, with a semicircle on top having the same diameter (4 ft). Find the total area. Leave π exact.
  • ThinkSemicircle has diameter 4 ft, so radius = 2 ft.
  • Solve A_rectangle = 4 × 6 = 24 ft² A_semicircle = ½π(2)² = ½(4π) = 2π ft² A_total = (24 + 2π) ft²
  • Check2π ≈ 6.28, so total ≈ 30.28 ft². Reasonable. ✓
Answer: (24 + 2π) ft² ≈ 30.3 ft²
Example 11.9.3
Rectangle with Circular Hole
  • ProblemA square tile is 10 inches on each side. A circular hole of radius 3 inches is cut from the center. Find the remaining area. Leave π exact.
  • Solve A_square = 10² = 100 in² A_circle = π(3)² = 9π in² A_remaining = (100 − 9π) in²
Answer: (100 − 9π) in² ≈ 71.7 in²
Tip — Label Each Region

Label each region before computing. In a composite figure with three or more parts, it is easy to lose track of which area goes where. A quick sketch with region labels prevents most errors.

Section 11.10

Surface Area of Prisms

A prism is a three-dimensional figure with two congruent, parallel polygonal bases connected by rectangular lateral faces. The surface area of a prism is the total area of all its faces: both bases and all lateral faces.

A net is a flat diagram showing all faces of a three-dimensional figure unfolded into a plane. The net of a rectangular prism looks like a cross: a center rectangle surrounded by four more rectangles — two of each dimension.

Diagram Instruction

Draw the net of a rectangular prism. Label the two base rectangles (top and bottom) with dimensions l × w. Label the four lateral face rectangles: two with dimensions l × h and two with dimensions w × h. Show fold lines between adjacent faces.

Surface Area — Rectangular Prism

SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)

General Prism: SA = 2B + Ph    where B = base area, P = base perimeter, h = prism height.

The lateral surface of a prism, when unrolled, forms a rectangle with width = perimeter of base and height = prism height.

Example 11.10.1
Surface Area of a Rectangular Prism
  • ProblemA rectangular box is 10 cm long, 6 cm wide, and 4 cm tall. Find the surface area.
  • Solve SA = 2(lw + lh + wh) = 2(10×6 + 10×4 + 6×4) = 2(60 + 40 + 24) = 2(124) = 248 cm²
Answer: 248 cm²
Example 11.10.2
Surface Area of a Triangular Prism
  • ProblemA triangular prism has an equilateral triangular base with side length 6 ft and triangle height 3√3 ft. The prism height is 10 ft. Find the surface area. Leave radical exact.
  • ThinkFind base area B and base perimeter P, then use SA = 2B + Ph.
  • Solve B = ½(6)(3√3) = 9√3 ft² P = 3 × 6 = 18 ft SA = 2(9√3) + (18)(10) = 18√3 + 180 ft²
  • Check18√3 ≈ 31.2, so SA ≈ 211.2 ft². Reasonable. ✓
Answer: (180 + 18√3) ft² ≈ 211.2 ft²
Section 11.11

Surface Area of Cylinders

A cylinder has two congruent circular bases connected by a curved lateral surface. When the lateral surface is unrolled, it becomes a rectangle. The rectangle's width equals the circumference of the base circle (2πr) and its height equals the cylinder height h.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a cylinder and beside it its net: two circles and a rectangle. Label the rectangle's width as 2πr (the circumference) and its height as h. Label the circles with radius r. Show the fold lines connecting each circle to the rectangle's long edges.

Surface Area of a Cylinder

SA = 2πr² + 2πrh

2πr² = both circular bases. 2πrh = lateral surface.

Lateral surface only: SA_lateral = 2πrh

Example 11.11.1
Surface Area of a Cylinder
  • ProblemA cylinder has a radius of 5 m and a height of 12 m. Find the exact total surface area.
  • Solve SA = 2π(5)² + 2π(5)(12) = 2π(25) + 2π(60) = 50π + 120π = 170π m²
  • Check170π ≈ 534.1 m². Reasonable for a large cylinder. ✓
Answer: 170π m² ≈ 534.1 m²
Student Warning — Total vs. Lateral Surface Area

Some problems ask for total surface area (both bases plus lateral). Others ask for lateral surface area only (no bases). Read the problem carefully before choosing the formula.

Section 11.12

Surface Area of Pyramids and Cones

Pyramids

A pyramid has a polygonal base and triangular lateral faces that meet at a single apex. The slant height ℓ is the distance from the apex to the midpoint of a base edge, measured along a lateral face — not the vertical height h of the pyramid.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a square pyramid. Label the vertical height h from the apex down to the center of the base. Label the slant height ℓ from the apex down the face to the midpoint of a base edge. Show clearly that h and ℓ are different lengths. Mark the right angle where h meets the base.

Surface Area of a Regular Pyramid

SA = B + ½Pℓ

B = base area. P = base perimeter. ℓ = slant height.

Each triangular lateral face has area ½ · (side) · ℓ. Adding all n faces gives ½Pℓ.

Finding slant height: ℓ = √(h² + a²)    where a = apothem of the base.

Example 11.12.1
Surface Area of a Square Pyramid
  • ProblemA square pyramid has base side 8 ft and slant height 10 ft. Find the total surface area.
  • Solve B = 8² = 64 ft² P = 4 × 8 = 32 ft SA = 64 + ½(32)(10) = 64 + 160 = 224 ft²
Answer: 224 ft²
Example 11.12.2
Finding Slant Height First
  • ProblemA square pyramid has base side 6 m and vertical height 4 m. Find the slant height and surface area.
  • ThinkApothem of square base with side 6 m: a = 6/2 = 3 m. Find ℓ using the Pythagorean Theorem.
  • Solve ℓ = √(h² + a²) = √(4² + 3²) = √(16 + 9) = √25 = 5 m B = 6² = 36 m² P = 4 × 6 = 24 m SA = 36 + ½(24)(5) = 36 + 60 = 96 m²
Answer: ℓ = 5 m; SA = 96 m²

Cones

A cone has a circular base and a lateral surface tapering to a single apex. The slant height ℓ is the distance from the apex to any point on the base edge along the lateral surface. The lateral surface, when unrolled, forms a sector of a circle whose arc length equals the base circumference 2πr — giving lateral area πrℓ.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a right cone. Label the apex at the top. Label vertical height h from apex to center of base. Label radius r of the base. Label slant height ℓ from apex to base edge. Show that h, r, and ℓ form a right triangle with the right angle at the center of the base.

Surface Area of a Cone

SA = πr² + πrℓ

πr² = circular base. πrℓ = lateral surface.

Slant height: ℓ = √(h² + r²)

Example 11.12.3
Surface Area of a Cone (Slant Height Given)
  • ProblemA cone has base radius 6 cm and slant height 10 cm. Find the exact total surface area.
  • SolveSA = π(6)² + π(6)(10) = 36π + 60π = 96π cm²
Answer: 96π cm² ≈ 301.6 cm²
Example 11.12.4
Surface Area of a Cone (Vertical Height Given)
  • ProblemA cone has base radius 5 in and vertical height 12 in. Find the slant height and exact total surface area.
  • Slant heightℓ = √(12² + 5²) = √(144 + 25) = √169 = 13 in
  • Surface areaSA = π(5)² + π(5)(13) = 25π + 65π = 90π in²
Answer: ℓ = 13 in; SA = 90π in² ≈ 282.7 in²

Section 11.13

Surface Area in Real Situations

Real surface area problems require reading carefully to decide which faces to include. A cardboard box with no lid omits the top face. A pipe open at both ends uses only the lateral surface. A tank sitting on the ground needs the lateral surface and top base painted, but not the bottom. Identifying which faces matter is as important as calculating them correctly.

Example 11.13.1
Painting a Storage Silo
  • ProblemA cylindrical grain silo has radius 8 ft and height 30 ft. It sits on a concrete pad (bottom not painted) and has a flat circular top cap (painted). Find the paintable surface area.
  • ThinkInclude lateral surface and top circle only.
  • Solve SA_lateral = 2π(8)(30) = 480π ft² A_top = π(8)² = 64π ft² SA_total = 480π + 64π = 544π ft²
Answer: 544π ft² ≈ 1,709.5 ft²
Example 11.13.2
Wrapping Paper for a Gift Box

Section 11.14

Common Area and Surface Area Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Using the Slant Side as the Height
Area of parallelogram with base 9 and slant side 7: A = 9 × 7 = 63 cm²

The height must be the perpendicular distance between the parallel sides. The slant side is always longer, so using it produces an area that is too large. Draw the perpendicular height and use that value.

Mistake 2 — Forgetting to Square the Units
A = 36 cm    (missing the square)

Area is always measured in squared units. Writing "36 cm" instead of "36 cm²" is an incomplete and incorrect answer. Every area calculation must end with a squared unit.

Mistake 3 — Confusing Diameter and Radius
Circle with diameter 10: A = π(10)² = 100π

The formula A = πr² uses the radius. If the diameter is 10, then r = 5, and A = π(5)² = 25π. Using the diameter instead of the radius gives an answer four times too large.

Mistake 4 — Omitting the ½ in Triangle and Kite Formulas
Triangle with base 8 and height 5: A = 8 × 5 = 40

A triangle is half a parallelogram. Always include the ½: A = ½(8)(5) = 20. The same applies to kite and rhombus diagonal formulas: A = ½d₁d₂.

Mistake 5 — Adding Side Lengths to Find Area (Confusing Perimeter and Area)
Area of equilateral triangle with side 6: A = 6 + 6 + 6 = 18

That computes the perimeter, not the area. Area requires the perpendicular height: A = ½(6)(3√3) = 9√3. Perimeter and area use different formulas and have different units.

Mistake 6 — Confusing Vertical Height and Slant Height in Pyramids/Cones
Cone with radius 5 and vertical height 12: SA = π(5)² + π(5)(12) = 85π

Surface area uses the slant height ℓ, not the vertical height h. Find the slant height first: ℓ = √(h² + r²) = √(144 + 25) = 13. Then SA = π(5)² + π(5)(13) = 90π.

Mistake 7 — Forgetting Both Bases in Prism/Cylinder Surface Area
Cylinder with r = 4 and h = 10: SA = 2π(4)(10) = 80π    (lateral only)

Total surface area includes both circular bases: SA = 2π(4)² + 2π(4)(10) = 32π + 80π = 112π. Only use the lateral formula when the problem explicitly asks for lateral surface area only.

Mistake 8 — Mixing Units Within a Problem
Rectangle 2 feet by 18 inches: A = 2 × 18 = 36    (mixing feet and inches)

Convert all measurements to the same unit before computing. Either convert 2 ft to 24 inches: A = 24 × 18 = 432 in², or convert 18 in to 1.5 ft: A = 2 × 1.5 = 3 ft².

Chapter 11 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1Area measures two-dimensional space enclosed by a boundary. It is always expressed in squared units. Perimeter and area are different quantities with different units and different formulas.
  • Sec 11.2Rectangles and parallelograms share the formula A = bh, but the height must always be perpendicular to the base — never the slant side of a parallelogram.
  • Sec 11.3A triangle is half a parallelogram: A = ½bh. The height is perpendicular to the base and may fall outside the triangle for obtuse triangles.
  • Sec 11.4Trapezoid: A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h (average base times height). Kite: A = ½d₁d₂ (half the product of the diagonals).
  • Sec 11.5A rhombus uses A = bh (base-height) or A = ½d₁d₂ (diagonal formula). Use whichever measurements are given.
  • Sec 11.6Regular polygons: A = ½Pa, where P is the perimeter and a is the apothem. Derived by dividing the polygon into n congruent triangles from the center.
  • Sec 11.7Circles: C = 2πr (circumference); A = πr² (area). Pi is the constant ratio of circumference to diameter. Always use the radius in A = πr².
  • Sec 11.8Sectors: arc length ℓ = (θ/360)·2πr; sector area A = (θ/360)·πr². The central angle fraction determines the sector's share of the full circle.
  • Sec 11.9Composite figures are solved by identifying basic shapes, then adding or subtracting their areas. Label each region before computing.
  • Sec 11.10Surface area of prisms: SA = 2B + Ph. The lateral surface, when unrolled, forms a rectangle with width equal to the base perimeter and height equal to the prism height.
  • Sec 11.11Surface area of a cylinder: SA = 2πr² + 2πrh. The unrolled lateral surface is a rectangle with width 2πr and height h.
  • Sec 11.12Regular pyramid: SA = B + ½Pℓ. Cone: SA = πr² + πrℓ. In both, ℓ is the slant height, found via ℓ = √(h² + a²) for pyramids and ℓ = √(h² + r²) for cones.
  • Sec 11.13Real problems require reading carefully to identify which faces are relevant. Not every surface area problem includes all faces of a figure.
  • Sec 11.14Eight recurring mistakes: slant side as height; omitting squared units; confusing diameter and radius; dropping the ½; confusing perimeter and area; mixing vertical and slant height; forgetting both bases; mismatched units.

Bridge Note — Looking Ahead
Chapter 12: Volume and Three-Dimensional Geometry

Chapter 11 prepared you for two closely related topics in Chapter 12. Volume is the three-dimensional counterpart of area: just as area measures the flat space inside a boundary, volume measures the three-dimensional space inside an enclosed solid. The base area B you computed for surface area becomes the starting point for volume formulas: V = Bh for prisms and cylinders, V = ⅓Bh for pyramids and cones.

Scaling reveals how area and volume change when a figure is enlarged or reduced. When all lengths of a figure are multiplied by a scale factor k, all areas scale by k² and all volumes scale by k³. This is why a box twice as long, wide, and tall holds eight times the volume, not twice — and why it needs four times the wrapping paper, not twice. Keep the formulas from Chapter 11 handy throughout Chapter 12.

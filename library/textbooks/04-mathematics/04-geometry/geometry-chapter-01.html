Chapter 1 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter is the opening gateway into geometry. Before students can work with triangles, circles, proof, coordinates, trigonometry, or transformations, they need a clear picture of what geometry is and why it matters. Geometry is not a collection of formulas for named shapes. It is a logical system — a precise language for describing space and reasoning about relationships within it.

Geometry studies shape, size, space, position, measurement, and logical relationships. It is one of the oldest branches of mathematics, and it remains one of the most useful. Architects design buildings with it. Engineers analyze forces with it. Physicists describe motion through it. Surveyors measure land with it. Artists use it for perspective and proportion. Programmers use it to build computer graphics and games. Navigation systems use it to calculate routes.

But geometry is also more than a practical tool. Its conclusions do not come from guessing or measuring with a ruler. They follow by reasoning from definitions, postulates, and previously established facts. This chapter introduces the fundamental objects of geometry — points, lines, planes, segments, rays, and angles — and the notation and habits of thought that will support proof, algebraic reasoning, and spatial analysis throughout the entire course.

Opening Question

You are an architect designing a new public library. Every measurement must be exact. Every angle must be correct. Every line must be straight, or deliberately curved, by a specific amount. Before you can draw a single plan, you need a language for describing space precisely enough that your plans can be read and built correctly by others. What language do you use? The answer is geometry — and this chapter introduces that language from the ground up.

Section 1.1

What Geometry Studies

Geometry comes from Greek roots meaning earth measurement. Ancient Egyptians and Babylonians used geometric ideas to survey land, build pyramids, and study astronomy. Greek mathematicians, especially Euclid around 300 BCE, organized these ideas into a logical system built on definitions, postulates, and proofs. That system, refined and expanded over centuries, is what students study today. Modern geometry studies six interconnected things.

What Geometry StudiesWhat It AsksReal-World Example
ShapeWhat forms do figures have? How do shapes relate?The triangular trusses in a bridge
SizeHow long, wide, or large is a figure?The area of a floor to be tiled
PositionWhere is a point? How far apart are objects?GPS coordinates of two cities
SpaceWhat is the structure of flat surfaces and solids?The volume of a storage tank
MeasurementWhat numbers describe geometric quantities?The angle of a solar panel for maximum efficiency
Logical relationshipsWhy must a conclusion follow from given information?Proving that two support cables are equal length
Student Note

This course is called algebra-based geometry for a reason. When two angles are supplementary, writing and solving an equation is algebra. When a triangle's sides satisfy the Pythagorean Theorem, finding a missing side uses algebraic operations. Expect to write equations, solve for variables, work with radicals, use proportions, and apply coordinate methods throughout every chapter — these are not distractions from geometry. They are part of how geometry is done.

Section 1.2

Geometry as Measurement and Reasoning

Geometry has two sides that belong together: measurement and reasoning. Measurement assigns numbers to geometric quantities — the length of a segment is a number, the measure of an angle is a number, area is a number, volume is a number. Reasoning draws logical conclusions from information — if two lines are parallel and a third crosses them, certain angles must be equal; if three sides of one triangle match three sides of another, the triangles are congruent. These conclusions do not come from guessing. They follow from definitions and established theorems.

Both sides matter. A student who can measure but cannot reason will struggle with proof. A student who can reason but ignores measurement will miss the connection to real applications. This textbook develops both sides together throughout every chapter.

Section 1.3

Points, Lines, Planes, and Space

Geometry begins with three undefined terms: point, line, and plane. They are called undefined not because they have no meaning, but because they are so fundamental that they cannot be built from simpler geometric ideas. Instead, they are described intuitively and used to define everything else.

Point
An exact location in space
No size, no length, no width, no thickness. Labeled with a capital letter. Shown as a small dot in diagrams. Example: point A.
Line
A straight path, both directions forever
No width, no thickness. One-dimensional. Arrows at both ends. Named by a lowercase letter or two points. Example: line ↔AB.
Plane
A flat surface, all directions forever
No thickness. Two-dimensional. Drawn as a parallelogram shape. Named with a capital script letter. Example: plane P.
Space
The set of all points
Three-dimensional. Contains all geometric figures. Later chapters study solids, volume, and three-dimensional relationships in space.
Postulates 1-1 through 1-3

Postulate 1-1: Through any two points, there is exactly one line.

Postulate 1-2: Through any three non-collinear points, there is exactly one plane.

Postulate 1-3: If two lines intersect, they intersect in exactly one point.

Student Warning

Points, lines, and planes are idealized objects. Real pencil marks have width. Real surfaces have thickness. Real walls end. In geometry, we reason about perfect idealized versions. The physical world approximates the geometry, but geometry itself is more precise.

Section 1.4

Segments, Rays, and Lines

A full line extends forever in both directions. Geometry also uses parts of lines with different properties: segments (bounded, with two endpoints) and rays (one endpoint, extending forever in one direction).

FigureDefinitionNotationArrowsDefinite Length?
Line ABExtends in both directions forever↔ABBoth endsNo — infinite
Segment ABTwo endpoints and all points betweenAB̄Neither endYes
Ray ABOne endpoint; extends one direction forever→ABOne end (not the endpoint)No — infinite
Segment AB (blue) is bounded. Ray AB (gold) starts at A and extends forever. Line AB (teal) extends forever in both directions.
Student Note

The order of letters matters for rays but not segments. Ray →AB starts at A and goes through B. Ray →BA starts at B and goes through A — a completely different ray pointing the opposite direction. Segment AB̄ and segment BĀ are the same figure.

Section 1.5

Collinearity and Coplanarity

Points are collinear if they all lie on the same line. Any two points are always collinear — the interesting question is whether a third point also lies on that same line. Points are coplanar if they lie in the same plane. Any three non-collinear points are always coplanar; the question is whether additional points share their plane.

Two lines in the same plane either intersect or are parallel. Two lines in different planes that neither intersect nor are parallel are skew lines — a concept that will return in Chapter 12 when we study three-dimensional figures.

Example 1.5.1
Identifying Collinear and Coplanar Points
  • ProblemPoints A, B, C, D, E are shown. Points A, B, C are on line ℓ. Points D, E are not on ℓ. Points A, B, D are in plane P. Which points are collinear? Which are coplanar with A and B?
  • ThinkCollinear means on the same line. Coplanar means in the same plane. Use only the stated information — do not read into the diagram.
  • CollinearA, B, C are on line ℓ → collinear. D and E are not on ℓ → not collinear with A, B, C.
  • CoplanarA, B, D are in plane P → coplanar. The problem does not state that C or E are in plane P.
Collinear: A, B, C  |  Coplanar (with A and B): A, B, D — in plane P

Section 1.6

Distance and Length

The distance between two points is the length of the segment connecting them — always a non-negative number. The Ruler Postulate guarantees that distances can be measured consistently along any line.

Postulate 1-4 — Ruler Postulate

The points on a line can be matched one-to-one with the real numbers so that the distance between any two points equals the absolute value of the difference of their coordinates:

AB = |a − b|

Postulate 1-5 — Segment Addition Postulate

If B is between A and C on a line, then:

AB + BC = AC

The length of the whole equals the sum of its two parts. This postulate requires that B is actually between A and C on the same line.

B is between A and C. AB + BC = AC.
Example 1.6.1
Using the Segment Addition Postulate
  • ProblemPoints A, B, C are collinear with B between A and C. If AB = 14 and AC = 31, find BC.
  • RepresentAB + BC = AC → 14 + BC = 31
  • SolveBC = 31 − 14 = 17
  • Check14 + 17 = 31 = AC ✓
Answer: BC = 17
Example 1.6.2
Segment Addition with Algebra
  • ProblemPoints P, Q, R are collinear with Q between P and R. PQ = 2x + 3, QR = x + 7, PR = 28. Find x, PQ, and QR.
  • RepresentPQ + QR = PR (2x + 3) + (x + 7) = 28
  • Solve3x + 10 = 28 3x = 18 → x = 6 PQ = 2(6) + 3 = 15 | QR = 6 + 7 = 13
  • Check15 + 13 = 28 = PR ✓
Answer: x = 6, PQ = 15, QR = 13
Section 1.7

Midpoints and Segment Bisectors

Midpoint Definition

The midpoint of a segment is the point on the segment that divides it into two segments of equal length. If M is the midpoint of AB̄, then:

AM = MB = AB / 2

A segment bisector is any line, segment, ray, or plane that passes through the midpoint of a segment.

Diagram Instruction

Draw segment AB. Mark point M at its exact center. Place matching single tick marks on AM and MB to indicate they are equal. If a second segment CD crosses AB at M, draw it and label M as the intersection — this makes CD a bisector of AB.

Example 1.7.1
Finding a Midpoint with Algebra
  • ProblemM is the midpoint of RS̄. RM = 4x − 1 and MS = 2x + 9. Find RM, MS, and RS.
  • ThinkBecause M is the midpoint, RM = MS. Set the expressions equal.
  • Solve4x − 1 = 2x + 9 2x = 10 → x = 5 RM = 4(5) − 1 = 19 | MS = 2(5) + 9 = 19 | RS = 38
  • CheckRM = MS = 19 ✓   RM + MS = 38 = RS ✓
Answer: RM = 19, MS = 19, RS = 38

Section 1.8

Angles and Angle Measure

An angle is formed by two rays that share a common endpoint. The shared endpoint is the vertex. The two rays are the sides. An angle is named by its vertex alone (when unambiguous), by three points with the vertex in the middle, or by a number placed inside the angle.

Three Ways to Name an Angle

By vertex only (no ambiguity): ∠B

By three points, vertex in middle: ∠ABC or ∠CBA

By a number inside the angle: ∠1

The measure of angle ABC is written m∠ABC and is given in degrees. Never write ∠ABC = 45°. Write m∠ABC = 45°.

Angle ABC: vertex B, sides ray BA and ray BC. The arc marks the angle opening.
Postulate 1-7 — Angle Addition Postulate

If point D is in the interior of ∠ABC, then:

m∠ABD + m∠DBC = m∠ABC

The measure of the whole angle equals the sum of its two parts. This parallels the Segment Addition Postulate exactly.

Example 1.8.1
Angle Addition with Algebra
  • ProblemRay PQ is in the interior of ∠MPR. m∠MPQ = (3x + 5)°, m∠QPR = (x + 15)°, m∠MPR = 60°. Find x and both smaller angles.
  • Representm∠MPQ + m∠QPR = m∠MPR (3x + 5) + (x + 15) = 60
  • Solve4x + 20 = 60 → 4x = 40 → x = 10 m∠MPQ = 3(10) + 5 = 35° m∠QPR = 10 + 15 = 25°
  • Check35 + 25 = 60 ✓
Answer: x = 10, m∠MPQ = 35°, m∠QPR = 25°
Section 1.9

Angle Types

Acute Angle
0° < m∠ < 90°
A "sharp" or narrow angle. Examples: corner of a triangle that comes to a narrow peak, tip of a wedge, the angle of a steep roof.
Right Angle
m∠ = 90° exactly
Marked in diagrams with a small square at the vertex — not an arc. Corners of rooms, edges of furniture, perpendicular lines.
Obtuse Angle
90° < m∠ < 180°
Wider than a right angle but not yet a straight line. Examples: an open book laid flat, an obtuse triangle's large interior angle.
Straight Angle
m∠ = 180° exactly
The two sides form a straight line, pointing in exactly opposite directions. Same as a half-rotation; two opposite rays from a common point.
The four angle types. A right angle is always marked with a square, never an arc — this is a required convention in geometry diagrams.
Student Warning

A right angle must measure exactly 90°. An angle that looks close to 90° in a diagram is not a right angle unless it carries the small square mark, or the problem explicitly states m∠ = 90°. Never assume a right angle from appearance alone.

Section 1.10

Congruent Segments and Congruent Angles

Two figures are congruent if they have the same shape and the same size. For segments, congruence means equal length. For angles, congruence means equal measure. The notation and the distinction between congruence and equality are among the most important habits in geometry.

ConceptFigure StatementNumeric EquivalentDiagram Mark
Congruent segmentsAB̄ ≅ CD̄AB = CDMatching tick marks
Congruent angles∠A ≅ ∠Bm∠A = m∠BMatching arc marks
Student Warning — The Most Common Proof Error

Do not write AB̄ = CD̄ or ∠A = ∠B. Segments and angles are geometric figures, not numbers. Equality applies to numbers; congruence applies to figures. Write AB̄ ≅ CD̄ when making a geometric statement, and AB = CD when equating their numeric lengths. Getting this distinction right is essential in every proof from Chapter 2 onward.

Example 1.10.1
Reading Congruence from a Diagram
  • ProblemA diagram shows triangle ABC with one tick mark on AB̄ and one tick mark on ĀC, and one arc mark on ∠B and one arc mark on ∠C. What congruences does the diagram indicate?
  • ThinkMatching tick marks → congruent segments. Matching arc marks → congruent angles.
Diagram indicates: AB̄ ≅ ĀC  and  ∠B ≅ ∠C

Section 1.11

Diagrams, Labels, and Geometric Notation

Diagrams convey geometric structure — not measurements. Reading a geometry textbook means reading text and diagrams together. A good diagram shows figures, labels, and the marks that indicate specific properties. A diagram without those marks tells you the general structure but not the specific measurements.

What You Can and Cannot Assume from a Diagram

You may assume: that labeled points are where they appear; that right angle squares indicate exactly 90°; that tick marks and arc marks are accurate. You may not assume: that segments that look equal are equal; that angles that look equal are equal; that lines that look parallel are parallel. Never conclude from appearance alone — only from marks or stated conditions.

Section 1.12

Geometry in Real Situations

Example 1.12.1 — Urban Planning
Distance on a Straight Road
  • ProblemThree bus stops A, B, C lie in order on a straight road. The distance from A to C is 415 feet and from A to B is 240 feet. How far is it from B to C?
  • RepresentAB + BC = AC → 240 + BC = 415
  • SolveBC = 415 − 240 = 175 feet
  • Check240 + 175 = 415 ✓
Answer: The distance from B to C is 175 feet.
Example 1.12.2 — Carpentry
Angle at a Picture Frame Corner
  • ProblemA picture frame corner must be a right angle. The carpenter measures the two parts as (4x + 5)° and (2x − 1)°. Together they must equal 90°. Find x and each angle.
  • Represent(4x + 5) + (2x − 1) = 90
  • Solve6x + 4 = 90 → 6x = 86 → x = 43/3 ≈ 14.3° m∠1 ≈ 62.3° | m∠2 ≈ 27.7°
  • Check62.3 + 27.7 = 90 ✓
Answer: m∠1 ≈ 62.3° and m∠2 ≈ 27.7°
Section 1.13

Common Early Geometry Mistakes

Six mistakes appear repeatedly in early geometry. Recognizing them now prevents them from becoming habits.

MistakeThe ErrorThe Correction
1 — Figure vs. Measure∠A = 90° or AB̄ = 8Write m∠A = 90° and AB = 8. ∠A is a figure; m∠A is its degree measure.
2 — Congruence vs. EqualityAB̄ = CD̄ or ∠A = ∠BWrite AB̄ ≅ CD̄ (figures) or AB = CD (numbers). Not both.
3 — Assuming from Diagrams"These look equal so they must be equal."Only conclude equality when tick marks appear or the problem states it explicitly.
4 — Misidentifying BetweennessApplying AB + BC = AC when B is not between A and C on the same line.Verify betweenness from the diagram before writing the postulate equation.
5 — Ambiguous Angle NotationWriting ∠B when four rays meet at vertex B.Always use three letters when the vertex is shared: ∠ABC, ∠ABD, etc.
6 — Forgetting Units"The answer is 12." (No unit given.)Write "12 feet" or "12°" or "12 cm." Include the unit in every measurement answer.
Section 1.14

Preparing for Proof and Algebraic Geometry

Chapter 1 has built the vocabulary and tools the rest of the book will use. The notation learned in Section 1.11 will be used in every proof step for the entire course. Chapter 1 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 1.1Geometry studies shape, size, space, position, measurement, and logical relationships. It connects to algebra, construction, physics, engineering, art, navigation, computer graphics, and design. Algebra is a natural partner throughout every chapter.
  • Sec 1.2Geometry has two inseparable sides: measurement (assigning numbers to geometric quantities) and reasoning (drawing logical conclusions from information). Both are developed together throughout this book.
  • Sec 1.3The three undefined terms are point, line, and plane. Space is the set of all points. Postulates 1-1 through 1-3 describe how these objects behave. Physical objects approximate geometric ideals but are not identical to them.
  • Sec 1.4A segment has two endpoints and a definite length. A ray has one endpoint and extends forever in one direction. A line has no endpoints. Notation: AB̄ (segment), →AB (ray starts at A), ↔AB (line). The order of letters matters for rays.
  • Sec 1.5Points are collinear if on the same line; coplanar if in the same plane. Two lines in different planes that neither intersect nor are parallel are skew. Collinearity and coplanarity are determined from marks or stated information, not from appearance.
  • Sec 1.6The Ruler Postulate: AB = |a − b|. The Segment Addition Postulate: if B is between A and C, then AB + BC = AC. This postulate enables algebraic equations whenever two of the three lengths are expressed in terms of a variable.
  • Sec 1.7The midpoint of AB̄ divides it into two equal halves: AM = MB = AB/2. A bisector passes through the midpoint. Midpoint problems are solved by setting the two halves equal and solving for the variable.
  • Sec 1.8An angle is formed by two rays with a common endpoint (vertex). Measure is in degrees; m∠ABC ≠ ∠ABC. The Angle Addition Postulate: if D is interior to ∠ABC, then m∠ABD + m∠DBC = m∠ABC. This enables algebraic angle problems.
  • Sec 1.9Acute: 0°–90°. Right: exactly 90° (marked with a square). Obtuse: 90°–180°. Straight: exactly 180°. Right angles must be confirmed by the square mark or stated measure — never assumed from appearance.
  • Sec 1.10Congruent segments have equal lengths: AB̄ ≅ CD̄ ↔ AB = CD. Congruent angles have equal measures: ∠A ≅ ∠B ↔ m∠A = m∠B. Diagrams use tick marks for congruent segments and arc marks for congruent angles.
  • Sec 1.11Diagrams convey structure using marks — tick marks, arc marks, right angle squares. Only conclude what is marked or stated. The notation reference table covers segments, rays, lines, angles, measures, congruence, and triangles.
  • Sec 1.12Geometry applies to urban planning (distance), carpentry (angles), architecture (right angles), art (angle sums), and more. The Segment Addition and Angle Addition Postulates are the primary tools for real-situation problems in Chapter 1.
  • Sec 1.13Six named mistakes: figure vs. measure; congruence vs. equality; assuming from diagrams; misidentifying betweenness; ambiguous angle notation; forgetting units. Recognizing these early prevents them from becoming proof-breaking habits.
  • Sec 1.14All seven postulates and the notation from Chapter 1 appear immediately in Chapter 2 proofs and throughout every later chapter. Chapter 2 adds conditional statements, deductive reasoning, properties of equality, and two-column proof structure.

Bridge Note — Looking Ahead
Chapter 2: Logic, Definitions, and Proof

Chapter 1 has built the language of geometry: the objects (points, lines, planes, segments, rays, angles), the measurements (distance, angle measure), the relationships (congruence, midpoint, bisector), the notation (AB̄, →AB, ↔AB, ∠ABC, m∠ABC), and the postulates that give all of these precise meaning. These are not background skills to be set aside. They are the active vocabulary of geometry, used in every chapter that follows.

Chapter 2 introduces the reasoning engine that drives geometry. Where Chapter 1 described what the objects are, Chapter 2 asks: how do we know our conclusions are true? It introduces definitions, conditional statements, converse and contrapositive, counterexamples, deductive reasoning, the properties of equality and congruence, algebraic proofs, and — at the center of it all — two-column geometric proof. Proof is not a ritual to be memorized. It is organized explanation: a way to show, step by definite step, why a conclusion must follow from what is given. Students who understand proof will find every later chapter richer, more connected, and more satisfying.

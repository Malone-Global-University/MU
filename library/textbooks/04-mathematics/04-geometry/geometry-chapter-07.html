Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Right triangles are among the most important figures in all of mathematics. They appear in architecture, engineering, navigation, physics, computer graphics, surveying, and design. They connect algebra, measurement, coordinate geometry, and trigonometry in ways that no other triangle can.

In Chapter 6, you studied similarity and proportional reasoning. You learned that similar triangles have equal angle measures and proportional side lengths, and that this proportionality can be used to find unknown distances. Chapter 7 builds directly on that foundation. Right triangles are a special case of triangles, but their special structure — one angle measuring exactly 90 degrees — gives them remarkable properties that allow precise calculation.

This chapter will develop those properties carefully. You will prove and apply the Pythagorean Theorem, use its converse to identify right triangles, work with the special patterns of 45-45-90 and 30-60-90 triangles, find distances on the coordinate plane, simplify radical expressions that arise from triangle measurement, and explore how the altitude of a right triangle creates geometric mean relationships among the sides.

Every idea in this chapter is a bridge. The Pythagorean Theorem bridges algebra and geometry. Distance on the coordinate plane bridges measurement and coordinates. Special right triangles bridge exact ratio patterns and algebraic simplification. Geometric mean bridges proportionality and right-triangle structure. Together, these ideas prepare you for right-triangle trigonometry in Chapter 8, coordinate geometry in Chapter 13, and applications throughout physics, engineering, and design.

Opening Question

A cable needs to be attached from the top of a 30-foot vertical wall to a point on the ground that is 40 feet from the base of the wall. How long must the cable be? Before you read further, think about what you already know that might help you solve this problem. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer this question exactly — and many more like it.

Why This Chapter Matters

Measurement and design. Any time you need to find a length that cannot be measured directly — a diagonal, a height, a distance across a gap — a right triangle often provides the path. Builders, engineers, surveyors, and designers use right-triangle relationships constantly.

Coordinates and distance. The formula for distance between two points on the coordinate plane comes directly from the Pythagorean Theorem. Every distance calculation in coordinate geometry depends on what you will learn in this chapter.

Trigonometry. Sine, cosine, and tangent — the fundamental tools of trigonometry — are defined using the ratios of sides in right triangles. Chapter 8 begins exactly where Chapter 7 ends.

Physics and science. Vector components, projectile motion, forces on inclined planes, optics, and wave behavior all rely on right-triangle relationships.

Algebra and radicals. Right triangles produce square roots naturally. Working with exact radical lengths rather than decimal approximations is an important algebraic skill that this chapter develops.

Section 7.1

Right Triangles

A right triangle is a triangle with exactly one angle measuring 90 degrees. That 90-degree angle is called the right angle, and it is typically marked in diagrams with a small square symbol at the vertex.

Every right triangle has three sides. The two sides that meet at the right angle are called the legs. The side that is opposite the right angle is called the hypotenuse. The hypotenuse is always the longest side of a right triangle, because the largest side is always opposite the largest angle, and the right angle is always the largest angle in a right triangle.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a right triangle. Label the right angle vertex C, and place the right angle square at C. Label the other two vertices A and B. The side AC is one leg. The side BC is the other leg. The side AB, opposite the right angle at C, is the hypotenuse. Mark the hypotenuse with a small label c along the side, and label the legs a and b opposite their respective vertices.

Key Rule

A right triangle can be oriented in many positions. What matters is which angle measures 90 degrees and which side is opposite that angle. Always identify the right angle first, and then identify the hypotenuse as the side that is opposite it.

Student Note — Algebra in Right Triangles

In a right triangle, you will use algebra to find missing sides regularly. The process always involves setting up an equation, substituting the known values, and solving for the unknown. Keep track of which side is the hypotenuse, because the Pythagorean Theorem treats the hypotenuse differently from the legs.

Section 7.2

The Pythagorean Theorem

Theorem
The Pythagorean Theorem
In a right triangle with legs of length a and b and hypotenuse of length c:
a² + b² = c²
This equation holds for every right triangle without exception. If you square both leg lengths and add the results, you always get the square of the hypotenuse length.
Geometric Interpretation

Draw a right triangle with legs of length 3 and 4 and hypotenuse of length 5. On each side, draw a square. The square on the leg of length 3 has area 9. The square on the leg of length 4 has area 16. The square on the hypotenuse has area 25. Label each square with its area. Observe that 9 + 16 = 25, confirming the theorem. The theorem literally connects the areas of squares built on the triangle's sides.

Proof of the Pythagorean Theorem
Proof by area — arranging four copies of the right triangle in a large square
GivenA right triangle with legs a and b and hypotenuse c.
Provea² + b² = c²
PlanBuild a large square with side length (a + b). Arrange four congruent copies of the right triangle inside it so that their hypotenuses form a tilted inner square of side length c. Compute the total area two ways and set them equal.
Solve Large square area: (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
Four triangles total area: 4 · (½ab) = 2ab
Inner tilted square area: c²
Setting equal: c² + 2ab = a² + 2ab + b²
Subtract 2ab: c² = a² + b² ∎
ReasoningBoth expressions describe the same total area of the large square. Setting them equal and simplifying isolates c², giving the theorem.
Example 7.2.1
Finding the Hypotenuse
  • ProblemA right triangle has legs of length 6 and 8. Find the hypotenuse.
  • ThinkI know both legs. I need the hypotenuse. Use a² + b² = c² with a = 6, b = 8.
  • Solve6² + 8² = c² 36 + 64 = c² 100 = c² c = √100 = 10
  • Check6² + 8² = 36 + 64 = 100 = 10² ✓
Answer: The hypotenuse is 10.
Example 7.2.2
Finding a Missing Leg
  • ProblemA right triangle has one leg of length 5 and a hypotenuse of length 13. Find the other leg.
  • ThinkI know one leg and the hypotenuse. Use a² + b² = c² with a = 5, c = 13, and solve for b.
  • Solve5² + b² = 13² 25 + b² = 169 b² = 144 b = 12
  • Check5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13² ✓
Answer: The missing leg is 12.
Example 7.2.3
A Result That Is Not a Whole Number
  • ProblemA right triangle has legs of length 3 and 7. Find the hypotenuse in simplest radical form.
  • ThinkUse a² + b² = c². The result may not be a perfect square.
  • Solve3² + 7² = c² 9 + 49 = c² 58 = c² c = √58
  • Check58 = 2 × 29. No perfect square factors, so √58 is already in simplest form. ✓
Answer: The hypotenuse is √58.
Student Note — Exact vs. Approximate

Not every right triangle produces a whole-number hypotenuse. When the result is not a perfect square, leave the answer in radical form unless a decimal approximation is specifically requested. Radical form is exact; a decimal approximation introduces rounding error.

Opening Question — Solved
The 30-foot wall and 40-foot cable
  • SetupThe wall is vertical and the ground is horizontal, so they form a right angle. The cable is the hypotenuse. Legs: 30 and 40.
  • Solve30² + 40² = c² 900 + 1600 = c² 2500 = c² c = 50
Answer: The cable must be 50 feet long.
Section 7.3

The Converse of the Pythagorean Theorem

Converse and Triangle Classification

For three side lengths a, b, c where c is the longest side:

a² + b² = c² → Right triangle

a² + b² > c² → Acute triangle

a² + b² < c² → Obtuse triangle

As the angle opposite side c increases above 90°, c gets longer and c² grows larger than a² + b². As the angle decreases below 90°, c gets shorter and c² falls below a² + b². This reasoning connects to the Law of Cosines, which you will study in Trigonometry and Precalculus.

Example 7.3.1
Identifying Triangle Types from Side Lengths
  • ProblemClassify each set of sides: (a) 7, 24, 25   (b) 5, 7, 9   (c) 4, 5, 8
  • ThinkIdentify the longest side as c. Compute a² + b² and c² and compare.
  • Solve (a)c = 25 a² + b² = 49 + 576 = 625 c² = 625 625 = 625 → Right triangle
  • Solve (b)c = 9 a² + b² = 25 + 49 = 74 c² = 81 74 < 81 → Obtuse triangle
  • Solve (c)c = 8 (check: 4 + 5 = 9 > 8 ✓) a² + b² = 16 + 25 = 41 c² = 64 41 < 64 → Obtuse triangle
Answers: (a) Right triangle   (b) Obtuse triangle   (c) Obtuse triangle
Student Warning — Triangle Inequality First

Always verify that three given lengths actually form a triangle before applying the converse. The Triangle Inequality (Chapter 5) requires that the sum of any two sides exceeds the third. If the sides do not form a triangle, the question of acute, right, or obtuse does not apply.

Section 7.4

Pythagorean Triples

A Pythagorean triple is a set of three positive integers (a, b, c) such that a² + b² = c². Because all three values are whole numbers, Pythagorean triples produce right triangles with no radical lengths. Every whole-number multiple of a Pythagorean triple is also a Pythagorean triple.

TripleVerificationCommon Multiples
(3, 4, 5)9 + 16 = 25(6,8,10), (9,12,15), (15,20,25)
(5, 12, 13)25 + 144 = 169(10,24,26), (15,36,39)
(8, 15, 17)64 + 225 = 289(16,30,34)
(7, 24, 25)49 + 576 = 625(14,48,50)
(9, 40, 41)81 + 1600 = 1681(18,80,82)
Example 7.4.1
Recognizing a Multiple of a Triple
  • ProblemA right triangle has legs of length 9 and 12. Find the hypotenuse without computing.
  • ThinkThe ratio 9 : 12 simplifies to 3 : 4. This matches (3, 4, 5) scaled by 3. So the hypotenuse should be 5 × 3 = 15.
  • Verify9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225 = 15² ✓
Answer: The hypotenuse is 15.

Section 7.5

Distance on the Coordinate Plane

Any two points that are not on the same horizontal or vertical line form the endpoints of the hypotenuse of a right triangle. The legs run horizontally and vertically, and their lengths can be read directly from the coordinates.

Diagram Instruction

Draw two points A = (1, 2) and B = (5, 5) on a coordinate plane. Draw a horizontal segment from A to C = (5, 2), and a vertical segment from C to B. Triangle ACB is a right triangle with the right angle at C. Label the horizontal leg AC = 4 (because 5 − 1 = 4) and the vertical leg CB = 3 (because 5 − 2 = 3). The hypotenuse is AB. Apply the Pythagorean Theorem: AB² = 4² + 3² = 25, so AB = 5.

The Distance Formula

For any two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

d = √((x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²)

This is the Pythagorean Theorem applied to coordinate geometry. Squaring the differences eliminates the need for absolute value signs.

Example 7.5.1
Using the Distance Formula
  • ProblemFind the distance between P = (2, −1) and Q = (−3, 4).
  • Solved = √((−3 − 2)² + (4 − (−1))²) = √((−5)² + (5)²) = √(25 + 25) = √50 = 5√2
  • CheckHorizontal distance is 5, vertical distance is 5 — right isosceles triangle. Hypotenuse of legs-5 isosceles right triangle: 5√2. ✓
Answer: 5√2 ≈ 7.07
Example 7.5.2
Verifying a Right Triangle on the Coordinate Plane
  • ProblemTriangle RST has vertices R = (0, 0), S = (4, 0), T = (0, 3). Is it a right triangle?
  • SolveRS = √(16 + 0) = 4 RT = √(0 + 9) = 3 ST = √(16 + 9) = √25 = 5 RS² + RT² = 16 + 9 = 25 = ST² ✓
Answer: Yes — right angle at R. (RS and RT are perpendicular along the axes.)
Section 7.6

Special Right Triangles: 45-45-90

A 45-45-90 triangle is an isosceles right triangle — both legs are equal. Since the two legs are equal, both base angles are 45°. Let each leg have length x. The Pythagorean Theorem gives the hypotenuse:

45-45-90 Side Ratio
leg : leg : hypotenuse = x : x : x√2 = 1 : 1 : √2

Given a leg → hypotenuse = leg × √2

Given the hypotenuse → leg = hypotenuse ÷ √2 = hypotenuse · √2 / 2

Diagram Instruction

Draw an isosceles right triangle. Label both legs with variable x. Label the hypotenuse x√2. Mark the right angle with a square symbol. Mark both 45° angles at the base vertices. Add matching tick marks to both legs to show they are equal.

Example 7.6.1
Finding the Hypotenuse (45-45-90)
  • ProblemA 45-45-90 triangle has legs of length 7. Find the hypotenuse.
  • Solvec = 7√2
Answer: 7√2
Example 7.6.2
Finding a Leg from the Hypotenuse (45-45-90)
  • ProblemA 45-45-90 triangle has a hypotenuse of length 10. Find the legs.
  • Solveleg = 10 / √2 = (10 / √2) · (√2 / √2) = 10√2 / 2 = 5√2
Answer: Each leg is 5√2.
Example 7.6.3
Diagonal of a Square (45-45-90)
  • ProblemA square floor tile has a side length of 12 inches. Find the length of its diagonal.
  • ThinkThe diagonal divides the square into two 45-45-90 triangles. Each leg is 12 inches.
  • Solved = 12√2 ≈ 16.97 inches
Answer: 12√2 inches ≈ 16.97 inches.
Section 7.7

Special Right Triangles: 30-60-90

A 30-60-90 triangle has angle measures of exactly 30°, 60°, and 90°. It appears naturally when an altitude divides an equilateral triangle in half. Starting with an equilateral triangle of side 2s, the altitude creates a right triangle with hypotenuse 2s, short leg s, and long leg s√3 (from the Pythagorean Theorem: (2s)² − s² = 3s², so h = s√3).

30-60-90 Side Ratio
short leg : long leg : hypotenuse = s : s√3 : 2s = 1 : √3 : 2

Short leg (opposite 30°) = s

Long leg (opposite 60°) = s√3

Hypotenuse (opposite 90°) = 2s

The hypotenuse is always exactly twice the short leg.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a right triangle with the right angle at C. Label the angle at A as 30° and the angle at B as 60°. Label the short leg (opposite 30°) as s, the long leg (opposite 60°) as s√3, and the hypotenuse (opposite 90°) as 2s.

Example 7.7.1
Given the Short Leg (30-60-90)
  • ProblemA 30-60-90 triangle has a short leg of length 4. Find the other sides.
  • SolveLong leg = 4√3 Hypotenuse = 8
Answer: Long leg = 4√3, hypotenuse = 8.
Example 7.7.2
Given the Hypotenuse (30-60-90)
  • ProblemA 30-60-90 triangle has a hypotenuse of length 14. Find the legs.
  • Solve2s = 14 → s = 7 Short leg = 7 Long leg = 7√3
Answer: Short leg = 7, long leg = 7√3.
Example 7.7.3
Given the Long Leg (30-60-90)
  • ProblemA 30-60-90 triangle has a long leg of length 6√3. Find the other sides.
  • Solves√3 = 6√3 → s = 6 Short leg = 6 Hypotenuse = 12
Answer: Short leg = 6, hypotenuse = 12.
Memory Rule

The hypotenuse is twice the short leg. Start there. If you know any one side, you can find all three: identify s, then multiply by 1, √3, and 2 for the three sides.

Section 7.8

Simplifying Radical Lengths

Right triangles frequently produce side lengths that are not whole numbers. Simplest radical form means the expression under the square root has no perfect square factors other than 1. Use the product rule: √(ab) = √a · √b. Factor out any perfect square, then pull it outside the radical.

Simplification Method

√72 = √(36 × 2) = √36 · √2 = 6√2

√180 = √(36 × 5) = 6√5

√50 = √(25 × 2) = 5√2

To rationalize a denominator: multiply numerator and denominator by √r.

10/√2 · (√2/√2) = 10√2/2 = 5√2

Example 7.8.1
Simplifying a Radical from a Triangle
  • ProblemA right triangle has legs of length 6 and 9. Find the hypotenuse in simplest radical form.
  • Solvec² = 36 + 81 = 117 c = √117 = √(9 × 13) = 3√13
Answer: 3√13
Student Warning — A Critical Nonrule

√(a + b) ≠ √a + √b. For example, √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5, but √9 + √16 = 3 + 4 = 7. Always simplify under the radical sign before taking the root.

Section 7.9

Geometric Mean as an Introduction

Definition — Geometric Mean

The geometric mean of two positive numbers a and b is the positive number x such that:

a / x = x / b → x² = ab → x = √(ab)
Example 7.9.1
Computing Geometric Means
  • Find GM of 4 and 9x = √(4 × 9) = √36 = 6 Check: 4/6 = 6/9 = 2/3 ✓
  • Find GM of 3 and 12x = √(3 × 12) = √36 = 6
  • Find GM of 5 and 8x = √40 = 2√10
Why This Matters

Geometric mean appears naturally when the altitude is drawn from the right angle to the hypotenuse. The altitude itself equals the geometric mean of the two segments it creates. This is developed in Section 7.10.

Section 7.10

Altitude to the Hypotenuse

In right triangle △ABC with the right angle at C, draw altitude CD from C perpendicular to hypotenuse AB. Let D be the foot of the altitude on AB. This creates two segments: AD = p and DB = q, with the full hypotenuse c = p + q. The altitude CD = h, leg AC = b, and leg BC = a.

Diagram Instruction

Draw right triangle △ABC with the right angle at C. Draw altitude CD to hypotenuse AB. Label AD = p, DB = q, CD = h, AC = b, BC = a, and AB = c = p + q. Mark all right angles: the original right angle at C and the two right angles at D. The altitude creates two smaller right triangles inside the original.

The Three Geometric Mean Relationships

Drawing the altitude from C to AB creates three similar triangles: △ABC, △ACD, and △CBD. Their proportional sides produce:

h = √(p · q) (altitude = GM of the two hypotenuse segments) b = √(c · p) (each leg = GM of hypotenuse and adjacent segment) a = √(c · q)
Example 7.10.1
Finding the Altitude
  • ProblemIn △ABC, altitude CD is drawn to AB. AD = 4, DB = 9. Find CD.
  • ThinkThe altitude is the geometric mean of the two hypotenuse segments.
  • SolveCD = √(4 × 9) = √36 = 6
Answer: CD = 6.
Example 7.10.2
Finding a Leg from Hypotenuse Segments
  • ProblemIn △ABC, AB = 20 and AD = 5. Find leg AC.
  • ThinkAC is adjacent to segment AD. So AC² = AB × AD.
  • SolveAC² = 20 × 5 = 100 AC = 10
Answer: AC = 10.
Section 7.11

Similar Right Triangles

Right triangles are similar if they share one pair of equal acute angles (AA Similarity, since both already have a 90° angle). When writing similarity statements, matching vertices must have matching angle measures. Incorrect vertex order produces wrong proportions.

Student Warning — Vertex Order Matters

If you write a similarity statement with vertices in the wrong order, every proportion you write from it will be wrong. Always identify which angles correspond — match them by measure — before writing the similarity statement.

Example 7.11.1
Finding All Three Sides from Hypotenuse Segments
  • ProblemIn △ABC with right angle at C, altitude CD is drawn to AB. AD = 3, DB = 12. Find AC, BC, and CD.
  • ThinkAB = 3 + 12 = 15. Apply all three geometric mean relationships.
  • SolveCD = √(3 × 12) = √36 = 6 AC = √(15 × 3) = √45 = 3√5 BC = √(15 × 12) = √180 = 6√5
  • CheckAC² + BC² = 45 + 180 = 225 = 15² = AB² ✓
Answer: CD = 6, AC = 3√5, BC = 6√5.

Section 7.12

Right Triangles in Measurement and Design

The Pythagorean Theorem and the right-triangle relationships of this chapter appear in virtually every technical field. Builders use the 3-4-5 method to check right angles. Navigators find distances across water using triangle reasoning. Physicists resolve vector components into legs. Architects calculate rafter lengths, stair rise-and-run, and ramp slopes. Surveyors measure distances across inaccessible terrain by constructing right triangles with measurable legs.

Example 7.12.1
Ladder Problem
  • ProblemA ladder 20 feet long leans against a vertical wall. The base is 8 feet from the wall. How high does the ladder reach?

Section 7.13

Common Right-Triangle Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Treating a leg as the hypotenuse
5² + 13² = c² (using hyp 13 as a leg)

The hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle — always the longest side. Identify the right angle first. Then c goes opposite it.

Mistake 2 — Forgetting to take the square root
c² = 100, so c = 100

After solving for c², always take the positive square root. c² = 100 means c = √100 = 10.

Mistake 3 — Incorrect radical simplification
√50 = 5√10 or √(a+b) = √a + √b

√50 = √(25 × 2) = 5√2. The product rule is √(ab) = √a · √b. Never split a sum under the radical.

Mistake 4 — Confusing 45-45-90 and 30-60-90 ratios
Using 1 : 1 : √2 for a 30-60-90 triangle

Always identify the angles of the triangle before applying a special ratio. 45-45-90 gives 1 : 1 : √2. 30-60-90 gives 1 : √3 : 2.

Mistake 5 — Reversing short and long legs in a 30-60-90 triangle
Labeling the long leg as opposite 30°

The short leg is opposite the smallest angle (30°). The long leg is opposite 60°. Larger angle → larger side.

Mistake 6 — Wrong altitude-to-hypotenuse formula
CD = (AD + DB) / 2 = arithmetic mean instead of geometric mean

CD = √(AD × DB). The altitude is the geometric mean of the two hypotenuse segments. Each leg is the geometric mean of the whole hypotenuse and the adjacent segment.

Mistake 7 — Applying Pythagorean Theorem to non-right triangles
Using a² + b² = c² for an obtuse triangle

The Pythagorean Theorem only holds for right triangles. Confirm a right angle exists before applying it.

Mistake 8 — Distance formula errors
Forgetting to square the differences, or forgetting the square root

Write the full formula: d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²). Squaring the differences makes order irrelevant. But you must take the final square root.

Section 7.14

Preparing for Trigonometry

Chapter 7 established that right triangles have precise, predictable relationships between their sides and angles. Chapter 8 will take one more step: it will name those side ratios and show that they depend only on the angle, not on the size of the triangle.

In Section 7.6, every 45-45-90 triangle had a leg-to-hypotenuse ratio of 1/√2 = √2/2. In Section 7.7, every 30-60-90 triangle had a short-leg-to-hypotenuse ratio of 1/2 and a long-leg-to-hypotenuse ratio of √3/2. These ratios are consistent because similar triangles have proportional sides. Any two right triangles with the same acute angle measures are similar and therefore have identical side ratios.

Chapter 7 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1A right triangle has exactly one 90° angle. The sides forming the right angle are the legs; the side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse, always the longest side.
  • Sec 7.2The Pythagorean Theorem: a² + b² = c². It can be proven by arranging four copies of the right triangle in a large square and computing area two ways. It finds any side when two are known.
  • Sec 7.3The Converse classifies triangles from side lengths using c (longest side): a² + b² = c² (right), a² + b² > c² (acute), a² + b² < c² (obtuse). Always verify the Triangle Inequality first.
  • Sec 7.4Pythagorean triples are integer solutions to a² + b² = c². The most common are (3,4,5), (5,12,13), (8,15,17), and (7,24,25). Any whole-number multiple of a triple is also a triple.
  • Sec 7.5The distance formula d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²) is the Pythagorean Theorem applied to coordinates. Any two points form the hypotenuse of a right triangle with horizontal and vertical legs.
  • Sec 7.645-45-90 triangles have sides in ratio 1 : 1 : √2. Hypotenuse = leg × √2. Leg = hypotenuse × √2/2. Appears whenever a square is divided by its diagonal.
  • Sec 7.730-60-90 triangles have sides in ratio 1 : √3 : 2. Hypotenuse = 2 × short leg. Long leg = s√3. Derived from the altitude of an equilateral triangle.
  • Sec 7.8Simplest radical form: no perfect square factors under the radical sign. Use √(ab) = √a · √b. Rationalize denominators by multiplying by √r/√r. Never split a sum: √(a+b) ≠ √a + √b.
  • Sec 7.9The geometric mean of a and b is √(ab) — the number x where a/x = x/b. It is the multiplicative "middle" between two numbers.
  • Sec 7.10Altitude CD to hypotenuse AB (with AD = p, DB = q): CD = √(pq); each leg = √(hypotenuse × adjacent segment). All three triangles formed are similar.
  • Sec 7.11Right triangles are similar when they share one pair of equal acute angles (AA). Similarity must be written with corresponding vertices in matching order to produce correct proportions.
  • Sec 7.12Right triangles appear in construction (square corners), surveying (distance across obstacles), navigation, physics (vector components), architecture (rafters, ramps), and engineering (bracing).
  • Sec 7.13Eight major errors: misidentifying the hypotenuse, forgetting the square root, incorrect radical simplification, mixing special triangle ratios, reversing 30-60-90 legs, wrong altitude formula, applying theorem to non-right triangles, distance formula mistakes.
  • Sec 7.14Trigonometry names the consistent side ratios of similar right triangles as sine, cosine, and tangent. These ratios depend only on the angle measure, which follows from the proportionality of similar triangles established in Chapter 6.

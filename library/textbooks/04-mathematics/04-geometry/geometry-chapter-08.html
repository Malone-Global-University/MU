Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces trigonometry as a system of ratios that arise naturally in right triangles. Students who have worked through similarity in Chapter 6 and the Pythagorean Theorem in Chapter 7 are well-positioned to see trigonometry not as a new and mysterious subject, but as a direct consequence of ideas they already understand: similar triangles preserve ratios of corresponding sides, and in a right triangle with a fixed acute angle, those ratios never change, regardless of how large or small the triangle is.

This chapter develops three fundamental trigonometric ratios — sine, cosine, and tangent — and shows students how to use them to find missing side lengths and missing angle measures in right triangles. It then extends this reasoning to angles of elevation and depression, and to real-world measurement problems in surveying, navigation, physics, and engineering. The chapter closes with a bridge note to advanced trigonometry and to the circle-based definitions of trigonometric functions students will encounter in later courses.

Trigonometry should feel like a natural tool, not a collection of formulas to memorize. Every ratio introduced in this chapter has a geometric meaning, and that meaning is grounded in the similarity and proportionality ideas students have already studied.

Opening Question

A surveyor standing 200 feet from the base of a building uses a measuring instrument to sight the top of the building. The instrument reads that the angle from the horizontal ground up to the top of the building is 38°. The surveyor does not need to climb the building or measure its height directly. Using only the distance from the base and the angle of elevation, she can calculate the building's height to within a few inches. How is this possible? What is the connection between an angle and the ratio of the sides of a right triangle? And why do those ratios remain reliable no matter how far from the building the surveyor stands? This chapter answers all of these questions.

Section 8.1

Why Trigonometry Begins with Right Triangles

Ratios, Similarity, and the Origin of Trigonometry

In Chapter 6, you learned that similar triangles have matching angle measures and proportional side lengths. If two triangles are similar, the ratio of any two corresponding sides in one triangle equals the ratio of the corresponding sides in the other. This proportionality is the exact foundation on which trigonometry is built.

Consider a right triangle with an acute angle of 30°. Now imagine scaling that triangle up to make it twice as large, or three times as large, or ten times as large. Each enlarged triangle is similar to the original because all three angles remain the same: 30°, 60°, and 90°. In each version of the triangle, the side opposite the 30° angle, the side adjacent to the 30° angle, and the hypotenuse all grow proportionally.

Because the sides grow proportionally, the ratios between the sides stay exactly the same. This is the central insight: for any fixed acute angle, the ratios of the sides of a right triangle depend only on the angle, not on the size of the triangle. This means we can assign a specific numerical value to each ratio for each angle, and those values will be consistent and reliable. Trigonometry is the study of these ratios and their applications.

Why Right Triangles Specifically?

Right triangles have one angle that is always exactly 90°. This means that if you know one of the other angles, you automatically know all three: 90°, the given angle, and the complement. Because the angles are fully determined by one acute angle, the shape of the triangle is fully determined. All right triangles with the same acute angle are similar — and that is what makes right-triangle trigonometry possible.

Historical Note

The word trigonometry comes from Greek words meaning "triangle measurement." The study of angle-side relationships in triangles was developed over centuries by ancient Greek, Indian, and Arabic mathematicians, who used these ratios to compute distances to stars and planets, map coastlines, and design structures. What seems like an abstract topic was, and still is, one of the most practical tools in applied science.

Student Note

Some students feel that trigonometry is difficult because it introduces three new ratios with technical-sounding names. The names — sine, cosine, and tangent — do sound unfamiliar at first. But each ratio is simply a fraction: a specific side length divided by another specific side length. Once you understand which sides are which relative to a given angle, writing the ratios becomes a clear and consistent process.

Section 8.2

Opposite, Adjacent, and Hypotenuse

Every right triangle has three sides. Before writing any trigonometric ratio, you must correctly identify each side relative to the acute angle you are working with.

LabelDefinitionNote
HypotenuseThe side opposite the right angleAlways the longest side; does not change with reference angle
OppositeThe side directly across from the reference angleChanges depending on which acute angle is chosen
AdjacentThe side next to the reference angle that is not the hypotenuseChanges depending on which acute angle is chosen
Diagram Instruction

Draw right triangle △ABC with the right angle at C. Label the vertices A (upper left), B (lower right), and C (lower left), with C at the corner of the right angle. The hypotenuse is AB. Now consider angle A: the opposite side is BC (directly across from A), and the adjacent side is AC (next to A, not the hypotenuse). Now switch to angle B: the opposite side becomes AC, and the adjacent side becomes BC. Notice how the labels swap when the reference angle changes, while the hypotenuse stays fixed.

Example 8.2.1
Identifying Opposite, Adjacent, and Hypotenuse
  • ProblemIn right triangle △PQR, the right angle is at R. The sides have lengths PQ = 13, QR = 5, and PR = 12. Identify the hypotenuse, the side opposite ∠P, and the side adjacent to ∠P.
  • ThinkThe hypotenuse is always the side opposite the right angle. Since ∠R = 90°, the hypotenuse is PQ. For the remaining sides, the opposite side is across from the reference angle ∠P, and the adjacent side is next to it.
  • Represent Reference angle: ∠P (at vertex P) Hypotenuse: PQ = 13 (opposite the right angle at R) Opposite (∠P): QR = 5 (directly across from ∠P) Adjacent (∠P): PR = 12 (next to ∠P, not the hypotenuse)
  • CheckConfirm: 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13². The Pythagorean Theorem holds, so the labeling is consistent. ✓
Answer: Hypotenuse = 13, Opposite = 5, Adjacent = 12 (all relative to ∠P).
Student Warning

A very common mistake is to confuse the adjacent and opposite sides. Always start by locating the reference angle and asking: which side is directly across from it? That is the opposite side. The remaining non-hypotenuse side is the adjacent side. Do not rely on which side looks bigger or smaller in the diagram — locate the reference angle first, every time.

Section 8.3

Sine Ratio

Definition — Sine

The sine of an acute angle in a right triangle is the ratio of the length of the opposite side to the length of the hypotenuse.

sin(θ) = opposite / hypotenuse

Because all right triangles with the same angle θ are similar, this ratio is the same regardless of the size of the triangle. Sine values increase from 0 to 1 as the angle increases from 0° to 90°.

Anglesin(θ)Source
0°0Opposite side has length zero
30°0.500030-60-90 special right triangle
45°≈ 0.7071 (√2/2)45-45-90 special right triangle
60°≈ 0.8660 (√3/2)30-60-90 special right triangle
90°1Opposite side equals hypotenuse
Example 8.3.1
Writing and Evaluating a Sine Ratio
  • ProblemRight triangle △ABC has a right angle at C. The side opposite ∠A has length 8, and the hypotenuse has length 17. Write sin A as a fraction and as a decimal rounded to four places.
  • ThinkSine is opposite over hypotenuse. I have both values.
  • Representsin A = opposite / hypotenuse = 8/17
  • Solve8 ÷ 17 ≈ 0.4706
  • Check0.4706 is between 0 and 1, which is the valid range for the sine of an acute angle. ✓
Answer: sin A = 8/17 ≈ 0.4706
Example 8.3.2
Finding a Side Using Sine
  • ProblemIn right triangle △DEF, ∠D = 90°, ∠E = 52°, and the hypotenuse EF = 20. Find the side opposite ∠E to the nearest tenth.
  • Think∠E is the reference angle. I need the side opposite ∠E and I know the hypotenuse. Opposite and hypotenuse → sine.
  • Representsin(52°) = opposite / 20
  • Solve opposite = 20 · sin(52°) opposite = 20 × 0.7880 opposite ≈ 15.76
  • CheckA 52° angle is slightly more than 45°, so the opposite side should be slightly more than the adjacent side and less than the hypotenuse. 15.76 out of 20 is reasonable. ✓
Answer: The side opposite ∠E is approximately 15.8.
Section 8.4

Cosine Ratio

Definition — Cosine

The cosine of an acute angle in a right triangle is the ratio of the length of the adjacent side to the length of the hypotenuse.

cos(θ) = adjacent / hypotenuse

Cosine values decrease from 1 to 0 as the angle increases from 0° to 90°. The sine of an angle equals the cosine of its complement: sin(θ) = cos(90° − θ). This is called the co-function identity, and it is the origin of the word cosine: cosine means "sine of the complement."

Example 8.4.1
Writing and Using a Cosine Ratio
  • ProblemRight triangle △JKL has a right angle at L. Angle J = 38° and the hypotenuse JK = 25. Find the length of the side adjacent to ∠J to the nearest tenth.
  • ThinkAdjacent over hypotenuse is cosine. I know the angle and the hypotenuse. I want the adjacent side.
  • Representcos(38°) = adjacent / 25
  • Solve adjacent = 25 · cos(38°) adjacent = 25 × 0.7880 adjacent ≈ 19.70
  • CheckThe adjacent side should be shorter than the hypotenuse: 19.70 < 25 ✓. For a 38° angle (less than 45°), the adjacent side should be larger than the opposite side — consistent here. ✓
Answer: The side adjacent to ∠J is approximately 19.7.
Section 8.5

Tangent Ratio

Definition — Tangent

The tangent of an acute angle in a right triangle is the ratio of the length of the opposite side to the length of the adjacent side.

tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent

Tangent does not involve the hypotenuse. It equals 1 at 45° (since opposite = adjacent in an isosceles right triangle) and grows without bound as the angle approaches 90°. There is also a useful connection: tan(θ) = sin(θ) / cos(θ).

Example 8.5.1
Writing and Using a Tangent Ratio
  • ProblemIn right triangle △RST, the right angle is at T. The side opposite ∠R has length 9, and the side adjacent to ∠R has length 12. Write tan R as a fraction in lowest terms, and find tan R as a decimal.
  • ThinkTangent is opposite over adjacent.
  • Solvetan R = 9/12 = 3/4 = 0.75
  • Checktan(45°) = 1. Since tan R = 0.75 < 1, angle R is less than 45°. This makes sense because the opposite side (9) is shorter than the adjacent side (12). ✓
Answer: tan R = 3/4 = 0.75
Example 8.5.2
Using Tangent to Find a Missing Side
  • ProblemRight triangle △ABC has a right angle at C, ∠A = 65°, and the side adjacent to ∠A is AC = 10. Find BC (the side opposite ∠A) to the nearest tenth.
  • ThinkI know the angle and the adjacent side. I want the opposite side. Tangent connects opposite and adjacent.
  • Representtan(65°) = BC / 10
  • Solve BC = 10 · tan(65°) BC = 10 × 2.1445 BC ≈ 21.4
  • CheckA 65° angle is larger than 45°, so the opposite side should be longer than the adjacent side. 21.4 > 10 ✓.
Answer: BC ≈ 21.4

Section 8.6

Choosing the Correct Trigonometric Ratio

SOH-CAH-TOA

SOH — Sine = Opposite / Hypotenuse

CAH — Cosine = Adjacent / Hypotenuse

TOA — Tangent = Opposite / Adjacent

SOH-CAH-TOA is a useful memory tool, but it should support understanding, not replace it. Always be able to explain what each ratio means geometrically.

OppositeHypotenuseSine

Example 8.6.1
Selecting the Correct Ratio
  • ProblemIn right triangle △XYZ, right angle at Z, ∠X = 42°, hypotenuse XY = 30. You want to find YZ, the side opposite ∠X. Which ratio?
  • ThinkKnown: angle at X and the hypotenuse. Wanted: side opposite ∠X. Opposite and hypotenuse → Sine.
Answer: Use sine — sin(42°) = YZ / 30
Example 8.6.2
Selecting When You Have Both Legs
  • ProblemRight triangle △MNP, right angle at P. MP = 7 (adjacent to ∠M), NP = 11 (opposite ∠M). You want to find ∠M. Which ratio?
  • ThinkKnown: opposite = 11 and adjacent = 7. No hypotenuse involved. Opposite and adjacent → Tangent. To find the angle from the ratio, use inverse tangent (Section 8.9).
Answer: Use tangent — tan M = 11/7
Student Tip

If you are not sure which ratio to use, label all three sides of the right triangle relative to your reference angle. Mark which side you know and which side you want. Then match that pair to sine, cosine, or tangent. Never guess — the labeling will always tell you which ratio to use.

Section 8.7

Solving for Missing Sides

Once you have chosen the correct trigonometric ratio, solving for a missing side is an algebraic problem. The ratio gives you an equation with one unknown, and you solve it using multiplication or division.

Example 8.7.1
Finding the Side Opposite the Reference Angle
  • ProblemRight triangle △ABC, right angle at C. Angle A = 27°, hypotenuse AB = 40. Find BC to the nearest tenth.
  • ThinkBC is opposite ∠A. Hypotenuse = 40. Opposite and hypotenuse → sine.
  • Representsin(27°) = BC / 40
  • Solve BC = 40 · sin(27°) BC ≈ 40 × 0.4540 BC ≈ 18.16
  • CheckAt 30°, opposite/hypotenuse = 0.5, giving opposite = 20. Getting 18.16 for 27° (slightly less than 30°) is consistent. ✓
Answer: BC ≈ 18.2
Example 8.7.2
Finding the Hypotenuse
  • ProblemRight triangle △PQR, right angle at R. Angle P = 55°, adjacent side PR = 14. Find the hypotenuse PQ to the nearest tenth.
  • ThinkI know the adjacent side and want the hypotenuse. Adjacent and hypotenuse → cosine.
  • Representcos(55°) = 14 / PQ
  • Solve PQ · cos(55°) = 14 PQ = 14 / cos(55°) PQ ≈ 14 / 0.5736 PQ ≈ 24.4
  • CheckThe hypotenuse must be longer than any leg: 24.4 > 14 ✓.
Answer: PQ ≈ 24.4
Example 8.7.3
Finding the Adjacent Side Using Tangent
  • ProblemRight triangle △DEF, right angle at F. Angle D = 71°, opposite side EF = 35. Find adjacent side DF to the nearest tenth.
  • ThinkOpposite = 35, want adjacent. Opposite and adjacent → tangent.
  • Representtan(71°) = 35 / DF
  • Solve DF = 35 / tan(71°) DF ≈ 35 / 2.9042 DF ≈ 12.1
  • CheckA 71° angle is large, so the opposite side should be much longer than the adjacent side. Indeed, 35 >> 12. ✓
Answer: DF ≈ 12.1
Student Note — Unknown in the Denominator

When the unknown is in the denominator (as in Examples 8.7.2 and 8.7.3), isolate it by multiplying both sides by the unknown first, then dividing both sides by the trig ratio value. This requires one extra step but is otherwise straightforward algebra.

Sections 8.8 – 8.9

Solving for Missing Angles — Inverse Trigonometric Functions

In Sections 8.3 through 8.7, you used a known angle to find an unknown side. Now you reverse the process: given two sides of a right triangle, you find an unknown angle. The inverse trigonometric functions answer the question: If I know the ratio, what is the angle?

The Inverse Functions

sin⁻¹(r) — the angle whose sine equals r  (also written arcsin)

cos⁻¹(r) — the angle whose cosine equals r  (also written arccos)

tan⁻¹(r) — the angle whose tangent equals r  (also written arctan)

The notation sin⁻¹ does not mean 1/sin. It means "the angle whose sine is." On most calculators, these appear as 2nd or Shift functions above SIN, COS, and TAN.

Example 8.9.1
Using Inverse Sine to Find an Angle
  • ProblemRight triangle △ABC, right angle at C. Side opposite ∠A is 9, hypotenuse is 15. Find m∠A to the nearest tenth of a degree.
  • ThinkOpposite and hypotenuse → sine. To find the angle from the ratio, use inverse sine.
  • Represent sin A = 9/15 = 3/5 = 0.6 A = sin⁻¹(0.6)
  • SolveA = sin⁻¹(0.6) ≈ 36.87°
  • Checksin(36.87°) ≈ 0.6 ✓
Answer: m∠A ≈ 36.9°
Example 8.9.2
Using Inverse Cosine
  • ProblemRight triangle △JKL, right angle at L. Adjacent side to ∠J is 11, hypotenuse is 20. Find m∠J.
  • Representcos J = 11/20 = 0.55 → J = cos⁻¹(0.55)
  • SolveJ = cos⁻¹(0.55) ≈ 56.6°
Answer: m∠J ≈ 56.6°
Example 8.9.3
Using Inverse Tangent
  • ProblemRight triangle △RST, right angle at T. Legs: RT = 6 (adjacent to ∠R), ST = 10 (opposite ∠R). Find m∠R.
  • Representtan R = 10/6 = 5/3 ≈ 1.6667 → R = tan⁻¹(1.6667)
  • SolveR = tan⁻¹(1.6667) ≈ 59.0°
  • CheckThe opposite side (10) is longer than the adjacent side (6), so the angle should be greater than 45°. 59° > 45° ✓.
Answer: m∠R ≈ 59.0°
Example 8.9.4
Solving a Right Triangle Completely
  • ProblemRight triangle △PQR, right angle at R. Hypotenuse PQ = 26, leg PR = 10. Find QR, m∠P, and m∠Q.
  • Step 1Find QR using the Pythagorean Theorem: PR² + QR² = PQ² 100 + QR² = 676 QR² = 576 → QR = 24
  • Step 2Find ∠P. Adjacent to ∠P is PR = 10, hypotenuse = 26: cos P = 10/26 = 5/13 m∠P = cos⁻¹(5/13) ≈ 67.4°
  • Step 3Find ∠Q by angle sum: m∠Q = 180° − 90° − 67.4° = 22.6°
  • CheckVerify: sin Q = 10/26 ≈ 0.3846 → sin⁻¹(0.3846) ≈ 22.6° ✓. Also 67.4° + 22.6° = 90° ✓.
Answer: QR = 24, m∠P ≈ 67.4°, m∠Q ≈ 22.6°
Quick Check for Solved Right Triangles

Always verify that your two acute angles add to exactly 90°. Since the right angle accounts for 90° of the 180° total, the two remaining angles must sum to 90°. This is a fast and reliable check whenever you solve a right triangle completely.

Section 8.10

Angles of Elevation

An angle of elevation is the angle measured upward from the horizontal to a line of sight looking toward a point that is above the observer. In a right triangle formed by an angle of elevation, the horizontal distance from the observer to the base of the object is the adjacent side, the vertical height is the opposite side, and the line of sight is the hypotenuse.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a horizontal ground line. From a point on the ground (the observer), draw a diagonal line going up and to the right toward an object at the top of a vertical structure. Draw a small angle arc between the horizontal ground line and the diagonal line of sight; label this arc "angle of elevation." The structure should be drawn as a vertical segment with a right angle where it meets the ground. This creates a right triangle: horizontal ground = adjacent side, vertical structure = opposite side, line of sight = hypotenuse.

Example 8.10.1
Finding Height Using an Angle of Elevation
  • ProblemA person standing 80 feet from the base of a flagpole measures the angle of elevation to the top of the pole as 52°. Find the height of the flagpole to the nearest foot.
  • ThinkHorizontal distance = adjacent (80 ft). Height = opposite (unknown). Use tangent.
  • Representtan(52°) = h / 80
  • Solve h = 80 · tan(52°) h ≈ 80 × 1.2799 h ≈ 102.4
  • CheckA 52° angle is steeper than 45°. At 45°, h would equal 80. Getting 102 ft (greater than 80) for 52° is consistent. ✓
Answer: The flagpole is approximately 102 feet tall.
Example 8.10.2
Finding Distance Using an Angle of Elevation
  • ProblemFrom the ground, a person sees the top of a 120-foot building at an angle of elevation of 34°. How far is the person from the base? Round to the nearest foot.
  • Representtan(34°) = 120 / d
  • Solve d = 120 / tan(34°) d ≈ 120 / 0.6745 d ≈ 177.9
  • CheckA 34° angle is less than 45°, so the horizontal distance should exceed the height. 178 > 120 ✓.
Answer: The person is approximately 178 feet from the base.
Section 8.11

Angles of Depression

An angle of depression is the angle measured downward from the horizontal to a line of sight looking toward a point that is below the observer. An important geometric fact: because the observer's horizontal and the ground below are parallel (both horizontal), the angle of depression equals the angle of elevation from the lower point looking up — these are alternate interior angles formed by a transversal cutting two parallel horizontal lines.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a horizontal line at the level of the observer (an elevated position). From the observer's point, draw a diagonal line going down and to the right toward an object at a lower level. Draw a small angle arc between the horizontal line and the downward diagonal; label it "angle of depression." Draw a vertical segment from the observer's level down to the level of the object, and a horizontal segment from the object to directly below the observer. This creates a right triangle with the angle of depression at the top. Note that the alternate interior angle at the bottom of the diagram equals the angle of depression.

Key Relationship

Angle of depression from above = Angle of elevation from below

These are alternate interior angles formed by parallel horizontal lines cut by the line of sight as a transversal. Use whichever perspective makes setting up the right triangle easier.

Example 8.11.1
Angle of Depression from a Cliff
  • ProblemA hiker stands at the edge of a cliff 200 feet above the base. The angle of depression to a boat on the water is 28°. How far is the boat from the base of the cliff?
  • ThinkThe angle of depression equals the angle at the base of the right triangle (alternate interior angles). Height = 200 ft (opposite), horizontal distance = unknown (adjacent). Use tangent.
  • Representtan(28°) = 200 / d
  • Solve d = 200 / tan(28°) d ≈ 200 / 0.5317 d ≈ 376.2
  • Check28° < 45°, so horizontal distance > vertical height. 376 > 200 ✓.
Answer: The boat is approximately 376 feet from the base of the cliff.
Example 8.11.2
Line-of-Sight Distance
  • ProblemFrom the top of a 350-foot building, the angle of depression to a car is 42°. What is the straight-line distance from the building top to the car?
  • ThinkVertical height = 350 ft (opposite). Want the line of sight (hypotenuse). Use sine.
  • Representsin(42°) = 350 / hypotenuse
  • Solve hypotenuse = 350 / sin(42°) hypotenuse ≈ 350 / 0.6691 hypotenuse ≈ 523.1
  • CheckThe line of sight must be longer than 350 ft. 523 > 350 ✓.
Answer: The straight-line distance is approximately 523 feet.
Student Warning

Always draw a clear diagram before computing with angles of elevation or depression. Mark the observer's position, the object's position, and the horizontal reference line at the observer's level. Identifying the correct angle and the correct sides of the right triangle from the diagram will prevent most errors in these problems.

Section 8.12

Trigonometry in Surveying, Navigation, and Physics

Example 8.12.1
Surveying — Height of a Hill
  • ProblemA surveyor at point A is 430 meters from the base of a hill. The angle of elevation to the top of the hill is 17°. What is the height of the hill?
  • Representtan(17°) = h / 430
  • Solveh = 430 · tan(17°) ≈ 430 × 0.3057 ≈ 131.5
Answer: The hill is approximately 132 meters tall.
Example 8.12.2
Navigation — Displacement Components
  • ProblemAn airplane flies 500 miles on a heading 35° north of due east. How far north and how far east has it traveled?
  • ThinkTotal flight path (500 mi) = hypotenuse. Northward component = opposite the 35° angle. Eastward = adjacent.
  • Solve Northward = 500 · sin(35°) ≈ 500 × 0.5736 ≈ 286.8 miles Eastward = 500 · cos(35°) ≈ 500 × 0.8192 ≈ 409.6 miles
Answer: Approximately 287 miles north and 410 miles east.
Example 8.12.3
Physics — Ramp Problem
  • ProblemA box sits on a ramp that makes an angle of 25° with the horizontal. The weight of the box is 200 newtons, acting straight downward. What is the component of the weight force acting along the ramp?
  • ThinkThe component along the ramp is opposite the 25° angle in the triangle formed between the weight vector and the ramp surface.
  • SolveF_along = 200 · sin(25°) ≈ 200 × 0.4226 ≈ 84.5 N

Section 8.13

Common Trigonometry Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Mixing Up Opposite and Adjacent
Labeling sides based on their position (left/right/bottom) rather than their relationship to the reference angle

Fix: Always identify the reference angle first. The opposite side is directly across from that angle. The adjacent side is next to that angle and is not the hypotenuse. Label before computing, every time.

Mistake 2 — Using the Wrong Trig Ratio
Using sine when the known and unknown sides call for tangent, or vice versa

Fix: Label all three sides relative to the reference angle. Identify which two sides are involved. Match that pair to the correct ratio using the selection table in Section 8.6.

Mistake 3 — Using the 90° Angle as the Reference Angle
Anchoring side labels at the right-angle vertex, producing incorrect opposite/adjacent assignments

Fix: Sine, cosine, and tangent are always defined for one of the two acute angles, never the right angle. The right angle is your indicator for the hypotenuse, not the reference angle for a ratio.

Mistake 4 — Calculator in the Wrong Mode
sin(30) computed in radian mode gives ≈ −0.988 instead of the correct 0.5

Fix: Always verify that your calculator displays DEG (degree mode) before computing trig values. Radian mode is used in calculus and advanced trigonometry, not in this chapter. If an answer seems wildly unreasonable, check your mode first.

Mistake 5 — Setting Up the Equation Incorrectly
Writing sin(42°) = x/15 when x is the hypotenuse (it should be sin(42°) = 15/x)

Fix: Write the formula in words first — sin(angle) = opposite/hypotenuse — then substitute numbers. Never substitute before identifying which side plays which role.

Mistake 6 — Interpreting sin⁻¹ as a Reciprocal
sin⁻¹(0.5) = 1/sin(0.5) ≈ 2.086 — treating the inverse notation as a negative exponent

Fix: In the context of inverse functions, sin⁻¹ means "the angle whose sine is," not 1/sin. The correct result is sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30°. If you want the reciprocal of sine, that is the cosecant function, which is not used in this chapter.

Mistake 7 — Confusing Elevation and Depression Setup
Placing the angle at the wrong vertex, or forgetting that angle of depression = angle of elevation from below

Fix: Always draw the diagram. Mark the observer's position, the object's position, and the horizontal reference line. Mark the angle between the horizontal and the line of sight. The alternate interior angle relationship confirms that depression = elevation from the reverse direction.

Preparing for Circles and Advanced Trigonometry

Chapter 8 completes Part III of this textbook. You have developed a solid foundation in right-triangle trigonometry that will reappear throughout future mathematics, science, and technical courses.

In Chapter 10 (Circles): You will study arc length using the formula s = rθ, where θ is an angle measured in radians rather than degrees. This is your first encounter with radian measure, and it leads directly to the unit circle definition of sine and cosine.

In Trigonometry and Precalculus: You will extend sine, cosine, and tangent to all angles — including obtuse angles, angles greater than 360°, and negative angles — using the unit circle. In Calculus: The derivatives and integrals of trigonometric functions appear throughout the study of motion, oscillation, area under curves, and infinite series. Students who understand why the ratios are consistent and how to set up equations correctly will find every subsequent encounter with trigonometry both manageable and meaningful.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1Trigonometric ratios are consistent across all similar right triangles with the same acute angle because corresponding sides are proportional. This proportionality, established in Chapter 6, is the geometric foundation of trigonometry.
  • Sec 8.2The hypotenuse is always the side opposite the right angle. The opposite and adjacent sides are defined relative to the chosen reference angle — and they swap roles when the reference angle changes.
  • Sec 8.3Sine = opposite / hypotenuse. Sine increases from 0 to 1 as the angle increases from 0° to 90°. sin(30°) = 0.5 and sin(45°) = √2/2 are worth knowing.
  • Sec 8.4Cosine = adjacent / hypotenuse. Cosine decreases from 1 to 0 as the angle increases. The co-function identity: sin(θ) = cos(90° − θ). The word cosine means "sine of the complement."
  • Sec 8.5Tangent = opposite / adjacent. Tangent does not involve the hypotenuse. It equals 1 at 45° and grows without bound toward 90°. tan(θ) = sin(θ)/cos(θ).
  • Sec 8.6Choose the ratio by identifying which two sides are involved (known and unknown). Label sides first, then match the pair to sine (opp/hyp), cosine (adj/hyp), or tangent (opp/adj). SOH-CAH-TOA is a memory aid, not a substitute for reasoning.
  • Sec 8.7Write the trig ratio equation with the unknown side. When the unknown is in the numerator, multiply by the denominator. When the unknown is in the denominator, multiply both sides by it, then divide by the trig value.
  • Secs 8.8–8.9Inverse trig functions find the angle from the ratio: sin⁻¹(r), cos⁻¹(r), and tan⁻¹(r). The notation sin⁻¹ means "the angle whose sine is," not 1/sin. Always verify that the two acute angles sum to 90°.
  • Sec 8.10An angle of elevation is measured upward from horizontal to the line of sight. In the right triangle: horizontal distance = adjacent, vertical height = opposite, line of sight = hypotenuse.
  • Sec 8.11An angle of depression is measured downward from horizontal. It equals the angle of elevation from the lower object due to alternate interior angles formed by parallel horizontal lines. Always draw a clear diagram.
  • Sec 8.12Trigonometry is used in surveying (measuring inaccessible heights), navigation (decomposing displacement into directional components), and physics (resolving forces). The core setup is always a right triangle with a known angle and side.
  • Sec 8.13Seven common mistakes: swapping opposite/adjacent; wrong ratio; using 90° as reference; calculator in radian mode; incorrect equation setup; treating sin⁻¹ as a reciprocal; confusing elevation/depression setup.
  • Sec 8.14Right-triangle trigonometry extends to the unit circle, periodic functions, Law of Sines and Cosines, vector decomposition in physics, and calculus. The ratios and setup skills from this chapter carry directly into all future work.

Key Terms Review

trigonometry
The study of the ratios between sides of triangles as functions of angle measure, and their applications to measurement and modeling.
sine
sin(θ) = opposite / hypotenuse. Increases from 0 to 1 as the angle increases from 0° to 90°.
cosine
cos(θ) = adjacent / hypotenuse. Decreases from 1 to 0 as the angle increases from 0° to 90°.
tangent
tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent. Does not involve the hypotenuse. Equals 1 at 45°.
hypotenuse
The side of a right triangle that is opposite the right angle. Always the longest side. Does not change with the reference angle.
opposite side
The side directly across from the reference angle in a right triangle. Changes depending on which acute angle is chosen.
adjacent side
The side next to the reference angle that is not the hypotenuse. Also changes with the reference angle.
inverse sine
sin⁻¹(r) — the angle whose sine equals r. Written arcsin on some calculators. Not the same as 1/sin.
inverse cosine
cos⁻¹(r) — the angle whose cosine equals r. Written arccos on some calculators.
inverse tangent
tan⁻¹(r) — the angle whose tangent equals r. Written arctan on some calculators.
angle of elevation
The angle measured upward from the horizontal to a line of sight toward a point above the observer.
angle of depression
The angle measured downward from the horizontal to a line of sight toward a point below the observer.
solving a right triangle
Finding all three sides and all three angles of a right triangle from sufficient given information.
reference angle
The acute angle chosen as the basis for a trigonometric ratio. Never the right angle itself.

Chapter 5 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 4 established what triangles are, how to classify them, how to prove them congruent, and what congruence means precisely. This chapter goes deeper. A triangle is not merely three sides and three angles held together by a congruence shortcut. It is a system — a structure in which the lengths of sides and the measures of angles are connected by relationships that hold in every triangle, without exception.

This chapter develops that internal structure. You will study four special segments that can be drawn in any triangle: perpendicular bisectors, angle bisectors, medians, and altitudes. Each kind of segment has a unique definition, a unique construction, and a unique set of properties. When three of the same kind of segment are drawn in one triangle, they always meet at a single point — a triangle center. There are four classical triangle centers, each with its own geometric significance. You will learn to locate them, prove their existence, and use them in problems.

In the second half of the chapter, you will study inequality relationships. Not all sides in a triangle are equal; not all angles are equal. The relative sizes of sides and angles are not random — they follow predictable rules. The Triangle Inequality Theorem tells you which combinations of three lengths can form a triangle. The side-angle inequalities tell you how the largest side and the largest angle in a triangle relate. The Hinge Theorem extends this reasoning to comparisons between two triangles. Finally, indirect reasoning gives you a powerful proof strategy: assume the opposite of what you want to prove, then show that assumption leads to a contradiction.

Work through this chapter with care. The relationships here will reappear in similarity, right triangle geometry, coordinate geometry, and design applications. Triangles are not simple — they carry more structure than any other polygon, and understanding that structure will serve you in every part of mathematics that follows.

Opening Question

Three cell towers must be positioned so that they cover an entire city with the fewest gaps. An engineer wants to find the one point inside the triangular region they form that is equally distant from all three towers. Does such a point always exist? Is it unique? How would you find it without measuring every possible point?

The answer involves one of the four triangle centers you will study in this chapter. By the end of Section 5.5, you will know exactly how to locate that point — and why it must exist, no matter what triangle the three towers form.

Section 5.1

Perpendicular Bisectors

A perpendicular bisector of a segment is a line, ray, or segment that passes through the midpoint of the segment and is perpendicular to it. It bisects the segment (cuts it into two equal pieces) and meets it at a right angle.

The perpendicular bisector of a segment has a remarkable property regarding distance. Every point on the perpendicular bisector of a segment is equidistant — equally far — from the two endpoints of that segment. And the converse is also true: if a point is equidistant from the two endpoints of a segment, then it must lie on the perpendicular bisector of that segment.

Theorem
Perpendicular Bisector Theorem

If a point lies on the perpendicular bisector of a segment, then it is equidistant from the endpoints of that segment.

If P is on the ⊥ bisector of AB, then PA = PB.
Theorem (Converse)
Converse of the Perpendicular Bisector Theorem

If a point is equidistant from the endpoints of a segment, then it lies on the perpendicular bisector of that segment.

If PA = PB, then P is on the ⊥ bisector of AB.
Diagram Instruction

Draw segment AB horizontally. Mark its midpoint M. Draw a vertical line through M that is perpendicular to AB. Mark the right angle at M. Place point P anywhere on the perpendicular bisector above M. Draw PA and PB. Mark PA and PB with equal tick marks to show PA = PB.

Example 5.1.1
Applying the Perpendicular Bisector Theorem
  • ProblemPoint P lies on the perpendicular bisector of segment AB. If PA = 3x + 1 and PB = 5x − 7, find PA.
  • ThinkBy the Perpendicular Bisector Theorem, any point on the perpendicular bisector is equidistant from both endpoints. So PA = PB.
  • Solve PA = PB 3x + 1 = 5x − 7 1 + 7 = 5x − 3x 8 = 2x x = 4 PA = 3(4) + 1 = 13
  • CheckPB = 5(4) − 7 = 13. PA = PB = 13. ✓
Answer: PA = 13
Construction Note

To construct the perpendicular bisector of a segment with a compass and straightedge: place the compass at one endpoint and draw an arc with radius greater than half the segment. Without changing the radius, place the compass at the other endpoint and draw another arc. The two arcs intersect at two points. Draw the line through those two intersection points. That line is the perpendicular bisector.

Practice 5.1Perpendicular Bisectors

  2. Is the following possible? Point P is equidistant from A and B, but P does not lie on the perpendicular bisector of AB. Explain.
  3. In triangle ABC, the perpendicular bisector of BC passes through vertex A. What does this tell you about triangle ABC? (Hint: use the Perpendicular Bisector Theorem.)
  4. Point R is on the perpendicular bisector of PQ. RP = 4a − 3 and RQ = a + 9. Find RP.
Section 5.2

Angle Bisectors

An angle bisector is a ray that begins at the vertex of an angle and divides the angle into two congruent halves. Every angle has exactly one angle bisector.

A point on the angle bisector of an angle is equidistant from the two sides of that angle. The distance from a point to a line is always measured perpendicularly — it is the length of the perpendicular segment from the point to the line.

Theorem
Angle Bisector Theorem

If a point lies on the bisector of an angle, then it is equidistant from the two sides of the angle (measured perpendicularly).

If P is on the bisector of ∠A, then the perpendicular distance from P to each side of ∠A is equal.
Theorem (Converse)
Converse of the Angle Bisector Theorem

If a point in the interior of an angle is equidistant from the two sides of that angle, then it lies on the angle bisector.

Diagram Instruction

Draw angle ∠ABC with ray BD as the bisector, so ∠ABD = ∠DBC. Place point P on ray BD. From P, draw a perpendicular segment to ray BA and label its foot E; draw a perpendicular segment to ray BC and label its foot F. Mark right angles at E and F. Mark PE and PF with equal tick marks to show PE = PF.

Example 5.2.1
Using the Angle Bisector Theorem
  • ProblemRay BD bisects ∠ABC. The perpendicular distance from point P on BD to ray BA is 3n + 2, and the perpendicular distance from P to ray BC is 7n − 10. Find n and the distance.
  • ThinkBy the Angle Bisector Theorem, P is equidistant from both sides. Set the distances equal.
  • Solve 3n + 2 = 7n − 10 12 = 4n n = 3 Distance = 3(3) + 2 = 11
  • Check7(3) − 10 = 11. Both distances equal 11. ✓
Answer: n = 3; the perpendicular distance from P to each side is 11.
Student Warning

The distance in the Angle Bisector Theorem is always the perpendicular distance from the point to each side — not the distance to the vertex or to any other point along the side. Always draw the perpendicular segment from the point to the line containing each side.

Section 5.3

Medians of Triangles

A median of a triangle is a segment that connects a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. Every triangle has exactly three medians — one from each vertex. The midpoint that the median reaches must be the true midpoint of the opposite side, dividing it into two equal segments.

It is important not to confuse a median with a perpendicular bisector. A perpendicular bisector is perpendicular to the side at its midpoint, but it does not necessarily pass through a vertex. A median passes through a vertex and reaches the midpoint of the opposite side, but it is not necessarily perpendicular to that side. These are different segments. In some special triangles (like equilateral triangles), certain medians may coincide with perpendicular bisectors or other special segments, but in general they are distinct.

Diagram Instruction

Draw triangle ABC. Mark the midpoint of BC as D, the midpoint of AC as E, and the midpoint of AB as F. Draw segments AD, BE, and CF — these are the three medians. Use tick marks to show BD = DC, AE = EC, and AF = FB.

Section 5.4

Altitudes of Triangles

An altitude of a triangle is a segment from a vertex perpendicular to the line containing the opposite side. Every triangle has exactly three altitudes — one from each vertex.

In an acute triangle, all three altitudes land inside the triangle on the opposite sides. In a right triangle, two of the three altitudes are the legs themselves: the leg from one vertex is perpendicular to the other leg, so it falls exactly on the opposite side at the right angle vertex. The third altitude is drawn from the right-angle vertex to the hypotenuse and lands inside the triangle. In an obtuse triangle, two altitudes fall outside the triangle — their feet lie on the extensions of the sides, not on the sides themselves.

The point where an altitude meets the opposite side (or its extension) is called the foot of the altitude. The altitude itself is perpendicular to the line containing the opposite side, even if the foot is outside the triangle.

Diagram Instruction — Three Triangle Types

Draw three triangles side by side: one acute, one right, one obtuse. In the acute triangle, draw all three altitudes inside; mark right angles at each foot. In the right triangle, show the two legs as altitudes meeting at the right angle vertex, and draw the altitude from the right angle vertex to the hypotenuse; mark the right angle. In the obtuse triangle, draw the two altitudes that land outside the triangle; extend the appropriate sides with dashed lines to show where the altitudes meet them; mark right angles at the feet.

Tip — Altitudes vs. Heights

When you compute the area of a triangle using the formula A = ½bh, the height h is the altitude corresponding to the base b. The altitude must be perpendicular to the base (or its extension). It is not simply the side of the triangle; it is the perpendicular distance from the opposite vertex to the line of the base.

Sections 5.5 – 5.8
The Four Triangle Centers
Circumcenter · Incenter · Centroid · Orthocenter

The three perpendicular bisectors of a triangle always meet at a single point. So do the three angle bisectors, the three medians, and the three altitudes. This remarkable fact — that three lines which seem to have no particular reason to meet at one point always do — is called concurrency. The single point where they meet is called the point of concurrency. Each of the four types of special segments produces its own point of concurrency, and each has a distinct geometric meaning.

Section 5.5

Circumcenter

The circumcenter of a triangle is the point where the three perpendicular bisectors meet. Because every point on a perpendicular bisector is equidistant from the two endpoints of the corresponding side, the circumcenter is equidistant from all three vertices of the triangle. This means a circle can be drawn centered at the circumcenter that passes through all three vertices. This circle is called the circumscribed circle (or circumcircle) of the triangle, and its radius is the distance from the circumcenter to any vertex.

Circumcenter Theorem

The three perpendicular bisectors of a triangle are concurrent. Their point of concurrency, the circumcenter, is equidistant from all three vertices of the triangle.

If O is the circumcenter of △ABC, then OA = OB = OC.

Location of the Circumcenter

Triangle TypeLocation of Circumcenter
Acute triangleInside the triangle
Right triangleOn the hypotenuse (at its midpoint)
Obtuse triangleOutside the triangle
Example 5.5.1
Using the Circumcenter
  • ProblemPoint O is the circumcenter of △PQR. If OP = 5x − 3 and OQ = 2x + 9, find OR.
  • ThinkThe circumcenter is equidistant from all three vertices, so OP = OQ = OR.
  • Solve OP = OQ 5x − 3 = 2x + 9 3x = 12 x = 4 OP = 5(4) − 3 = 17
  • CheckOQ = 2(4) + 9 = 17. OP = OQ, so OR = 17 as well. ✓
Answer: OR = 17
Section 5.6

Incenter

The incenter of a triangle is the point where the three angle bisectors meet. Because every point on an angle bisector is equidistant from the two sides of that angle, the incenter is equidistant from all three sides of the triangle. This means a circle can be drawn centered at the incenter that is tangent to (just touches) all three sides. This circle is called the inscribed circle (or incircle) of the triangle, and its radius is the perpendicular distance from the incenter to any side.

Unlike the circumcenter, the incenter is always inside the triangle, regardless of whether the triangle is acute, right, or obtuse.

Incenter Theorem

The three angle bisectors of a triangle are concurrent. Their point of concurrency, the incenter, is equidistant from all three sides of the triangle (measured perpendicularly).

If I is the incenter of △ABC, then the perpendicular distance from I to AB = to BC = to AC.

Example 5.6.1
Using the Incenter
  • ProblemI is the incenter of △ABC. The perpendicular distance from I to side AB is 3k + 1, and the perpendicular distance from I to side BC is 7k − 11. Find the distance from I to side AC.
  • ThinkThe incenter is equidistant from all three sides. Set the two known distances equal.
  • Solve 3k + 1 = 7k − 11 12 = 4k k = 3 Distance = 3(3) + 1 = 10
  • Check7(3) − 11 = 10. All three perpendicular distances from I to the sides equal 10. ✓
Answer: The distance from I to side AC is also 10.
Section 5.7

Centroid

The centroid of a triangle is the point where the three medians meet. The centroid is the balance point of the triangle — if you cut a triangle out of uniform material and try to balance it on a pencil tip, the triangle will balance at the centroid.

The centroid has a special property regarding the medians. It divides each median in a 2:1 ratio, measured from the vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. In other words, the centroid is located two-thirds of the way from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side, and one-third of the way from each midpoint to the opposite vertex.

Centroid Theorem

The three medians of a triangle are concurrent. Their point of concurrency, the centroid, divides each median in the ratio 2:1 from vertex to midpoint.

If G is the centroid and AD is a median from vertex A to midpoint D of BC, then:

AG = (2/3) · AD and GD = (1/3) · AD

Diagram Instruction

Draw triangle ABC with medians AD, BE, and CF meeting at centroid G. On median AD, mark the two segments AG and GD with the ratio 2:1 — label AG with two tick marks and GD with one. Repeat for the other two medians. Label the centroid G with a dot.

Example 5.7.1
Applying the Two-Thirds Property
  • ProblemG is the centroid of △ABC and AD is a median from A to D. If AG = 14, find GD and AD.
  • ThinkThe centroid divides each median 2:1 from vertex. So AG = (2/3)AD, which means GD = (1/3)AD, and AG is twice GD.
  • Solve AG = 2 · GD 14 = 2 · GD GD = 7 AD = AG + GD = 14 + 7 = 21
  • Check(2/3)(21) = 14 = AG ✓ and (1/3)(21) = 7 = GD ✓
Answer: GD = 7 and AD = 21.
Example 5.7.2
Finding the Full Median from a Segment
  • ProblemG is the centroid of △PQR and QM is a median. If GM = 5, find QM and QG.
  • ThinkGM = (1/3)QM, so I can find QM first, then QG.
  • Solve GM = (1/3) · QM 5 = (1/3) · QM QM = 15 QG = (2/3) · QM = (2/3)(15) = 10
  • CheckQG + GM = 10 + 5 = 15 = QM ✓
Answer: QM = 15 and QG = 10.

Section 5.8

Orthocenter

The orthocenter of a triangle is the point where the three altitudes meet. Unlike the circumcenter and incenter — which have elegant equidistance properties — the orthocenter is defined purely by the perpendicularity of the altitudes. It does not have a simple equidistance property, but it plays an important role in advanced geometry and in the Euler line (a line connecting four special triangle points, studied in higher courses).

Section 5.9

Triangle Inequality Theorem

Not every set of three lengths can be the sides of a triangle. If you try to build a triangle with sides of 2, 3, and 10, you will find that the two shorter sides, even when laid end to end, do not reach across the longest side. No triangle is possible.

The condition that determines whether three lengths can form a triangle is expressed by the Triangle Inequality Theorem: the sum of any two side lengths of a triangle must be strictly greater than the third side length. You must check all three combinations — if even one fails, no triangle is possible.

Theorem
Triangle Inequality Theorem

The sum of the lengths of any two sides of a triangle is greater than the length of the third side.

For any triangle with sides a, b, c: a + b > c, a + c > b, and b + c > a.
Example 5.9.1
Checking Whether Three Lengths Form a Triangle
  • ProblemCan 5, 8, and 14 be the sides of a triangle?
  • ThinkCheck all three pairwise sums against the remaining side.
  • Solve 5 + 8 = 13 and 13 > 14? No. 13 < 14. ✗
Answer: No. These lengths cannot form a triangle because 5 + 8 = 13, which is not greater than 14.
Example 5.9.2
Finding the Range of the Third Side
  • ProblemTwo sides of a triangle are 7 and 11. What are the possible lengths for the third side x?
  • ThinkApply the Triangle Inequality to all three combinations involving x.
  • Solve 7 + 11 > x → x < 18 7 + x > 11 → x > 4 11 + x > 7 → x > −4 (always true for positive x) The binding constraints are: 4 < x < 18
  • CheckTry x = 10: 7 + 11 = 18 > 10 ✓, 7 + 10 = 17 > 11 ✓, 11 + 10 = 21 > 7 ✓. Works.
Answer: The third side must satisfy 4 < x < 18 (greater than 4 and less than 18).
Shortcut for the Range

If two sides of a triangle are a and b (with a < b), the third side x must satisfy: (b − a) < x < (a + b). In Example 5.9.2: 11 − 7 = 4 and 7 + 11 = 18, giving 4 < x < 18. This shortcut works because the most restrictive constraints always come from the difference and the sum of the two known sides.

Section 5.10

Side-Angle Inequality Relationships

Within any triangle, the sides and angles are not independent. They are linked: the largest angle is always opposite the longest side, the smallest angle is always opposite the shortest side, and the middle angle is always opposite the middle side. This relationship works in both directions.

Side-Angle Inequality Theorems

Larger Angle → Longer Side: If one angle of a triangle is larger than another, then the side opposite the larger angle is longer than the side opposite the smaller angle.

Longer Side → Larger Angle: If one side of a triangle is longer than another, then the angle opposite the longer side is larger than the angle opposite the shorter side.

Example 5.10.1
Ordering Sides from Angle Measures
  • ProblemIn △ABC, m∠A = 50°, m∠B = 80°, m∠C = 50°. List the sides in order from shortest to longest.
  • ThinkThe longest side is opposite the largest angle. ∠B = 80° is largest, so AC is longest. ∠A = ∠C = 50°, so BC = AB (the triangle is isosceles).
  • Solve ∠B = 80° (largest) → AC is longest ∠A = ∠C = 50° → BC = AB (shortest, tied)
Answer: BC = AB < AC. (The two shorter sides are equal.)
Example 5.10.2
Ordering Angles from Side Lengths
  • ProblemIn △PQR, PQ = 14, QR = 9, PR = 11. List the angles in order from smallest to largest.
  • ThinkSmallest side is opposite smallest angle; largest side is opposite largest angle.
  • Solve Sides ordered: QR = 9 < PR = 11 < PQ = 14 QR is opposite ∠P → ∠P is smallest PR is opposite ∠Q → ∠Q is middle PQ is opposite ∠R → ∠R is largest
Answer: ∠P < ∠Q < ∠R.

Section 5.11

Hinge Theorem

The Hinge Theorem — sometimes called the SAS Inequality — compares two triangles that have two pairs of congruent sides but different included angles. Imagine holding two sides of a triangle fixed and opening or closing the angle between them, like a hinge. When you open the hinge wider, the third side (opposite the hinge) gets longer. When you close the hinge, the third side gets shorter. The Hinge Theorem formalizes this intuition.

Theorem
Hinge Theorem (SAS Inequality)

If two sides of one triangle are congruent to two sides of another triangle, but the included angle of the first triangle is larger than the included angle of the second triangle, then the third side of the first triangle is longer than the third side of the second triangle.

If AB = DE, AC = DF, and m∠A > m∠D, then BC > EF.
Theorem (Converse)
Converse of the Hinge Theorem

If two sides of one triangle are congruent to two sides of another triangle, but the third side of the first is longer than the third side of the second, then the included angle of the first is larger than the included angle of the second.

If AB = DE, AC = DF, and BC > EF, then m∠A > m∠D.
Diagram Instruction

Draw two triangles side by side. Label the first △ABC and the second △DEF. Mark AB = DE and AC = DF with matching tick marks. Draw the included angle at A larger than the included angle at D — show this clearly with angle arcs of different sizes. Draw BC longer than EF and label them to show the inequality. This illustrates that the wider hinge produces the longer opposite side.

Example 5.11.1

  • ProblemIn △ABC and △DEF: AB = DE = 10, AC = DF = 14, m∠A = 72°, m∠D = 55°. Compare BC and EF.
  • ThinkBoth triangles have two pairs of equal sides. Compare the included angles: ∠A = 72° > ∠D = 55°. By the Hinge Theorem, the side opposite the larger angle is longer.
  • SolveAB = DE, AC = DF, and m∠A > m∠D. By the Hinge Theorem: BC > EF.

Example 5.11.2

Section 5.12

Indirect Reasoning as an Introduction

All the proofs you have studied so far have been direct proofs: you start with what is given, apply definitions and theorems step by step, and arrive at what you want to prove. But sometimes a direct path is not obvious — or a statement is difficult to establish by working toward the conclusion. In these cases, mathematicians use a different strategy: indirect reasoning.

An indirect proof (also called proof by contradiction) works as follows. You want to prove that statement P is true. Instead of proving it directly, you temporarily assume that P is false — that is, you assume the negation of P. You then reason carefully from this assumption until you reach a contradiction: a statement that is impossible, or that directly contradicts something you know to be true. Since the assumption of "not P" led to a contradiction, the assumption must be wrong. Therefore P must be true.

Structure of an Indirect Proof

Step 1: Identify the statement you want to prove. Call it P.

Step 2: Assume that "not P" is true (the negation of P).

Step 3: Reason from the assumption, using definitions, theorems, and previously proven results.

Step 4: Arrive at a contradiction — something that is impossible or contradicts a known fact.

Step 5: Conclude that the assumption "not P" must be false, so P must be true.

Example 5.12.1 — Indirect Proof
Prove that a triangle cannot have two obtuse angles.

PlanUse indirect reasoning. Assume the opposite is true — that the triangle does have two obtuse angles — and show this leads to a contradiction.

Student Warning — What "Contradiction" Means

A contradiction in an indirect proof is not merely a surprising result or an unlikely one. It must be a logical impossibility — something that cannot be true given what we already know. The contradiction must directly conflict with a definition, a given fact, a theorem, or the laws of mathematics (such as "angle measures are non-negative"). If your argument merely shows something is unlikely, that is not a valid indirect proof.

Section 5.13

Triangle Relationships in Design and Measurement

The relationships developed in this chapter are not purely theoretical. They appear constantly in structural engineering, architecture, surveying, robotics, and design. Triangles are the fundamental unit of structural strength because a triangle, once its side lengths are fixed, is rigid — it cannot change shape without changing a side length. Quadrilaterals and other polygons can be distorted without changing side lengths, but a triangle cannot. This rigidity is why triangles are used everywhere in structures that must hold their shape under force.

Section 5.14

Common Triangle Relationship Mistakes

The concepts in this chapter involve several distinct definitions that look similar on the surface. Keeping them separate is essential. The following are the most frequent errors students make.

Mistake 1 — Confusing the four triangle centers
"The centroid is equidistant from all three vertices."

The circumcenter is equidistant from the vertices. The centroid is the balance point formed by the medians and divides each median 2:1. Use the table in Section 5.8 to keep all four centers distinct.

Mistake 2 — Applying the centroid ratio backwards
"If GD = 8, then AG = 4 (half of 8)."

The centroid is two-thirds from the vertex, one-third from the midpoint. If GD = 8 (the short segment from centroid to midpoint), then AG = 2 × 8 = 16, not half. Always determine which segment is the vertex-to-centroid piece and which is the centroid-to-midpoint piece before applying the 2:1 ratio.

Mistake 3 — Checking only one inequality in the Triangle Inequality Theorem
"5 + 12 = 17 > 15, so 5, 12, 15 form a triangle. Done."

You must check all three combinations: 5 + 12 > 15 ✓, but also 5 + 15 > 12 ✓, and 12 + 15 > 5 ✓. In this case all three work, but if you stop at the first, you may miss a failing combination in other problems.

Mistake 4 — Confusing the altitude with the median
"The altitude from A goes to the midpoint of BC."

The median from A goes to the midpoint of BC. The altitude from A goes to the foot of the perpendicular from A to line BC. These are different points except in special cases (like equilateral triangles). An altitude is perpendicular to the opposite side; a median bisects the opposite side.

Mistake 5 — Misidentifying the larger angle in the Hinge Theorem
"In the Hinge Theorem, the larger angle gives the shorter third side."

It is the opposite: the larger included angle corresponds to the longer third side. Think of opening a hinge wider — the gap between the free ends increases. The larger angle opens the triangle more, making the opposite side longer.

Mistake 6 — Beginning an indirect proof by assuming the conclusion is true
"To prove ∠A > ∠B, assume ∠A > ∠B."

An indirect proof begins by assuming the negation — the opposite — of what you want to prove. To prove ∠A > ∠B, assume ∠A ≤ ∠B (not greater than). Then derive a contradiction from that assumption.

Mistake 7 — Claiming the circumcenter is always inside the triangle
"The circumcenter is always inside because you draw the perpendicular bisectors inside the triangle."

The circumcenter is inside only for acute triangles. For right triangles, it is on the hypotenuse. For obtuse triangles, it is outside. You must extend the perpendicular bisectors beyond the triangle to find the circumcenter in those cases.

Chapter 5 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 5.1A perpendicular bisector passes through the midpoint of a segment and is perpendicular to it. Every point on the perpendicular bisector is equidistant from the two endpoints. The converse also holds.
  • Sec 5.2An angle bisector divides an angle into two congruent halves. Every point on the angle bisector is equidistant (perpendicularly) from the two sides of the angle. The converse also holds.
  • Sec 5.3A median connects a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. Each triangle has three medians. Medians are not necessarily perpendicular to the opposite side.
  • Sec 5.4An altitude is a perpendicular segment from a vertex to the line containing the opposite side. In acute triangles, all altitudes are inside. In right triangles, two altitudes are the legs. In obtuse triangles, two altitudes fall outside.
  • Sec 5.5The circumcenter is the intersection of the three perpendicular bisectors. It is equidistant from all three vertices and is the center of the circumscribed circle. It is inside for acute, on the hypotenuse for right, and outside for obtuse triangles.
  • Sec 5.6The incenter is the intersection of the three angle bisectors. It is equidistant from all three sides and is the center of the inscribed circle. It is always inside the triangle.
  • Sec 5.7The centroid is the intersection of the three medians. It divides each median 2:1 from vertex to midpoint. It is always inside and is the triangle's balance point.
  • Sec 5.8The orthocenter is the intersection of the three altitudes. It is inside for acute, at the right-angle vertex for right, and outside for obtuse triangles. It has no simple equidistance property.
  • Sec 5.9The Triangle Inequality Theorem: the sum of any two sides of a triangle must be greater than the third. If two sides are known, the third must be between their difference and their sum.
  • Sec 5.10The largest angle of a triangle is opposite the longest side; the smallest angle is opposite the shortest side. This relationship is bidirectional: larger angle ↔ longer opposite side.
  • Sec 5.11The Hinge Theorem: if two triangles share two pairs of equal sides, the triangle with the larger included angle has the longer third side. The converse also holds.
  • Sec 5.12Indirect proof assumes the negation of the desired conclusion, reasons to a contradiction, and concludes that the assumption was false. The contradiction must be a logical impossibility, not merely an unlikely result.
  • Sec 5.13Triangle centers appear in engineering (circumcenter for equidistant positioning, centroid for balance, incenter for inscribed fitting). The Triangle Inequality governs which structural lengths are feasible. The Hinge Theorem governs hinged mechanical systems.
  • Sec 5.14Common errors include confusing the four centers, applying the centroid ratio backwards, not checking all three Triangle Inequality combinations, confusing medians with altitudes, misidentifying which angle is larger in the Hinge Theorem, and incorrectly beginning an indirect proof by assuming the conclusion rather than its negation.

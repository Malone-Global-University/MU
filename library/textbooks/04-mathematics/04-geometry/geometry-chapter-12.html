Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 11 developed the measurement of flat surfaces: area and surface area. This chapter moves into the third dimension. Volume measures how much three-dimensional space an object occupies, and understanding volume requires thinking about shape, depth, cross section, and the structure of solids.

Students who understand area well are ready for volume. The essential insight is that most volume formulas grow directly from area: take the area of a base, multiply by height, and you fill a solid with layers. From there, pyramids, cones, and spheres follow from that same foundation with elegant adjustments.

Chapter 12 also develops broader spatial reasoning. Cross sections reveal the inner structure of solids. Scaling shows that doubling a length does not simply double volume. Density connects volume to physical substance. These ideas appear in architecture, manufacturing, engineering, medicine, packaging, chemistry, physics, and construction.

Opening Question

A cylindrical water tank and a rectangular tank are both advertised as holding 500 gallons. A manufacturing plant needs to choose between them for a tight installation space. Which shape fits a narrow room better? Does changing the height versus the radius affect the volume differently? Can a small change in radius produce a large gain in capacity? These questions require volume reasoning. By the end of Chapter 12, you will have the tools to answer them precisely.

Section 12.1

Three-Dimensional Figures

Geometry began with points, lines, and planes. From there, the book built into flat, two-dimensional shapes: segments, angles, polygons, circles. In Chapter 12, the setting changes. Three-dimensional figures occupy space in all three directions — length, width, and height. They have volume, not just area.

Polyhedra

A polyhedron is a solid bounded entirely by flat faces. Each face is a polygon. Two faces meet along a line segment called an edge. Three or more edges meet at a point called a vertex. Every polyhedron satisfies Euler's Formula, which relates its counts of faces, edges, and vertices:

Euler's Formula for Polyhedra

F + V − E = 2

where F = number of faces, V = number of vertices, E = number of edges.

Example: A rectangular prism has 6 faces, 8 vertices, and 12 edges. Check: 6 + 8 − 12 = 2 ✓

Draw a triangular prism. Shade the two triangular bases with light hatching. Label the three rectangular lateral faces. Mark the lateral edges — the three vertical segments connecting the two triangular bases. Label one vertex where three edges meet.

Naming Prisms and Pyramids

Prisms and pyramids are named by the shape of their base. A prism with a hexagonal base is a hexagonal prism. A pyramid with a rectangular base is a rectangular pyramid. The base shape determines the name — not the shape of the lateral faces.

Section 12.2

Nets and Cross Sections

Nets

A net is a flat pattern that can be folded into a three-dimensional solid. Nets help make the connection between area and surface area visible, and they help students understand how the faces of a solid relate to each other.

Example 12.2.1
Drawing and interpreting a net for a rectangular prism
  • ProblemDescribe the net for a rectangular prism with length 5, width 3, and height 2.
  • ThinkA rectangular prism has six rectangular faces. Unfolding the prism flat produces a cross-shaped pattern.
  • RepresentThe bottom face is a 5 × 3 rectangle at the center. Above it: a 5 × 2 front face. Below it: a 5 × 2 back face. Left side: a 3 × 2 face. Right side: a 3 × 2 face. On top of the front face: the 5 × 3 top face.
  • CheckCount six faces. Verify matching faces have equal dimensions: two 5 × 3, two 5 × 2, two 3 × 2. ✓
Answer: Six rectangles of dimensions 5×3, 5×3, 5×2, 5×2, 3×2, and 3×2.
Tip — Validating a Net

Not every flat arrangement of faces is a valid net. A net must fold without overlap and cover the solid completely. When you sketch a net, mentally fold each flap to check it lands in the correct position.

Cross Sections

A cross section is the figure produced when a plane slices through a solid. The shape of a cross section depends on the angle of the cut and the shape of the solid. Cross sections reveal the internal structure of a solid and appear in engineering, medicine, geology, and design.

Example 12.2.2
Identifying a cross section of a pyramid
  • ProblemA plane cuts a square pyramid horizontally, parallel to the base, halfway between the base and the apex. What shape is the cross section?
  • ThinkWhen a horizontal plane cuts a pyramid parallel to the base, the cross section is similar to the base — same shape, smaller size.
  • AnswerA square, smaller than and similar to the base.
Section 12.3

What Volume Measures

Volume is the amount of three-dimensional space that an object occupies or a container encloses. While area measures how much flat surface a figure covers, volume measures how much space a solid fills. The natural unit for volume is a unit cube: a cube with side length 1. A region with volume n can be filled with n unit cubes without overlap or gaps.

Because volume is built from three linear dimensions, volume units are always cubic units. If lengths are in centimeters, volume is in cubic centimeters (cm³). If lengths are in feet, volume is in cubic feet (ft³).

The Layer Idea — Foundation of All Volume Formulas

Imagine stacking thin horizontal layers inside a solid. Each layer has cross-sectional area equal to the area of the base B. The total volume equals the base area times the height.

V = Bh

where B is the area of the base and h is the perpendicular height. This single formula is the foundation for all prism and cylinder volumes. Pyramids, cones, and spheres build from it with further reasoning.

Crucial Distinction — Units

Length is in linear units (cm, ft, m). Area is in square units (cm², ft², m²). Volume is in cubic units (cm³, ft³, m³). Never mix or mislabel these.

Section 12.4

Volume of Prisms

For any prism, the volume is V = Bh, where B is the area of the polygonal base and h is the perpendicular height between the two bases. The key step is always to identify the shape of the base, compute B using the correct area formula, and then multiply by h.

Base ShapeBase Area Formula
Rectangle (length ℓ, width w)B = ℓw
Triangle (base b, height h_b)B = ½b·h_b
Trapezoid (bases b₁, b₂, height h_b)B = ½(b₁ + b₂)h_b
Regular Hexagon (side s)B = (3√3 / 2)s²
Example 12.4.1
Volume of a rectangular prism
  • ProblemFind the volume of a rectangular prism with length 8 cm, width 5 cm, and height 4 cm.
  • RepresentB = ℓw = 8 × 5 = 40 cm²
  • SolveV = Bh = 40 × 4 = 160 cm³
  • CheckAlso: V = ℓwh = 8 × 5 × 4 = 160 cm³ ✓
Answer: 160 cm³
Example 12.4.2
Volume of a triangular prism
  • ProblemA triangular prism has a triangular base with base 6 m and height 4 m. The prism's depth is 10 m. Find the volume.
  • RepresentB = ½(6)(4) = 12 m²
  • SolveV = Bh = 12 × 10 = 120 m³
  • CheckThe triangular base covers 12 square meters. Ten layers of that base fill 120 cubic meters. ✓
Answer: 120 m³
Example 12.4.3
Finding a missing dimension
  • ProblemA rectangular prism has base area 24 ft² and volume 96 ft³. Find the height.
  • Solve96 = 24h h = 96 ÷ 24 = 4 ft
Answer: The height is 4 ft.
Student Warning — Identify the Correct Base

In a triangular prism, the base is a triangle — not one of the rectangular lateral faces. Always ask: which face defines the cross-sectional shape that is repeated along the height? That face is the base. Calculate B for that shape before applying V = Bh.

Section 12.5

Volume of Cylinders

A cylinder's base is a circle with radius r. Since the base area is B = πr², the volume formula becomes V = πr²h, where r is the radius and h is the perpendicular height.

Example 12.5.1
Volume of a cylinder
  • ProblemFind the volume of a cylinder with radius 3 in and height 10 in. Leave in terms of π.
  • SolveV = π(3)²(10) = π · 9 · 10 = 90π in³
Answer: 90π ≈ 282.7 in³
Example 12.5.2
Finding the radius from volume
  • ProblemA cylinder has height 8 cm and volume 200π cm³. Find the radius.
  • Solve200π = π r²(8) 200π = 8π r² r² = 200π ÷ 8π = 25 r = 5 cm
  • CheckV = π(5)²(8) = 200π ✓
Answer: The radius is 5 cm.
Example 12.5.3
Comparing two cylinders
  • ProblemCylinder A: radius 2 ft, height 9 ft. Cylinder B: radius 3 ft, height 4 ft. Which has greater volume?
  • SolveV_A = π(2)²(9) = 36π ft³ V_B = π(3)²(4) = 36π ft³
Answer: Both have equal volume, 36π ft³. Different shapes can enclose equal volumes.
Section 12.6

Volume of Pyramids

A pyramid fits inside a prism with the same base and height. Through geometric reasoning, a pyramid's volume is exactly one-third of the prism with the same base and height. This can be shown by dividing a cube into exactly three congruent pyramids, or more rigorously using Cavalieri's Principle (Section 12.10) or integral calculus.

Volume of a Pyramid

V = ⅓Bh

where B is the area of the polygonal base and h is the perpendicular height from the base to the apex. The ⅓ factor applies to all pyramids, regardless of the base shape.

Example 12.6.1
Volume of a square pyramid
  • ProblemA square pyramid has base side 6 ft and height 10 ft. Find the volume.
  • RepresentB = 6² = 36 ft²
  • SolveV = ⅓(36)(10) = 360/3 = 120 ft³
Answer: 120 ft³
Example 12.6.2
Volume of a triangular pyramid
  • ProblemA triangular pyramid has a triangular base with base 8 m and base height 5 m. The pyramid's height is 9 m. Find the volume.
  • RepresentB = ½(8)(5) = 20 m²
  • SolveV = ⅓(20)(9) = 180/3 = 60 m³
Answer: 60 m³
Example 12.6.3
Finding the height of a pyramid
  • ProblemA square pyramid has base area 49 cm² and volume 343/3 cm³. Find the height.
  • Solve343/3 = ⅓(49)h 343 = 49h h = 7 cm
Answer: The height is 7 cm.
Height vs. Slant Height

Do not confuse the perpendicular height of the pyramid (straight-line distance from base to apex, measured at a right angle) with the slant height (distance from the apex down a lateral face to a base edge). Volume uses perpendicular height. Slant height appears in surface area. If you are given slant height and need perpendicular height, use the Pythagorean Theorem.

Section 12.7

Volume of Cones

Just as the pyramid formula is one-third of the prism formula, the cone formula is one-third of the cylinder formula. Since the base is a circle with area πr²:

Volume of a Cone

V = ⅓πr²h

where r is the radius of the circular base and h is the perpendicular height from the base to the apex.

Example 12.7.1
Volume of a cone
  • ProblemFind the volume of a cone with radius 5 cm and height 12 cm.
  • SolveV = ⅓π(5)²(12) = ⅓π(25)(12) = 300π/3 = 100π cm³
Answer: 100π ≈ 314.2 cm³
Example 12.7.2
Finding the radius from the volume of a cone
  • ProblemA cone has height 6 m and volume 24π m³. Find the radius.
  • Solve24π = ⅓πr²(6) 24π = 2πr² r² = 24π ÷ 2π = 12 r = √12 = 2√3 m
Answer: r = 2√3 ≈ 3.46 m
Example 12.7.3
Comparing a cone and cylinder with the same base
  • ProblemA cylinder and a cone share the same base and height. The cylinder has volume 300 cm³. Find the cone's volume.
  • ThinkA cone always has volume exactly ⅓ of the cylinder with the same base and height.
  • SolveV_cone = ⅓ × 300 = 100 cm³
Answer: The cone has volume 100 cm³.
Section 12.8

Volume of Spheres

The volume of a sphere depends only on its radius. The formula cannot be derived from simple layering without calculus, but Cavalieri's Principle (Section 12.10) provides a strong geometric justification. Notice that the sphere formula involves r³ — because volume scales with the cube of the linear dimension.

Volume of a Sphere

V = (4/3)πr³

where r is the radius. A hemisphere (half sphere) has volume (2/3)πr³.

Example 12.8.1
Volume of a sphere
  • ProblemFind the volume of a sphere with radius 6 ft. Round to the nearest tenth.
  • SolveV = (4/3)π(6)³ = (4/3)π(216) = 864π/3 = 288π ≈ 904.8 ft³
Answer: 288π ≈ 904.8 ft³
Example 12.8.2
Finding the radius from the volume of a sphere
  • ProblemA sphere has volume 500π/3 cm³. Find the radius.
  • Solve500π/3 = (4/3)πr³ Multiply both sides by 3/(4π): r³ = (500π/3) · (3/4π) = 500/4 = 125 r = ∛125 = 5 cm
Answer: r = 5 cm
Example 12.8.3
Volume of a hemisphere
  • ProblemFind the volume of a hemisphere with radius 4 m.
  • ThinkA hemisphere is half of a sphere. Use V = (2/3)πr³.
  • SolveV = (2/3)π(4)³ = (2/3)π(64) = 128π/3 ≈ 134.0 m³
Answer: 128π/3 ≈ 134.0 m³
Section 12.9

Composite Solids

Many real objects are combinations or subtractions of multiple simpler solids joined together. The approach: break the composite figure into recognizable parts, find the volume of each, then add or subtract as needed.

Composite Solid Strategy

Addition: When solids are joined, add their volumes.

V_composite = V₁ + V₂ + ...

Subtraction: When a region is removed from a solid, subtract.

V_composite = V_outer − V_removed

Example 12.9.1
Cylinder with a cone on top
  • ProblemA cylinder has radius 4 cm and height 10 cm. A cone with the same radius and height 6 cm sits on top. Find the total volume.
  • SolveV_cylinder = π(4)²(10) = 160π cm³ V_cone = ⅓π(4)²(6) = 32π cm³ V_total = 160π + 32π = 192π ≈ 603.2 cm³
Answer: 192π ≈ 603.2 cm³
Example 12.9.2
Rectangular prism with a cylindrical hole
  • ProblemA rectangular prism (12 × 8 × 6 cm) has a cylindrical hole (radius 2 cm, depth 6 cm) bored through its height. Find the remaining volume.
  • SolveV_prism = 12 × 8 × 6 = 576 cm³ V_cylinder = π(2)²(6) = 24π ≈ 75.4 cm³ V_remaining = 576 − 24π ≈ 500.6 cm³
Answer: 576 − 24π ≈ 500.6 cm³
Example 12.9.3
Silo shape — cylinder and hemisphere
  • ProblemA grain silo is a cylinder (radius 5 m, height 20 m) topped by a hemisphere (radius 5 m). Find the total volume.
  • SolveV_cylinder = π(5)²(20) = 500π m³ V_hemisphere = (2/3)π(5)³ = 250π/3 m³ V_total = 500π + 250π/3 = 1500π/3 + 250π/3 = 1750π/3 ≈ 1832.6 m³
Answer: 1750π/3 ≈ 1832.6 m³
Diagram Instruction

Draw a tall vertical cylinder with a dome (hemisphere) sitting on top, sharing the same circular rim. Label the cylinder's radius r and height h. Label the hemisphere's radius r (same as the cylinder). Show the total height as h plus r.

Section 12.10

Cavalieri's Principle as an Introduction

Cavalieri's Principle

If two solids have the same height and, at every level parallel to the base, the cross-sectional areas of the two solids are equal, then the two solids have equal volumes.

This principle — named for Italian mathematician Bonaventura Cavalieri (1598–1647) — explains why volume formulas hold even for oblique (leaning) solids. A right cylinder and an oblique cylinder with the same radius and perpendicular height have identical circular cross sections at every level. By Cavalieri's Principle, they have identical volumes.

Cavalieri's Principle also explains why the formula V = ⅓Bh applies to all pyramids, not just right pyramids. Any oblique pyramid can be compared to a right pyramid with equal cross sections at every height. The full justification of the sphere formula using this principle awaits Calculus I.

Diagram Instruction

Draw two cylinders side by side. The left is a right cylinder — perfectly upright. The right is an oblique cylinder — leaning at an angle. Both have the same radius and the same perpendicular height h. Draw horizontal dashed lines at several levels cutting through both. At each level, both cross sections are circles of the same radius. Label the perpendicular height h on both, and note that the slant edge of the oblique cylinder is longer than h.

Perpendicular Height, Not Slant Length

In an oblique prism or cylinder, always use the perpendicular height — the straight-line distance between the two bases, measured at a right angle — not the length of the slanted lateral edge. The lateral edge of an oblique solid is always longer than the perpendicular height.

Example 12.10.1
Applying Cavalieri's Principle
  • ProblemA right cylinder and an oblique cylinder both have radius 3 cm and perpendicular height 8 cm. Do they have the same volume?
  • ThinkAt every horizontal level, both cylinders have a circular cross section of radius 3 cm. The heights are equal. By Cavalieri's Principle, the volumes are equal.
  • SolveV = π(3)²(8) = 72π cm³
Answer: Yes. Both cylinders have volume 72π cm³.
Section 12.11

Scaling Length, Area, and Volume

When a solid is scaled (enlarged or reduced) by a scale factor k, the effect on length, area, and volume is different because each measurement has a different dimension. This is one of the most important — and counterintuitive — ideas in three-dimensional geometry.

MeasurementDimensionEffect of Scale Factor kExample (k = 3)
Length1-dimensionalMultiply by k3 × original length
Area / Surface Area2-dimensionalMultiply by k²9 × original area
Volume3-dimensionalMultiply by k³27 × original volume
Example 12.11.1
Scaling a cube
  • ProblemA cube has side length 3 cm. A second cube has side length 6 cm. Compare their volumes.
  • ThinkScale factor k = 6/3 = 2. Volume should scale by k³ = 8.
  • SolveV_small = 3³ = 27 cm³ V_large = 6³ = 216 cm³ Ratio = 216/27 = 8 = 2³ ✓
Answer: Doubling the side length multiplies the volume by 8.
Example 12.11.2
Scaling a cylinder
  • ProblemA cylinder has radius 4 m and height 5 m. Every dimension is multiplied by a scale factor of 3. Find both volumes and compare.
  • SolveV_original = π(4)²(5) = 80π m³ New radius = 12 m, new height = 15 m V_scaled = π(12)²(15) = 2160π m³ Ratio = 2160π / 80π = 27 = 3³ ✓
Answer: Tripling every dimension multiplies the volume by 27.
Example 12.11.3
Reverse scaling — finding the scale factor from a volume ratio
  • ProblemTwo similar pyramids have volumes 125 ft³ and 8 ft³. Find the ratio of their corresponding lengths.
  • ThinkVolumes scale as k³. Find k, then the length ratio is k.
  • Solvek³ = 125/8 k = ∛(125/8) = 5/2
Answer: The ratio of corresponding lengths is 5/2.
Section 12.12

Density and Capacity Applications

Density

The Density Formula

D = m / V    (Density = mass ÷ volume)

Rearranged:  m = DV    and    V = m / D

Common units: g/cm³, kg/m³. Use whichever form matches what is given and what is needed.

Example 12.12.1
Finding mass from volume and density
  • ProblemA solid aluminum cylinder has radius 3 cm and height 8 cm. Density of aluminum is 2.7 g/cm³. Find the mass.
  • Step 1 — VolumeV = π(3)²(8) = 72π ≈ 226.2 cm³
  • Step 2 — Massm = DV = 2.7 × 226.2 ≈ 610.7 g
Answer: Approximately 610.7 grams.
Example 12.12.2
Finding volume from mass and density
  • ProblemA piece of oak wood has mass 900 g. Density of oak is 0.75 g/cm³. What volume does the oak occupy?
  • SolveV = m / D = 900 / 0.75 = 1200 cm³
Answer: The oak occupies 1200 cm³.

Capacity

Capacity is the maximum volume a container can hold, often expressed in liquid units. Common conversions: 1 liter = 1000 cm³; 1 gallon ≈ 231 in³; 1 cubic foot ≈ 7.48 gallons.

Example 12.12.3
Finding the capacity of a cylindrical tank
  • ProblemA cylindrical water tank has radius 1.5 m and height 4 m. How many liters can it hold? (1 m³ = 1000 liters)
  • Step 1 — VolumeV = π(1.5)²(4) = 9π ≈ 28.27 m³
  • Step 2 — Convert28.27 m³ × 1000 liters/m³ ≈ 28,270 liters
Answer: The tank holds approximately 28,270 liters.

Section 12.13

Common Volume Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Using diameter instead of radius
V = π(10)²(5) when the problem says diameter = 10

Correct: Radius = diameter ÷ 2 = 5. So V = π(5)²(5) = 125π. Always divide by 2 before substituting into any circle-based formula.

Mistake 2 — Forgetting the ⅓ factor for pyramids and cones
V = π(3)²(7) = 63π for a cone with radius 3 and height 7

Correct: V = ⅓π(3)²(7) = 21π. The ⅓ is required for every pyramid and cone, without exception.

Mistake 3 — Using slant height instead of perpendicular height
V = ⅓π(5)²(13) for a cone with slant height 13 and radius 5

Correct: First find perpendicular height using the Pythagorean Theorem: h² + 5² = 13², so h² = 144, h = 12. Then V = ⅓π(5)²(12) = 100π. Volume formulas always use perpendicular height.

Mistake 4 — Forgetting to cube the radius in the sphere formula
V = (4/3)π(4)² = 64π/3 for a sphere with radius 4

Correct: V = (4/3)π(4)³ = (4/3)π(64) = 256π/3. The sphere formula uses r³, not r².

Mistake 5 — Assuming volume scales linearly with the scale factor
"If the radius doubles, the volume of a sphere doubles."

Correct: If radius doubles, volume multiplies by 2³ = 8. For a cylinder, doubling only the radius multiplies volume by 2² = 4 (because r appears squared in πr²h). Always track which dimensions are being scaled and by what power they appear in the formula.

Mistake 6 — Mixing up volume and surface area
Computing surface area when volume is requested (or vice versa)

Volume is the space inside a solid — always in cubic units. Surface area is the total area of all faces — always in square units. They are fundamentally different measurements. Read the problem carefully before choosing a formula.

Mistake 7 — Using mismatched units without converting first
V = π(3 in)²(2 ft) = 18π without converting

Correct: Convert all dimensions to the same unit before computing. 2 ft = 24 in. Then V = π(3)²(24) = 216π in³.

Mistake 8 — Choosing the wrong face as the base of a prism
Using a rectangular lateral face as the base of a triangular prism

The base is the polygon that defines the cross section repeated along the height — in a triangular prism, that is the triangular face. Compute B for the triangle, then multiply by the prism's length (height).

Section 12.14

Preparing for Geometry in Physics and Engineering

Chapter 12 has developed the tools for three-dimensional measurement: identifying solids, finding volumes, combining solids, applying Cavalieri's Principle, scaling, and connecting volume to density and capacity. These tools bridge directly into technical and scientific applications.

Physics uses volume to find mass (density × volume), to study pressure in fluids, to analyze thermodynamic processes, and to model spherical and cylindrical charge distributions. Engineering requires cylinder, prism, pyramid, and sphere calculations for sizing pipes, tanks, beams, foundations, and structural members. Architecture calculates interior volume, material quantities, and HVAC capacity based on room geometry. Manufacturing uses composite solid reasoning to analyze machined parts with drilled holes, cast fillets, and added features. Biology uses volume and surface area to understand cell efficiency, and medicine uses volumetric imaging to interpret scans.

  • Sec 12.2Nets unfold a solid into a flat diagram showing all faces. Cross sections reveal shapes produced when planes slice through solids. Horizontal cuts parallel to the base produce shapes similar to the base; vertical cuts reveal profiles through the interior.
  • Sec 12.3Volume measures three-dimensional space, always in cubic units. The core idea is layering: volume equals base area times height. V = Bh is the foundation for all prism and cylinder volumes.
  • Sec 12.4Prism volume: V = Bh, where B is the area of the polygonal base and h is the perpendicular height. Always identify the base shape, compute B using the correct area formula, then multiply by h.
  • Sec 12.5Cylinder volume: V = πr²h. Identify radius (not diameter), height, and substitute. The formula follows directly from V = Bh with B = πr².
  • Sec 12.6Pyramid volume: V = ⅓Bh. The ⅓ factor applies to all pyramids regardless of base shape. Perpendicular height is used, never slant height.
  • Sec 12.7Cone volume: V = ⅓πr²h. The cone is to the cylinder as the pyramid is to the prism: exactly one-third the volume for the same base and height.
  • Sec 12.8Sphere volume: V = (4/3)πr³. Notice r³, not r². A hemisphere is half a sphere: V = (2/3)πr³.
  • Sec 12.9Composite solids are analyzed by adding or subtracting simpler volumes. Identify each component solid, find its volume, and combine appropriately.
  • Sec 12.10Cavalieri's Principle: two solids with equal height and equal cross-sectional area at every level have equal volume. This justifies using V = Bh for oblique prisms and cylinders, and V = ⅓Bh for oblique pyramids.
  • Sec 12.11Scaling by factor k: length multiplies by k, area by k², volume by k³. To find the scale factor from a volume ratio, take the cube root. This is one of the most important — and counterintuitive — ideas in spatial geometry.
  • Sec 12.12Density links volume to mass: D = m/V, so m = DV and V = m/D. Capacity describes how much a container holds and is often converted between cubic units and liquid units using standard ratios.
  • Sec 12.13Eight recurring errors: using diameter instead of radius; forgetting ⅓; using slant height; cubing incorrectly; assuming linear volume scaling; confusing volume and surface area; mixing units; misidentifying the base.
  • Sec 12.14Volume reasoning appears throughout physics, engineering, architecture, biology, manufacturing, and design. Chapter 12 builds the foundation for Calculus I volume integrals, Physics density problems, and Engineering design calculations.

Key Terms Review

polyhedron
A solid bounded entirely by flat polygonal faces. Satisfies Euler's Formula: F + V − E = 2.
prism
A polyhedron with two congruent parallel polygonal bases connected by rectangular lateral faces (in a right prism).
pyramid
A polyhedron with one polygonal base and triangular lateral faces meeting at a single apex point.
cylinder
A solid with two congruent circular bases connected by a curved lateral surface.
cone
A solid with one circular base and a curved lateral surface tapering to a single apex.
sphere
The set of all points in space at distance r from a fixed center point. V = (4/3)πr³.
net
A flat diagram that folds into a three-dimensional solid, showing every face in its correct relationship.
cross section
The intersection of a solid with a plane. Shape depends on solid type and cut angle.
volume
The measure of space inside a three-dimensional solid. Always expressed in cubic units.
apex
The top point of a pyramid or cone, where all lateral faces or the curved surface converges.
slant height
The distance from the apex to a base edge along a lateral face. Used in surface area, not volume.
composite solid
A solid formed by combining or subtracting simpler solids. Volume found by adding or subtracting component volumes.
Cavalieri's Principle
If two solids have equal height and equal cross-sectional areas at every level, their volumes are equal.
scale factor
The ratio k by which lengths are multiplied. Area scales by k², volume scales by k³.
density
Ratio of mass to volume: D = m/V. Common units: g/cm³ or kg/m³.
capacity
The maximum volume a container can hold. Often expressed in liquid units: liters, gallons.

Real-World Applications

  1. Architecture: A concrete column is a cylinder with radius 0.5 m and height 4 m. Concrete has density 2400 kg/m³. Find the mass of one column.
  2. Manufacturing: A machined part is a rectangular prism (12 × 8 × 5 cm) with a cylinder (radius 2 cm, height 5 cm) bored through the center. Find the volume of material remaining.
  3. Agriculture: A grain bin is a cylinder (radius 4 m, height 6 m) topped by a cone (same radius, height 2 m). What is the total volume the bin can hold?
  4. Physics: An iron sphere has radius 6 cm. Iron has density 7.87 g/cm³. Find the mass to the nearest gram.
  5. Packaging: A sphere of radius 4 cm sits inside a cubic box. What is the minimum side length of the cube? What percentage of the box's volume does the sphere occupy? Round to the nearest percent.
  6. Engineering: An engineer scales a design so every linear dimension is multiplied by 1.2. By what factor does the volume of the part increase?
  7. Capacity: A water tower is a sphere with radius 5 m. How many liters can it hold? (1 m³ = 1000 liters.) Round to the nearest liter.
  8. Geology: A cylindrical core sample has radius 3 cm and height 20 cm. The rock has density 2.65 g/cm³. Find the mass to the nearest gram.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 13: Coordinate Geometry and Algebraic Proof

Chapter 12 closes the part on polygons, circles, area, and volume. The tools developed here — identifying solids, computing volume, analyzing cross sections, applying Cavalieri's Principle, scaling, and working with density and capacity — are now part of your permanent geometric toolkit.

Chapter 13 will bring algebra and geometry together in a new way. The coordinate plane becomes the setting for proving geometric facts about triangles, quadrilaterals, and circles using distance, slope, and midpoint formulas. Many solid shapes from Chapter 12 have coordinate-plane cross sections that can be described algebraically, connecting three-dimensional thinking to equations on the plane.

In Algebra II, polynomial functions will model volume relationships — for example, expressing the volume of a box as a function of a variable length leads directly to polynomial optimization. In Calculus I, the volume formulas for cones, pyramids, and spheres are derived rigorously using integration. The layer idea from Section 12.3 is exactly the foundation for disk and shell methods.

Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Geometry began in this book with points, lines, planes, angles, and diagrams drawn on paper. Since then, you have classified triangles, proven congruence and similarity, worked with right triangles and trigonometry, studied polygons and circles, and calculated area and volume. Each of those topics used geometric reasoning — reasoning based on shapes, relationships, and logical argument.

Chapter 13 brings a powerful new tool to all of that work: the coordinate plane. When you place a geometric figure on a coordinate plane, every point becomes an ordered pair, every segment becomes a measurable distance, every midpoint becomes a calculable location, and every relationship between lines becomes a relationship between slopes. Suddenly, algebra can do what diagrams could only suggest: it can measure, verify, and prove geometric facts with arithmetic precision.

This chapter is the clearest bridge in the entire book between Algebra I and Geometry. You will review the distance formula, midpoint formula, and slope, then use them as tools for geometric reasoning. You will classify triangles and quadrilaterals using coordinates. You will write coordinate proofs — formal arguments based on algebraic calculations rather than on diagram reasoning alone. And you will prove congruence and similarity using the algebra of coordinates.

Opening Question

A city planner is designing a park. She places the park's boundary on a coordinate grid, with corners at A(0, 0), B(6, 0), C(6, 4), and D(0, 4). She wants to know: What is the length of each side? Where is the center of the park? Is the park a rectangle, or only a parallelogram? Are the diagonals equal in length?

Every one of these questions can be answered using only algebra — the distance formula, the midpoint formula, and slope. No protractor, no compass. Just coordinates and calculation. This is what coordinate geometry does. It turns spatial questions into algebraic ones.

Section 13.1

Why Coordinate Geometry Matters

Pure geometry is powerful: it describes shape, proves relationships, and reasons from diagrams. But it works in the abstract. When you say that triangle ABC is congruent to triangle DEF, you have proven a logical relationship — but you cannot measure the exact position of each vertex, the exact length of each side, or the exact coordinates of the centroid without a measurement system.

The coordinate plane supplies that measurement system. When a geometric figure is placed on a coordinate plane, every point gets an address — an ordered pair (x, y). Once points have addresses, distances become computable, midpoints become findable, slopes become calculable, and equations become available to describe every line, circle, and curve. This is the central idea: coordinates turn geometric relationships into algebraic relationships.

Geometric QuestionAlgebraic Answer
How long is this segment?Distance formula: d = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]
Where is the halfway point?Midpoint formula: M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)
Are these lines parallel?Compare slopes: m₁ = m₂
Do these sides meet at 90°?Check: m₁ · m₂ = −1
What shape is this quadrilateral?Use distance, slope, and midpoint together
Historical Note

The coordinate system we use was formalized by the French mathematician René Descartes in the seventeenth century. Before Descartes, algebra and geometry were studied as entirely separate fields. His insight — that curves can be described by equations and equations can be visualized as curves — made calculus possible. The system is named the Cartesian coordinate system in his honor.

Section 13.2

Distance Formula Review

The distance formula is simply the Pythagorean Theorem applied to the coordinate plane. If you want to find the distance between two points P₁(x₁, y₁) and P₂(x₂, y₂), build a right triangle: draw a horizontal leg of length |x₂ − x₁| and a vertical leg of length |y₂ − y₁|. The segment P₁P₂ is the hypotenuse.

The Distance Formula
d = √[ (x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)² ]
Derived from a² + b² = c². Squaring removes all negative signs, so order of subtraction does not affect the result.
Diagram Instruction

Plot P₁(1, 2) and P₂(5, 5) on a coordinate plane. Draw a horizontal dashed segment from P₁ to (5, 2), and a vertical dashed segment from (5, 2) to P₂. Label the horizontal leg 4 and the vertical leg 3. Label the hypotenuse d. Mark the right angle at (5, 2). This is the right triangle whose hypotenuse gives the distance between P₁ and P₂.

Example 13.2.1
Finding a segment length — whole number answer
  • ProblemFind the length of AB if A(2, 1) and B(8, 9).
  • ThinkI know two endpoints. I will apply the distance formula directly.
  • Solve d = √[(8 − 2)² + (9 − 1)²] = √[6² + 8²] = √[36 + 64] = √100 = 10
  • Check6-8-10 is a Pythagorean triple (a multiple of 3-4-5). The answer is reasonable.
Answer: AB = 10
Example 13.2.2
Finding an exact length in radical form
  • ProblemFind the distance between P(−3, 2) and Q(1, −4).
  • ThinkThe answer may not simplify to a whole number. I will leave it in simplified radical form.
  • Solve d = √[(1 − (−3))² + (−4 − 2)²] = √[4² + (−6)²] = √[16 + 36] = √52 = √(4 · 13) = 2√13
  • Check√52 ≈ 7.2. The horizontal distance is 4 and the vertical distance is 6, so a distance of about 7.2 is reasonable.
Answer: PQ = 2√13
Student Warning

Do not add the differences before squaring. The formula requires squaring each difference separately: (x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)² ≠ (x₂ − x₁ + y₂ − y₁)². Adding first is one of the most common distance formula errors.

Section 13.3

Midpoint Formula Review

The midpoint of a segment is the point exactly halfway between the two endpoints. On a number line, the midpoint between two values is their average. The same idea extends to two dimensions: take the average of the x-coordinates and the average of the y-coordinates.

The Midpoint Formula
M = ( (x₁ + x₂) / 2 , (y₁ + y₂) / 2 )
Average the x-coordinates; average the y-coordinates. The result is the coordinates of the midpoint.
Example 13.3.1
Finding a midpoint
  • ProblemFind the midpoint of CD if C(4, 6) and D(10, 2).
  • Solve M = ((4 + 10)/2, (6 + 2)/2) = (14/2, 8/2) = (7, 4)
  • CheckIs (7, 4) between C(4, 6) and D(10, 2)? The x-coordinate 7 is between 4 and 10. The y-coordinate 4 is between 6 and 2. Yes. ✓
Answer: The midpoint of CD is (7, 4).
Example 13.3.2
Using the midpoint to find a missing endpoint
  • ProblemThe midpoint of EF is M(3, −1). One endpoint is E(1, 2). Find F.
  • ThinkI know M and E. Set up two equations using the midpoint formula — one for x, one for y.
  • Solve x: (1 + x₂)/2 = 3 → 1 + x₂ = 6 → x₂ = 5 y: (2 + y₂)/2 = −1 → 2 + y₂ = −2 → y₂ = −4
  • CheckMidpoint of (1, 2) and (5, −4): M = ((1+5)/2, (2+(−4))/2) = (3, −1) ✓
Answer: F = (5, −4)
Tip — Finding a Missing Endpoint

To find a missing endpoint, write the midpoint formula as two separate equations — one for x and one for y — and solve each individually. This keeps the algebra clean and reduces errors.

Section 13.4

Slope in Geometry

Slope measures how steep a line is and in what direction it rises or falls. In Algebra I, you used slope to describe linear equations. In geometry, slope is a tool for describing relationships between lines — whether they are parallel, perpendicular, or neither.

Slope Formula
m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)
Rise divided by run. The order of subtraction must be the same in both numerator and denominator.
Slope ValueGeometric Meaning
Positive slopeLine rises from left to right
Negative slopeLine falls from left to right
Zero slopeLine is horizontal
Undefined slopeLine is vertical (x₂ = x₁, division by zero)
Example 13.4.1
Calculating slope
  • ProblemFind the slope of the line through A(2, 5) and B(6, 11).
  • Solvem = (11 − 5) / (6 − 2) = 6/4 = 3/2
Answer: The slope of line AB is 3/2.
Student Warning

The order of subtraction must be consistent. If you write y₂ − y₁ in the numerator, you must write x₂ − x₁ in the denominator — using the same labeling for both. Writing m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₁ − x₂) produces the wrong sign.

Section 13.5

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Parallel Lines Theorem

Two nonvertical lines are parallel if and only if they have the same slope.

ℓ₁ ∥ ℓ₂ ⟺ m₁ = m₂

Two vertical lines are parallel to each other even though their slopes are undefined.

Perpendicular Lines Theorem

Two nonvertical lines are perpendicular if and only if the product of their slopes is −1.

ℓ₁ ⊥ ℓ₂ ⟺ m₁ · m₂ = −1

Equivalently: each slope is the negative reciprocal of the other. If m₁ = 2/3, then m₂ = −3/2.

Example 13.5.1
Identifying parallel and perpendicular lines
  • ProblemThree lines pass through these pairs of points. Identify any parallel or perpendicular pairs.
    Line 1: (0, 1) and (3, 5)  ·  Line 2: (1, 3) and (4, 7)  ·  Line 3: (−2, 4) and (2, 1)
  • Solve m₁ = (5−1)/(3−0) = 4/3 m₂ = (7−3)/(4−1) = 4/3 m₃ = (1−4)/(2−(−2)) = −3/4
  • CompareLine 1 and Line 2: m₁ = m₂ = 4/3 → Lines 1 and 2 are parallel.
    Lines 1 & 3: m₁ · m₃ = (4/3)(−3/4) = −1 → Lines 1 and 3 are perpendicular (Line 2 ⊥ Line 3 also).
Answer: Lines 1 ∥ Line 2. Lines 1 and 2 ⊥ Line 3.
Tip — Finding the Negative Reciprocal

To find the negative reciprocal of a fraction, flip it and change its sign. For example: 5/2 becomes −2/5. The number −3 (written as −3/1) becomes 1/3. Always check: the product of the original slope and its negative reciprocal must equal −1.

Example 13.6.1
Writing an equation through two points
  • ProblemWrite the equation of the line through A(2, 3) and B(6, 7).
  • ThinkFind slope first, then use point-slope form.
  • Solve m = (7 − 3)/(6 − 2) = 4/4 = 1 Using point A(2, 3): y − 3 = 1(x − 2) y = x + 1
  • CheckDoes B(6, 7) satisfy y = x + 1? → 7 = 6 + 1 = 7 ✓
Answer: y = x + 1
Example 13.6.2
Writing a perpendicular line through a point
  • ProblemLine ℓ has equation y = 2x − 3. Write the equation of the line through P(4, 1) that is perpendicular to ℓ.
  • ThinkSlope of ℓ is 2. Perpendicular slope is −1/2.
  • Solve y − 1 = −(1/2)(x − 4) y − 1 = −x/2 + 2 y = −x/2 + 3
  • CheckProduct of slopes: 2 · (−1/2) = −1 ✓. At P(4, 1): y = −(4/2) + 3 = −2 + 3 = 1 ✓
Answer: y = −(1/2)x + 3

Section 13.7

Classifying Triangles on the Coordinate Plane

From Chapter 4, you know triangles can be classified by sides (scalene, isosceles, equilateral) and by angles (acute, right, obtuse). On the coordinate plane, you can classify triangles using only the distance formula and slope. Use the distance formula to compare side lengths. Use perpendicular slopes to identify right angles.

Example 13.7.1
Classifying by side length
  • ProblemClassify △PQR with P(0, 0), Q(4, 0), R(2, 3) by its sides.
  • ThinkFind PQ, QR, and PR using the distance formula, then compare.
  • Solve PQ = √[(4−0)² + (0−0)²] = √16 = 4 QR = √[(2−4)² + (3−0)²] = √[4 + 9] = √13 PR = √[(2−0)² + (3−0)²] = √[4 + 9] = √13
  • CompareQR = PR = √13 and PQ = 4 ≠ √13. Exactly two sides are equal.
Answer: △PQR is isosceles because QR = PR.
Example 13.7.2
Classifying as a right triangle using slope
  • ProblemDetermine whether △ABC with A(1, 1), B(5, 1), C(5, 4) is a right triangle.
  • ThinkFind slopes of all three sides and check for perpendicularity.
  • Solve m_AB = (1−1)/(5−1) = 0 (horizontal) m_BC = (4−1)/(5−5) = 3/0 (undefined — vertical) m_AC = (4−1)/(5−1) = 3/4
  • ObserveAB is horizontal; BC is vertical. A horizontal line ⊥ a vertical line, so AB ⊥ BC.
Answer: △ABC is a right triangle with the right angle at B.
Tip — Double-Check with the Pythagorean Theorem

After computing side lengths for classification, you can verify a right triangle without slopes by checking whether the three lengths satisfy a² + b² = c². If the two shorter sides squared add to the longest side squared, the triangle is a right triangle.

Example 13.8.1
Classifying a quadrilateral
  • ProblemClassify quadrilateral ABCD with A(0, 0), B(4, 0), C(5, 3), D(1, 3).
  • ThinkCompute slopes of all four sides. Check for parallel pairs.
  • Solve m_AB = 0, m_DC = 0 → AB ∥ DC m_AD = 3, m_BC = 3 → AD ∥ BC
  • Check rectanglm_AB · m_AD = 0 · 3 = 0 ≠ −1. No right angles.
  • Check rhombusAB = 4, AD = √(1 + 9) = √10. Opposite sides equal but AB ≠ AD.
Answer: ABCD is a parallelogram — both pairs of opposite sides parallel and equal, but not a rectangle or rhombus.
Example 13.8.2
Verifying a rectangle
  • ProblemVerify that EFGH with E(0, 0), F(5, 0), G(5, 3), H(0, 3) is a rectangle.
  • Solve m_EF = 0 (horizontal), m_GH = 0 (horizontal) → EF ∥ GH m_FG = undefined (vertical), m_EH = undefined (vertical) → FG ∥ EH Horizontal ⊥ vertical → all four angles are right angles. Diagonals: EG = √(25 + 9) = √34 FH = √(25 + 9) = √34 → Diagonals are equal.
Answer: EFGH is a rectangle. Both pairs of opposite sides parallel, all right angles, and equal diagonals.

Section 13.9

Coordinate Proofs

A coordinate proof is a geometric proof that uses the coordinate plane. Instead of working with abstract diagrams and listing theorems, a coordinate proof assigns coordinates to key points and uses algebra to verify a geometric relationship. Coordinate proofs must apply to all cases, not just one example — so variables are used for coordinates whenever the figure's dimensions are not fixed.

Setup Strategies for Coordinate Proofs

Place one vertex at the origin — simplifies expressions because one or both coordinates are zero.

Place one side along the x-axis — makes slope of that side zero, simplifying comparisons.

Use variables for unknown lengths — the proof holds for all valid values, not just one case.

Use even numbers for coordinates — midpoint formula divides by 2, so even coordinates avoid fractions.

Proof 13.9.1
The Triangle Midsegment Theorem — coordinate proof
  • Given△ABC with midpoints M on AB and N on AC.
  • ProveMN ∥ BC and MN = (1/2)BC.
  • SetupLet A = (0, 0), B = (2b, 0), C = (2c, 2d). Using 2b, 2c, 2d ensures midpoints are clean.
  • Midpoints M = midpoint of AB = ((0 + 2b)/2, (0 + 0)/2) = (b, 0) N = midpoint of AC = ((0 + 2c)/2, (0 + 2d)/2) = (c, d)
  • Slopes m_MN = (d − 0)/(c − b) = d/(c − b) m_BC = (2d − 0)/(2c − 2b) = 2d/[2(c − b)] = d/(c − b) → m_MN = m_BC, so MN ∥ BC ✓
  • Lengths MN = √[(c − b)² + d²] BC = √[(2c − 2b)² + (2d)²] = √[4(c − b)² + 4d²] = 2√[(c − b)² + d²] → MN = (1/2)BC ✓
Reasoning Notes: This proof works for every triangle — not just the one sketched — because b, c, and d are arbitrary real numbers. The use of 2b, 2c, 2d prevented fractions from cluttering the midpoint calculation. The proof is now complete. □
Proof 13.9.2
The diagonals of a rectangle are equal in length
  • SetupLet the rectangle have vertices A(0, 0), B(a, 0), C(a, b), D(0, b) where a > 0 and b > 0.
  • Diagonals AC = √[(a − 0)² + (b − 0)²] = √[a² + b²] BD = √[(0 − a)² + (b − 0)²] = √[a² + b²]
  • ConcludeAC = BD = √(a² + b²). The diagonals of the rectangle are equal in length. □
Reasoning Notes: The key is placing the rectangle with sides along the axes. This gives all four vertices simple coordinates. The proof holds for any rectangle, since a and b can be any positive values.
Section 13.10

Proving Congruence with Coordinates

Two triangles are congruent if all three pairs of corresponding sides are equal in length. This is the SSS Congruence criterion applied to coordinates. Two segments are congruent if their distances are equal.

Example 13.10.1
Proving triangles congruent by SSS
  • ProblemShow that △PQR with P(0,0), Q(4,0), R(2,3) is congruent to △XYZ with X(1,1), Y(5,1), Z(3,4).
  • Solve △PQR PQ = √[16 + 0] = 4 QR = √[(2−4)² + 9] = √13 PR = √[4 + 9] = √13
  • Solve △XYZ XY = √[(5−1)² + 0] = 4 YZ = √[(3−5)² + (4−1)²] = √13 XZ = √[(3−1)² + (4−1)²] = √13
  • ComparePQ = XY = 4, QR = YZ = √13, PR = XZ = √13. All three pairs of corresponding sides are equal.
Answer: △PQR ≅ △XYZ by SSS.
Proof 13.10.2
The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other
  • SetupLet parallelogram have vertices A(0, 0), B(a, 0), C(a + c, d), D(c, d). This makes AB ∥ DC and AD ∥ BC.
  • Midpoints Midpoint of diagonal AC: ((0 + a + c)/2, (0 + d)/2) = ((a+c)/2, d/2) Midpoint of diagonal BD: ((a + c)/2, (0 + d)/2) = ((a+c)/2, d/2)
  • ConcludeBoth diagonals share the midpoint ((a+c)/2, d/2). Therefore the diagonals of the parallelogram bisect each other. □
Section 13.11

Proving Similarity with Coordinates

Two triangles are similar if all three ratios of corresponding side lengths are equal. On the coordinate plane, compute all six side lengths, then verify that the ratios form a constant scale factor k.

Example 13.11.1
Proving similarity using side length ratios
  • ProblemShow △ABC with A(0,0), B(4,0), C(0,3) is similar to △DEF with D(0,0), E(8,0), F(0,6).
  • Sides △ABCAB = 4, BC = √(16+9) = 5, CA = 3
  • Sides △DEFDE = 8, EF = √(64+36) = 10, FD = 6
  • Ratios DE/AB = 8/4 = 2 EF/BC = 10/5 = 2 FD/CA = 6/3 = 2
Answer: △ABC ∼ △DEF with scale factor k = 2 by SSS Similarity.

Section 13.13

Common Coordinate Proof Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Using specific numbers in a general proof
Proving a theorem only for one specific example (e.g., a rectangle with a = 5, b = 3).

Why it is wrong: A proof must apply to all cases. One example illustrates but does not prove. Use variables a, b, c, d so the proof holds for all valid values.

Mistake 2 — Adding differences before squaring
d = √[(x₂ − x₁) + (y₂ − y₁)]² instead of √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]

Square each difference separately. Adding them first produces a completely different calculation.

Mistake 3 — Confusing midpoint with distance
Using the distance formula when asked for a midpoint location (or vice versa).

Midpoint gives a location — an ordered pair. Distance gives a length — a single number. Read the problem carefully before choosing a formula.

Mistake 4 — Checking only one pair of sides for parallelogram
Showing one pair of opposite sides is parallel and concluding the figure is a parallelogram.

A trapezoid also has one pair of parallel sides. Show both pairs of opposite sides are parallel to conclude parallelogram.

Mistake 5 — Computing slope inconsistently
m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₁ − x₂) — denominator order is reversed.

The order of subtraction must be the same in both numerator and denominator. Switching the denominator order produces the wrong sign for the slope.

Mistake 6 — Building the conclusion into the setup
Placing a "general rectangle" with all four corners at obviously right-angle positions, then claiming the rectangle property is proven.

A coordinate proof should use coordinates that represent a general figure of the given type, not one with the desired properties already forced in. Use offset vertices (e.g., (a + c, d)) for parallelograms.

Mistake 7 — Concluding parallel from a diagram alone
Looking at the diagram and writing "these segments appear parallel" without computing slopes.

In a coordinate proof, always verify parallel relationships algebraically. Appearances can deceive, especially with oblique figures.

Mistake 8 — Leaving the proof conclusion unstated
Computing all relevant distances or slopes but ending the proof without stating what was proven.

Every coordinate proof should end with a sentence stating the geometric conclusion: "Therefore, MN ∥ BC and MN = (1/2)BC." The square symbol □ marks the end.

Section 13.14

Preparing for Analytic Geometry and Precalculus

Chapter 13 has established the algebraic language of geometry: distance, midpoints, slope, equations of lines, and coordinate proof. You are now ready to use those tools in the most dynamic setting the book offers.

Chapter 14 extends coordinate geometry into the study of transformations — motions that move, reflect, rotate, and scale geometric figures. Every transformation rule is a coordinate rule:

TransformationCoordinate Rule
Translation(x, y) → (x + a, y + b)
Reflection over x-axis(x, y) → (x, −y)
Rotation 90° counterclockwise(x, y) → (−y, x)
Dilation by factor k(x, y) → (kx, ky)

Beyond this book, coordinate geometry expands in two directions. Toward more geometry: analytic geometry studies conic sections — circles, ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas — as equations on the coordinate plane, in Algebra II and Precalculus. Toward higher dimensions: linear algebra uses vectors, matrices, and coordinate systems in three or more dimensions. Machine learning uses distances and slopes in spaces with hundreds of dimensions.

The central idea to carry forward: algebra and geometry are the same subject viewed through two different lenses. Coordinates make the connection between them precise, verifiable, and usable for every kind of mathematical and scientific work.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1Coordinate geometry places figures on a coordinate plane, turning geometric relationships into algebraic ones. Every point gets an address; every segment gets a length; every line gets a slope and an equation. Algebra and geometry become two languages for the same world.
  • Sec 13.2Distance formula: d = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]. Derived from the Pythagorean Theorem. Gives the exact length of any segment. Always square each difference before adding under the radical.
  • Sec 13.3Midpoint formula: M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2). Averages the x-coordinates and averages the y-coordinates. To find a missing endpoint, write two equations from the midpoint formula and solve each.
  • Sec 13.4Slope m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁). Positive slope rises left to right; negative slope falls; zero slope is horizontal; undefined slope is vertical. Subtraction order must be consistent in both numerator and denominator.
  • Sec 13.5Parallel lines: equal slopes (m₁ = m₂). Perpendicular lines: slopes multiply to −1 (m₁ · m₂ = −1), meaning each is the negative reciprocal of the other. Both are biconditional theorems.
  • Sec 13.6Slope-intercept form: y = mx + b. Point-slope form: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁). Equations of lines are used to show parallel or perpendicular sides, to find intersection points, and to verify that a point lies on a line.
  • Sec 13.7Classify triangles by computing all three side lengths with the distance formula. Equal sides → isosceles or equilateral. Perpendicular slopes between two sides → right angle. Check with the Pythagorean Theorem as needed.
  • Sec 13.8Classify quadrilaterals using slope (for parallel and perpendicular sides), distance (for equal sides), and midpoint (for diagonals that bisect each other). Both pairs of opposite sides parallel → parallelogram; add right angles → rectangle; add equal sides → rhombus; both → square.
  • Sec 13.9Coordinate proofs assign coordinates to vertices and use algebra to verify geometric theorems. Use variables for unknown lengths so the proof applies to all cases. Place one vertex at the origin and one side along the x-axis to simplify computation.
  • Sec 13.10Two triangles are congruent on the coordinate plane if all three pairs of corresponding sides have equal length (SSS). Two segments are congruent if their distances are equal. The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other — proven by showing equal midpoints.
  • Sec 13.11Two triangles are similar if all three ratios of corresponding side lengths are equal. Find the scale factor k by dividing corresponding sides. Verify all three ratios produce the same k.
  • Sec 13.12Coordinate geometry solves real problems in architecture, navigation, construction, robotics, engineering, and surveying. Distance, midpoint, and slope connect geometric reasoning to physical measurement.
  • Sec 13.13Eight common mistakes: using specific numbers in general proofs; adding before squaring; confusing midpoint and distance; checking only one pair of sides; inconsistent slope order; building conclusions into setup; concluding parallel from appearance; leaving conclusion unstated.
  • Sec 13.14Coordinate geometry is the foundation for Chapter 14 transformations, Algebra II conic sections, Precalculus trigonometry on the unit circle, Calculus derivatives and integrals, physics vectors, and machine learning in higher-dimensional spaces.

Key Terms Review

coordinate plane
A flat surface defined by a horizontal x-axis and a vertical y-axis intersecting at the origin. Every point has an address (x, y).
ordered pair
A pair of numbers (x, y) that identifies the location of a point on the coordinate plane.
distance formula
d = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]. Gives the length of the segment between two points. Derived from the Pythagorean Theorem.
midpoint formula
M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2). Gives the point exactly halfway between two endpoints by averaging each coordinate.
slope
m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁). The ratio of rise to run. Measures the steepness and direction of a line.
parallel lines
Lines in the same plane that never intersect. Verified on the coordinate plane by equal slopes: m₁ = m₂.
perpendicular lines
Lines that intersect at a right angle. Verified on the coordinate plane by the condition m₁ · m₂ = −1.
negative reciprocal
The slope of a line perpendicular to a given line. To find it, flip the given slope and change its sign.
slope-intercept form
y = mx + b. The equation of a line written with slope m and y-intercept b.
point-slope form
y − y₁ = m(x − x₁). The equation of a line written using a known point and the slope.
coordinate proof
A proof that uses algebraic calculations on coordinates to establish a geometric relationship. Variables are used so the proof applies to all cases.
scale factor
The constant ratio k by which all corresponding sides of two similar figures are related. Found by dividing corresponding side lengths.
collinear
Three or more points that lie on the same line. Verified by showing they satisfy the same linear equation or have the same slope between every pair.
SSS Similarity
Two triangles are similar if all three ratios of corresponding side lengths are equal (the same constant k for all three pairs).

Looking Ahead
Chapter 14: Transformations, Symmetry, and Geometric Modeling

Chapter 13 gave you the algebraic language of geometry. Chapter 14 will use it to study transformations — rules that move, reflect, rotate, and scale figures on the coordinate plane.

A translation shifts all coordinates by a fixed amount: (x, y) → (x + a, y + b). A reflection flips figures across a line of symmetry. A rotation turns figures around a center point. A dilation scales figures by a factor k. Every one of these operations is a coordinate rule you can apply, compose, and reason about algebraically.

Chapter 14 will also connect geometry to art, design, robotics, computer graphics, physics, and engineering — showing that geometry is not merely a class you take, but a system of ideas that describes the structure of the physical and digital world.

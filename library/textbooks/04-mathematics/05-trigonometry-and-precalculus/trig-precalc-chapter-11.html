Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 11 deepens the study of conic sections begun in Algebra II and connects it to the broader discipline of analytic geometry — the use of algebra to describe, analyze, and interpret geometric curves and shapes. Students will study the four classical conic sections: circles, parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas. They will learn to identify each curve from its equation, write equations from geometric descriptions, complete the square to convert equations into standard form, graph each conic with attention to key features, and interpret conics in applications drawn from astronomy, optics, engineering, architecture, and design.

This chapter also serves as a bridge into Chapter 12, where parametric equations and polar coordinates will offer additional languages for describing curves. Everything here is coordinate geometry: the meeting of algebra and geometry on the Cartesian plane.

Opening Question

A satellite dish focuses incoming signals at a single point called the focal point. An elliptical orbit carries a planet closer to and farther from the sun. A flashlight projects a cone of light that, when intercepted by a flat surface, creates a curved shape. What do all of these have in common? Each one involves a conic section — a curve formed by the intersection of a plane with a double cone. These four curves appear everywhere in the natural world, in engineering design, in optics, in physics, and in astronomy. Understanding their equations, their graphs, and their geometric properties is essential for any student preparing for calculus, physics, or applied mathematics.

Section 11.1

Analytic Geometry as Algebra and Shape

Analytic geometry is the branch of mathematics that uses a coordinate system to study geometric figures through their equations. It unites two great traditions: the geometry of shapes developed by the ancient Greeks, and the algebra of equations developed through the Renaissance and into the seventeenth century. The French mathematician René Descartes is usually credited with formalizing the connection between algebra and geometry. By assigning numerical coordinates to points in the plane, Descartes made it possible to describe curves as sets of points satisfying algebraic conditions.

A straight line is no longer just a ruler-drawn object; it is the set of all points (x, y) satisfying y = 2x + 3. A circle is the set of all points a fixed distance from a center, described by x² + y² = r². Conic sections are among the most important families of curves in analytic geometry.

What Is a Conic Section?

A double cone is a three-dimensional figure formed by two identical cones placed tip-to-tip at the origin, extending infinitely in both directions. When a flat plane intersects this double cone, the intersection traces a curve. Depending on the angle and position of the plane, that curve is one of four types.

Plane OrientationConic SectionDescription
Perpendicular to axisCircleEqual distance in all directions from the cone's axis
Tilted slightly from perpendicularEllipseCuts one nappe of the cone in a closed loop
Parallel to one side of the coneParabolaCuts one nappe, opening infinitely in one direction
Cuts both nappesHyperbolaTwo separate open branches, one on each nappe

The Role of the Second-Degree Equation

Every conic section is described algebraically by a second-degree equation in x and y. The general second-degree equation (with no xy cross-term) has the form:

General Second-Degree Equation
Ax² + Cy² + Dx + Ey + F = 0
When B = 0 (no xy cross-term), the conic's axes are aligned with the coordinate axes.
Identifying Conics from Coefficients
  • A = C (equal nonzero values, same sign) → Circle
  • A ≠ C, both same sign → Ellipse
  • Exactly one of A, C is zero → Parabola
  • A and C have opposite signs → Hyperbola

Section 11.2

Circles

The circle is the most familiar of the four conics. A circle is defined as the set of all points in a plane at a fixed distance from a given center point. Let the center be (h, k) and the radius be r > 0. A point (x, y) lies on the circle if and only if its distance from the center equals r. Applying the distance formula and squaring gives the standard form.

Standard Form of a Circle
(x − h)² + (y − k)² = r²
Center: (h, k) · Radius: r · When center is at origin: x² + y² = r²
FeatureValue
Center(h, k)
Radiusr
Diameter2r
Domain[h − r, h + r]
Range[k − r, k + r]
SymmetrySymmetric about any line through the center

Writing the equation of a circle
  • ProblemWrite the standard form equation of the circle with center (3, −2) and radius 5.
  • ThinkUse standard form (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r² with h = 3, k = −2, and r = 5.
  • RepresentStandard form of a circle.
  • Solve (x − 3)² + (y − (−2))² = 5² (x − 3)² + (y + 2)² = 25
  • CheckCheck the topmost point (3, 3): (3 − 3)² + (3 + 2)² = 0 + 25 = 25 ✓
Answer: (x − 3)² + (y + 2)² = 25
Example 11.2.2
Identifying center and radius
  • ProblemIdentify the center and radius of (x + 1)² + (y − 4)² = 9.
  • ThinkRewrite to match (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r² by reading h, k, and r² directly. Note (x + 1)² = (x − (−1))².
  • Solveh = −1, k = 4, r² = 9, so r = 3.
Answer: Center (−1, 4), radius 3.
Graph Instruction — Circle

Draw a circle centered at (−1, 4) with radius 3. Mark the center with a dot. Mark the four boundary points: directly above (−1, 7), below (−1, 1), left (−4, 4), and right (2, 4). Draw the circle through these four points. Label the center and the radius.

Student Warning — Reading the Center's Sign

In standard form (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r², the center uses subtraction inside the parentheses. When the equation shows (x + 1)², the center has x-coordinate −1, not +1. Always rewrite as (x − (−1))² to read the center correctly. This same issue affects ellipses and hyperbolas.

Section 11.3

Parabolas

The parabola was encountered in Algebra I and Algebra II as the graph of a quadratic function. Here, the parabola is studied more deeply as a geometric curve defined by a focus-directrix relationship, and equations are written both in vertex form and standard conic form. Parabolas that open upward, downward, left, or right are all studied — including horizontal parabolas, which are not functions.

Geometric Definition of a Parabola

A parabola is the set of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point (the focus) and a fixed line (the directrix).

The vertex is the point on the parabola closest to the directrix and focus. The signed distance from the vertex to the focus is p. When p > 0 the parabola opens away from the directrix in the positive direction; when p < 0 it reverses.

Standard Forms — Vertex at (h, k)

OrientationStandard FormFocusDirectrixAxis
Vertical (up if p > 0, down if p < 0)(x − h)² = 4p(y − k)(h, k + p)y = k − px = h
Horizontal (right if p > 0, left if p < 0)(y − k)² = 4p(x − h)(h + p, k)x = h − py = k
Why the Focus-Directrix Definition?

A parabolic mirror or dish is designed so that incoming parallel rays all reflect to the same focus point. Conversely, a light source at the focus reflects off the parabola as perfectly parallel rays. This is why flashlights, car headlights, satellite dishes, and reflecting telescopes use parabolic shapes. The geometric equidistance property produces this exact focusing behavior.

Writing the equation from focus and directrix
  • ProblemFind the equation of the parabola with vertex at the origin, axis along the y-axis, and focus at (0, 3).
  • ThinkThe focus is above the vertex, so the parabola opens upward. The form is x² = 4py with p = 3.
  • RepresentVertical parabola: x² = 4py.
  • Solvex² = 4(3)y = 12y
  • CheckFocus at (0, p) = (0, 3) ✓. Directrix: y = −3. Vertex (0, 0): distance to focus = 3, distance to directrix = 3 ✓.
Answer: x² = 12y
Example 11.3.2
Identifying features from equation — horizontal parabola
  • ProblemIdentify the vertex, focus, directrix, and direction of opening for (y + 2)² = −8(x − 1).
  • ThinkThe y variable is squared, so this is a horizontal parabola: (y − k)² = 4p(x − h). Read k = −2, h = 1, and 4p = −8, so p = −2.

Student Warning — Horizontal Parabolas Are Not Functions

A horizontal parabola (y² = ···) fails the vertical line test and is therefore not a function. Do not confuse it with the parabolas-as-functions from Algebra II. In that setting, only vertical parabolas (opening up or down) were treated as functions. In conic section study, all four directions are studied as curves without this restriction.

Section 11.4

Ellipses

An ellipse is the set of all points in a plane for which the sum of the distances from two fixed points (the foci) is constant. Let the two foci be F₁ and F₂. Then a point P lies on the ellipse if and only if PF₁ + PF₂ = 2a, where 2a is the constant sum, always greater than the distance between the foci.

The ellipse has two special axes. The major axis passes through both foci; its half-length is a (the semi-major axis). The minor axis is perpendicular to the major axis at the center; its half-length is b (the semi-minor axis). The distance from the center to each focus is c, and these three values are related by:

Ellipse Focal Relationship
a² = b² + c²   or equivalently   c² = a² − b²
Always: a > b > 0 and a > c > 0. The foci lie inside the ellipse.

Standard Forms of Ellipses — Center at (h, k)

FormStandard EquationVertices (major axis)Foci
Horizontal major axis[(x−h)²/a²] + [(y−k)²/b²] = 1, a > b(h ± a, k)(h ± c, k)
Vertical major axis[(x−h)²/b²] + [(y−k)²/a²] = 1, a > b(h, k ± a)(h, k ± c)
Key Identification Rule for Ellipses

The larger denominator is always a². The variable under the larger denominator tells you the direction of the major axis: if a² is under x², the major axis is horizontal; if a² is under y², the major axis is vertical. Do not assume the major axis is always horizontal just because x appears first.

Example 11.4.1
Writing and graphing an ellipse
  • ProblemFind the center, vertices, co-vertices, and foci of (x−2)²/25 + (y+1)²/9 = 1.
  • ThinkThe larger denominator is 25, under (x−2)², so the major axis is horizontal. a² = 25, b² = 9, center at (2, −1).
  • Solve a = 5, b = 3 c² = 25 − 9 = 16, c = 4 Center: (2, −1) Vertices: (2 ± 5, −1) = (7, −1) and (−3, −1) Co-vertices: (2, −1 ± 3) = (2, 2) and (2, −4) Foci: (2 ± 4, −1) = (6, −1) and (−2, −1)
  • CheckVerify c < a: 4 < 5 ✓. Foci at x = −2 and x = 6 lie between vertices at x = −3 and x = 7 ✓.
Answer: Center (2, −1); vertices (7, −1) and (−3, −1); co-vertices (2, 2) and (2, −4); foci (6, −1) and (−2, −1).
Graph Instruction — Horizontal Ellipse

Draw an ellipse centered at (2, −1). Mark the two vertices at (−3, −1) and (7, −1) on the horizontal axis and label them. Mark the two co-vertices at (2, 2) and (2, −4) on the vertical axis and label them. Mark the two foci at (−2, −1) and (6, −1) as interior points (not on the ellipse) and label them. Draw the major axis as a dashed horizontal segment from vertex to vertex, and the minor axis as a dashed vertical segment from co-vertex to co-vertex. The ellipse is wider than it is tall.

Example 11.4.2
Writing an ellipse from given information
  • ProblemWrite the standard form equation of the ellipse with center (0, 0), one focus at (0, 4), and one vertex at (0, 5).
  • ThinkThe focus and vertex are both on the y-axis, so the major axis is vertical. Use x²/b² + y²/a² = 1 with a > b.
  • Solve a = 5 (vertex to center distance) c = 4 (focus to center distance) b² = a² − c² = 25 − 16 = 9 Equation: x²/9 + y²/25 = 1
Answer: x²/9 + y²/25 = 1
Student Warning — Confusing a and b

Students often assign a² to whichever denominator appears first, or assume the major axis is always horizontal. Remember: a² is always the larger denominator, and the direction of the major axis is determined by which variable sits under a². Always identify the larger denominator first before labeling anything as a, b, or c.

Section 11.5

Hyperbolas

A hyperbola is the set of all points in a plane for which the absolute difference of the distances from two fixed points (the foci) is constant: |PF₁ − PF₂| = 2a. This makes the hyperbola the "opposite" of an ellipse — an ellipse adds distances, a hyperbola subtracts them. A hyperbola has two separate branches, each curving away from the center. It has a transverse axis connecting the two vertices, a conjugate axis perpendicular to it at the center, and two asymptotes that the branches approach without ever reaching.

The relationship between a, b, and focal distance c is fundamentally different from the ellipse:

Hyperbola Focal Relationship
c² = a² + b²
Always: c > a. The foci lie outside the curve — beyond the vertices. Compare with the ellipse: c² = a² − b².

Standard Forms of Hyperbolas — Center at (h, k)

OrientationStandard FormVerticesAsymptotes
Horizontal transverse axis (opens left/right)[(x−h)²/a²] − [(y−k)²/b²] = 1(h ± a, k)y − k = ±(b/a)(x − h)
Vertical transverse axis (opens up/down)[(y−k)²/a²] − [(x−h)²/b²] = 1(h, k ± a)y − k = ±(a/b)(x − h)
The Asymptote Rectangle

Draw a rectangle centered at (h, k) with width 2a in the direction of the transverse axis and height 2b in the other direction. The asymptotes are the diagonals of this rectangle, extended as lines. Each branch of the hyperbola opens away from the center, bending toward one pair of corners of this rectangle. The branches get closer and closer to the asymptote lines but never touch them.

Example 11.5.1
Graphing a hyperbola — horizontal transverse axis
  • ProblemIdentify center, vertices, foci, and asymptotes of x²/16 − y²/9 = 1.
  • ThinkThe positive term is x², so the transverse axis is horizontal. Center at origin, a² = 16, b² = 9.
  • Solve a = 4, b = 3 c² = 16 + 9 = 25, c = 5 Center: (0, 0) Vertices: (±4, 0) Foci: (±5, 0) Asymptotes: y = ±(3/4)x
  • CheckFor a horizontal hyperbola, c > a: 5 > 4 ✓. Foci are outside the vertices along the x-axis ✓.
Answer: Center (0, 0); vertices (±4, 0); foci (±5, 0); asymptotes y = ±(3/4)x.
Graph Instruction — Horizontal Hyperbola

Example 11.5.2
Writing an equation from features — vertical hyperbola
  • ProblemWrite the standard form equation of the hyperbola with center (1, −3), vertices at (1, 0) and (1, −6), and foci at (1, 2) and (1, −8).
  • ThinkThe vertices and foci share the same x-coordinate as the center, so the transverse axis is vertical.
  • Solve a = |0 − (−3)| = 3 c = |2 − (−3)| = 5 b² = c² − a² = 25 − 9 = 16 Equation: (y + 3)²/9 − (x − 1)²/16 = 1

Student Warning — Ellipse vs. Hyperbola Formula

For an ellipse: c² = a² − b² (foci are inside, so c < a, so c² < a², so you subtract).
For a hyperbola: c² = a² + b² (foci are outside, so c > a, so c² > a², so you add).
A common error is using the ellipse formula for a hyperbola, producing a negative c², which is impossible. Always check whether your answer has c > a (hyperbola) or c < a (ellipse).

Section 11.6

Completing the Square for Conics

Conic equations often appear in general form — expanded, with squared and linear terms mixed together. To analyze a conic's features, it must be converted to standard form. The tool for doing this is completing the square, extended here to two variables simultaneously.

General Procedure

1. Group x-terms and y-terms. Move the constant to the right side.

2. Factor out the leading coefficient of each squared term if not already 1.

3. Complete the square for the x-group and y-group separately. Add the same values to both sides.

4. Write each completed square as a binomial squared.

5. Divide both sides if needed to reach standard form.

Example 11.6.1
Completing the square — circle
  • ProblemWrite x² + y² − 6x + 4y − 3 = 0 in standard form.
  • ThinkBoth variables squared with equal coefficients: circle. Group and complete the square for each variable.
  • Solve x² − 6x + y² + 4y = 3 Complete x: (−6/2)² = 9. Add 9 to both sides. Complete y: (4/2)² = 4. Add 4 to both sides. (x² − 6x + 9) + (y² + 4y + 4) = 3 + 9 + 4 (x − 3)² + (y + 2)² = 16
Answer: Circle with center (3, −2) and radius 4.
Example 11.6.2
Completing the square — ellipse with leading coefficients
  • ProblemWrite 4x² + 9y² − 8x + 36y + 4 = 0 in standard form.
  • ThinkTwo squared terms, different positive coefficients: ellipse. Factor out coefficients before completing the square.
  • Solve 4(x² − 2x) + 9(y² + 4y) = −4 For x: (−2/2)² = 1. Add 4 · 1 = 4 to both sides. For y: (4/2)² = 4. Add 9 · 4 = 36 to both sides. 4(x² − 2x + 1) + 9(y² + 4y + 4) = −4 + 4 + 36 4(x − 1)² + 9(y + 2)² = 36 Divide by 36: (x − 1)²/9 + (y + 2)²/4 = 1
Answer: Ellipse with center (1, −2), a² = 9 (horizontal major axis), b² = 4.
Example 11.6.3
Completing the square — hyperbola
  • ProblemWrite x² − 4y² − 2x + 16y − 19 = 0 in standard form.
  • ThinkTwo squared terms with opposite signs: hyperbola.
  • Solve (x² − 2x) − 4(y² − 4y) = 19 For x: (−2/2)² = 1. Add 1 to both sides. For y (inside −4): (−4/2)² = 4. Add −4 · 4 = −16 to both sides. (x² − 2x + 1) − 4(y² − 4y + 4) = 19 + 1 − 16 (x − 1)² − 4(y − 2)² = 4 Divide by 4: (x − 1)²/4 − (y − 2)² = 1
Answer: Hyperbola with center (1, 2), a² = 4, b² = 1, horizontal transverse axis.
Student Warning — Multiplying When Completing the Square

When the equation has −4(y² − 4y) and you add 4 inside the parentheses, you must add −4 × 4 = −16 to the right side of the equation, not just 4. The factor outside the parentheses multiplies whatever is added inside. This is the most common completing-the-square error with conics.

Section 11.7

Translated Conics

Translation is a rigid motion that shifts a curve horizontally and vertically without changing its shape, orientation, or size. The algebraic effect is replacing x with (x − h) and y with (y − k). All features — center, vertices, co-vertices, foci, asymptotes — shift by the same vector (h, k) from their origin-centered positions.

Origin-Centered FormTranslated Form
x² + y² = r²(x−h)² + (y−k)² = r²
x² = 4py(x−h)² = 4p(y−k)
x²/a² + y²/b² = 1(x−h)²/a² + (y−k)²/b² = 1
x²/a² − y²/b² = 1(x−h)²/a² − (y−k)²/b² = 1
Section 11.8

Given a second-degree equation with no xy cross-term, the conic type can be identified before converting to standard form, simply by examining the structure of the squared terms.

Example 11.8.1
Identifying conics — four cases
  • (a)3x² + 3y² − 12x + 6y − 5 = 0 → Both squared, equal coefficients → Circle
  • (b)x² + 4y² − 2x + 16y − 19 = 0 → Both squared, different positive coefficients → Ellipse

Section 11.10

Eccentricity as an Introduction

Eccentricity is a single number that measures how much a conic deviates from being a circle. It is denoted by e (not to be confused with Euler's number e ≈ 2.718, which uses the same letter in a different context — always read from context which meaning applies).

Eccentricity
e = c/a
c = distance from center to focus · a = semi-major axis (ellipse/hyperbola) or vertex-to-focus distance (parabola)
ConicValue of cEccentricity e = c/aShape
Circlec = 0e = 0Perfectly round
Ellipse0 < c < a0 < e < 1Elongated; more elongated as e → 1
Parabolac = a (in a sense)e = 1Open, unbounded
Hyperbolac > ae > 1Two open branches; wider as e increases
Eccentricity in Astronomy

Earth's orbit is an ellipse with eccentricity approximately 0.017 — nearly circular. Mars has eccentricity approximately 0.093. Comets often have orbits with eccentricities close to 1. Some objects passing through the solar system have hyperbolic trajectories (e > 1) and never return. Eccentricity is the single number that completely characterizes the shape of any conic.

Section 11.11

Conics in Astronomy and Optics

Johannes Kepler showed in 1609 that the planets travel in elliptical orbits with the sun at one focus. Isaac Newton later proved theoretically that any two objects interacting through an inverse-square gravitational law must travel in paths that are conic sections — ellipses for closed orbits, parabolas for trajectories at exactly escape velocity, and hyperbolas for trajectories above escape velocity.

In an elliptical orbit with semi-major axis a and focal distance c, the perihelion (closest point to the sun) is at distance a − c, and the aphelion (farthest point) is at distance a + c. This is why planets speed up when closer to the sun and slow down when farther.

Example 11.11.1
Planetary orbit from perihelion and aphelion
  • ProblemAn elliptical orbit has perihelion 1.4 AU and aphelion 2.6 AU. Write the equation of the orbit and find its eccentricity.
  • ThinkPerihelion = a − c = 1.4 and aphelion = a + c = 2.6. Solve this system.
  • Solve Adding: 2a = 4.0, so a = 2.0 Subtracting: 2c = 1.2, so c = 0.6 b² = a² − c² = 4.0 − 0.36 = 3.64 Equation: x²/4 + y²/3.64 = 1 Eccentricity: e = c/a = 0.6/2.0 = 0.3
Answer: x²/4 + y²/3.64 = 1; eccentricity 0.3.
Example 11.11.2
Satellite dish focal point
  • ProblemA satellite dish is 3 meters wide and 1.2 meters deep at its center. Where should the receiver be placed?
  • ThinkSet up a vertical parabola with vertex at the origin. The rim is at x = ±1.5 (half the width) and y = 1.2 (the depth). Use x² = 4py.
  • Solve Substitute (1.5, 1.2): 1.5² = 4p(1.2) 2.25 = 4.8p p = 2.25/4.8 ≈ 0.47 meters
  • CheckThe focus at p = 0.47 m is inside the dish (0.47 < 1.2 m deep) ✓. Reasonable position for a receiver mount.
Answer: The receiver should be placed approximately 47 cm above the vertex (center bottom) of the dish.
Section 11.12

Conics in Engineering and Design

Conics appear throughout engineering, architecture, and design. An elliptical room (whispering gallery) has the remarkable acoustic property that a whisper at one focus can be heard clearly at the other focus, because all sound waves from one focus reflect off the elliptical wall and converge at the other focus. Famous examples include the National Statuary Hall in the United States Capitol.

Hyperbolic cooling towers at power plants use the hyperbola's inward curve for structural strength with minimal material and efficient air flow. Parabolic arch bridges use the parabola's load-distribution properties. The LORAN navigation system (used before GPS) located ships by measuring time differences between radio signals from two stations. Since a constant difference of distances from two fixed points traces a hyperbola, LORAN placed ships at the intersection of two hyperbolas.

Example 11.12.1
Whispering gallery — finding the foci
  • ProblemA whispering gallery has an elliptical cross-section 80 feet long and 34 feet wide. Where should two people stand to be at the foci?
  • ThinkMajor axis: 2a = 80, so a = 40. Minor axis: 2b = 34, so b = 17.
  • Solve c² = a² − b² = 1600 − 289 = 1311 c = √1311 ≈ 36.2 feet Distance from end wall: a − c = 40 − 36.2 = 3.8 feet
Answer: Each person should stand approximately 3.8 feet from the nearest end wall of the room, near each end of the long axis.

Section 11.13

Common Conic Section Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Reading the center's sign incorrectly
"Center of (x + 4)² + (y − 1)² = 9 is (4, 1)."

Correct: (x + 4)² = (x − (−4))², so the center is (−4, 1). Always rewrite to see the subtraction explicitly.

Mistake 2 — Confusing a and b in ellipses
"For x²/9 + y²/25 = 1, the major axis is horizontal because x appears first."

Correct: a² is the larger denominator. Here a² = 25 is under y², so the major axis is vertical. Always identify the larger denominator first.

Mistake 3 — Using c² = a² − b² for hyperbolas
Finding foci of a hyperbola using c² = a² − b².

Correct: For ellipses: c² = a² − b². For hyperbolas: c² = a² + b². Hyperbola foci are outside the curve (c > a), so you must add. Subtracting often gives a negative value, which signals the wrong formula.

Mistake 4 — Forgetting to multiply when completing the square
4(x² − 2x + 1) + 9(y² + 4y + 4) = −4 + 1 + 4 (adding 1 and 4 to the right side)

Correct: Adding 1 inside the factor 4( ) adds 4 × 1 = 4 to the right side. Adding 4 inside the factor 9( ) adds 9 × 4 = 36 to the right side. The right side should be −4 + 4 + 36 = 36.

Mistake 5 — Thinking asymptotes touch the hyperbola
"The hyperbola intersects its asymptotes at large x-values."

Correct: Asymptotes are lines that the branches approach but never reach. The branches get closer and closer to the asymptote lines as the values grow but do not intersect them at any finite point.

Mistake 6 — Confusing horizontal and vertical orientation
Assuming ellipse major axis is always horizontal, or assuming the first squared term determines direction.

Correct: For ellipses, the major axis direction corresponds to the variable with the larger denominator. For hyperbolas, the transverse axis direction corresponds to the variable with the positive sign.

Mistake 7 — Treating e in eccentricity as Euler's number
Using e ≈ 2.718 in an eccentricity calculation.

Correct: In the context of conic sections, e stands for eccentricity (the ratio c/a), not the base of the natural logarithm. Context determines which meaning applies.

Mistake 8 — Graphing without identifying key features first
Drawing a rough curve before computing vertices, foci, or asymptotes.

Correct: Always identify center (or vertex), a, b, c, and relevant features before sketching. Rushing to a sketch without key features produces incorrectly shaped or positioned curves that are useless for problem solving.

Section 11.14

Preparing for Parametric Equations

Chapter 12 will introduce parametric equations — a way of describing a curve by giving both x and y as functions of a third variable called the parameter (usually t). Circles and ellipses can be described parametrically in a natural way that connects directly to the trigonometric functions studied in Parts I and II of this book.

For a circle of radius r centered at the origin, as t sweeps from 0 to 2π, the point (r cos t, r sin t) traces the entire circle. The parameter t is the angle on the unit circle — exactly the connection built in Chapter 4.

ConicParametric FormParameter Range
Circle x² + y² = r²x(t) = r cos t, y(t) = r sin t0 ≤ t ≤ 2π
Ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1x(t) = a cos t, y(t) = b sin t0 ≤ t ≤ 2π
Hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1x(t) = a sec t, y(t) = b tan tappropriate intervals
Connection to the Unit Circle

The parametric form of the ellipse, x(t) = a cos t and y(t) = b sin t, is a direct generalization of the unit circle. When a = b = 1, it reduces exactly to the unit circle from Chapter 4. The parameter t represents the angle, and the coordinates (a cos t, b sin t) stretch the unit circle by factor a horizontally and b vertically. This is a geometric transformation of exactly the type studied in Chapter 2.

Chapter 11 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1Conic sections are curves formed by intersecting a plane with a double cone. They are classified by the second-degree equation's coefficient structure: equal nonzero → circle; different same-sign → ellipse; one absent → parabola; opposite signs → hyperbola.
  • Sec 11.2A circle is the set of all points equidistant from a center (h, k). Standard form: (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r². Signs inside parentheses are subtractive — (x + 1)² has center x-coordinate −1.
  • Sec 11.3A parabola is the set of all points equidistant from the focus and directrix. The signed distance p from vertex to focus determines direction and width. Vertical: (x − h)² = 4p(y − k). Horizontal: (y − k)² = 4p(x − h).
  • Sec 11.4An ellipse satisfies PF₁ + PF₂ = 2a. Key relationship: c² = a² − b², with a > b and c < a. The larger denominator is always a², and its variable determines the major axis direction.
  • Sec 11.5A hyperbola satisfies |PF₁ − PF₂| = 2a. Key relationship: c² = a² + b², with c > a. The positive term determines the transverse axis direction. Asymptotes pass through corners of the central rectangle.
  • Sec 11.6Completing the square converts general form to standard form. Group by variable, factor out leading coefficients, complete each square, and add equal amounts to both sides — remembering that adding k inside a factor of A adds A·k to the right side.
  • Sec 11.7Translation shifts all conic features by (h, k). Every feature formula still applies; the center or vertex simply moves from the origin to (h, k).
  • Sec 11.8A conic can be identified without converting to standard form by examining whether one or both variables are squared, whether the coefficients are equal or different, and whether the signs are the same or opposite.
  • Sec 11.9The latus rectum is the chord through a focus perpendicular to the major axis. Its length is |4p| for a parabola and 2b²/a for an ellipse or hyperbola. It helps determine how open or narrow a conic is at its focus.
  • Sec 11.10Eccentricity e = c/a measures departure from circularity. For a circle e = 0; for an ellipse 0 < e < 1; for a parabola e = 1; for a hyperbola e > 1. In astronomy, eccentricity describes orbital shape.
  • Sec 11.11Planetary orbits are ellipses with the sun at one focus (Kepler). Parabolic mirrors focus parallel rays at the focal point, which is why satellite dishes, telescopes, headlights, and solar cookers use parabolic shapes.
  • Sec 11.12Engineering applications include whispering galleries (elliptic rooms), hyperbolic cooling towers, parabolic arch bridges, and LORAN hyperbolic navigation. Each application exploits a specific geometric property of the conic.
  • Sec 11.13Eight recurring errors: sign of center, a vs. b identification, ellipse vs. hyperbola focal formula, completing-the-square multiplication, asymptotes never touching hyperbola, orientation from variable position, e vs. Euler's number, graphing before computing features.
  • Sec 11.14Circles and ellipses have natural parametric forms using cosine and sine. The ellipse x(t) = a cos t, y(t) = b sin t generalizes the unit circle by stretching coordinates. This connects conics to trigonometry and to Chapter 12.

Key Terms Review

analytic geometry
The use of algebra and coordinate systems to study geometric figures through their equations.
conic section
A curve formed by the intersection of a plane and a double cone; includes circles, parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas.
circle
The set of all points equidistant from a center (h, k). Standard form: (x−h)² + (y−k)² = r².
parabola
The set of all points equidistant from a focus and a directrix. Opens in one of four directions based on the sign of p.
vertex
The turning point of a parabola; the point closest to both the focus and the directrix.
focus
A fixed point involved in the definition of a parabola, ellipse, or hyperbola. Ellipses and hyperbolas have two foci.
directrix
A fixed line in the definition of a parabola; every point on the parabola is equidistant from the focus and directrix.
ellipse
The set of all points for which the sum of distances from two foci equals the constant 2a.
major axis
The longer axis of an ellipse, passing through both foci. Half-length = a (semi-major axis).
minor axis
The shorter axis of an ellipse, perpendicular to the major axis at the center. Half-length = b (semi-minor axis).
co-vertices
The endpoints of the minor axis of an ellipse.
hyperbola
The set of all points for which the absolute difference of distances from two foci equals the constant 2a. Has two branches.
transverse axis
The axis of a hyperbola connecting the two vertices, passing through both foci.
conjugate axis
The axis of a hyperbola perpendicular to the transverse axis at the center. Half-length = b.
asymptotes
Lines that the branches of a hyperbola approach but never reach. They are the diagonals of the central rectangle.
completing the square
An algebraic technique that rewrites a quadratic in x (or y) as (x − h)² plus a constant, converting general conic form to standard form.
eccentricity (e)
The ratio c/a measuring how far a conic deviates from circular. Circle: e = 0; ellipse: 0 < e < 1; parabola: e = 1; hyperbola: e > 1.
latus rectum
The chord of a conic passing through a focus and perpendicular to the major axis or axis of symmetry.
standard form
A conic equation written with the center or vertex explicit and all squared terms on the left with coefficient 1 or 1/a².
general form
A second-degree equation expanded with all terms on one side: Ax² + Cy² + Dx + Ey + F = 0.

Looking Ahead — Bridge Note
Chapter 12: Parametric Equations, Polar Coordinates, and Vectors

Chapter 11 described curves using equations in x and y. Chapter 12 introduces two new languages for describing position, motion, and curves. Parametric equations give x and y each as functions of a parameter t, which is natural for motion along a path — a planet orbiting the sun, a projectile in flight, a point tracing a circle. Polar coordinates locate points using distance r and angle θ, which is natural for curves with rotational symmetry. Vectors describe quantities that have both magnitude and direction — force, velocity, displacement — and form the mathematical language of physics and engineering. All three topics in Chapter 12 grow naturally from the trigonometry, conics, and function work done so far in this book.

Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 3 introduced angles as rotations, radian measure as a natural way to quantify those rotations, and arc length as a connection between angle and circle. Now it is time to ask a deeper question: what happens at the point where a rotating ray meets a circle?

Chapter 4 builds the unit circle as the foundation for trigonometric functions. The unit circle is not merely a diagram to memorize. It is the definition of sine and cosine as functions of rotation. Once you understand the unit circle, trigonometry transforms from a collection of triangle ratios into a unified function system that describes periodic motion, waves, rotation, oscillation, and circular behavior throughout mathematics, science, and engineering.

This chapter should be read carefully and slowly. The unit circle is one of the most important structures in all of precalculus. Every section of Chapter 5 (trigonometric graphs), Chapter 6 (identities and equations), and many sections of Chapters 7, 12, and 13 depend on understanding this chapter well.

Opening Question

A point starts at (1, 0) on a circle of radius 1 and travels counterclockwise. After rotating through an angle of π/3 radians, where is the point? What are its coordinates? How do those coordinates relate to the sine and cosine of the angle? By the end of this chapter, you will answer this question precisely and extend it to every angle on the full unit circle.

Why This Chapter Matters

In Algebra II and in right-triangle trigonometry, the sine, cosine, and tangent of an angle were defined as ratios of side lengths in a right triangle. That definition works, but it has serious limitations. Right triangles have acute angles only — angles between 0° and 90° can be described by triangle ratios, but what about 135°? What about 270°? What about a negative angle or one greater than 2π? Right triangles cannot describe those cases.

Trigonometry needs a definition that works for every angle — positive, negative, acute, obtuse, reflex, and beyond. The unit circle provides that definition. On the unit circle, sine and cosine are coordinates, and this definition extends seamlessly to every real-number angle.

Section 4.1

Why the Unit Circle Matters

In earlier mathematics, trigonometry was introduced through right triangles. Given a right triangle with an acute angle θ, the three primary trigonometric ratios were defined as:

sin(θ) = opposite / hypotenuse     cos(θ) = adjacent / hypotenuse     tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent

These definitions are useful for solving triangles and computing distances, and they will return in Chapter 7. However, they have a fundamental limitation: they only work for angles strictly between 0° and 90°. A right triangle cannot have an angle of 120°, 270°, or −π/4.

For trigonometry to become a true function system — one that accepts any real number as input and produces an output — it must be freed from the right triangle. The unit circle achieves this.

Why a Circle of Radius 1?

The choice of radius 1 is deliberate. On any circle of radius r, the sine of an angle could be defined as y/r and the cosine as x/r, where (x, y) is the point on the circle. When r = 1, those fractions simplify to just y and x directly. The unit circle makes the connection between trigonometric functions and coordinates as clean as possible.

Trigonometry as a Function System

When sine and cosine are defined using the unit circle, they become genuine functions of a real-number input. The input is an angle (in radians or degrees), and the output is a coordinate value between −1 and 1. This unlocks a rich function theory:

Section 4.2

The Unit Circle Definition

Definition — The Unit Circle

The unit circle is the circle centered at the origin with radius 1. Its equation is:

x² + y² = 1

Every point (x, y) on the unit circle satisfies this equation, and every point satisfying this equation lies on the unit circle.

Connecting Angles to Points

Place an angle θ in standard position: its initial side along the positive x-axis, rotating counterclockwise for positive θ and clockwise for negative θ. The terminal side of this angle intersects the unit circle at exactly one point. That intersection point is defined to have coordinates (cos θ, sin θ).

This is the core definition. Read it carefully:

The Unit Circle Definitions of Sine and Cosine

For any angle θ in standard position, the terminal side intersects the unit circle at the point (x, y) where:

x = cos θ     (the horizontal coordinate) y = sin θ     (the vertical coordinate)
Diagram Instruction — Unit Circle with Angle

Draw the unit circle centered at the origin. Draw a ray from the origin at angle θ measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis. Mark the intersection point (x, y) on the circle. Label x = cos θ and y = sin θ. Draw perpendicular dashed lines from the point to both axes to show the coordinate values. Label the radius = 1.

Why This Works for Acute Angles

This definition is consistent with the right-triangle definition for angles between 0° and 90°. When θ is acute, the terminal side and the x-axis form a right triangle whose hypotenuse is the unit circle radius = 1. In that triangle, sin θ = opposite/1 = y and cos θ = adjacent/1 = x. The unit circle definition reduces to the right-triangle definition in the acute case and extends naturally beyond it.

The Fundamental Identity

Since every point on the unit circle satisfies x² + y² = 1, and the coordinates are cos θ and sin θ, substituting gives the most important identity in trigonometry:

cos²θ + sin²θ = 1

This is the Pythagorean identity, and it holds for every angle θ without exception. It comes directly from the equation of the unit circle.

Why the Coordinates Are Bounded

Because every point on the unit circle satisfies x² + y² = 1, both coordinates must satisfy −1 ≤ x ≤ 1 and −1 ≤ y ≤ 1. This means the values of sine and cosine are always between −1 and 1 inclusive. They never exceed 1 or fall below −1.

Student Warning — Impossible Values

Students sometimes write sin θ = 2 or cos θ = −3 when solving equations. These are impossible values. If your work produces a sine or cosine outside [−1, 1], something has gone wrong. No angle on the unit circle produces such a result.

Section 4.4

Sine and Cosine as Coordinates

Formal Definitions

For any angle θ in standard position, let (x, y) be the point where the terminal side of θ meets the unit circle. Then:

cos θ = x     (the horizontal coordinate) sin θ = y     (the vertical coordinate)

Domain: all real numbers (−∞, ∞)     Range: [−1, 1]

What This Means Geometrically

Think of a point traveling counterclockwise around the unit circle. As the point moves, its x-coordinate oscillates between −1 and 1 — this oscillation is the cosine function. Its y-coordinate oscillates between −1 and 1 as well — this is the sine function. The two coordinates trace out the same oscillating pattern but offset from each other: cosine starts at its maximum (x = 1 when θ = 0), while sine starts at 0 (y = 0 when θ = 0). This offset relationship becomes central in Chapter 5.

Example 4.4.1
Reading sine and cosine from a unit circle point
  • ProblemA point on the unit circle is at (−√2/2, √2/2). What are the sine and cosine of the corresponding angle?
  • ThinkBy the unit circle definition, the x-coordinate is cosine and the y-coordinate is sine. Both coordinates have magnitude √2/2. The negative x and positive y place this in Quadrant II.
  • RepresentLet θ be the angle in standard position whose terminal side passes through (−√2/2, √2/2).
  • Solve cos θ = −√2/2     sin θ = √2/2 This corresponds to θ = 135° (or 3π/4)
  • CheckDoes (−√2/2)² + (√2/2)² = 1? → 1/2 + 1/2 = 1. ✓
Answer: cos(135°) = −√2/2 and sin(135°) = √2/2.
Section 4.5

Tangent as a Ratio

Definition — Tangent

The tangent of angle θ is the ratio of sine to cosine:

tan θ = sin θ / cos θ = y / x     (provided x ≠ 0)

Domain: all real numbers except θ = π/2 + nπ for any integer n     Range: (−∞, ∞)

Tangent as Slope

Notice that y/x is the slope of the line from the origin to the point (x, y). The terminal side of θ passes through the origin and the unit circle point (cos θ, sin θ). Its slope is:

slope = (sin θ − 0) / (cos θ − 0) = sin θ / cos θ = tan θ

So tan θ is the slope of the terminal side of θ. This explains why tangent is undefined at θ = π/2 and θ = 3π/2 — the terminal sides there are vertical lines, which have undefined slope.

Critical Notation Warning — sin⁻¹ vs csc

The notation sin⁻¹(x) does not mean 1/sin(x). It means the inverse sine function (arcsine), which gives the angle whose sine is x. The reciprocal of sine is written csc(x). These are completely different functions. Never confuse them.

Example 4.6.1
Evaluating reciprocal trigonometric functions
  • ProblemA point on the unit circle is (1/2, √3/2). Find sec θ, csc θ, and cot θ.
  • ThinkIdentify cos θ = x = 1/2 and sin θ = y = √3/2, then apply the reciprocal definitions.
  • Solve sec θ = 1 / cos θ = 1 / (1/2) = 2 csc θ = 1 / sin θ = 1 / (√3/2) = 2/√3 = 2√3/3 cot θ = cos θ / sin θ = (1/2) / (√3/2) = 1/√3 = √3/3
  • Checkcos²θ + sin²θ = 1/4 + 3/4 = 1. ✓

Section 4.8

Special Angles in Degrees

Most angles on the unit circle do not have coordinates expressible as simple fractions or square roots. However, certain angles have unit circle coordinates that can be computed exactly using geometry. These are called special angles, and they correspond to the 30-60-90 and 45-45-90 triangle structures.

The 45-45-90 Triangle and 45°

A right triangle with two 45° angles and hypotenuse 1 has both legs equal to √2/2 (by the Pythagorean theorem). Placing this triangle inside the unit circle with the angle at the origin, the far vertex lands at (√2/2, √2/2). Therefore for θ = 45°: cos(45°) = √2/2 and sin(45°) = √2/2.

The 30-60-90 Triangle and 30°, 60°

A right triangle with angles 30°-60°-90° and hypotenuse 1 has the shorter leg opposite 30° equal to 1/2, and the longer leg opposite 60° equal to √3/2. Placing each angle at the origin gives the coordinates below.

Pattern for Memorizing Sine Values

Reading sine from 0° to 90°, the values are √0/2, √1/2, √2/2, √3/2, √4/2 — which simplify to 0, 1/2, √2/2, √3/2, 1. The cosine values follow the same pattern in reverse. Understanding the geometric source (30-60-90 and 45-45-90 triangles) is more important than memorization, but this pattern can help as a check.

Section 4.9

Special Angles in Radians

In calculus, physics, and most advanced applications, radians are the preferred unit. Every special angle has an exact radian equivalent. The five Quadrant I reference angles in radians are 0, π/6, π/4, π/3, π/2 — corresponding to 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°. Once the Quadrant I values are known, all four quadrants follow from the reference angle method and sign patterns.

Example 4.9.1
Identifying a radian angle from unit circle coordinates
  • ProblemWhat is the radian measure for the angle corresponding to the point (−1/2, −√3/2)?
  • ThinkThe absolute values 1/2 and √3/2 match the 60° angle. Both signs are negative, so the point is in Quadrant III.
  • RepresentReference angle is π/3 (since cos(π/3) = 1/2 and sin(π/3) = √3/2). In Quadrant III, angle = π + reference angle.
  • Solveπ + π/3 = 3π/3 + π/3 = 4π/3

Section 4.10

Reference Angles on the Unit Circle

The reference angle is the acute angle between the terminal side of θ and the nearest part of the x-axis. It is always between 0 and π/2 and always positive. Reference angles unlock the entire unit circle from just the Quadrant I values.

The Reference Angle Method

Step 1. Find the reference angle θ̂ for the given angle θ.

Step 2. Evaluate the trig function at θ̂ using Quadrant I values.

Step 3. Determine the sign based on which quadrant the original angle θ is in.

Step 4. Combine the magnitude from Step 2 with the sign from Step 3.

Reference Angle Formulas by Quadrant

QuadrantRange of θReference Angle θ̂
I0 < θ < π/2θ̂ = θ
IIπ/2 < θ < πθ̂ = π − θ
IIIπ < θ < 3π/2θ̂ = θ − π
IV3π/2 < θ < 2πθ̂ = 2π − θ

Section 4.13

Unit Circle Symmetry

Symmetry Across the x-Axis (Even and Odd Functions)

If (x, y) is the point on the unit circle at angle θ, then the reflection of this point across the x-axis is (x, −y), which is the point at angle −θ. Therefore:

cos(−θ) = x = cos(θ)     → cosine is an EVEN function: f(−x) = f(x) sin(−θ) = −y = −sin(θ)     → sine is an ODD function: f(−x) = −f(x) tan(−θ) = sin(−θ)/cos(−θ) = −sin(θ)/cos(θ) = −tan(θ)     → tangent is ODD

An even function has a graph symmetric across the y-axis. An odd function has a graph symmetric about the origin. Cosine is even; sine and tangent are odd. Secant is even; cosecant and cotangent are odd.

Symmetry Across the y-Axis

If (x, y) is at angle θ, its reflection across the y-axis is (−x, y), which is at angle π − θ. This gives:

cos(π − θ) = −cos(θ)     sin(π − θ) = sin(θ)

This explains why sin(150°) = sin(30°) = 1/2: the points 30° and 150° are reflections across the y-axis, sharing the same y-coordinate.

Symmetry Across the Origin

cos(θ + π) = −cos(θ)     sin(θ + π) = −sin(θ)

This explains why sin(210°) = −sin(30°): the points 30° and 210° are reflections through the origin, with both coordinates negated.

Periodicity

The unit circle has a complete cycle every 2π radians. After rotating 2π, the terminal side returns to the same position and the same unit circle point.

Periodicity of Sine and Cosine

cos(θ + 2π) = cos(θ)     Cosine has period 2π.

sin(θ + 2π) = sin(θ)     Sine has period 2π.

tan(θ + π) = tan(θ)     Tangent has period π.

Section 4.14

Common Unit Circle Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Sine and Cosine as Coordinates
"sin θ = x (horizontal) and cos θ = y (vertical)" Correction: cos θ = x (horizontal) and sin θ = y (vertical). Cosine is the x-coordinate; sine is the y-coordinate. Cosine reaches its maximum of 1 at θ = 0, where the point is at (1, 0) on the positive x-axis.
Mistake 2 — Claiming Sine or Cosine Can Exceed 1
sin θ = 2  (or any value outside [−1, 1]) Correction: All points on the unit circle satisfy x² + y² = 1, so x and y are always in [−1, 1]. No angle produces a sine or cosine outside this interval. If an equation leads to sin θ = 2, the equation has no solution.
Mistake 3 — Correct Magnitude, Wrong Sign
sin(240°) = √3/2  (sign error) Correction: 240° is in Quadrant III where sine is negative. The reference angle is 60°, so |sin(240°)| = sin(60°) = √3/2, but with the Quadrant III sign: sin(240°) = −√3/2. Always check the sign after computing the magnitude.
Mistake 4 — Confusing sin⁻¹ with csc
sin⁻¹(x) = 1/sin(x) = csc(x) Correction: sin⁻¹(x) is the inverse sine function (arcsine), giving the angle whose sine is x. The reciprocal of sine is csc(x). The superscript −1 on a trigonometric function name always means inverse function, not reciprocal.
Mistake 5 — Wrong Reference Angle Formula
For θ in Quadrant III, reference angle = 180° + θ (wrong direction) Correction: In Quadrant III (π < θ < 3π/2), the reference angle is θ − π (or θ − 180°), which is positive because θ > π. The reference angle is always the positive acute angle between the terminal side and the nearest x-axis.
Mistake 6 — Not Applying Periodicity
"7π/3 is not a standard angle so I cannot evaluate it." Correction: Subtract 2π to find a coterminal angle: 7π/3 − 2π = 7π/3 − 6π/3 = π/3. Then evaluate normally: cos(7π/3) = cos(π/3) = 1/2.
Mistake 7 — Mixing Up QII and QIII Reference Angle Formulas
For 2π/3 (QII), reference angle = 2π/3 − π = −π/3 (negative!) Correction: In Quadrant II (π/2 < θ < π), the reference angle is π − θ. For 2π/3: π − 2π/3 = π/3. In Quadrant III (π < θ < 3π/2), the reference angle is θ − π. Never subtract a larger number from a smaller one and accept a negative reference angle.
Chapter 4 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 4.1The unit circle extends trigonometry beyond right triangles to all real-number angles. With radius 1, it produces the cleanest connection between angles and coordinates, turning trigonometry into a complete function system with domain (−∞, ∞) and range [−1, 1].
  • Sec 4.2The unit circle is the graph of x² + y² = 1. For any angle θ in standard position, the terminal side meets the unit circle at the point (cos θ, sin θ). The Pythagorean identity cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 follows directly from the circle equation.
  • Sec 4.3The four axis points are (1,0), (0,1), (−1,0), and (0,−1) at θ = 0, π/2, π, 3π/2. Since all unit circle coordinates satisfy x² + y² = 1, both cos θ and sin θ are always in [−1, 1].
  • Sec 4.4Cosine is the x-coordinate; sine is the y-coordinate. Both are genuine functions with domain all real numbers and range [−1, 1]. As a point travels the circle, the coordinates oscillate in opposite phase.
  • Sec 4.5tan θ = sin θ / cos θ = y/x. Tangent equals the slope of the terminal side. Domain: all reals except π/2 + nπ. Range: (−∞, ∞).
  • Sec 4.6Secant = 1/cos, cosecant = 1/sin, cotangent = cos/sin. Their ranges are (−∞,−1]∪[1,∞) for sec and csc, and (−∞,∞) for cot. The notation sin⁻¹ means inverse function, not 1/sin.
  • Sec 4.7Signs in each quadrant follow the ASTC pattern: All positive in QI, Sine in QII, Tangent in QIII, Cosine in QIV. Each reciprocal function carries the same sign as its corresponding primary function.
  • Sec 4.8Special angles at 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90° have exact coordinates derivable from 45-45-90 and 30-60-90 triangles. Quadrant I values: (1,0), (√3/2, 1/2), (√2/2, √2/2), (1/2, √3/2), (0,1).
  • Sec 4.9Radian equivalents of special angles: 0, π/6, π/4, π/3, π/2. All 16 standard positions follow from these five values and the reference angle method applied to all four quadrants.
  • Sec 4.10The reference angle is the acute angle between the terminal side and the nearest x-axis. By quadrant: QI: θ̂ = θ; QII: θ̂ = π − θ; QIII: θ̂ = θ − π; QIV: θ̂ = 2π − θ.
  • Sec 4.11Full evaluation: find quadrant → find reference angle → get QI coordinates → apply quadrant signs → compute tangent and reciprocals. Axis angles have special coordinate values where tan or cot may be 0 or undefined.
  • Sec 4.12Exact values are fractions and radicals, more precise than decimal approximations. All 16 standard positions have exact coordinates. Tangent exact values are computed from the ratio of the coordinates at each position.
  • Sec 4.13Cosine is even: cos(−θ) = cos(θ). Sine is odd: sin(−θ) = −sin(θ). Tangent is odd. Sine and cosine are periodic with period 2π; tangent with period π. Reflections across x-axis, y-axis, and origin each produce a family of related values.
  • Sec 4.14Seven key mistakes: swapping x and y for cos/sin; values outside [−1,1]; correct magnitude wrong sign; sin⁻¹ ≠ csc; wrong reference angle formula; not using periodicity; mixing QII and QIII reference formulas.

Key Terms Review

unit circle
The circle centered at the origin with radius 1, defined by the equation x² + y² = 1. The foundation of the trigonometric function system.
sine (sin θ)
The y-coordinate of the point on the unit circle at angle θ. Domain: (−∞, ∞). Range: [−1, 1]. An odd function.
cosine (cos θ)
The x-coordinate of the point on the unit circle at angle θ. Domain: (−∞, ∞). Range: [−1, 1]. An even function.
tangent (tan θ)
The ratio sin θ / cos θ = y/x. Geometrically, the slope of the terminal side of θ. Undefined where cos θ = 0. An odd function. Range: (−∞, ∞).
secant (sec θ)
The reciprocal of cosine: 1/cos θ. Undefined where cos θ = 0. Range: (−∞,−1] ∪ [1,∞). An even function.
cosecant (csc θ)
The reciprocal of sine: 1/sin θ. Undefined where sin θ = 0. Range: (−∞,−1] ∪ [1,∞). An odd function.
cotangent (cot θ)
The ratio cos θ / sin θ = x/y; the reciprocal of tangent. Undefined where sin θ = 0. Range: (−∞, ∞). An odd function.
special angle
An angle whose unit circle coordinates can be expressed exactly. The standard special angles are 0, π/6, π/4, π/3, π/2 and their equivalents in all four quadrants.
reference angle
The acute angle between the terminal side of a given angle and the nearest part of the x-axis. Always in [0, π/2]. Used to evaluate trig functions at any angle.
exact value
A trigonometric value expressed as a fraction or radical rather than a decimal approximation. Preferred in algebra, calculus, and physics.
Pythagorean identity
The identity cos²θ + sin²θ = 1, derived directly from the unit circle equation x² + y² = 1. Holds for every real angle θ.
even function
A function satisfying f(−x) = f(x) for all x. Its graph is symmetric across the y-axis. Cosine and secant are even.
odd function
A function satisfying f(−x) = −f(x) for all x. Its graph is symmetric about the origin. Sine, tangent, cosecant, and cotangent are odd.
periodic function
A function that repeats its values at regular intervals. Sine and cosine have period 2π. Tangent and cotangent have period π.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 5: Graphs of Trigonometric Functions

Chapter 4 established that as θ increases from 0 to 2π, the point (cos θ, sin θ) travels around the unit circle. The y-coordinate — sine — starts at 0, rises to 1, returns to 0, falls to −1, and returns to 0. The x-coordinate — cosine — starts at 1, falls to 0, continues to −1, rises to 0, and returns to 1. These oscillating values are functions of θ. When plotted with θ on the horizontal axis and the trigonometric value on the vertical axis, they produce continuous wave-shaped graphs.

In Chapter 5, students will graph these waves and analyze their features. Every graph feature connects directly to unit circle behavior:

  • Amplitude — how far the graph rises and falls from the midline
  • Period — the length of one complete cycle (related to 2π)
  • Phase shift — horizontal displacement caused by changing the input
  • Vertical shift — moving the midline up or down
  • Asymptotes of tangent — where cosine equals zero
  • Modeling tides, sound, seasons, rotation, and oscillation

Students who understand the unit circle thoroughly will find trigonometric graphs intuitive and natural. Students who memorize without understanding will find Chapter 5 confusing. The time invested in Chapter 4 pays forward through every remaining chapter of this book.

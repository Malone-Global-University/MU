Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter serves as the bridge between precalculus and Calculus I. Every idea here grows out of something students have already studied: functions, graphs, domain, rate of change, and behavior at the edges of a domain. The goal is not to teach calculus here. The goal is to make calculus feel inevitable — like the natural next question after everything precalculus has built.

Calculus is the mathematics of change and accumulation. Precalculus is the foundation on which calculus stands. Students who understand functions deeply, who can read a graph carefully, who know what a rate of change means, and who have thought about what happens to a function's output as the input approaches a value — those students are genuinely ready for Calculus I.

This chapter introduces three central ideas of calculus as intuition, not formal theory: the limit, continuity, and the rate of change. Students will estimate limits from tables and graphs, interpret one-sided behavior, understand what continuity means and what breaks it, review average rates of change, see how secant lines approach tangent lines, get a first look at instantaneous rate of change as an idea, and preview the concept of area under a curve.

Opening Question

A car is traveling along a highway. Its speed is not constant — it accelerates, decelerates, and changes with traffic. You can measure the car's average speed over the last ten minutes: divide the total distance by ten minutes. But what is the car's speed at this exact instant — right now, at this precise moment?

You cannot measure an instant. An instant has zero length. But you can get closer and closer to an instant by shrinking the time interval: measure speed over five minutes, then two minutes, then thirty seconds, then ten seconds. As the interval shrinks toward zero, the average speed over that interval approaches a number. That number is the instantaneous speed. This is the idea of a limit. This is the beginning of calculus.

Section 13.1

Why Calculus Needs Precalculus

Every major idea in calculus — derivatives, integrals, infinite series — depends directly on the function concepts studied in precalculus. Before students can understand what a derivative is, they must understand what a function is, how to evaluate it, how to read its graph, how to compute rates of change, and how to reason about its behavior near special values. Before students can understand integrals, they must be comfortable with area, accumulation, and summation.

Precalculus is not an obstacle before calculus. It is the architecture on which calculus is built.

Branch of CalculusCentral QuestionKey Tool
Differential CalculusAt any given instant, how fast is this function changing?The derivative
Integral CalculusHow much has accumulated over a period of time or distance?The integral
Common FoundationAs the input approaches a value, what does the output approach?The limit
Key Insight

Students have already studied average rates of change in Chapter 1. This chapter will show how that familiar idea leads naturally to instantaneous rate of change — and to calculus. The limit is the bridge that connects the two.

Section 13.2

What a Limit Describes

Imagine approaching a building on foot. As you walk toward it, the building gets closer and closer. The concept of a limit is similar: you are asking what value a function's output approaches as the input gets closer and closer to a particular number.

Given a function f(x) and a value x = a, the central question is: as x gets closer and closer to a — from either side — what value does f(x) approach?

Limit Notation

The limit of f(x) as x approaches a equals L is written:

lim[x→a] f(x) = L

Read: "The limit of f of x as x approaches a equals L."

This is not asking for the value of f(a). It is asking what value f(x) gets closer and closer to as x approaches a. The input approaches a but may never equal a.

A Concrete Example Before Formulas

Consider the function f(x) = (x² – 1) / (x – 1). This function is undefined at x = 1 because the denominator equals zero there. But for x ≠ 1, the numerator factors:

f(x) = (x–1)(x+1) / (x–1) = x + 1 for x ≠ 1

So even though f(1) does not exist, the function behaves like y = x + 1 near x = 1. As x approaches 1, the output approaches 1 + 1 = 2. Therefore: lim[x→1] (x²–1)/(x–1) = 2.

Informal Definition

The limit of f(x) as x approaches a equals L if the values of f(x) can be made arbitrarily close to L by taking x sufficiently close to a (but not equal to a) on either side. A limit is about approaching, not arriving.

Example 13.2.1
Interpreting limit notation in plain English
  • ProblemState what lim[x→3] f(x) = 7 means in plain English.
  • ThinkLimit notation is a statement about approaching behavior. Translate each part separately.
  • SolveAs the input x gets closer and closer to 3 (from either side, without equaling 3), the output f(x) gets closer and closer to 7.
  • CheckThis statement says nothing about f(3) itself. The function could equal 7 at x = 3, equal something else entirely, or be undefined there.
Answer: As x approaches 3, the output of f approaches 7.
Section 13.3

Estimating Limits from Tables

One of the most concrete ways to investigate a limit is by constructing a table of values. Students choose inputs that approach the target value from both sides and observe what the outputs approach.

Strategy: Estimating a Limit from a Table

1. Choose input values approaching a from the left (slightly less than a).

2. Choose input values approaching a from the right (slightly greater than a).

3. Compute the output f(x) for each input.

4. If outputs from both sides appear to approach the same value L, then lim[x→a] f(x) = L.

Example 13.3.1
Estimating a limit from a table
  • ProblemEstimate lim[x→2] (x² – 4) / (x – 2) using a table.
  • ThinkThis function is undefined at x = 2. Build a table with inputs approaching 2 from both sides.
  • Represent

    1.993.99
    1.9993.999
    2.0014.001
    2.014.01
    2.14.1
  • SolveFrom the left, outputs approach 4. From the right, outputs also approach 4.
  • CheckAlgebraically: (x² – 4)/(x – 2) = (x–2)(x+2)/(x–2) = x + 2 for x ≠ 2. As x → 2, x + 2 → 4. ✓
Answer: lim[x→2] (x² – 4)/(x – 2) = 4
Example 13.3.2
A limit that does not exist — oscillation
  • ProblemEstimate lim[x→0] sin(1/x) using a table.
  • ThinkAs x → 0, the input to sine, 1/x, grows without bound. Sine oscillates between −1 and 1 infinitely many times.
  • Represent

Student Warning

A table can only suggest a limit. It cannot prove one. Use tables as estimation tools, not as definitive proofs. If the table values are not settling clearly toward a single number, suspect that the limit may not exist.

Section 13.4

Estimating Limits from Graphs

A graph of a function gives visual evidence about limits. Students can trace a graph from either side of a target input value and observe whether the graph appears to approach a single output value from both sides. Crucially, look at where the graph is going — not at any dot that may or may not be marked at x = a.

Example 13.4.1
Reading a limit at a hole
  • ProblemEstimate lim[x→2] f(x) from the graph described below.
  • Represent
    Graph Description

    Draw the graph of y = x + 1 as a straight line with slope 1 and y-intercept 1. At the point (2, 3), place an open circle — indicating that the function is not defined at x = 2. There is no filled dot at x = 2. The graph is a straight line missing exactly one point.

  • SolveFrom the left, as x approaches 2, the graph rises toward height 3. From the right, as x approaches 2, the graph falls toward height 3. Both sides approach the same height.
  • CheckThe open circle confirms f(2) is undefined, but the limit still exists.
Answer: lim[x→2] f(x) = 3, even though f(2) is undefined.
Example 13.4.2
Reading a limit when the function value disagrees
  • ProblemEstimate lim[x→2] g(x) from the graph described below.
  • Represent
    Graph Description

    Draw the graph of y = x + 1 for all x ≠ 2, with an open circle at (2, 3). Also draw a filled dot at (2, 5), indicating that g(2) = 5. The isolated filled dot floats above the open circle of the line.

  • SolveFrom the left and right, the graph approaches height 3. The filled dot at 5 is an isolated point — the graph is not approaching 5 from either side.
Answer: lim[x→2] g(x) = 3, even though g(2) = 5.
Key Insight

The limit describes where the graph is heading, not where the dot is placed. These two things can be different, and recognizing that difference is the first step in understanding continuity.

Section 13.5

One-Sided Limits as an Introduction

Sometimes a function approaches different values from the left and from the right. When this happens, the two-sided limit does not exist, but each one-sided limit may still be defined.

One-Sided Limit Notation

lim[x→a⁻] f(x) — the left-hand limit. As x approaches a from values less than a.

lim[x→a⁺] f(x) — the right-hand limit. As x approaches a from values greater than a.

The two-sided limit lim[x→a] f(x) = L exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and are equal. If they differ, the two-sided limit does not exist.

Example 13.5.1
One-sided limits from a piecewise function
  • Problem Let f(x) = { x + 1 if x < 2 | 5 if x = 2 | 3x − 2 if x > 2 }.
    Find the left-hand limit, right-hand limit, and two-sided limit at x = 2.
  • ThinkUse the rule that applies for each approach direction. The value at x = 2 exactly (f(2) = 5) is irrelevant to the limits.
  • Solve Left-hand limit (x → 2⁻): use f(x) = x + 1 lim[x→2⁻] f(x) = 2 + 1 = 3 Right-hand limit (x → 2⁺): use f(x) = 3x − 2 lim[x→2⁺] f(x) = 3(2) − 2 = 4
  • CheckLeft limit = 3, right limit = 4. They are not equal. The two-sided limit does not exist.
Answer: Left-hand limit = 3, right-hand limit = 4. The two-sided limit does not exist.
Graph Description — Example 13.5.1

Draw three pieces: (1) A ray from the left following y = x + 1, approaching the open circle at (2, 3). (2) A filled dot at (2, 5), isolated above the graph. (3) A ray from the right following y = 3x − 2, approaching the open circle at (2, 4). The two open circles sit at different heights, showing clearly that the left and right approaches do not meet.

Section 13.6

Infinite Behavior and End Behavior

Some limits describe what happens as the input grows without bound. Others describe what happens as a function's output grows without bound near a particular input. Both types appear in graphs students have already studied.

TypeNotationMeaningGraph Feature
Limit at infinitylim[x→∞] f(x) = LAs x grows without bound, f(x) approaches LHorizontal asymptote y = L
Limit at −∞lim[x→−∞] f(x) = LAs x decreases without bound, f(x) approaches LHorizontal asymptote y = L
Infinite limitlim[x→a] f(x) = ±∞f(x) grows without bound near x = aVertical asymptote x = a
Example 13.6.1
Limit at infinity for a rational function
  • ProblemEvaluate lim[x→∞] (3x + 1) / (x − 2).
  • ThinkFor large x, divide numerator and denominator by x to isolate the dominant behavior.
  • Solve (3x + 1) / (x − 2) = (3 + 1/x) / (1 − 2/x) As x → ∞: 1/x → 0 and 2/x → 0 lim = (3 + 0) / (1 − 0) = 3
  • CheckThe horizontal asymptote is y = 3, consistent with the degree of numerator and denominator being equal and the ratio of leading coefficients being 3/1.
Answer: lim[x→∞] (3x + 1)/(x − 2) = 3. Horizontal asymptote at y = 3.
Example 13.6.2
Infinite limit near a vertical asymptote
  • ProblemDescribe the behavior of f(x) = 1/(x − 3) as x → 3.
  • ThinkAs x approaches 3, the denominator approaches 0 while the numerator stays at 1. The sign of the denominator determines whether the output shoots to +∞ or −∞.
  • Solve From the right (x → 3⁺): denominator is small and positive 1 / (small positive) → +∞ From the left (x → 3⁻): denominator is small and negative 1 / (small negative) → −∞
  • CheckThe two one-sided limits are not equal (one is +∞, one is −∞), so the two-sided limit does not exist. There is a vertical asymptote at x = 3.
Answer: lim[x→3⁺] f(x) = +∞, lim[x→3⁻] f(x) = −∞. Two-sided limit does not exist.
Student Warning — Infinity Is Not a Number

When we write lim[x→a] f(x) = ∞, we mean the function grows without bound — this is a description of behavior, not a value the limit equals. Technically, such a limit does not exist as a finite number. ∞ is not a number you can substitute or compute with.

Section 13.7

Continuity as Unbroken Behavior

A function is continuous if its graph can be drawn without lifting the pencil. That informal description captures something precise: a function is continuous at a point if the function has a value there, if the limit exists there, and if the value and the limit agree.

Three Conditions for Continuity at x = a

1. f(a) is defined.

2. lim[x→a] f(x) exists.

3. lim[x→a] f(x) = f(a).

If any one of these three conditions fails, f is not continuous at x = a — it has a discontinuity there.

Example 13.7.1
Checking continuity at a point — a hole
  • ProblemIs f(x) = (x² − 9)/(x − 3) continuous at x = 3?
  • ThinkCheck all three conditions in order.
  • Solve Condition 1: f(3) = (9 − 9)/(3 − 3) = 0/0 → Undefined. ✗ Since Condition 1 fails, f is not continuous at x = 3. (Note: the limit does exist — lim[x→3] f(x) = 6 — but that cannot rescue continuity if f(3) is undefined.)
Answer: Not continuous at x = 3. Removable discontinuity (hole) at (3, 6).
Example 13.7.2
Verifying continuity for a polynomial
  • ProblemIs g(x) = x² + 3x − 1 continuous at x = 2?
  • Solve Condition 1: g(2) = 4 + 6 − 1 = 9 ✓ (defined) Condition 2: lim[x→2] (x² + 3x − 1) = 9 ✓ (limit exists) Condition 3: limit (9) = function value (9) ✓
Answer: g is continuous at x = 2. All three conditions hold.
Shortcut for Familiar Functions

Polynomial, exponential, sine, and cosine functions are continuous everywhere on their domains. For these functions, the limit at any point always equals the function value — no need to verify each condition separately.

Section 13.8

Discontinuities

When a function fails to be continuous at a point, it has a discontinuity there. There are three main types, each with a distinct graph appearance.

Type 1: Removable Discontinuity (Hole)

A removable discontinuity occurs when the limit exists, but either f(a) is undefined or f(a) does not equal the limit. This appears as a hole in the graph. The discontinuity is called "removable" because it can be repaired by redefining the function's value at that one point.

Graph Description — Removable Discontinuity

Draw the line y = x + 2. At the point (2, 4), place an open circle. The graph is a straight line missing exactly one point. Optionally, a filled dot at (2, 7) would indicate a redefined but still-discontinuous value.

Type 2: Jump Discontinuity

A jump discontinuity occurs when both one-sided limits exist but are not equal. The graph appears to jump from one height to another at x = a.

Graph Description — Jump Discontinuity

Draw a horizontal ray at height 2 for x < 1, ending in an open circle at (1, 2). Draw a horizontal ray at height 5 for x ≥ 1, starting with a filled dot at (1, 5). There is a visible vertical gap between the two pieces — the graph literally jumps from height 2 to height 5 at x = 1.

Type 3: Infinite Discontinuity

An infinite discontinuity occurs when f(x) grows without bound as x approaches a. The graph has a vertical asymptote at x = a.

Graph Description — Infinite Discontinuity

Draw the hyperbola y = 1/x. As x → 0⁺, the right branch rises toward +∞. As x → 0⁻, the left branch falls toward −∞. A dashed vertical line marks the y-axis (x = 0) as the asymptote. The function is not defined at x = 0.

Section 13.9

Average Rate of Change Review

Students have studied average rate of change throughout precalculus. This section reviews that idea carefully, because it is the direct algebraic ancestor of the derivative.

Average Rate of Change

The average rate of change of f on the interval [a, b] is:

[f(b) − f(a)] / (b − a)

This is the slope of the line connecting the two points (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)) on the graph of f. It measures how much the output changes per unit of input, averaged across the entire interval.

Example 13.9.1
Computing average rate of change
  • ProblemFind the average rate of change of f(x) = x² on [1, 4].
  • ThinkThis is the slope of the line from (1, f(1)) to (4, f(4)).
  • Solve f(1) = 1, f(4) = 16 Average rate of change = (16 − 1) / (4 − 1) = 15 / 3 = 5
  • CheckOn average, the output increases by 5 for every 1-unit increase in input across [1, 4].
Answer: Average rate of change = 5.
Example 13.9.2
Average rate of change with context
  • ProblemA population of bacteria is modeled by P(t) = 200 · 2^t, where t is hours and P is the number of bacteria. Find the average rate of change from t = 1 to t = 3.
  • Solve P(1) = 200 · 2¹ = 400 P(3) = 200 · 2³ = 1600 Average rate = (1600 − 400) / (3 − 1) = 1200 / 2 = 600
  • CheckUnits: bacteria per hour. This is an average — the actual rate varies because the function is exponential, not linear.
Answer: The population grows at an average rate of 600 bacteria per hour between t = 1 and t = 3 hours.
Section 13.10

Secant Lines

The line connecting two points on a curve is called a secant line. Its slope equals the average rate of change of the function between those two points.

Secant Line Slope

Given a function f and two distinct input values a and b, the secant line through (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)) has slope:

m_sec = [f(b) − f(a)] / (b − a)

As the two points are moved closer together — as b is moved toward a — the secant line changes its angle. In the limit, it becomes the tangent line.

Example 13.10.1
Finding and interpreting a secant line
  • ProblemFind the equation of the secant line through (1, f(1)) and (3, f(3)) for f(x) = x².
  • Solve f(1) = 1, f(3) = 9 Slope: m = (9 − 1) / (3 − 1) = 8/2 = 4 Point-slope form using (1, 1): y − 1 = 4(x − 1) y = 4x − 3
  • Represent
    Graph Description

    Draw the parabola y = x². Mark the points (1, 1) and (3, 9). Draw the straight line y = 4x − 3 passing through both points. This is the secant line. It crosses the parabola at two locations and passes above the parabola between them.

Answer: The secant line is y = 4x − 3.

Section 13.11

Tangent Lines as a Preview

A tangent line at a point on a curve is the line that just touches the curve at that point and has the same direction (slope) as the curve at that exact location. Unlike a secant line, which crosses the curve at two points, the tangent line touches the curve at one point and represents the curve's local direction there.

The Tangent Line as a Limit of Secant Lines

As b approaches a, the secant line through (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)) rotates and approaches a limiting position: the tangent line at x = a. Its slope is:

m_tangent = lim[b→a] [f(b) − f(a)] / (b − a)

In Calculus I, this limit is called the derivative f′(a).

Example 13.11.1
Slope of the tangent line via a limit
  • ProblemFind the slope of the tangent line to f(x) = x² at x = 1 using a limit.
  • ThinkCompute [f(b) − f(1)] / (b − 1) and take b → 1.
  • Solve [f(b) − f(1)] / (b − 1) = (b² − 1) / (b − 1) = (b − 1)(b + 1) / (b − 1) = b + 1 for b ≠ 1 lim[b→1] (b + 1) = 2
  • Represent
    Graph Description

    Draw the parabola y = x². Mark the point (1, 1). Draw the tangent line y = 2x − 1 with slope 2. The tangent line touches the parabola exactly at (1, 1) and runs alongside the curve locally, neither crossing it steeply nor flattening away.

  • CheckIn Calculus I, the derivative of f(x) = x² is f′(x) = 2x. At x = 1, f′(1) = 2. ✓ This chapter derived that result using limits alone.
Answer: Slope of tangent = 2. Tangent line: y = 2x − 1.
Section 13.12

Instantaneous Rate of Change as a Preview

The instantaneous rate of change of a function at a single point describes how fast the function is changing at that exact input — not over an interval, but at a single moment. Limits resolve the apparent paradox of measuring change at a single point.

Instantaneous Rate of Change (Limit Definition — Preview)

The instantaneous rate of change of f at x = a is:

lim[h→0] [f(a + h) − f(a)] / h

Here, h is the width of the interval. As h → 0, the two points (a, f(a)) and (a+h, f(a+h)) merge into one, and the secant slope approaches the tangent slope. This limit is the definition of f′(a) in Calculus I.

Example 13.12.1
Instantaneous rate of change via the difference quotient
  • ProblemFind the instantaneous rate of change of f(x) = x² + 1 at x = 2.
  • ThinkCompute [f(2 + h) − f(2)] / h, simplify, and take the limit as h → 0.
  • Solve f(2) = 4 + 1 = 5 f(2 + h) = (2 + h)² + 1 = 4 + 4h + h² + 1 = 5 + 4h + h² [f(2+h) − f(2)] / h = [(5 + 4h + h²) − 5] / h = (4h + h²) / h = h(4 + h) / h = 4 + h lim[h→0] (4 + h) = 4
  • CheckIn Calculus I, the derivative of f(x) = x² + 1 is f′(x) = 2x. At x = 2, f′(2) = 4. ✓
Answer: The instantaneous rate of change of f(x) = x² + 1 at x = 2 is 4. At exactly x = 2, the function is increasing at a rate of 4 output units per input unit.

Section 13.13

Area Under a Curve as a Preview

Integral calculus studies accumulation: how much of something accumulates as a variable changes. One of the most geometric ways to understand accumulation is through the idea of area under a curve.

Given a function f(x) ≥ 0 on an interval [a, b], what is the total area of the region bounded above by the graph, below by the x-axis, on the left by x = a, and on the right by x = b? For straight-line graphs, this question is straightforward. For curves, it requires limits.

Riemann Sums — Approximating Area with Rectangles

Divide [a, b] into n equal subintervals of width Δx = (b − a)/n. On each subinterval, build a rectangle whose height equals the function's value at a representative input. The area approximation is:

Area ≈ Σ f(xₖ) · Δx (sum from k = 1 to n)

As n → ∞ and Δx → 0, the rectangles fill the region precisely. The exact area is the limit of these Riemann sums, written in Calculus I as:

∫[a to b] f(x) dx

The Fundamental Connection

In Calculus I, students will discover that differentiation and integration are inverse processes. The derivative of the area function (with respect to the upper limit) returns the original function. This relationship is called the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — one of the most important results in the history of mathematics.

Section 13.14

Common Limit and Continuity Misunderstandings

Misunderstanding 1 — Confusing the Limit with the Function Value
lim[x→a] f(x) = f(a) (automatic assumption)

The limit describes approaching behavior, not the value at the point. They may differ. Check all three continuity conditions separately — never assume they agree without verification.

Misunderstanding 2 — Believing a Limit Must Be Achieved
"If lim[x→a] f(x) = L, then f reaches the value L at x = a."

A limit is about approaching, not arriving. The function may approach L without ever equaling L at x = a — for example, when there is a hole at (a, L).

Misunderstanding 3 — Treating ∞ as a Number
lim[x→a] f(x) = ∞, so the limit equals infinity.

Writing lim[x→a] f(x) = ∞ describes unbounded behavior, not a finite value the limit equals. Technically, the limit does not exist in this case. ∞ is not a number.

Misunderstanding 4 — Thinking Limits Only Matter at Undefined Points
"Limits only apply when the function is undefined."

Limits apply everywhere. Even perfectly continuous functions have limits at every point — they just happen to equal the function values. Limits are universal; continuity is the special case where limit and value agree.

Misunderstanding 5 — Confusing Average Rate with Instantaneous Rate
"Average rate of change over [1, 4] is 5, so the instantaneous rate at x = 1 is 5."

Average rate of change is the slope of the secant — it describes behavior over an interval. Instantaneous rate is the slope of the tangent — it describes behavior at one exact point. They are related through a limit, but not equal in general.

Misunderstanding 6 — Recording a Riemann Sum as the Exact Area
Riemann sum with 4 rectangles = 3.75 = exact area.

A Riemann sum is an approximation. The exact area is the limit as the number of rectangles grows without bound. The approximation improves as rectangles become thinner, but it equals the exact area only in the limit.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1Calculus has two main branches — differential (rates of change) and integral (accumulation) — both built on the limit. Precalculus is the direct foundation for all of calculus.
  • Sec 13.2A limit describes what a function's output approaches as the input approaches a particular value — independent of whether the function has a value at that point. Notation: lim[x→a] f(x) = L.
  • Sec 13.3Limits can be estimated from tables by choosing inputs approaching the target from both sides and observing whether the outputs converge to a single value.
  • Sec 13.4Limits can be estimated from graphs by tracing the approaching height from left and right. The limit is where the graph is heading, not where any dot is placed.
  • Sec 13.5One-sided limits distinguish left-hand (x→a⁻) from right-hand (x→a⁺) approaching behavior. The two-sided limit exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and are equal.
  • Sec 13.6Limits at infinity describe end behavior and connect to horizontal asymptotes. Infinite limits describe unbounded output near a point and correspond to vertical asymptotes.
  • Sec 13.7Continuity at x = a requires three conditions: f(a) is defined; the limit exists; the limit equals the function value. Polynomial, exponential, and trigonometric functions are continuous on their domains.
  • Sec 13.8Discontinuities appear in three forms: removable (hole — limit exists, value wrong or missing), jump (left and right limits differ), and infinite (vertical asymptote — output grows without bound).
  • Sec 13.9Average rate of change = [f(b) − f(a)] / (b − a). It is the slope of the secant line through two points on the graph, and it measures how much the output changes per unit of input on average across the interval.
  • Sec 13.10The secant line through two points on a curve has slope equal to the average rate of change. As the second point moves toward the first, the secant slope approaches a limiting value — the slope of the tangent line.
  • Sec 13.11The tangent line touches a curve at one point and matches its local direction. Its slope is the limit of the secant slopes as the two points merge: m = lim[b→a] [f(b) − f(a)]/(b − a). This limit is the derivative in Calculus I.
  • Sec 13.12Instantaneous rate of change is the limit of average rates of change as the interval shrinks to zero: lim[h→0] [f(a+h) − f(a)]/h. This is the derivative f′(a), central to all of differential calculus.
  • Sec 13.13Area under a curve is approximated by Riemann sums (rectangles). As the number of rectangles grows without bound, the sum approaches the exact area — the definite integral. This is the foundation of integral calculus.
  • Sec 13.14Six common misunderstandings: assuming the limit equals the function value; thinking limits require the function to reach L; treating ∞ as a number; believing limits only apply at undefined points; confusing average and instantaneous rates; recording Riemann sums as exact areas.

Key Terms Review

limit
The value L that f(x) approaches as x approaches a; written lim[x→a] f(x) = L. About approaching behavior, not the function value at the point.
left-hand limit
The limit as x approaches a from values less than a. Notation: lim[x→a⁻] f(x).
right-hand limit
The limit as x approaches a from values greater than a. Notation: lim[x→a⁺] f(x).
limit does not exist
Occurs when left and right limits differ, the output oscillates without settling, or the output grows without bound.
end behavior
How f(x) behaves as x → ∞ or x → −∞. Described by limits at infinity and connected to horizontal asymptotes.
infinite limit
A limit in which f(x) grows without bound as x approaches a. Associated with a vertical asymptote. Not a finite number.
continuity at a point
f(a) is defined, the limit at a exists, and they are equal. All three conditions must hold.
removable discontinuity
A hole in the graph. The limit exists, but f(a) is undefined or disagrees with it. Can be repaired by redefining the function at one point.
jump discontinuity
Both one-sided limits exist but are not equal. The graph jumps at x = a.
infinite discontinuity
The function grows without bound near x = a. The graph has a vertical asymptote.
average rate of change
[f(b) − f(a)] / (b − a). The slope of the secant line through two points on the graph of f.
secant line
The line through two points on a curve. Its slope equals the average rate of change between those points.
tangent line
The line touching a curve at one point, matching its local direction. Its slope is the limit of secant slopes as the interval shrinks to zero.
instantaneous rate of change
The limit of the average rate of change as the interval shrinks to zero. Equals the slope of the tangent line. The derivative in Calculus I.
Riemann sum
An approximation of the area under a curve using rectangles. Becomes more precise as the number of rectangles increases.
definite integral
The limit of Riemann sums as the number of rectangles grows without bound. The exact area under a curve on [a, b]. Written ∫[a to b] f(x) dx in Calculus I.

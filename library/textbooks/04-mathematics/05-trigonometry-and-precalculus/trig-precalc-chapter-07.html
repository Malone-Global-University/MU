Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 7 connects the unit-circle view of trigonometry back to triangle measurement. Students who have studied radian measure, the unit circle, trigonometric functions, their graphs, and their identities are now ready to see how those ideas root themselves in the physical world of shape, distance, and angle. This chapter reviews right-triangle trigonometry, introduces the powerful tools of the Law of Sines and the Law of Cosines, develops area formulas from trigonometric relationships, and places all of this in real contexts: navigation, surveying, engineering, and physics.

This chapter serves as the bridge between Geometry and the function-based trigonometry of Precalculus. Students should leave understanding that trigonometry is a measurement system — one that allows unknown lengths and angles to be found when direct measurement is difficult or impossible.

Opening Question

A forest ranger in a fire lookout tower spots smoke in two directions. From Tower A, the smoke is observed at a bearing of N 42° E. From Tower B, located 15 miles due east of Tower A, the same smoke is observed at a bearing of N 28° W. How far is the fire from each tower?

This kind of problem cannot be solved with a ruler or a protractor alone. It requires trigonometry — specifically, the tools developed in this chapter. By the time you finish Chapter 7, you will have everything you need to solve this problem and dozens like it.

Section 7.1

Right-Triangle Trigonometry Review

Before studying triangles without right angles, it is worth reviewing the foundation. Right-triangle trigonometry connects the six trigonometric functions to ratios of sides. Consider a right triangle with an acute angle θ at one vertex. Label the three sides relative to θ: the opposite side is the leg across from θ, the adjacent side is the leg next to θ (not the hypotenuse), and the hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle.

The Six Trigonometric Ratios

sin θ = opposite / hypotenuse     cos θ = adjacent / hypotenuse     tan θ = opposite / adjacent

csc θ = hypotenuse / opposite     sec θ = hypotenuse / adjacent     cot θ = adjacent / opposite

The Pythagorean Theorem

In every right triangle with legs a and b and hypotenuse c:

a² + b² = c²

Special Right Triangles

Triangle TypeSide RatiosKey Exact Values
45–45–901 : 1 : √2sin 45° = cos 45° = √2/2, tan 45° = 1
30–60–901 : √3 : 2sin 30° = 1/2, cos 30° = √3/2, sin 60° = √3/2, cos 60° = 1/2
Example 7.1.1
Finding a missing side in a right triangle
  • ProblemA right triangle has a hypotenuse of 13 and an acute angle of θ = 35°. Find the side opposite θ.
  • ThinkThe opposite side and hypotenuse are related by sine. Set up the ratio and solve for the unknown.
  • Representsin θ = opposite / hypotenuse
  • Solvesin 35° = opposite / 13 opposite = 13 sin 35° ≈ 13(0.5736) ≈ 7.46
  • CheckThe opposite side (7.46) is shorter than the hypotenuse (13) ✓. Since 35° < 45°, the opposite side should be less than the adjacent. The adjacent = 13 cos 35° ≈ 10.65 > 7.46 ✓
Answer: The side opposite the 35° angle is approximately 7.46 units.
Example 7.1.2
Finding an angle in a right triangle
  • ProblemIn a right triangle, the leg opposite angle A has length 8 and the leg adjacent to A has length 15. Find angle A.
  • Representtan A = opposite / adjacent = 8 / 15
  • SolveA = tan⁻¹(8/15) ≈ tan⁻¹(0.5333) ≈ 28.07°
  • CheckHypotenuse = √(8² + 15²) = √289 = 17. Verify: sin 28.07° ≈ 8/17 ≈ 0.471 ✓
Answer: Angle A ≈ 28.1°
Calculator Mode

When solving for an angle using an inverse trigonometric function, make sure your calculator is in degree mode for problems using degrees, or radian mode for problems using radians. A wrong mode produces an answer that looks completely reasonable but is entirely incorrect.

Section 7.2

Solving Right Triangles

To solve a right triangle means to find all three sides and all three angles. You need at least two pieces of information beyond the right angle — either two sides, or one side and one acute angle. The three most common procedures are summarized below.

GivenFindProcedure
Acute angle A and hypotenuse ca, b, and Ba = c sin A; b = c cos A; B = 90° − A
Acute angle A and opposite side ac, b, and Bc = a / sin A; b = a / tan A; B = 90° − A
Two legs a and bc, A, and Bc = √(a² + b²); A = tan⁻¹(a/b); B = 90° − A
Example 7.2.1
Solving a right triangle completely
  • ProblemIn right triangle ABC, angle C = 90°, angle A = 52°, and hypotenuse c = 24. Solve the triangle.
  • RepresentUse sine for the opposite side, cosine for the adjacent side, and the angle sum for the third angle.
  • Solvea = c sin A = 24 sin 52° ≈ 24(0.7880) ≈ 18.91 b = c cos A = 24 cos 52° ≈ 24(0.6157) ≈ 14.78 B = 90° − 52° = 38°
  • CheckPythagorean Theorem: 18.91² + 14.78² ≈ 357.6 + 218.5 = 576.1 ≈ 24² = 576 ✓
Answer: a ≈ 18.91, b ≈ 14.78, B = 38°
Section 7.3

Angles of Elevation and Depression

Many practical problems involve looking upward or downward at an object from a horizontal reference line.

Angle of elevation: The angle formed between a horizontal line and a line of sight directed upward toward an object above.

Angle of depression: The angle formed between a horizontal line and a line of sight directed downward toward an object below.

A key geometric fact: the angle of elevation from the ground to an elevated observer equals the angle of depression from the elevated observer back to the ground. These are alternate interior angles formed by a transversal crossing two parallel horizontal lines — they are equal.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a horizontal reference line. From a point on that line, draw a ray going upward — the angle between the horizontal and the ray is the angle of elevation. From a raised point, draw a ray going downward — the angle between the horizontal and the downward ray is the angle of depression. Label the horizontal clearly in every problem.

Example 7.3.1
Finding the height of a building
  • ProblemFrom a point 80 feet from the base of a building, the angle of elevation to the top is 62°. How tall is the building?
  • ThinkHeight is opposite; distance from base is adjacent. Use tangent.
  • Solvetan 62° = h / 80 h = 80 tan 62° ≈ 80(1.8807) ≈ 150.5 feet
  • CheckThe angle exceeds 45°, so the height should exceed the base distance. 150.5 > 80 ✓
Answer: The building is approximately 150.5 feet tall.
Example 7.3.2
Using an angle of depression
  • ProblemA lighthouse keeper 120 feet above sea level spots a boat at an angle of depression of 18°. How far is the boat from the base of the lighthouse?
  • ThinkThe angle of depression from the top equals the angle of elevation from the boat (alternate interior angles). The height is opposite; the horizontal distance is adjacent.
  • Solvetan 18° = 120 / d d = 120 / tan 18° ≈ 120 / 0.3249 ≈ 369.3 feet
  • CheckA shallow angle of depression means the boat is far away. 369 feet for a 120-foot height is reasonable ✓
Answer: The boat is approximately 369 feet from the base of the lighthouse.
Student Warning

The angle of depression is at the top of the triangle — the elevated viewpoint — not at the base. Many errors come from placing the angle incorrectly in the diagram. Draw and label the horizontal reference line at the elevated position before setting up any ratio.

Section 7.4

Oblique Triangles

A triangle with no right angle is called an oblique triangle. Oblique triangles require new tools because the right-triangle ratios alone are not enough. Throughout this chapter we use a standard labeling system: vertices are labeled A, B, and C; the angle at each vertex carries the same letter; and the side opposite each vertex has the corresponding lowercase letter — side a is opposite angle A, side b is opposite angle B, and side c is opposite angle C.

To solve any triangle you need at least three pieces of information, and at least one must be a side length. Four standard cases arise, each calling for a different tool.

CaseGiven InformationTool to Use
AAS or ASATwo angles and one sideLaw of Sines
SSATwo sides and a non-included angleLaw of Sines — watch for ambiguous case
SASTwo sides and the included angleLaw of Cosines
SSSThree sides, no anglesLaw of Cosines
Note on AAA

AAA (three angles, no sides) does not uniquely determine a triangle. It determines only the shape — similar triangles all share the same three angles but can be any size. You must always know at least one side length.

Section 7.5

The Law of Sines

The Law of Sines is the first major tool for oblique triangles. It relates every angle in a triangle to the side directly opposite it.

Law of Sines

For any triangle with angles A, B, C and opposite sides a, b, c:

a / sin A = b / sin B = c / sin C

Equivalently:   sin A / a = sin B / b = sin C / c

Use the first form to find a missing side. Use the second form to find a missing angle.

Why the Law of Sines Works

Draw an oblique triangle ABC. Drop a perpendicular from C to side AB and call its height h. In the two right triangles formed: h = b sin A and h = a sin B. Since both equal h, set them equal: b sin A = a sin B, which gives a / sin A = b / sin B. A similar argument with a perpendicular from A completes the full proportion. The Law of Sines follows directly from right-triangle trigonometry applied to an altitude.

Example 7.5.1
AAS case — two angles and a non-included side
  • ProblemIn triangle ABC, A = 40°, B = 75°, and a = 22. Find sides b and c.
  • ThinkFind the third angle first. Then apply the Law of Sines using the known side a opposite angle A.
  • SolveC = 180° − 40° − 75° = 65° b = (22 sin 75°) / sin 40° = (22)(0.9659) / 0.6428 ≈ 33.1 c = (22 sin 65°) / sin 40° = (22)(0.9063) / 0.6428 ≈ 31.0
  • CheckThe largest angle is B = 75°, so the longest side should be b. b ≈ 33.1 > c ≈ 31.0 > a = 22 ✓
Answer: b ≈ 33.1, c ≈ 31.0, C = 65°
Example 7.5.2
ASA case — two angles and the included side
  • ProblemTwo angles of a triangle are A = 55° and C = 82°, and the side between them is b = 14.5. Find the remaining sides.
  • SolveB = 180° − 55° − 82° = 43° a = (14.5 sin 55°) / sin 43° = (14.5)(0.8192) / 0.6820 ≈ 17.4 c = (14.5 sin 82°) / sin 43° = (14.5)(0.9903) / 0.6820 ≈ 21.1
Answer: a ≈ 17.4, c ≈ 21.1
Section 7.6

The Ambiguous Case

The ambiguous case arises when you are given two sides and a non-included angle — the SSA configuration. Unlike other triangle cases, SSA does not always determine a unique triangle. Depending on the given values, there may be two triangles, one triangle, or no triangle at all.

This ambiguity occurs because a given side opposite a given angle might swing to two possible positions, one position, or not reach the other side at all. Imagine fixing angle A and side b at one vertex, then swinging side a like a compass arc from the end of b — the arc can cross the base line twice, once, or not at all.

ConditionNumber of Triangles
Angle A is obtuse or 90°, and a > bOne triangle
Angle A is obtuse or 90°, and a ≤ bNo triangle
Angle A is acute, and a < h = b sin ANo triangle (side a too short)
Angle A is acute, and a = h = b sin AOne right triangle
Angle A is acute, and h < a < bTwo triangles
Angle A is acute, and a ≥ bOne triangle
Example 7.6.1
SSA — two triangles exist
  • ProblemGiven A = 35°, a = 10, b = 14. Find all possible triangles.
  • ThinkAngle A is acute. Compute h = b sin A and compare to a.
  • Solveh = 14 sin 35° ≈ 14(0.5736) ≈ 8.03 Since h ≈ 8.03 < a = 10 < b = 14 → TWO triangles. sin B = (b sin A) / a = (14)(0.5736) / 10 ≈ 0.8030 B₁ = sin⁻¹(0.8030) ≈ 53.5° Triangle 1: B₁ = 53.5°, C₁ = 180° − 35° − 53.5° = 91.5° c₁ = (10 sin 91.5°) / sin 35° ≈ (10)(0.9997) / 0.5736 ≈ 17.4 B₂ = 180° − 53.5° = 126.5° (check: 35° + 126.5° = 161.5° < 180° ✓) C₂ = 180° − 35° − 126.5° = 18.5° c₂ = (10 sin 18.5°) / sin 35° ≈ (10)(0.3173) / 0.5736 ≈ 5.5
Triangle 1: B ≈ 53.5°, C ≈ 91.5°, c ≈ 17.4  |  Triangle 2: B ≈ 126.5°, C ≈ 18.5°, c ≈ 5.5
Example 7.6.2
SSA — no triangle exists
  • ProblemGiven A = 50°, a = 6, b = 9. How many triangles are possible?
  • Solveh = b sin A = 9 sin 50° ≈ 9(0.7660) ≈ 6.89 Since a = 6 < h ≈ 6.89, side a does not reach the base.
Answer: No triangle is possible with these measurements.
Student Warning — The Ambiguous Case

The most frequent mistake is finding one value of B with sin⁻¹ and stopping there. Because sin⁻¹ always returns a value between 0° and 90°, you must always check whether 180° minus that value also gives a valid triangle. Ask: "Does A + B₂ < 180°?" If yes, a second triangle exists. Never skip this check in SSA problems.

Section 7.7

The Law of Cosines

When you know two sides and the included angle (SAS), or when you know all three sides (SSS), the Law of Sines is insufficient — there is no matched angle-side pair available. The Law of Cosines fills that gap.

Law of Cosines — Three Forms

a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A

b² = a² + c² − 2ac cos B

c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C

To find an angle when all three sides are known, solve for cosine:

cos A = (b² + c² − a²) / (2bc)

cos B = (a² + c² − b²) / (2ac)

cos C = (a² + b² − c²) / (2ab)

Connection to the Pythagorean Theorem

When angle A = 90°, cos A = 0, and the Law of Cosines reduces to a² = b² + c². The Pythagorean Theorem is a special case of the Law of Cosines. The derivation places the triangle in a coordinate system and applies the distance formula; the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 appears naturally, connecting the Law of Cosines directly to the unit circle.

Example 7.7.1
SAS case — finding a side
  • ProblemIn triangle ABC, b = 15, c = 20, and A = 48°. Find side a.
  • Representa² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A
  • Solvea² = 15² + 20² − 2(15)(20) cos 48° = 225 + 400 − 600(0.6691) = 625 − 401.5 = 223.5 a ≈ √223.5 ≈ 14.95
  • CheckThe triangle inequality requires |c − b| < a < c + b, i.e., 5 < a < 35. Indeed 5 < 14.95 < 35 ✓
Answer: a ≈ 15.0
Example 7.7.2
SSS case — finding all angles
  • ProblemA triangle has sides a = 8, b = 11, c = 13. Find all three angles.
  • ThinkFind the largest angle first (opposite the longest side) to reduce error risk. The largest angle is C, opposite c = 13.
  • Solvecos C = (a² + b² − c²) / (2ab) = (64 + 121 − 169) / (2·8·11) = 16 / 176 ≈ 0.0909 C = cos⁻¹(0.0909) ≈ 84.8° cos B = (a² + c² − b²) / (2ac) = (64 + 169 − 121) / (2·8·13) = 112 / 208 ≈ 0.5385 B = cos⁻¹(0.5385) ≈ 57.4° A = 180° − 84.8° − 57.4° = 37.8°
  • CheckAngle sum: 37.8° + 57.4° + 84.8° = 180° ✓   Largest angle C is opposite longest side c ✓
Answer: A ≈ 37.8°, B ≈ 57.4°, C ≈ 84.8°
Advantage Over Law of Sines for Angles

When using the Law of Cosines to find an angle, the result is always between 0° and 180° because cos⁻¹ returns a unique value in that range. This means the ambiguous case does not arise when you use the Law of Cosines to find angles.

Section 7.8

Choosing Between Law of Sines and Law of Cosines

Given InformationWhich LawWhy
AAS — two angles, non-included sideLaw of SinesAlready have a matched pair (angle + opposite side)
ASA — two angles, included sideLaw of SinesFind third angle first; then a matched pair exists
SSA — two sides, non-included angleLaw of SinesCheck for ambiguous case carefully
SAS — two sides, included angleLaw of CosinesNo matched pair yet; included angle is between two sides
SSS — three sides, no anglesLaw of CosinesNo angles known at all
Memory Guide

Start with the Law of Sines when you have a matched pair — one angle and the side directly opposite it. Start with the Law of Cosines when you do not have a matched pair. In SAS, the known angle is between two known sides, not opposite a known side. In SSS, no angles are known at all.

Example 7.8.1
Identifying the correct law — four situations
  • (a)A = 62°, B = 45°, c = 18  →  AAS. Find C = 73°, then use Law of Sines.
  • (b)a = 7, b = 9, C = 110°  →  SAS. Use Law of Cosines to find c first.
  • (c)a = 5, b = 8, c = 10  →  SSS. Use Law of Cosines to find all angles.
  • (d)A = 30°, a = 6, b = 10  →  SSA. Use Law of Sines, but check for the ambiguous case.
Section 7.9

Area of a Triangle Using Trigonometry

The familiar formula Area = ½bh requires knowing the base and perpendicular height. When the height is not known directly, trigonometry provides it. Drop a perpendicular of height h from vertex C to side c. Since sin A = h/b, we have h = b sin A. Substituting into ½ · c · h gives the trigonometric area formula.

Trigonometric Area Formula

For any triangle with two sides b and c and the included angle A:

Area = (1/2) bc sin A

Similarly:   Area = (1/2) ab sin C   or   Area = (1/2) ac sin B

When all three sides are known, use Heron's Formula. Let s = (a + b + c)/2 (the semi-perimeter). Then:

Area = √(s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c))

Example 7.9.1
Area with SAS
  • ProblemFind the area of a triangle with sides b = 12 and c = 17 and included angle A = 66°.
  • SolveArea = (1/2)(12)(17) sin 66° ≈ (1/2)(204)(0.9135) ≈ 93.2 square units
Answer: Area ≈ 93.2 square units
Example 7.9.2
Area using Heron's Formula
  • ProblemFind the area of a triangle with sides a = 9, b = 12, c = 15.
  • ThinkNote: 9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225 = 15². This is a right triangle. Heron's Formula will confirm the answer.
  • Solves = (9 + 12 + 15) / 2 = 18 Area = √(18 · 9 · 6 · 3) = √2916 = 54
  • CheckRight-triangle formula: (1/2)(9)(12) = 54 ✓
Answer: Area = 54 square units

Section 7.10

Bearings and Navigation

Navigation problems require knowing direction as well as distance. Two bearing systems are commonly used:

True bearing: Measured clockwise from north, 0° to 360°. Due east = 090°; due south = 180°; due west = 270°.

Compass bearing: Given as a reference direction (N or S), followed by an acute angle, followed by a second direction (E or W). Example: N 42° E means 42° east of north.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a vertical north-south line through your starting point. Angles are measured from north (up) toward east (right) or west (left), or from south (down) toward east or west. Label the reference direction and the acute angle at every point in the diagram before setting up equations.

Example 7.10.1
The opening question — two-bearing problem
  • ProblemFrom Tower A, smoke is observed at N 42° E. From Tower B, 15 miles due east of A, the same smoke is observed at N 28° W. How far is the fire from each tower?
  • ThinkLet the fire be F. Triangle AFB has known side AB = 15. Identify the interior angles at A and B.
  • SolveInterior angle at A: N 42° E means 42° east of north. Side AB runs due east (90° from north). Angle at A = 90° − 42° = 48° Interior angle at B: N 28° W means 28° west of north. Side BA runs due west (90° from north). Angle at B = 90° − 28° = 62° Angle at F = 180° − 48° − 62° = 70° Law of Sines (AB = 15 is opposite angle F): AF = (15 sin 62°) / sin 70° ≈ (15)(0.8829) / 0.9397 ≈ 14.1 miles BF = (15 sin 48°) / sin 70° ≈ (15)(0.7431) / 0.9397 ≈ 11.9 miles
Answer: The fire is approximately 14.1 miles from Tower A and 11.9 miles from Tower B.
Example 7.10.2
Two-leg navigation problem
  • ProblemShip A sails 35 km in the direction N 68° E, then 50 km in the direction S 20° E. Find the straight-line distance from port to the final position.
  • ThinkResolve each leg into x (east) and y (north) components. Add components, then apply the distance formula.
  • SolveLeg 1 (N 68° E → 68° from north toward east): x₁ = 35 sin 68° ≈ 32.45 km y₁ = 35 cos 68° ≈ 13.11 km Leg 2 (S 20° E → south and slightly east): x₂ = x₁ + 50 sin 20° ≈ 32.45 + 17.10 = 49.55 km y₂ = y₁ − 50 cos 20° ≈ 13.11 − 46.98 = −33.87 km d = √(49.55² + 33.87²) ≈ √(2455.2 + 1147.2) ≈ √3602.4 ≈ 60.0 km
Answer: The ship is approximately 60.0 km from port.
Section 7.11

Surveying and Indirect Measurement

Surveying uses trigonometry to measure land features when direct measurement is not possible. The key principle is always the same: identify a triangle (or system of triangles) whose missing measurements can be found using the laws of sines and cosines.

Example 7.11.1
Width of a river
  • ProblemFrom point A on one bank, a stake C is driven directly across the river. A surveyor walks 200 feet to point B, then measures: angle at A = 82°, angle at B = 55°. Find the width (perpendicular distance from AB to C).
  • SolveAngle at C = 180° − 82° − 55° = 43° AC = (200 sin 55°) / sin 43° ≈ (200)(0.8192) / 0.6820 ≈ 240.1 ft Width h = AC sin A = 240.1 sin 82° ≈ 240.1(0.9903) ≈ 237.8 ft
Answer: The river is approximately 237.8 feet wide.
Example 7.11.2
Two-angle height measurement
  • ProblemFrom level ground, the angle of elevation to a hilltop is 28°. After walking 300 feet toward the hill, the elevation angle is 43°. Find the height of the hill.
  • ThinkLet h = height and d = distance from the nearer point to the base. Form two right-triangle equations.
  • SolveFrom far point: tan 28° = h / (d + 300) From near point: tan 43° = h / d → d = h / tan 43° Substitute: tan 28° = h / (h/tan 43° + 300) tan 28° · (h/tan 43° + 300) = h 300 tan 28° = h(1 − tan 28°/tan 43°) h = 300 tan 28° / (1 − tan 28°/tan 43°) = 300(0.5317) / (1 − 0.5317/0.9325) ≈ 159.5 / 0.4297 ≈ 371 feet
Answer: The hill is approximately 371 feet tall.
Section 7.12

Trigonometry in Physics and Engineering

Trigonometry is essential in physics and engineering because many quantities — forces, velocities, displacements — have both magnitude and direction. When a force acts at an angle, finding its horizontal and vertical components using trigonometry is a fundamental step. For a force F acting at angle θ above the horizontal:

Force Component Formulas

F_x = F cos θ    (horizontal component)

F_y = F sin θ    (vertical component)

This decomposition is the foundation of vector analysis, developed formally in Chapter 12.

Example 7.12.1
Inclined plane
  • ProblemA 150-pound block rests on a ramp inclined at 22°. Find the component of weight parallel to the ramp (tending to cause sliding) and perpendicular to the ramp (pressing into the surface).
  • SolveParallel (down the slope): W_∥ = 150 sin 22° ≈ 150(0.3746) ≈ 56.2 lb Perpendicular (into the surface): W_⊥ = 150 cos 22° ≈ 150(0.9272) ≈ 139.1 lb
  • Check√(56.2² + 139.1²) ≈ √(3158 + 19349) ≈ √22507 ≈ 150 lb ✓
Parallel: ≈ 56.2 lb (tends to slide the block)  |  Perpendicular: ≈ 139.1 lb (presses into the ramp)
Example 7.12.2
Combining two non-perpendicular forces
  • ProblemRope 1 pulls with 120 N at 30° above horizontal. Rope 2 pulls with 95 N at 55° above horizontal (same side). Find the combined force.
  • SolveRope 1: F₁ₓ = 120 cos 30° ≈ 103.9 N, F₁ᵧ = 120 sin 30° = 60.0 N Rope 2: F₂ₓ = 95 cos 55° ≈ 54.5 N, F₂ᵧ = 95 sin 55° ≈ 77.8 N Totals: Rₓ = 103.9 + 54.5 = 158.4 N Rᵧ = 60.0 + 77.8 = 137.8 N |R| = √(158.4² + 137.8²) ≈ √(25090 + 18989) ≈ √44079 ≈ 209.9 N θ = tan⁻¹(137.8 / 158.4) ≈ tan⁻¹(0.870) ≈ 41.0°
Answer: Combined force ≈ 210 N at approximately 41° above horizontal.

Section 7.13

Common Triangle Trigonometry Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Law of Sines without a matched pair
Applying the Law of Sines to an SAS problem where no complete angle-side pair exists yet.

Correction: In SAS and SSS, start with the Law of Cosines. The Law of Sines requires one known angle and the side directly opposite it.

Mistake 2 — Stopping after one triangle in the ambiguous case
Using sin⁻¹ to find angle B and writing the solution without checking whether B₂ = 180° − B also works.

Correction: In every SSA problem, compute B₂ = 180° − B₁ and check whether A + B₂ < 180°. If yes, a second valid triangle exists.

Mistake 3 — Calculator in the wrong mode
Leaving the calculator in radian mode while solving a degree problem, or vice versa.

Correction: Check calculator mode before every computation. Most chapter 7 problems use degrees.

Mistake 4 — Incorrect triangle labeling
Pairing side b with angle A, or otherwise mismatching sides and their opposite angles in the Law of Sines.

Correction: Before applying either law, clearly label all six parts using the a–A, b–B, c–C convention. Sketch the triangle even for abstract problems.

Mistake 5 — Using sin⁻¹ for obtuse angles in SSS
Finding all angles in an SSS triangle using the Law of Sines and getting an acute angle where an obtuse angle belongs.

Correction: In SSS problems, use the Law of Cosines to find angles. cos⁻¹ returns values up to 180° and handles obtuse angles correctly.

Mistake 6 — Assuming all triangles are solvable
Proceeding to solve without checking that the given values can actually form a triangle.

Correction: The triangle inequality requires each side to be less than the sum of the other two. In SSA, compute h = b sin A and compare to a before proceeding.

Mistake 7 — Not checking angle sums
Finishing a solution without verifying that A + B + C = 180°.

Correction: Always add the three angles as a final check. This step catches most computational errors at no additional effort.

Mistake 8 — Misplacing the angle of depression
Placing the angle of depression at the base of the right triangle instead of at the elevated viewpoint.

Correction: The angle of depression is measured downward from the horizontal at the elevated position. Draw and label the horizontal reference line at the top of the diagram, not the bottom.

Section 7.14

Preparing for Polar and Parametric Thinking

Chapter 7 has shown that trigonometry is a system for measuring triangles. As you move into the next part of this textbook, trigonometry will appear in a broader context: describing position, direction, motion, and curves.

Connection to polar coordinates: In Chapter 12, every point in the plane is described by a distance r and an angle θ. The connection to triangle trigonometry is immediate — x = r cos θ and y = r sin θ are the same component relationships used throughout this chapter.

Connection to parametric equations: A circle of radius r can be described parametrically as x = r cos t, y = r sin t. The motion of any object on a circular path — a satellite in orbit, a wheel turning — is described by exactly these equations.

Connection to vectors: The force problems of Section 7.12 previewed vectors. When you decomposed a force into horizontal and vertical components, you were writing a vector in component form: ⟨F cos θ, F sin θ⟩. Chapter 12 develops vectors formally.

Connection to the dot product: The Law of Cosines and the vector dot product are the same idea expressed differently. If u and v are vectors with angle θ between them, then u · v = |u||v| cos θ. Recognizing this connection will make Chapter 12 and Calculus III feel natural.

Chapter 7 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Right-triangle ratios define sine, cosine, and tangent as opposite/hypotenuse, adjacent/hypotenuse, and opposite/adjacent. The Pythagorean Theorem connects the three sides. Special triangles (45-45-90 and 30-60-90) give exact values.
  • Sec 7.2Solving a right triangle means finding all three sides and all three angles. With any two pieces of information (beyond the right angle), the remaining parts can be found using the six trigonometric ratios.
  • Sec 7.3Angles of elevation are measured upward from horizontal; angles of depression are measured downward. The angle of elevation from the ground equals the angle of depression from the elevated viewpoint (alternate interior angles).
  • Sec 7.4An oblique triangle has no right angle. The standard labeling pairs each side with the lowercase letter of the opposite vertex's angle. Four solvable cases exist: AAS, ASA, SSA, SAS, SSS.
  • Sec 7.5The Law of Sines: a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C. It applies to AAS and ASA cases. The proof uses a perpendicular altitude, connecting the law directly to right-triangle trigonometry.
  • Sec 7.6The ambiguous case (SSA) can produce two triangles, one triangle, or no triangle. Always check whether a second triangle exists by testing whether B₂ = 180° − B₁ is valid.
  • Sec 7.7The Law of Cosines: a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A. It applies to SAS and SSS. When A = 90° it reduces to the Pythagorean Theorem. Use cos⁻¹ to find angles — no ambiguous case arises.
  • Sec 7.8Start with the Law of Sines when a matched angle-side pair exists. Start with the Law of Cosines when no matched pair exists (SAS or SSS).
  • Sec 7.9Area = (1/2)bc sin A for any triangle with sides b, c and included angle A. Heron's Formula, Area = √(s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)), uses only the three side lengths.
  • Sec 7.10True bearings measure clockwise from north (0°–360°). Compass bearings give an acute angle east or west of north or south. Resolve each leg into components and apply triangle laws or the distance formula.
  • Sec 7.11Surveying problems identify triangles from field data and apply the Law of Sines or Cosines. Indirect measurement finds distances and heights that cannot be measured directly.
  • Sec 7.12Forces are resolved into horizontal and vertical components using F_x = F cos θ and F_y = F sin θ. Resultant forces are found by adding components, then computing magnitude and direction.
  • Sec 7.13Eight common mistakes: wrong law choice, missing the second triangle in SSA, wrong calculator mode, mismatched labels, using sin⁻¹ for obtuse angles, invalid triangles, unchecked angle sums, misplaced elevation/depression angles.
  • Sec 7.14Triangle trigonometry connects directly to polar coordinates (x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ), parametric circles, vector component form, and the dot product. The triangle is the fundamental unit of indirect measurement.

Key Terms Review

oblique triangle
A triangle that contains no right angle. Requires the Law of Sines or Law of Cosines to solve.
Law of Sines
a / sin A = b / sin B = c / sin C. Applies to AAS, ASA, and SSA configurations.
Law of Cosines
a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A. Applies to SAS and SSS. Reduces to the Pythagorean Theorem when A = 90°.
ambiguous case
The SSA configuration where the given data may produce two triangles, one triangle, or no triangle.
angle of elevation
The angle measured upward from a horizontal line to a line of sight aimed at an object above.
angle of depression
The angle measured downward from a horizontal line to a line of sight aimed at an object below.
true bearing
Direction measured clockwise from north, expressed in degrees from 000° to 360°.
compass bearing
Direction expressed as N or S followed by an acute angle followed by E or W. Example: S 35° W.
Heron's Formula
Area = √(s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c)) where s = (a + b + c)/2. Computes triangle area from three sides alone.
included angle
The angle formed between two known sides of a triangle. The SAS case provides an included angle.
included side
The side between two known angles of a triangle. The ASA case provides an included side.
semi-perimeter
Half the perimeter of a triangle: s = (a + b + c)/2. Used in Heron's Formula.
component form
Expressing a force or vector as horizontal and vertical parts: ⟨F cos θ, F sin θ⟩. Foundational for vectors in Chapter 12.

Application Problems

Define all variables, set up the triangle, apply the correct law, and interpret your answer in context.

  1. Navigation: A plane leaves an airport and flies 180 miles on a bearing of N 52° E. It then turns and flies 240 miles on a bearing of S 38° E. How far is the plane from the airport?
  2. Lake survey: From point A on one shore, a baseline AC = 520 feet is measured along the shore to point C. From A, angle BAC = 74°. From C, angle BCA = 63°. Find the distance AB across the lake.
  3. Hiking: A hiker walks 3.5 km due north, then turns and walks 5.2 km on a bearing of N 65° E. How far is the hiker from the start, and on what bearing?
  4. Structural brace: A diagonal brace in a wall frame is 8.5 feet long and makes a 34° angle with the floor. Find the horizontal span it covers and its vertical rise.
  5. Harbor survey: Three buoys A, B, C mark a triangular harbor section. AB = 820 m, BC = 640 m, CA = 580 m. Find the area of the triangular region and the angle at buoy B.
  6. Rope tension: A sign weighing 250 N is suspended from two ropes. The left rope makes 35° with the horizontal; the right rope makes 50° with the horizontal. Find the tension in each rope.
  7. Sailing race: Buoy A to Buoy B is 0.8 miles on a bearing of S 72° E. Buoy A to Buoy C is 1.1 miles on a bearing of S 18° W. Find the distance from B to C.
  8. Forest fire (opening question): From Tower 1, smoke appears at 32° east of north. Tower 2 is 6.5 miles directly south of Tower 1. The ranger at Tower 2 spots the same smoke at 28° west of north. Find the distance from each tower to the fire.

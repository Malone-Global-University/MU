Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces three powerful new ways to describe position, motion, and relationships in the plane. Until now, most of the curves and relationships in this course have been described by a single equation relating x and y directly. That approach works well for many functions and curves, but it has limits. It cannot easily describe the path of a projectile, the shape of a spiral, the direction of a force, or the motion of a satellite.

Parametric equations allow both x and y to depend on a third variable — usually called a parameter — so that motion and curves can be described with a sense of direction, time, and position. Polar coordinates replace the rectangular grid with a system based on distance and angle, making circles, spirals, and symmetric curves far easier to describe. Vectors give mathematics a language for quantities that have both magnitude and direction, including force, velocity, displacement, and acceleration.

Together, these three ideas expand what mathematics can describe and how it describes it. They are essential tools in calculus, physics, engineering, navigation, robotics, computer graphics, and scientific modeling.

Opening Question

A baseball player hits a ball at an angle of 35° above the horizontal, and the ball leaves the bat at a speed of 120 feet per second from a height of 3 feet. Where will the ball be after 2 seconds? Will it clear a 10-foot fence that is 350 feet away? This problem cannot be answered with a single equation y = f(x). The ball's horizontal and vertical positions change together over time, and time is the missing variable. Parametric equations make this problem solvable — and by the end of this chapter, students will model this situation completely.

Section 12.1

Why Coordinates Can Be Described in Different Ways

Most of the functions and graphs in this course have been written in the form y = f(x). This is natural and useful because it makes y a direct function of x: for each input there is exactly one output. Rectangular coordinates are built for this setup. But consider a few situations that rectangular coordinates handle awkwardly.

A full circle. The unit circle satisfies x² + y² = 1, which is not a function. To graph the full circle, students must split it into two pieces: the upper and lower semicircles. This is cumbersome.

A moving object. The path of a particle might be a parabola or a circle, but knowing the shape is not enough. Where is the particle at time t = 2 seconds? Is it moving left or right? A single equation y = f(x) cannot answer these questions because it contains no time.

A spiral. The path of a snail shell or a slowly descending satellite is a spiral — almost impossible to describe with y = f(x), but elegantly simple in a distance-and-angle system.

A force. When a boat is driven by a motor and swept by a current, the actual direction and speed depend on both forces combined. A single number cannot describe this. A quantity with both size and direction is needed.

SituationWhy y = f(x) Falls ShortBetter Tool
Full circleNot a function; requires two piecesParametric or polar
Moving object with timeNo time variable; no directionParametric equations
Spiral or symmetric curveComplex rectangular formPolar coordinates
Force or velocityNo magnitude-and-direction encodingVectors
Unified Theme

Parametric equations, polar coordinates, and vectors do not replace rectangular coordinates. They are additions to the toolkit, each suited to a different kind of problem. Learning to move between systems is a skill that becomes increasingly important in calculus, physics, and engineering.

Section 12.2

Parametric Equations

Definition — Parametric Curve

A parametric curve is a set of points (x, y) defined by

x = f(t) and y = g(t)

where f and g are functions of the parameter t, and t ranges over some interval. The parameter t does not appear in the graph — it is a hidden variable that drives the point (x(t), y(t)) through the plane.

As t increases, the point (x, y) traces a path through the plane in a definite direction. This direction is called the orientation of the curve. Two different parametric descriptions can trace the same geometric curve in different directions or at different speeds — the parameter carries information that the rectangular equation cannot.

Graphing Parametric Curves

The most reliable way to graph a parametric curve by hand is to build a table of (t, x, y) values, plot the resulting points, and draw a smooth curve through them in the correct direction. Include endpoints of the t-interval, values where x or y has a maximum or minimum, and enough intermediate values to see the shape.

Section 12.4

Eliminating the Parameter

Two Methods for Eliminating the Parameter

Method 1 — Substitution: Solve x = f(t) for t. Substitute into y = g(t). Use when f(t) is easy to invert.

Method 2 — Trig Identity: When the equations involve cos(t) and sin(t), isolate each, square both, and add using cos²(t) + sin²(t) = 1.

Example 12.4.1
Eliminating by Substitution
  • ProblemEliminate the parameter from x = t + 1, y = t² − 2.
  • ThinkThe first equation is easy to solve for t.
  • Solve From x = t + 1: t = x − 1 Substitute: y = (x − 1)² − 2
  • CheckAt t = 0: x = 1, y = −2. Check: y = (1 − 1)² − 2 = −2. ✓
Answer: y = (x − 1)² − 2, a parabola with vertex (1, −2).
Example 12.4.2
Eliminating with the Pythagorean Identity
  • ProblemEliminate the parameter from x = 4 cos(t), y = 2 sin(t).
  • ThinkIsolate cos(t) and sin(t), then use cos²(t) + sin²(t) = 1.
  • Solve cos(t) = x/4 sin(t) = y/2 Apply the identity: (x/4)² + (y/2)² = 1 x²/16 + y²/4 = 1
Answer: x²/16 + y²/4 = 1, an ellipse with semi-axes 4 and 2.
Student Warning — Domain Restriction

Eliminating the parameter gives the shape of the curve but may expand the domain. If t is restricted (for example 0 ≤ t ≤ π), not all points on the rectangular equation are actually traced. After eliminating the parameter, determine the range of x values for the given t-interval and restrict the rectangular equation accordingly.

Student Warning — Do Not Divide Trig Functions

When eliminating the parameter from x = a cos(t) and y = b sin(t), do not divide to get y/x = (b/a) tan(t). This introduces a new trig function and fails to remove t. Always use the Pythagorean identity by isolating and squaring cos(t) and sin(t) separately.

Section 12.5

Motion and Parametric Models

Projectile Motion Equations

x(t) = v₀ cos(θ) · t + x₀

y(t) = −½ g t² + v₀ sin(θ) · t + y₀

where v₀ = initial speed, θ = launch angle, (x₀, y₀) = initial position, g = 32 ft/s² (or 9.8 m/s²). Horizontally: constant speed. Vertically: gravity pulls the object down.

Example 12.5.1
Baseball Problem
  • ProblemA baseball is hit at 35° with initial speed 120 ft/s from height 3 feet. Find the ball's position at t = 2 s. Will it clear a 10-foot fence 350 feet away?
  • Represent x(t) = 120 cos(35°) · t ≈ 98.3t y(t) = −16t² + 120 sin(35°) · t + 3 ≈ −16t² + 68.83t + 3 (using cos 35° ≈ 0.8192, sin 35° ≈ 0.5736)
  • Solve At t = 2: x(2) ≈ 98.3(2) = 196.6 ft y(2) ≈ −16(4) + 68.83(2) + 3 = −64 + 137.66 + 3 = 76.66 ft At the fence (x = 350 ft): 350 = 98.3t → t ≈ 3.56 s y(3.56) ≈ −16(12.67) + 68.83(3.56) + 3 ≈ −202.7 + 245.0 + 3 ≈ 45.3 ft
  • CheckThe ball is 45.3 feet high at the fence, well above 10 feet. The model is physically reasonable — a well-hit baseball at high speed covers ground quickly.
Answer: At t = 2 s, the ball is approximately 196.6 ft out and 76.7 ft high. At the 350-ft fence, the ball is approximately 45.3 ft high — it clears the fence easily.
Example 12.5.2
Circular Motion
  • ProblemA point moves on a circle of radius 5 centered at the origin, completing one full counterclockwise revolution every 4 seconds. Write parametric equations and find the position at t = 1 second.
  • ThinkPeriod = 4 seconds, so angular frequency ω = 2π/4 = π/2.
  • Solve x(t) = 5 cos(π/2 · t) y(t) = 5 sin(π/2 · t) At t = 1: x(1) = 5 cos(π/2) = 5(0) = 0 y(1) = 5 sin(π/2) = 5(1) = 5
Answer: After 1 second, the point is at (0, 5) — the top of the circle.

Section 12.6

Polar Coordinates

Definition — Polar Coordinates

The polar coordinates of a point P are written (r, θ) where:

r = radial distance from the pole to P

θ = angular coordinate, measured counterclockwise from the polar axis

The pole corresponds to the origin. The polar axis corresponds to the positive x-axis. Angles are usually measured in radians.

Unlike rectangular coordinates, polar coordinates allow negative values of r. If r < 0, the point is located in the direction opposite to θ, at distance |r| from the pole. This means a single point in the plane has infinitely many polar representations: (r, θ), (r, θ + 2πk), and (−r, θ + π) all describe the same geometric point.

Key Difference from Rectangular Coordinates

In rectangular coordinates, each point (x, y) has exactly one representation. In polar coordinates, every point has infinitely many representations. When solving polar equations, students should not look for a single "correct" polar form — there are always multiple valid representations of the same point.

Example 12.6.1
Plotting Polar Points
  • ProblemDescribe the location of each polar point: (3, π/6), (2, 3π/4), (4, −π/3), (−2, π/4).
  • ThinkFor each point, face the direction θ from the polar axis, then move a distance r. For negative r, face the opposite direction.
  • Solve (3, π/6): Face 30° counterclockwise. Move 3 units outward. → First quadrant. (2, 3π/4): Face 135° counterclockwise. Move 2 units outward. → Second quadrant. (4, −π/3): Face 60° clockwise (negative angle = clockwise). Move 4 units. → Fourth quadrant. (−2, π/4): Face 45° counterclockwise, but r = −2 is negative. Plot 2 units in the OPPOSITE direction: angle = π/4 + π = 5π/4. → Third quadrant.
Answer: Negative r reverses the direction. (−2, π/4) ends up in the third quadrant even though θ = π/4 points toward the first quadrant.

Example 12.7.2

  • ProblemConvert (−3, 3) to polar coordinates.

Example 12.7.3
Converting a Polar Equation to Rectangular
  • ProblemConvert r = 6 cos(θ) to rectangular form.
  • ThinkMultiply both sides by r, then use r² = x² + y² and r cos(θ) = x.
  • Solve r · r = 6 · r cos(θ) r² = 6x x² + y² = 6x x² − 6x + y² = 0 (x − 3)² + y² = 9

A polar equation relates r and θ. Its graph is the set of all points (r, θ) that satisfy the equation. Polar graphs are plotted on a polar coordinate plane: concentric circles centered at the pole and rays extending outward at regular angles.

Polar graphs often have symmetry. Testing for symmetry before graphing saves effort.

Section 12.9

Vectors as Directed Quantities

Scalar vs. Vector

Scalar: A quantity with magnitude only. Examples: temperature, mass, speed, time, area.

Vector: A quantity with both magnitude and direction. Examples: force, velocity, displacement, acceleration.

Two forces of the same size in different directions do not produce the same effect. A wind pushing a boat north is not the same as a wind pushing it south, even at the same speed.

A vector is represented geometrically by an arrow. The tail (starting point) shows where the vector begins. The head (arrowhead) shows the direction. The length of the arrow represents the magnitude.

In the coordinate plane, a vector is most usefully described by its component form: v = ⟨a, b⟩, where a is the horizontal component and b is the vertical component.

Vector vs. Point Notation

The notation ⟨a, b⟩ (angle brackets) is a vector. The notation (a, b) (parentheses) is a point. They look similar and are related, but they are not the same object. Use angle brackets for vectors consistently.

Component Form from Two Points

If a vector has its tail at (x₁, y₁) and its head at (x₂, y₂), then:

v = ⟨x₂ − x₁, y₂ − y₁⟩

Example 12.9.1
Writing a Vector in Component Form
  • ProblemA vector has its tail at (2, 1) and its head at (5, 6). Write it in component form.
  • Solvev = ⟨5 − 2, 6 − 1⟩ = ⟨3, 5⟩
Answer: v = ⟨3, 5⟩. The vector moves 3 units right and 5 units up.
Section 12.10

Magnitude and Direction

Magnitude, Direction, and Unit Vectors

Magnitude: |v| = √(a² + b²)

Direction angle: tan(θ) = b/a (determine quadrant from signs of a and b)

Unit vector: v̂ = v / |v| = ⟨a/|v|, b/|v|⟩

The standard unit vectors are i = ⟨1, 0⟩ and j = ⟨0, 1⟩. Any vector can be written as v = ai + bj.

Example 12.10.1
Magnitude and Direction Angle
  • ProblemFind the magnitude and direction angle of v = ⟨−4, 3⟩.
  • Solve |v| = √(16 + 9) = √25 = 5 tan(θ) = 3/(−4) → reference angle = arctan(3/4) ≈ 36.87° The vector is in Q II (a < 0, b > 0): θ = 180° − 36.87° ≈ 143.13°
Answer: |v| = 5, direction angle ≈ 143.13°.
Example 12.10.2
Finding a Unit Vector
  • ProblemFind the unit vector in the direction of w = ⟨5, −12⟩.
  • Solve |w| = √(25 + 144) = √169 = 13 ŵ = (1/13)⟨5, −12⟩ = ⟨5/13, −12/13⟩
  • Check|ŵ| = √(25/169 + 144/169) = √(169/169) = 1. ✓
Answer: ŵ = ⟨5/13, −12/13⟩
Section 12.11

Vector Addition and Scalar Multiplication

Vector Arithmetic

For u = ⟨a, b⟩ and v = ⟨c, d⟩ and scalar k:

u + v = ⟨a + c, b + d⟩ (add corresponding components)

u − v = ⟨a − c, b − d⟩ (subtract corresponding components)

ku = ⟨ka, kb⟩ (multiply each component by k)

If k > 0: same direction, stretched. If k < 0: reversed direction. The magnitude of the sum is NOT the sum of the magnitudes (except when vectors are parallel).

PropertyStatement
Commutativeu + v = v + u
Associative(u + v) + w = u + (v + w)
Zero vectorv + 0 = v
Additive inversev + (−v) = 0
Distributivek(u + v) = ku + kv
Example 12.11.1

  • ProblemLet u = ⟨3, −1⟩ and v = ⟨−2, 5⟩. Compute u + v, u − v, 3u, and 2u − 3v.

Section 12.12

Dot Product as an Introduction

Dot Product Definition and Geometric Interpretation

For u = ⟨a, b⟩ and v = ⟨c, d⟩:

u · v = ac + bd (always a scalar, never a vector)

Geometric formula:

u · v = |u| |v| cos(θ)

where θ is the angle between the vectors, 0° ≤ θ ≤ 180°.

Therefore: cos(θ) = (u · v) / (|u| |v|)

Two vectors are orthogonal (perpendicular) if and only if u · v = 0.

Dot Product SignAngle Between Vectors
PositiveAcute (less than 90°)
ZeroRight angle (exactly 90°) — orthogonal
NegativeObtuse (greater than 90°)
Example 12.12.1
Computing a Dot Product
  • ProblemLet u = ⟨3, 4⟩ and v = ⟨−2, 1⟩. Compute u · v.
  • Solveu · v = (3)(−2) + (4)(1) = −6 + 4 = −2
Answer: u · v = −2 (a negative scalar; the angle between the vectors is obtuse).
Example 12.12.2
Finding the Angle Between Two Vectors
  • ProblemFind the angle between u = ⟨2, 1⟩ and v = ⟨1, 3⟩.
  • Solve u · v = (2)(1) + (1)(3) = 5 |u| = √5 |v| = √10 cos(θ) = 5 / (√5 · √10) = 5 / √50 = 5 / (5√2) = 1/√2 θ = arccos(1/√2) = π/4 = 45°
Answer: The angle between the two vectors is 45°.
Example 12.12.3
Testing for Orthogonality
  • ProblemAre u = ⟨3, −2⟩ and v = ⟨4, 6⟩ orthogonal?
  • Solveu · v = (3)(4) + (−2)(6) = 12 − 12 = 0
Answer: Yes — the dot product is 0, so the vectors are orthogonal (perpendicular).

Projection of One Vector onto Another

The projection of u onto v gives the component of u in the direction of v:

proj_v u = (u · v / |v|²) · v

The scalar (u · v) / |v| is called the scalar projection. Projection appears in physics when computing work: the work done by a constant force F over a displacement d is W = F · d.

Section 12.13

Vectors in Physics, Navigation, and Computing

Example 12.13.1
Force Equilibrium
  • ProblemA 50-lb weight hangs from two ropes. Rope 1 makes 30° with the ceiling. Rope 2 makes 45° with the ceiling. Find the tension in each rope.
  • ThinkFor equilibrium, horizontal and vertical components must each sum to zero.
  • Represent Horizontal: −T₁ cos(30°) + T₂ cos(45°) = 0 Vertical: T₁ sin(30°) + T₂ sin(45°) = 50
  • Solve From horizontal: T₁ = T₂ · (√2 / √3) Substitute into vertical equation and solve: T₂ ≈ 44.8 lb T₁ ≈ 36.6 lb
Answer: Rope 1 ≈ 36.6 lb, Rope 2 ≈ 44.8 lb.
Example 12.13.2
Airplane Navigation
  • ProblemAn airplane heads due north at 300 mph. A wind blows from the west at 40 mph. Find the actual velocity vector, ground speed, and true course.
  • Represent v_plane = ⟨0, 300⟩ v_wind = ⟨40, 0⟩ v_total = ⟨40, 300⟩
  • Solve |v_total| = √(1600 + 90000) = √91600 ≈ 302.7 mph θ = arctan(40/300) ≈ 7.6° east of north
Answer: Ground speed ≈ 302.7 mph. True course: N 7.6° E.

Vectors in Computer Graphics

In computer graphics and game programming, the dot product determines how light illuminates a surface. If n̂ is the unit normal to the surface and L̂ is the unit vector toward the light source, then the lighting intensity is I = n̂ · L̂. When the vectors are perpendicular (dot product = 0), the surface receives no light. When they are parallel (dot product = 1), the surface is fully illuminated. This is why the dot product appears at the heart of every 3D rendering engine.

Vectors in Machine Learning

In data science and machine learning, data points are represented as vectors where each coordinate is a feature value. The similarity between two data vectors is measured using cosine similarity:

cosine similarity = (u · v) / (|u| |v|)

Two data vectors that are nearly parallel (cosine close to 1) are considered similar. This is why the dot product appears in recommendation systems, natural language processing, and neural networks.

Chapter 12 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1Rectangular coordinates handle most functions well but fail for circles without splitting, for motion without time, for spirals, and for directed quantities. Parametric equations, polar coordinates, and vectors each address a different gap.
  • Sec 12.2A parametric curve is defined by x = f(t) and y = g(t). The parameter t drives the point through the plane. Orientation — the direction of travel as t increases — is information that rectangular equations cannot carry.
  • Sec 12.3Parametric curves are graphed by building a table of (t, x, y) values. Key curves: circles use cos and sin with equal coefficients; ellipses use unequal coefficients; line segments use a linear parameter.
  • Sec 12.4The parameter is eliminated by substitution (solve for t, substitute) or by the Pythagorean identity (isolate cos and sin, square and add). Domain restrictions from the t-interval must be applied to the resulting rectangular equation.
  • Sec 12.5Projectile motion: x(t) = v₀ cos(θ)·t + x₀ and y(t) = −½gt² + v₀ sin(θ)·t + y₀. Horizontal motion is constant-speed; vertical motion includes the acceleration of gravity.
  • Sec 12.6Polar coordinates (r, θ) describe a point by distance r from the pole and angle θ from the polar axis. Every point has infinitely many polar representations. Negative r reverses the direction of θ.
  • Sec 12.7Polar-to-rectangular: x = r cos(θ), y = r sin(θ). Rectangular-to-polar: r = √(x²+y²), tan(θ) = y/x (with quadrant check). Use r² = x²+y², r cos(θ) = x, and r sin(θ) = y to convert equations.
  • Sec 12.8Polar graphs include circles (r = a), limaçons (r = a + b cos θ), cardioids (|a/b| = 1), rose curves (n petals if n odd; 2n if n even), lemniscates (r² = a²cos 2θ), and spirals (r = aθ).
  • Sec 12.9A vector ⟨a, b⟩ has both magnitude and direction. Scalars have magnitude only. Component form is computed from tail and head points by subtracting: ⟨x₂ − x₁, y₂ − y₁⟩. Vectors use angle brackets, points use parentheses.
  • Sec 12.10Magnitude: |v| = √(a²+b²). Direction angle: tan(θ) = b/a, adjusted for quadrant. Unit vector: v̂ = v/|v|. Standard unit vectors: i = ⟨1,0⟩ and j = ⟨0,1⟩.
  • Sec 12.11Vectors add component by component. Scalar multiplication multiplies each component. The magnitude of a sum is not the sum of the magnitudes — add components first, then compute the magnitude of the result.
  • Sec 12.12The dot product u · v = ac + bd is always a scalar. It equals |u||v|cos(θ). Orthogonal vectors have dot product zero. The sign of the dot product reveals the angle type: positive = acute, zero = right, negative = obtuse.
  • Sec 12.13Vectors model forces (equilibrium requires the resultant to be zero), navigation (add air velocity and wind velocity vectors), computer graphics (dot product determines lighting intensity), and machine learning (cosine similarity measures data point resemblance).
  • Sec 12.14Key mistakes: losing orientation when eliminating the parameter; ignoring domain restrictions; treating polar coordinates as unique; wrong quadrant when converting to polar; adding magnitudes instead of components; treating the dot product as a vector.

Section 12.14 — Common Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Losing Orientation
"After eliminating the parameter, the curve has no direction."

Error: Orientation (direction of increasing t) is lost when the parameter is eliminated. Correction: When orientation matters for physics, calculus, or animation, keep the parametric form. Eliminating the parameter only recovers the shape, not the motion.

Mistake 2 — Domain Loss After Eliminating
x = cos(t), y = sin(t) for 0 ≤ t ≤ π becomes x² + y² = 1 (full circle)

Error: The parameter only ranges over half the circle (the upper semicircle). Correction: Determine the range of x for 0 ≤ t ≤ π — here x goes from 1 down to −1. The rectangular equation must be restricted to the upper semicircle: x² + y² = 1 with y ≥ 0.

Mistake 3 — Wrong Quadrant When Converting to Polar
Convert (−3, 3): tan(θ) = 3/(−3) = −1, so θ = −π/4

Error: The point (−3, 3) is in Q II, but θ = −π/4 places it in Q IV. Correction: After finding the reference angle arctan(1) = π/4, use the signs of x and y to determine the quadrant. Since x < 0 and y > 0, θ = π − π/4 = 3π/4.

Mistake 4 — Adding Magnitudes Instead of Components
|u| + |v| = |u + v| (e.g. 5 + 13 = 18)

Error: The magnitude of a sum is NOT the sum of magnitudes (unless the vectors are parallel and point the same direction). Correction: Add component by component — ⟨3,4⟩ + ⟨5,12⟩ = ⟨8,16⟩ — then compute |⟨8,16⟩| = 8√5 ≈ 17.9. This happens to be close to 18 by coincidence, not by rule.

Mistake 5 — Dot Product as a Vector
⟨2, 3⟩ · ⟨4, −1⟩ = ⟨8, −3⟩

Error: The dot product produces a scalar, not a vector. The student computed the products but left them in angle brackets. Correction: ⟨2, 3⟩ · ⟨4, −1⟩ = (2)(4) + (3)(−1) = 8 − 3 = 5. No brackets. No arrow.

Mistake 6 — Confusing Speed and Velocity
"The velocity is 302.7 mph."

Error: Velocity is a vector (magnitude and direction). Speed is a scalar (magnitude only). Correction: "The speed is 302.7 mph and the velocity vector is ⟨40, 300⟩ at bearing N 7.6° E." Always identify whether a quantity is a scalar or a vector.

Key Terms Review

parameter
The independent variable t that drives both x and y in a parametric equation.
parametric curve
A curve described by x = f(t), y = g(t) as t varies over an interval.
orientation
The direction of travel along a parametric curve as t increases; shown by arrows on the graph.
eliminating the parameter
Combining x(t) and y(t) to produce a relationship between x and y only, removing t.
projectile motion
Motion under constant gravity modeled by x(t) = v₀ cos(θ)t + x₀ and y(t) = −½gt² + v₀ sin(θ)t + y₀.
polar coordinates
A coordinate system using (r, θ), where r is distance from the pole and θ is the angle from the polar axis.
pole
The fixed origin point in the polar coordinate system, corresponding to the origin in rectangular coordinates.
polar axis
The reference ray from the pole, corresponding to the positive x-axis in rectangular coordinates.
cardioid
A heart-shaped polar curve r = a ± a cos(θ) or r = a ± a sin(θ), where |a/b| = 1.
limaçon
A polar curve r = a + b cos(θ). Dimpled if |a/b| > 1; cardioid if equal; inner loop if |a/b| < 1.
rose curve
A polar curve r = a cos(nθ) with n petals if n is odd, 2n petals if n is even.
lemniscate
A figure-eight polar curve of the form r² = a² cos(2θ) or r² = a² sin(2θ).
vector
A quantity with both magnitude and direction, written in component form as ⟨a, b⟩.
scalar
A quantity described by a single number with no direction; the magnitude of a vector is a scalar.
magnitude
The length of a vector: |v| = √(a² + b²). Always a non-negative scalar.
unit vector
A vector with magnitude 1. Found by dividing any vector by its magnitude: v̂ = v / |v|.
resultant
The vector sum of two or more vectors, found by adding corresponding components.
dot product
u · v = ac + bd; a scalar equal to |u||v|cos(θ). Encodes the angle between two vectors.
orthogonal
Perpendicular. Two vectors are orthogonal if and only if their dot product equals zero.
projection
The component of one vector in the direction of another: proj_v u = (u·v / |v|²) v.

Chapter 13: Limits, Continuity, and Rates of Change as an Introduction

The three topics of this chapter are gateways into the most important applied mathematics of the next several years of study. In Calculus I, the slope of a parametric curve at a point is found using dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) — the first instance of differentiating a curve not written as y = f(x). In Calculus II, arc length along parametric curves and area enclosed by polar curves are computed by integration. In Calculus III, vectors extend to three dimensions, enabling the computation of surfaces, gradients, flux, and torque. In machine learning, vectors of hundreds or thousands of dimensions form the data representations on which every neural network operates.

Chapter 13 will now take all of the function work of this course — transformations, trigonometric graphs, polynomial behavior, exponential growth, and the modeling tools of this chapter — and show how they connect to the central idea of calculus: what happens as inputs approach a value? The concept of a limit is the mathematical language for that question, and it grows directly out of the function thinking practiced throughout this book.

Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 9 reviews and deepens exponential and logarithmic functions. Students who have met these functions briefly in Algebra II will now study them with greater precision, connect them to inverse function theory from Chapter 2, and apply them to serious mathematical models in finance, biology, chemistry, physics, and data science.

This chapter treats exponential and logarithmic functions as inverse perspectives on the same underlying structure. Both grow out of the idea that some quantities change by multiplication rather than addition. An exponential function describes multiplicative growth or decay directly. A logarithm inverts that process, asking: what exponent produces a given result?

Together, these two function families model more of the real world than any other pair in precalculus. They describe population growth, radioactive half-life, compound interest, pH, earthquake intensity, sound volume, stellar brightness, and the spread of information. Before calculus, students must understand their graphs, their algebra, and their connection to each other.

This chapter prepares students for Calculus I, where derivatives and integrals of exponential and logarithmic functions play a central role in modeling change.

Opening Question

A hospital needs to determine how long a radioactive tracer injected into a patient will remain at detectable levels. The tracer has a half-life of six hours, meaning that every six hours, exactly half of the remaining material decays. If the initial dose is 80 milligrams, how much remains after 24 hours? After one week? At what point does the remaining amount fall below 0.1 milligrams? These questions cannot be answered with linear or polynomial functions. By the end of this chapter, students will be able to build the model, evaluate it at any time, and solve for the moment when a threshold is crossed.

Section 9.1

Exponential Functions Review

What Makes a Function Exponential

An exponential function places the variable in the exponent. This is a fundamental difference from polynomial functions, where the variable is in the base. The function f(x) = x² raises a variable base to a fixed exponent. The function f(x) = 2ˣ raises a fixed base to a variable exponent. These two structures grow in completely different ways.

The general form of an exponential function is f(x) = bˣ, where b > 0 and b ≠ 1. The base b must be positive because negative bases produce undefined results for fractional exponents. The base b = 1 produces the constant function f(x) = 1, which is not useful as a model of change.

Example 9.1.1
Evaluating an exponential function
  • ProblemLet f(x) = 3ˣ. Evaluate f(0), f(1), f(–1), f(2), and f(½).
  • ThinkSubstitute each input and simplify using exponent rules. The output must always be positive.
  • Solve f(0) = 3⁰ = 1 f(1) = 3¹ = 3 f(–1) = 3⁻¹ = 1/3 f(2) = 3² = 9 f(½) = 3^(1/2) = √3 ≈ 1.732
  • CheckAll outputs are positive, which is always true for exponential functions with positive bases.
Answer: f(0) = 1, f(1) = 3, f(–1) = 1/3, f(2) = 9, f(½) = √3
Key Properties of f(x) = bˣ (b > 0, b ≠ 1)

Domain: all real numbers (–∞, ∞)

Range: all positive real numbers (0, ∞)

y-intercept: (0, 1) always, since b⁰ = 1 for any valid base

x-intercept: none — exponential functions never cross the x-axis

Horizontal asymptote: y = 0 as x → –∞ when b > 1; as x → ∞ when 0 < b < 1

One-to-one: passes the horizontal line test; inverse functions exist

Student Warning — Exponential vs. Polynomial

Students sometimes confuse f(x) = 2ˣ (exponential) with f(x) = x² (polynomial). These are different functions with entirely different behaviors. For large positive values of x, exponential functions grow far faster than any polynomial.

Section 9.2

Growth and Decay Models

Exponential growth occurs when the base is greater than 1. Each unit increase in input multiplies the output by the base — multiplicative change, not additive change. The standard growth model is A(t) = A₀ · bᵗ, where A₀ is the initial value, b > 1 is the growth factor, and t is time. The growth factor can be written b = 1 + r, where r is the growth rate as a decimal.

Exponential decay occurs when 0 < b < 1. The decay factor can be written b = 1 – r. Two important measures describe the rate of change: doubling time T (for growth) and half-life H (for decay).

Model TypeStandard FormInterpretation
Exponential GrowthA(t) = A₀ · bᵗ, b > 1Multiplied by b each unit of time
Exponential DecayA(t) = A₀ · bᵗ, 0 < b < 1Multiplied by b (a fraction) each unit of time
Doubling TimeA(t) = A₀ · 2^(t/T)Quantity doubles every T units of time
Half-LifeA(t) = A₀ · (½)^(t/H)Quantity halves every H units of time
Example 9.2.1
Radioactive decay — using the half-life model
  • ProblemA radioactive tracer has a half-life of 6 hours. The initial dose is 80 mg. How much remains after 24 hours?
  • ThinkUse the half-life model. After every 6 hours, the amount is multiplied by ½. Twenty-four hours represents 24 ÷ 6 = 4 half-lives.
  • RepresentA(t) = 80 · (½)^(t/6)
  • SolveA(24) = 80 · (½)^(24/6) = 80 · (½)⁴ = 80 · (1/16) = 5
  • CheckAfter 4 halvings: 80 → 40 → 20 → 10 → 5. Matches.
Answer: After 24 hours, 5 milligrams remain.
Example 9.2.2
Population growth — using a growth rate
  • ProblemA city has population 200,000 in 2020 and grows at 3% per year. Predict the population in 2035.
  • ThinkGrowth rate r = 0.03, so growth factor b = 1.03. From 2020 to 2035 is 15 years.
  • RepresentP(t) = 200,000 · (1.03)ᵗ
  • SolveP(15) = 200,000 · (1.03)¹⁵ ≈ 200,000 · 1.5580 ≈ 311,600
  • CheckAt 3% annual growth, the population roughly doubles in about 23 years. A result above 300,000 after 15 years is reasonable.
Answer: The projected population in 2035 is approximately 311,600.
Interpretation Note

Exponential growth models assume a constant growth rate, which real populations often do not maintain. Models must always be interpreted with awareness of their assumptions and limitations.

Section 9.3

The Number e as an Introduction

The number e is not invented arbitrarily. It emerges naturally when studying what happens to compound interest as the compounding period grows shorter and shorter. If a bank offers 100% annual interest on $1 compounded n times per year, the account balance after one year is (1 + 1/n)ⁿ. As n grows without bound, this expression approaches a specific limit — the number e.

Compounding Periods (n)FormulaResult
1 (annually)(1 + 1/1)¹2.0000
2 (semi-annually)(1 + 1/2)²2.2500
4 (quarterly)(1 + 1/4)⁴2.4414
12 (monthly)(1 + 1/12)¹²2.6130
365 (daily)(1 + 1/365)³⁶⁵2.7146
∞ (continuously)lim(n→∞) (1 + 1/n)ⁿe ≈ 2.71828…
Euler's Number

e = lim(n→∞) (1 + 1/n)ⁿ ≈ 2.71828…

The number e is irrational — its decimal expansion never terminates or repeats. It is one of the most important constants in mathematics, alongside π. The function f(x) = eˣ is its own derivative, making it the natural function for modeling continuous change.

Example 9.3.1
Evaluating the natural exponential function
  • ProblemEvaluate e¹, e², and e⁻¹ to four decimal places.
  • Solve e¹ ≈ 2.7183 e² ≈ 7.3891 e⁻¹ = 1/e ≈ 0.3679
Answer: These are standard reference values used throughout the chapter.

Section 9.4

Continuous Growth and Decay

In Section 9.2, growth was modeled by multiplying by a fixed factor per fixed time period — discrete compounding. The general compound interest formula for discrete compounding is A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where P is principal, r is annual rate, n is periods per year, and t is time in years.

As n increases — daily, hourly, by the minute — compounding approaches a limit. When compounding happens continuously, the formula becomes the continuous growth model:

The Continuous Growth and Decay Model

A(t) = A₀ · e^(rt)

A₀ is the initial value when t = 0. r is the continuous growth rate (positive for growth, negative for decay). When r > 0, the quantity grows. When r < 0, the quantity decays.

Example 9.4.1
Continuous compound interest
  • ProblemAn investment of $5,000 earns 4% annual interest compounded continuously. How much is the investment worth after 10 years?
  • RepresentA(t) = 5000 · e^(0.04t)
  • SolveA(10) = 5000 · e^(0.04 × 10) = 5000 · e^(0.4) ≈ 5000 · 1.4918 ≈ 7459
  • CheckAt 4% compounded annually: 5000(1.04)¹⁰ ≈ 7401. Continuous compounding gives slightly more. Reasonable.
Answer: The investment is worth approximately $7,459 after 10 years.
Example 9.4.2
Continuous decay
  • ProblemA sample of 200 grams decays continuously at 3% per year. How much remains after 20 years?
  • RepresentA(t) = 200 · e^(–0.03t)
  • SolveA(20) = 200 · e^(–0.6) ≈ 200 · 0.5488 ≈ 109.8
Answer: Approximately 109.8 grams remain after 20 years.
Student Warning — Discrete vs. Continuous Rates

The rate r in the continuous model is not the same as the growth rate in the discrete model. A discrete annual growth rate of 4% corresponds to a continuous rate of r = ln(1.04) ≈ 0.0392. For most problems in this course, use the stated rate directly, but be aware of this distinction.

Section 9.5

Logarithmic Functions Review

Exponential functions raise a base to a power. Logarithms answer the inverse question: given a result, what power was used? The logarithm base b of a number x is the exponent to which b must be raised to produce x:

The Fundamental Equivalence

log_b(x) = y <=> b^y = x

Read as: "the logarithm base b of x is y" means the same as "b raised to y equals x." Students should be able to move fluently in both directions.

Example 9.5.1
Converting between logarithmic and exponential forms
  • Problem AConvert log₂(32) = 5 to exponential form.
  • Answer A2⁵ = 32
  • Problem BConvert 10³ = 1000 to logarithmic form.
  • Answer Blog₁₀(1000) = 3
  • Problem CEvaluate log₃(81).
  • ThinkWhat power of 3 gives 81? Since 3⁴ = 81, the answer is 4.
Answers: 2⁵ = 32; log₁₀(1000) = 3; log₃(81) = 4

Logarithms as Inverse Functions

From Chapter 2, inverse functions reverse input-output relationships. If f(x) = bˣ, its inverse is f⁻¹(x) = log_b(x). This produces two cancellation laws used constantly when solving equations:

Cancellation Laws

b^(log_b(x)) = x    for all x > 0

log_b(bˣ) = x    for all real x

Student Warning — Domain of Logarithms

The domain of f(x) = log_b(x) is (0, ∞). The logarithm is undefined for zero and for negative inputs. If the argument inside a logarithm is an expression like –x, check whether that expression is positive — do not assume log_b(–x) is always undefined without examining the sign of x.

Section 9.6

Common and Natural Logarithms

LogarithmNotationBaseKey Values
Common logarithmlog(x)10log(10) = 1; log(100) = 2; log(0.1) = –1
Natural logarithmln(x)eln(1) = 0; ln(e) = 1; ln(e²) = 2; ln(1/e) = –1

The natural logarithm is the inverse of the natural exponential function. The cancellation laws become ln(eˣ) = x and e^(ln x) = x. The natural logarithm appears throughout calculus, physics, chemistry, biology, and economics because of its deep connection to rates of change.

Example 9.6.1
Evaluating common and natural logarithms without a calculator
  • ProblemEvaluate log(10000), log(0.001), ln(e⁵), and ln(1).
  • Solve log(10000) = log(10⁴) = 4 log(0.001) = log(10⁻³) = –3 ln(e⁵) = 5 ln(1) = ln(e⁰) = 0
Answers: 4, –3, 5, 0
Calculator Tip

When a logarithm has a base other than 10 or e, use the change of base formula (Section 9.7) to evaluate it on a calculator. The log button computes base 10; the ln button computes base e.

Section 9.7

Logarithmic Properties

Logarithmic properties allow complex expressions to be simplified, expanded, or condensed. They come directly from the laws of exponents and are essential for solving exponential and logarithmic equations.

The Four Core Properties (b > 0, b ≠ 1, M and N positive)

Product: log_b(MN) = log_b(M) + log_b(N)

Quotient: log_b(M/N) = log_b(M) – log_b(N)

Power: log_b(Mᵖ) = p · log_b(M)

Change of Base: log_b(x) = log(x)/log(b) = ln(x)/ln(b)

Student Warning — No Property for Sums

The product property says log_b(MN) = log_b(M) + log_b(N). It does not say log_b(M + N) = log_b(M) + log_b(N). There is no logarithm property for the log of a sum. This is one of the most frequent errors in logarithmic algebra.

Example 9.7.1
Expanding a logarithmic expression
  • ProblemExpand log₂(8x³/y) using logarithm properties.
  • ThinkUse the quotient property first, then product property, then power property.
  • Solve log₂(8x³/y) = log₂(8x³) – log₂(y) = log₂(8) + log₂(x³) – log₂(y) = 3 + 3·log₂(x) – log₂(y)
Answer: 3 + 3·log₂(x) – log₂(y)
Example 9.7.2
Condensing a logarithmic expression
  • ProblemWrite 2·ln(x) + ln(x–1) – ln(5) as a single logarithm.
  • ThinkApply the power property to the coefficient, then use product and quotient properties.
  • Solve 2·ln(x) + ln(x–1) – ln(5) = ln(x²) + ln(x–1) – ln(5) = ln(x²(x–1)) – ln(5) = ln(x²(x–1)/5)
Answer: ln(x²(x–1)/5)
Example 9.7.3
Using the change of base formula
  • ProblemEvaluate log₇(200) to four decimal places.
  • Solvelog₇(200) = ln(200)/ln(7) = 5.2983/1.9459 ≈ 2.7228
  • CheckVerify that 7^2.7228 ≈ 200 on a calculator.
Answer: log₇(200) ≈ 2.7228

Section 9.8

Solving Exponential Equations

Strategy for Exponential Equations

1. Isolate the exponential expression on one side.

2. Take the natural logarithm (or common logarithm) of both sides.

3. Apply the power property to bring the exponent down to a coefficient.

4. Solve for the variable algebraically.

5. Check the result — exponential equations do not produce extraneous solutions, but the computation should be verified.

Example 9.8.1
Basic exponential equation
  • ProblemSolve 3ˣ = 50.
  • Think50 is not a power of 3, so take the natural logarithm of both sides.
  • Solve ln(3ˣ) = ln(50) x · ln(3) = ln(50) x = ln(50)/ln(3) ≈ 3.9120/1.0986 ≈ 3.561
  • Check3^3.561 ≈ 50. Verify on a calculator. ✓
Answer: x ≈ 3.561
Example 9.8.2
Exponential equation with same base
  • ProblemSolve 5 · 2^(3x–1) = 80.
  • ThinkIsolate the exponential first. Then check whether both sides can be written with the same base.
  • Solve 2^(3x–1) = 16 Since 16 = 2⁴, set exponents equal: 3x – 1 = 4 3x = 5 x = 5/3
  • Check5 · 2^(3(5/3)–1) = 5 · 2⁴ = 5 · 16 = 80. ✓
Answer: x = 5/3
Example 9.8.3
Solving for a continuous growth rate
  • ProblemA population doubles in 12 years. Find the continuous growth rate r using the model P(t) = P₀·e^(rt).
  • ThinkWhen the population doubles, P(12) = 2P₀. Substitute and solve for r.
  • Solve 2P₀ = P₀ · e^(12r) 2 = e^(12r) ln(2) = 12r r = ln(2)/12 ≈ 0.6931/12 ≈ 0.0578
Answer: The continuous growth rate is approximately 5.78% per year. The formula r = ln(2)/T gives the rate when T is the doubling time.
Section 9.9

Solving Logarithmic Equations

Strategy for Logarithmic Equations

1. Isolate the logarithmic expression (or condense multiple logs into one).

2. Convert from logarithmic form to exponential form: log_b(x) = y means b^y = x.

3. Solve for the variable algebraically.

4. Check that all arguments of logarithms in the original equation are positive. Reject any extraneous solutions.

Example 9.9.1
Basic logarithmic equation
  • ProblemSolve log₄(x) = 3.
  • Solvex = 4³ = 64
  • Checklog₄(64) = 3 because 4³ = 64. The argument 64 is positive. ✓
Answer: x = 64
Example 9.9.2
Equation requiring condensing — checking for extraneous solutions
  • ProblemSolve log(x) + log(x – 3) = 1.
  • ThinkCondense the left side using the product property, then convert to exponential form.
  • Solve log(x(x–3)) = 1 x(x–3) = 10 x² – 3x – 10 = 0 (x–5)(x+2) = 0 x = 5 or x = –2
  • Checkx = 5: log(5) and log(2) are defined. ✓   x = –2: log(–2) is undefined. ✗ Reject x = –2.
Answer: x = 5. The solution x = –2 is extraneous and is rejected.
Example 9.9.3
Natural logarithm equation
  • ProblemSolve ln(2x + 1) = 4.
  • Solve 2x + 1 = e⁴ 2x = e⁴ – 1 x = (e⁴ – 1)/2 ≈ (54.598 – 1)/2 ≈ 26.799
  • CheckThe argument 2(26.799) + 1 = 54.598 > 0. ✓
Answer: x = (e⁴ – 1)/2 ≈ 26.799
Student Warning — Extraneous Solutions

After solving a logarithmic equation, always substitute back to check that every logarithm in the original equation has a positive argument. Extraneous solutions are common when the equation involves expressions like x, x – k, or x² inside logarithms.

Transformations

The transformation rules from Chapter 2 apply directly. For f(x) = a · b^(x–h) + k: a is a vertical stretch or reflection; h is a horizontal shift; k is a vertical shift that relocates the horizontal asymptote to y = k. For f(x) = a · log_b(x – h) + k: h is a horizontal shift that relocates the vertical asymptote to x = h; k is a vertical shift.

Example 9.10.1
Graphing a transformed exponential function
  • ProblemDescribe the graph of f(x) = 3^(x–2) + 1.
  • ThinkThis is the graph of y = 3ˣ shifted right 2 units and up 1 unit.
  • Key Features Horizontal asymptote: y = 1 (shifted from y = 0) y-intercept: f(0) = 3⁻² + 1 = 1/9 + 1 ≈ 1.11 Passes through (2, 2): 3⁰ + 1 = 2 Domain: (–∞, ∞); Range: (1, ∞)
  • Graph DescriptionA smooth increasing curve that approaches y = 1 from above as x → –∞, passes through (2, 2), and rises steeply for large x.
Answer: Asymptote y = 1; rightward and upward shift of y = 3ˣ.

Section 9.11

Logarithmic Scales

Many phenomena span enormous ranges of values. Earthquake energies vary by more than a trillion times. Sound intensities from a whisper to a jet engine vary by around a quadrillion times. pH values in chemistry reflect hydrogen ion concentrations differing by factors of a billion. On a standard linear scale, the small values would be invisible. Logarithmic scales solve this by compressing large ranges — on a logarithmic scale, each equal step represents multiplication by the same factor.

ScaleFormulaReference
Richter ScaleM = log(A/A₀)A₀ = reference amplitude
Decibel Scaleβ = 10 · log(I/I₀)I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m² (threshold of hearing)
pHpH = –log([H⁺])[H⁺] in moles per liter; neutral pH = 7
Key Insight — Logarithmic Scales

A magnitude 7 earthquake is not twice as powerful as a magnitude 6. Because the Richter scale is logarithmic, each whole-number increase represents a tenfold increase in amplitude. A difference of 2 units means 10² = 100 times the amplitude.

Example 9.11.1
Comparing earthquake magnitudes
  • ProblemHow many times larger in amplitude is a magnitude 8.2 earthquake than a magnitude 6.0 earthquake?
  • ThinkThe difference in magnitudes is 8.2 – 6.0 = 2.2. The amplitude ratio is 10 raised to that difference.
  • SolveAmplitude ratio = 10^2.2 ≈ 158
Answer: The magnitude 8.2 earthquake has a seismic wave amplitude approximately 158 times that of the magnitude 6.0 earthquake.
Example 9.11.2
Computing pH
  • ProblemA solution has [H⁺] = 4.3 × 10⁻⁵ mol/L. Find the pH.
  • Solve pH = –log(4.3 × 10⁻⁵) = –(log(4.3) + log(10⁻⁵)) = –(0.6335 – 5) = 4.37
Answer: pH ≈ 4.37, indicating an acidic solution (below 7).
Section 9.12

Exponential Models in Finance and Science

Compound Interest

Compound Interest Formulas

Discrete: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

Continuous: A = Pe^(rt)

P = principal; r = annual rate as a decimal; n = periods per year; t = time in years; A = final amount

Example 9.12.1
Comparing compounding methods
  • Problem$10,000 is invested at 5% annual interest for 20 years. Compare annual, monthly, and continuous compounding.
  • Solve Annual (n=1): 10000(1.05)²⁰ ≈ $26,533 Monthly (n=12): 10000(1 + 0.05/12)²⁴⁰ ≈ $27,126 Continuous: 10000 · e^(0.05×20) ≈ $27,183
  • InterpretContinuous compounding yields the highest return, but the difference between frequent discrete compounding and continuous compounding is small.
Answer: Annual → $26,533; Monthly → $27,126; Continuous → $27,183.

Newton's Law of Cooling

Newton's Law of Cooling describes how a warm object cools toward the temperature of its environment:

T(t) = T_env + (T₀ – T_env) · e^(–kt)

T_env is the surrounding temperature, T₀ is the initial temperature of the object, k > 0 is a cooling constant, and t is time.

Example 9.12.2
Newton's law of cooling — finding k and predicting temperature
  • ProblemCoffee at 90°C cools in a room at 22°C. After 10 minutes it is 70°C. Find k and the temperature after 30 minutes.
  • RepresentT(t) = 22 + 68 · e^(–kt)
  • Find k 70 = 22 + 68 · e^(–10k) 48 = 68 · e^(–10k) e^(–10k) = 48/68 = 12/17 –10k = ln(12/17) ≈ –0.3483 k ≈ 0.03483
  • Predict T(30) = 22 + 68 · e^(–0.03483 × 30) ≈ 22 + 68(0.3518) ≈ 45.9°C
Answer: k ≈ 0.0348; after 30 minutes the coffee is approximately 45.9°C.

Radioactive Decay and Half-Life

Half-Life and Continuous Decay Rate

H = ln(2)/r <=> r = ln(2)/H

If the half-life H is known, the continuous decay rate r is ln(2)/H ≈ 0.6931/H.

Example 9.12.3
Carbon-14 dating

  • Step 1r = ln(2)/5730 ≈ 0.6931/5730 ≈ 0.0001210
  • Step 2 0.30·A₀ = A₀ · e^(–0.0001210·t) 0.30 = e^(–0.0001210·t) ln(0.30) = –0.0001210·t –1.2040 = –0.0001210·t t = 1.2040/0.0001210 ≈ 9,950

Section 9.13

Common Exponential and Logarithmic Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Applying log properties to sums
log(x + y) = log(x) + log(y)

The product property applies to products, not sums. log(xy) = log(x) + log(y). There is no simple formula for log(x + y).

Mistake 2 — Misapplying the power property
ln(x²) = (ln x)²

Correct: ln(x²) = 2·ln(x). The power property moves the exponent to a coefficient in front — it does not square the logarithm itself.

Mistake 3 — Forgetting extraneous solutions
Keeping x = –2 in log(x) + log(x–3) = 1 because the algebra works out

Always verify that every argument of every logarithm in the original equation is positive after solving. If log(–2) appears, the solution is extraneous.

Mistake 4 — Treating inverse notation as a reciprocal
log⁻¹(x) = 1/log(x)

In the context of inverse functions, f⁻¹ means the inverse function, not the reciprocal. log⁻¹(x) means 10ˣ, the inverse of log base 10 — not 1/log(x).

Mistake 5 — Distributing exponents over addition
e^(x+y) = eˣ + eʸ

Correct: e^(x+y) = eˣ · eʸ. Exponents distribute over multiplication, not addition.

Mistake 6 — Setting eˣ = negative number
Solving e^(2x) + 5 = 0 and writing e^(2x) = –5, then ln(–5) = 2x

The exponential function eˣ is always positive. If an equation requires eˣ = negative number, there is no real solution.

Mistake 7 — Forgetting domain on logarithms
Writing log(–3) or ln(0) as part of a solution

Both are undefined. Any solution that requires evaluating a logarithm of a non-positive number must be discarded as extraneous.

Mistake 8 — Confusing continuous rate r with discrete rate
Using r = 0.08 in both A = P(1+r)ᵗ and A = Pe^(rt) as if they are the same

The continuous rate r in Pe^(rt) is not the same as the annual rate in P(1+r)ᵗ. The continuous rate is r = ln(1 + rate). For a 8% annual rate, the continuous rate is ln(1.08) ≈ 0.0770.

Section 9.14

Preparing for Calculus Applications

Exponential and logarithmic functions are not isolated topics in precalculus. They are the foundation for some of the most important ideas in Calculus I and beyond.

In Calculus I, students will prove that the derivative of eˣ is eˣ itself — no other function has this property. It means that the rate at which eˣ is growing at any point equals the value of the function at that point. This makes eˣ the natural solution to equations of the form "the rate of change of a quantity equals the quantity itself" — which describes population growth, radioactive decay, Newton's law of cooling, electric current, and quantum probability amplitudes.

The natural logarithm has an equally elegant derivative: the derivative of ln x is 1/x. This allows calculus to integrate functions of the form 1/x, which is impossible using polynomial antiderivatives alone.

Exponential functions are also the solutions to separable differential equations of the form dy/dt = ky. Students who understand these functions well will recognize these solution forms immediately when they appear in Calculus II and Differential Equations.

In Calculus II, the number e and the function eˣ appear in Taylor series, where students discover that eˣ can be written as an infinite sum of polynomial terms — one of the most surprising and beautiful results in all of mathematics.

Calculus Connections — Preview

d/dx (eˣ) = eˣ    The natural exponential function is its own derivative.

d/dx (ln x) = 1/x    The natural logarithm solves the integration problem for 1/x.

dy/dt = ky → y = Ce^(kt)    Exponential functions solve separable growth/decay equations.

Readiness for Calculus I

Before Calculus I, students should be fluent with: the shape and features of eˣ and ln x; the cancellation laws e^(ln x) = x and ln(eˣ) = x; solving exponential and logarithmic equations exactly; building exponential models from growth rate and initial value; and using the continuous growth formula A = A₀e^(rt).

Every calculation in this chapter was preparation for that work. Students who leave Chapter 9 fluent in exponential and logarithmic algebra, graphs, and models are genuinely ready for the next step.

Chapter 9 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 9.1Exponential functions f(x) = bˣ place the variable in the exponent. Domain is all real numbers; range is positive reals only; y-intercept is always (0,1); no x-intercept; horizontal asymptote at y = 0.
  • Sec 9.2Exponential growth models use b > 1; decay models use 0 < b < 1. Doubling time and half-life describe the rate of change. The model forms A(t) = A₀·bᵗ, A(t) = A₀·2^(t/T), and A(t) = A₀·(½)^(t/H) are all equivalent depending on what information is known.
  • Sec 9.3The number e ≈ 2.71828 arises naturally from continuous compounding. It is irrational. The natural exponential function f(x) = eˣ is its own derivative, making it central to all of calculus.
  • Sec 9.4The continuous growth and decay model is A(t) = A₀e^(rt). When r > 0, growth; when r < 0, decay. The continuous rate r is not identical to the discrete annual rate — they are related by r = ln(1 + annual rate).
  • Sec 9.5log_b(x) = y means b^y = x. The logarithm asks what exponent produces a given result. Domain is (0, ∞). Logarithmic and exponential functions are inverses: b^(log_b(x)) = x and log_b(bˣ) = x.
  • Sec 9.6The common logarithm uses base 10 and is written log(x). The natural logarithm uses base e and is written ln(x). Key values: log(10ⁿ) = n; ln(eⁿ) = n; ln(1) = 0; ln(e) = 1.
  • Sec 9.7Four properties: product (log of a product = sum of logs), quotient (log of a quotient = difference of logs), power (log of a power = coefficient times log), change of base (log_b(x) = ln(x)/ln(b)). There is no property for the log of a sum.
  • Sec 9.8To solve exponential equations: isolate the exponential, take the logarithm of both sides, apply the power property, solve for the variable. When both sides share the same base, set exponents equal directly.
  • Sec 9.9To solve logarithmic equations: condense to a single logarithm, convert to exponential form, solve, check all arguments. Extraneous solutions arise when an argument becomes non-positive.
  • Sec 9.10The graphs of bˣ and log_b(x) are reflections of each other across y = x. Transformations shift, stretch, and reflect the graph. Vertical shifts relocate the horizontal asymptote; horizontal shifts relocate the vertical asymptote.
  • Sec 9.11Logarithmic scales compress enormous ranges of values. The Richter scale, decibel scale, and pH each use logarithms to make large-range data readable. Each integer step in magnitude represents a tenfold multiplicative change.
  • Sec 9.12Standard models include compound interest (discrete and continuous), Newton's law of cooling, and radioactive decay/carbon dating. All require solving exponential equations using logarithms. The relationship H = ln(2)/r connects half-life to the continuous decay rate.
  • Sec 9.13Eight recurring errors: log of a sum; power property confusion; missing extraneous solutions; inverse vs. reciprocal notation; distributing exponents over addition; negative arguments; eˣ = negative; confusing continuous and discrete rates.
  • Sec 9.14The derivative of eˣ is eˣ; the derivative of ln x is 1/x. Exponential functions solve dy/dt = ky. Taylor series for eˣ connects exponential functions to polynomials. Fluency with Chapter 9 directly supports all of Calculus I.

Key Terms Review

exponential function
A function of the form f(x) = bˣ, where b > 0 and b ≠ 1. The variable is in the exponent.
base
The fixed positive number b raised to the variable exponent in an exponential function.
growth factor
The factor b > 1 by which an exponential function multiplies per unit step.
decay factor
The factor b where 0 < b < 1 by which an exponential decay function multiplies per unit step.
Euler's number e
The irrational number ≈ 2.71828, the base of the natural exponential function; arises from continuous compounding.
continuous growth model
A(t) = A₀·e^(rt), where r is the continuous growth rate and t is time.
logarithm
The exponent to which a base must be raised to produce a given value. log_b(x) = y means b^y = x.
common logarithm
log(x) = log₁₀(x). Uses base 10. On a calculator, the log button.
natural logarithm
ln(x) = log_e(x). Uses base e. On a calculator, the ln button.
product property
log_b(MN) = log_b(M) + log_b(N). The log of a product equals the sum of the logs.
quotient property
log_b(M/N) = log_b(M) – log_b(N). The log of a quotient equals the difference of the logs.
power property
log_b(Mᵖ) = p·log_b(M). An exponent inside a logarithm moves to a coefficient outside.
change of base
log_b(x) = ln(x)/ln(b) = log(x)/log(b). Rewrites any logarithm using base e or base 10.
half-life
The time H required for an exponential decay quantity to halve. Related to rate by H = ln(2)/r.
doubling time
The time T required for a growing quantity to double. Related to rate by T = ln(2)/r.
extraneous solution
An algebraically derived solution that fails to satisfy the original equation due to a domain violation.
logarithmic scale
A scale in which equal distances represent equal multiplicative factors, compressing large data ranges.
pH
pH = –log([H⁺]). A logarithmic measure of hydrogen ion concentration. Neutral = 7; acidic < 7; basic > 7.
decibel
β = 10·log(I/I₀). A logarithmic unit for measuring sound intensity relative to the threshold of hearing.
Richter scale
M = log(A/A₀). A logarithmic scale for earthquake magnitude; each unit increase represents 10× the amplitude.

Chapter 10: Sequences, Series, and Mathematical Induction

Chapter 9 studied functions defined on all real numbers — continuous, smooth, and modeled by algebraic formulas involving exponents and logarithms. Chapter 10 will shift to functions defined on the positive integers: sequences. A sequence is a discrete function whose inputs are whole numbers. This connects to the idea of compounding studied in Chapter 9, where discrete time steps produce discrete outputs. Chapter 10 will also extend to series — accumulated sums of sequences — and introduce mathematical induction as a method for proving statements about all integers. These ideas connect directly to limits, to calculus, and to the foundations of mathematics.

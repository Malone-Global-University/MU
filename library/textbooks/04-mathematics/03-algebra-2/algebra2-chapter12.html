Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces conic sections — a family of curves that includes circles, parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas. Each of these curves can be described both geometrically, in terms of distances and shapes, and algebraically, in terms of equations in two variables. The connection between these two descriptions is the central theme of the chapter.

You have already worked with parabolas in Chapters 3, 5, and 6. You know that a quadratic function produces a parabolic graph. In this chapter, you will see that the parabola is one member of a broader family, and that the others — circles, ellipses, and hyperbolas — arise from the same geometric idea and obey their own algebraic patterns.

Conic sections are not purely abstract. Planets travel in elliptical orbits. Radio telescopes, satellite dishes, and automobile headlights use parabolic shapes to focus and direct signals and light. Bridges are built using parabolic arches. The cooling towers of power plants are shaped like hyperbolas. Architects use ellipses in the design of arched ceilings and whispering galleries. Understanding conic sections gives you the mathematical vocabulary to describe all of these shapes precisely.

Opening Question

A satellite dish is designed so that all incoming signals that arrive parallel to the axis of the dish reflect off the surface and converge at a single point — the receiver. An engineer knows the dish is 4 meters across and 0.5 meters deep. Where exactly should the receiver be positioned inside the dish so that signals are focused correctly? This problem requires the equation of a parabola. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer it — and understand why the shape of a parabola makes it perfect for this task.

Section 12.1

What Conic Sections Are

Imagine a perfectly symmetrical double cone — two cone-shaped surfaces meeting at a central point, their axes aligned vertically. If you slice through this double cone with a flat plane at different angles, the outline of the intersection traces out a curve. Depending on the angle and position of the cut, you get one of four curves.

Cutting Plane OrientationResulting CurveCore Distance Condition
Perpendicular to the axisCircleAll points equidistant from one center point
Tilted, cuts one cone only (not parallel to edge)EllipseSum of distances to two foci is constant
Parallel to exactly one slant edgeParabolaEquidistant from one focus and one directrix line
Steeply tilted, cuts both conesHyperbolaAbsolute difference of distances to two foci is constant
Diagram Instruction

Draw a double cone (two cones joined at a central point, axis vertical). Then draw four separate cutting planes and label the resulting intersection curves: horizontal cut → circle; tilted cut → ellipse; cut parallel to one slant edge → parabola; steeply tilted cut through both cones → hyperbola.

These four curves are the conic sections. They are called conic sections because they are sections — slices — of a cone. The ancient Greek mathematician Apollonius of Perga studied them systematically around 200 BCE. Today they appear throughout science, engineering, and mathematics.

In this chapter, you will study conic sections through their algebraic equations in the coordinate plane. Each conic satisfies a distance condition that translates directly into an equation in $x$ and $y$. By writing the equation in a carefully organized standard form, you can read off the key features immediately.

Section 12.2

Distance and Midpoint Review

Before working with conic sections, you need two foundational tools from coordinate geometry. Both grow naturally from the Pythagorean theorem.

Distance Formula

The distance between $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$

Why it works: Form a right triangle. The horizontal leg has length $|x_2 - x_1|$, the vertical leg has length $|y_2 - y_1|$. The Pythagorean theorem gives $d^2 = (x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2$. Take the positive square root.

Midpoint Formula

The midpoint of the segment from $(x_1, y_1)$ to $(x_2, y_2)$ is:

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\ \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

Why it works: The midpoint is in the middle. Its $x$-coordinate is the average of the two $x$-coordinates; its $y$-coordinate is the average of the two $y$-coordinates.

Example 12.2.1
Finding the distance between two points
  • ProblemFind the distance between $A = (1, 3)$ and $B = (7, 11)$.
  • ThinkApply the distance formula with $(x_1, y_1) = (1, 3)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (7, 11)$.
  • Solve$$d = \sqrt{(7-1)^2 + (11-3)^2} = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100} = 10$$
  • CheckA 6-8-10 right triangle is a scaled 3-4-5 triangle. ✓
Answer: The distance between $A$ and $B$ is 10 units.
Example 12.2.2
Finding the midpoint of a segment
  • ProblemFind the midpoint of the segment connecting $P = (-3, 4)$ and $Q = (9, -2)$.
  • ThinkAverage the $x$-coordinates and $y$-coordinates separately.
  • Solve$$M = \left(\frac{-3 + 9}{2},\ \frac{4 + (-2)}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{6}{2},\ \frac{2}{2}\right) = (3, 1)$$
  • CheckDistance from $M=(3,1)$ to $P=(-3,4)$: $\sqrt{36+9} = \sqrt{45}$. Distance from $M$ to $Q=(9,-2)$: $\sqrt{36+9} = \sqrt{45}$. Equal. ✓
Answer: The midpoint is $(3, 1)$.
Student Tip

In conic section problems, you will frequently need the distance formula to verify a distance condition or to find the value of $a$, $b$, or $c$ in an equation. Keep the formula visible and practice it until it is automatic.

Section 12.3

Circles

A circle is the set of all points in a plane that are the same distance from a fixed point. The fixed point is called the center, and the fixed distance is called the radius. Every point on the circle is exactly $r$ units from the center — no closer, no farther.

Building the equation. Let the center be at $(h, k)$ and the radius be $r$. A point $(x, y)$ lies on the circle when its distance from $(h, k)$ equals $r$. Using the distance formula and squaring both sides:

Standard Form of a Circle

$$(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$$

Center: $(h, k)$    Radius: $r$

Student Warning — Sign of the Center

The equation uses subtraction: $(x - h)$ and $(y - k)$. If the equation reads $(x + 3)^2 + (y - 2)^2 = 25$, rewrite the first factor as $(x - (-3))^2$, so the center is $(-3, 2)$, not $(3, 2)$. Always read the center as the values that make each factor equal to zero.

Example 12.3.1
Writing the equation of a circle
  • ProblemWrite the equation of the circle with center $(4, -1)$ and radius 7.
  • ThinkSubstitute $h = 4$, $k = -1$, and $r = 7$ into the standard form.
  • Solve$$(x - 4)^2 + (y - (-1))^2 = 7^2 \implies (x - 4)^2 + (y + 1)^2 = 49$$
  • CheckTest $(11, -1)$, which is 7 units right of center: $(11-4)^2 + (-1+1)^2 = 49 + 0 = 49$. ✓
Answer: $(x - 4)^2 + (y + 1)^2 = 49$
Example 12.3.2
Identifying center and radius
  • ProblemIdentify the center and radius of $(x + 2)^2 + (y - 5)^2 = 36$.
  • ThinkRewrite $(x + 2)$ as $(x - (-2))$. So $h = -2$, $k = 5$, and $r^2 = 36$, giving $r = 6$.
Answer: Center $(-2, 5)$, radius $6$.
Diagram Instruction — Graphing a Circle

Plot the center at $(4, -1)$. From the center, mark four points by moving $r = 7$ units in each cardinal direction: right to $(11, -1)$, left to $(-3, -1)$, up to $(4, 6)$, down to $(4, -8)$. Sketch the circle passing through all four points with smooth, symmetric curves.

Section 12.4

Equations of Circles

When you expand the standard form of a circle, you get the general form: $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$. The center and radius are not visible in general form. The standard form is more useful for graphing and interpreting the circle.

Example 12.4.1
Writing standard form from center and a point
  • ProblemWrite the equation of the circle with center $(3, -2)$ that passes through $(7, 1)$.
  • ThinkThe radius is the distance from center to the given point.
  • Solve$$r = \sqrt{(7-3)^2 + (1-(-2))^2} = \sqrt{16 + 9} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$ $$(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$$
  • CheckSubstitute $(7, 1)$: $(7-3)^2 + (1+2)^2 = 16 + 9 = 25$. ✓
Answer: $(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$
Example 12.4.2
Expanding to general form
  • ProblemExpand $(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$ into general form.
  • Solve$$(x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 4y + 4) = 25$$ $$x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y + 13 = 25$$ $$x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0$$
Answer: $x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0$
Section 12.5

Completing the Square for Circles

When a circle equation is given in general form, you must rewrite it in standard form before you can identify the center and radius or graph it. The technique is completing the square, applied separately to the $x$-terms and the $y$-terms.

To complete the square for $x^2 + bx$: take half the coefficient of $x$, square it, and add it. This creates a perfect square trinomial: $x^2 + bx + \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = \left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2$. Whatever you add to one side of an equation, you must add to the other.

Example 12.5.1
Converting general form to standard form
  • ProblemRewrite $x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0$ in standard form. Identify the center and radius.
  • ThinkGroup $x$-terms and $y$-terms, move the constant to the right, then complete the square for each group.
  • Solve$$(x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 4y) = 12$$ \text{For } x: \text{ half of } {-6} \text{ is } {-3}, \text{ squared is } 9. \text{ Add 9 to both sides.} \text{For } y: \text{ half of } 4 \text{ is } 2, \text{ squared is } 4. \text{ Add 4 to both sides.} $$(x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 4y + 4) = 12 + 9 + 4$$ $$(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$$
Answer: Center $(3, -2)$, radius $5$.
Example 12.5.2
Completing the square with a leading coefficient
  • ProblemRewrite $4x^2 + 4y^2 - 8x + 24y + 4 = 0$ in standard form.
  • ThinkThe leading coefficient is 4. Divide every term by 4 first, then complete the square.
  • Solve$$x^2 + y^2 - 2x + 6y + 1 = 0$$ $$(x^2 - 2x) + (y^2 + 6y) = -1$$ \text{Complete for } x: \text{ add } 1. \quad \text{Complete for } y: \text{ add } 9. $$(x^2 - 2x + 1) + (y^2 + 6y + 9) = -1 + 1 + 9$$ $$(x - 1)^2 + (y + 3)^2 = 9$$
Answer: Center $(1, -3)$, radius $3$.
Student Warning — Divide First

Completing the square only works cleanly when the quadratic terms have coefficient 1. If the equation has $4x^2$ and $4y^2$, divide the entire equation by 4 before proceeding. Forgetting this step produces incorrect centers and radii.

Section 12.6

Parabolas as Conic Sections

You know parabolas as the graphs of quadratic functions. In this section, you will see the parabola from a new geometric angle — as a conic section defined by a distance condition. This definition is more general and more powerful than the function definition, and it explains why parabolic shapes are so useful in engineering.

A parabola is the set of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point, called the focus, and a fixed line, called the directrix. For every point $P$ on the parabola, the distance from $P$ to the focus equals the distance from $P$ to the directrix (measured perpendicularly).

The point on the parabola closest to both the focus and the directrix is the vertex. The vertex lies midway between focus and directrix. The distance from the vertex to the focus is called $p$. The directrix is also $p$ units from the vertex, on the opposite side from the focus.

Standard Forms of a Parabola

Vertex at $(h, k)$, opens vertically:

$$(x - h)^2 = 4p(y - k)$$

Focus: $(h,\ k+p)$  ·  Directrix: $y = k - p$  ·  Opens up if $p > 0$, down if $p < 0$

Vertex at $(h, k)$, opens horizontally:

$$(y - k)^2 = 4p(x - h)$$

Focus: $(h+p,\ k)$  ·  Directrix: $x = h - p$  ·  Opens right if $p > 0$, left if $p < 0$

Connecting to Algebra I

In Algebra I you learned $y = ax^2$ as a parabola with vertex at the origin. This is a special case of conic form. The equation $x^2 = 4py$ corresponds to $y = \frac{1}{4p}x^2$, so $a = \frac{1}{4p}$. The focus distance $p$ and the leading coefficient $a$ are inversely related — a narrow parabola (large $|a|$) has a focus close to the vertex (small $|p|$).

Example 12.6.1
Finding the focus and directrix
  • ProblemFind the focus and directrix of $x^2 = 12y$.
  • ThinkThis is the form $x^2 = 4py$ with vertex at the origin. Match: $4p = 12$, so $p = 3$.
  • SolveVertex: $(0, 0)$. Focus: $(0, 3)$ (above the vertex, since $p > 0$). Directrix: $y = -3$.
Answer: Focus $(0, 3)$, directrix $y = -3$.
Example 12.6.2
Writing the equation of a parabola
  • ProblemA parabola has vertex $(2, -1)$ and focus $(2, 3)$. Write its equation.
  • ThinkSame $x$-coordinate for vertex and focus → opens vertically. Focus is above the vertex → $p > 0$. Distance: $p = 3 - (-1) = 4$.
  • Solve$$(x - 2)^2 = 4(4)(y - (-1)) \implies (x - 2)^2 = 16(y + 1)$$
  • CheckAt vertex $(2, -1)$: $(2-2)^2 = 16(-1+1) \implies 0 = 0$. ✓
Answer: $(x - 2)^2 = 16(y + 1)$
Example 12.6.3
Answering the opening question — satellite dish placement
  • ProblemA satellite dish is 4 meters wide and 0.5 meters deep, vertex at the origin. Find the focus location.
  • ThinkThe rim passes through $(\pm 2, 0.5)$. The dish opens upward: $x^2 = 4py$. Substitute a rim point.
  • Solve$$4 = 4p(0.5) = 2p \implies p = 2$$
  • CheckThe focus is 2 meters above the vertex, inside the 0.5-meter-deep dish — that seems impossible until you note the dish is 4 m wide and the focus sits on the axis of symmetry, not at the rim. In this model the vertex is at the bottom center and the dish opens upward; the focus is 2 m above the vertex. ✓
Answer: The receiver (focus) should be placed 2 meters from the vertex along the axis of the dish.

Section 12.7

Ellipses

An ellipse is the set of all points in a plane for which the sum of the distances to two fixed points is constant. The two fixed points are called the foci (singular: focus).

Imagine placing two pushpins in a board and stretching a loose string between them. Holding a pencil against the string and keeping it taut as you trace a full curve gives you an ellipse. The two pins are the foci. No matter where the pencil is on the ellipse, the total string length from pin to pencil to pin remains constant.

Every ellipse has a major axis — the longer axis, running through both foci and connecting the two vertices — and a minor axis — the shorter axis, perpendicular to the major axis at the center, connecting the two co-vertices.

Key Measurements of an Ellipse

Let $2a$ = the constant sum of distances (total string length). Then:

$a$ = distance from center to each vertex (along the major axis)

$b$ = distance from center to each co-vertex (along the minor axis)

$c$ = distance from center to each focus

$$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 \quad \text{(equivalently, } c^2 = a^2 - b^2\text{)}$$

Note: $a > b$ (major axis is longer than minor axis) and $a > c$ (vertices are farther from center than foci).

Why $a^2 = b^2 + c^2$? Consider the point at the end of the minor axis (a co-vertex). Its distance to each focus is, by symmetry, exactly $a$ (the two distances must be equal and sum to $2a$). The center, a focus, and the co-vertex form a right triangle with legs $b$ and $c$ and hypotenuse $a$. The Pythagorean theorem gives $a^2 = b^2 + c^2$.

Diagram Instruction

Draw an ellipse centered at the origin with the major axis along the $x$-axis. Label: center at origin; foci at $(-c, 0)$ and $(c, 0)$; vertices at $(-a, 0)$ and $(a, 0)$; co-vertices at $(0, b)$ and $(0, -b)$. Draw the right triangle with legs $b$ (vertical) and $c$ (horizontal) and hypotenuse $a$, connecting the center, a focus, and a co-vertex to illustrate $a^2 = b^2 + c^2$.

Section 12.8

Equations of Ellipses

Standard Forms of an Ellipse (Center at $(h, k)$)

Horizontal major axis ($a^2$ under $x$-term, $a > b$):

$$\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Vertices: $(h \pm a,\ k)$  ·  Co-vertices: $(h,\ k \pm b)$  ·  Foci: $(h \pm c,\ k)$

Vertical major axis ($a^2$ under $y$-term, $a > b$):

$$\frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} = 1$$

Vertices: $(h,\ k \pm a)$  ·  Co-vertices: $(h \pm b,\ k)$  ·  Foci: $(h,\ k \pm c)$

Key Observation

The larger denominator ($a^2$) tells you the direction of the major axis. If $a^2$ is under the $x$-term, the major axis is horizontal. If $a^2$ is under the $y$-term, the major axis is vertical. Always compare denominators before deciding.

Example 12.8.1
Finding features of an ellipse from standard form
  • ProblemFor $\dfrac{x^2}{25} + \dfrac{y^2}{9} = 1$, find the center, vertices, co-vertices, and foci.
  • ThinkLarger denominator is 25 under the $x$-term → major axis is horizontal. $a^2 = 25$, $a = 5$. $b^2 = 9$, $b = 3$. Center at origin.
  • Solve$$c^2 = a^2 - b^2 = 25 - 9 = 16 \implies c = 4$$ Center: $(0,0)$  ·  Vertices: $(\pm 5, 0)$  ·  Co-vertices: $(0, \pm 3)$  ·  Foci: $(\pm 4, 0)$
  • CheckFrom vertex $(5, 0)$: distance to $(4,0)$ is 1, distance to $(-4,0)$ is 9. Sum $= 10 = 2a$. ✓
Answer: Center $(0,0)$; vertices $(\pm 5, 0)$; co-vertices $(0, \pm 3)$; foci $(\pm 4, 0)$.
Example 12.8.2
Writing the equation of an ellipse from features
  • ProblemWrite the equation of the ellipse with center $(3, -2)$, vertices $(3, 4)$ and $(3, -8)$, co-vertices $(6, -2)$ and $(0, -2)$.
  • ThinkVertices share the same $x$-coordinate as the center → vertical major axis. Distance from center $(3,-2)$ to vertex $(3,4)$: $a = 6$. Distance from center to co-vertex $(6,-2)$: $b = 3$.
  • Solve$$\frac{(x-3)^2}{9} + \frac{(y+2)^2}{36} = 1$$
Answer: $\dfrac{(x-3)^2}{9} + \dfrac{(y+2)^2}{36} = 1$
Example 12.8.3
Finding the equation from foci and a vertex
  • ProblemAn ellipse has foci at $(-3, 0)$ and $(3, 0)$ and a vertex at $(5, 0)$. Write its equation.
  • ThinkFoci on $x$-axis → horizontal major axis. $c = 3$, $a = 5$. Find $b^2 = a^2 - c^2$.
  • Solve$$b^2 = 25 - 9 = 16 \implies \frac{x^2}{25} + \frac{y^2}{16} = 1$$
Answer: $\dfrac{x^2}{25} + \dfrac{y^2}{16} = 1$

Section 12.9

Hyperbolas

A hyperbola is the set of all points in a plane for which the absolute value of the difference of distances to two fixed points (foci) is constant. Compare this with the ellipse: an ellipse adds distances; a hyperbola subtracts them (and takes the absolute value). This one change in the distance condition produces a dramatically different curve.

A hyperbola consists of two separate branches, curving away from the center in opposite directions. Each branch curves toward one vertex. Unlike the ellipse, the foci lie outside the vertices — they are farther from the center than the nearest point of the curve.

Key Measurements of a Hyperbola

The constant difference of distances is $2a$. Then:

$a$ = distance from center to each vertex (along the transverse axis)

$b$ = half-length of the conjugate axis (used for the asymptotes)

$c$ = distance from center to each focus

$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2$$

Note: $c > a$ for a hyperbola (the foci are farther from center than the vertices). This is the opposite of the ellipse relationship.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a hyperbola with a horizontal transverse axis. Mark the center at the origin; vertices at $(-a, 0)$ and $(a, 0)$; foci at $(-c, 0)$ and $(c, 0)$. Draw both branches curving outward from the vertices. Draw dashed asymptote lines through the center. Draw the dashed central rectangle with half-width $a$ and half-height $b$ — the asymptotes pass through its corners.

Section 12.10

Equations of Hyperbolas

Standard Forms of a Hyperbola (Center at $(h, k)$)

Horizontal transverse axis (opens left and right):

$$\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Vertices: $(h \pm a,\ k)$  ·  Foci: $(h \pm c,\ k)$  ·  Asymptotes: $y - k = \pm\dfrac{b}{a}(x-h)$

Vertical transverse axis (opens up and down):

$$\frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Vertices: $(h,\ k \pm a)$  ·  Foci: $(h,\ k \pm c)$  ·  Asymptotes: $y - k = \pm\dfrac{a}{b}(x-h)$

Ellipse vs. Hyperbola — Quick Comparison

An ellipse has a plus sign between fractions and equals 1. The foci are inside ($c < a$), and $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$. A hyperbola has a minus sign and equals 1. The foci are outside ($c > a$), and $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$. The positive term determines which direction the hyperbola opens.

Example 12.10.1
Identifying features of a hyperbola
  • ProblemFind the center, vertices, foci, and asymptotes of $\dfrac{x^2}{16} - \dfrac{y^2}{9} = 1$.

  • Solve$$c^2 = 16 + 9 = 25 \implies c = 5$$ Center: $(0,0)$  ·  Vertices: $(\pm 4, 0)$  ·  Foci: $(\pm 5, 0)$  ·  Asymptotes: $y = \pm\dfrac{3}{4}x$
Answer: Center $(0,0)$; vertices $(\pm 4, 0)$; foci $(\pm 5, 0)$; asymptotes $y = \pm\frac{3}{4}x$.

A hyperbola with a vertical transverse axis

  • Think$y$-term is positive → vertical transverse axis. $a^2 = 25$, $a = 5$. $b^2 = 11$. Center at $(-2, 1)$.
  • Solve$$c^2 = 25 + 11 = 36 \implies c = 6$$ Center: $(-2, 1)$  ·  Vertices: $(-2, 6)$ and $(-2, -4)$  ·  Foci: $(-2, 7)$ and $(-2, -5)$

The Asymptote Rectangle

A useful graphing aid for hyperbolas is the central rectangle. Draw a rectangle centered at $(h, k)$ with horizontal half-length $a$ and vertical half-length $b$. The diagonals of this rectangle, extended as lines through the center, give the asymptotes. The branches of the hyperbola pass through the vertices and curve outward, approaching the asymptotes but never reaching them.

Section 12.11

Identifying Conics from Equations

Any conic section (without rotation) can be represented by an equation of the form $Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$, where $A$ and $C$ are not both zero. You can identify which conic it is just by comparing the coefficients of $x^2$ and $y^2$ — no graphing required.

Condition on $A$ and $C$Conic TypeExample
$A = C$ (same nonzero value)Circle$3x^2 + 3y^2 + \cdots = 0$
$A \neq C$, same signEllipse$4x^2 + 9y^2 + \cdots = 0$
$A$ and $C$ opposite signsHyperbola$x^2 - 4y^2 + \cdots = 0$
Exactly one of $A$ or $C$ is zeroParabola$y^2 - 6y - 4x = 0$ or $x^2 + 3x + y = 0$
Example 12.11.1
Identifying conics from equations
  • ProblemIdentify each conic: (a) $3x^2 + 3y^2 - 6x + 12y - 9 = 0$; (b) $4x^2 + 9y^2 - 16x + 36y - 20 = 0$; (c) $x^2 - 4y^2 + 2x + 8y - 7 = 0$; (d) $y^2 - 6y - 4x + 5 = 0$.
  • ThinkCompare coefficients of $x^2$ and $y^2$ for each.
  • Solve(a) Coefficients 3 and 3: equal → Circle.
    (b) Coefficients 4 and 9: same sign, unequal → Ellipse.
    (c) Coefficients 1 and $-4$: opposite signs → Hyperbola.
    (d) No $x^2$ term ($A = 0$) → Parabola (opens horizontally).
Example 12.11.2
Rewriting a general conic in standard form
  • ProblemIdentify and rewrite $9x^2 + 4y^2 - 36x + 8y + 4 = 0$ in standard form. Find its key features.
  • ThinkCoefficients 9 and 4: same sign, unequal → ellipse. Complete the square for both variables.
  • Solve$$9(x^2 - 4x) + 4(y^2 + 2y) = -4$$ \text{For } x\text{: half of }{-4} = {-2},\text{ squared } = 4. \text{ Add } 9 \cdot 4 = 36 \text{ to right.} \text{For } y\text{: half of } 2 = 1,\text{ squared } = 1. \text{ Add } 4 \cdot 1 = 4 \text{ to right.} $$9(x-2)^2 + 4(y+1)^2 = -4 + 36 + 4 = 36$$ $$\frac{(x-2)^2}{4} + \frac{(y+1)^2}{9} = 1$$
Answer: Ellipse, center $(2, -1)$, $a = 3$ (vertical), $b = 2$, $c = \sqrt{5}$. Vertices: $(2, 2)$ and $(2, -4)$. Foci: $(2, -1 \pm \sqrt{5})$.
Student Warning — Factor Before Completing the Square

When completing the square on $9(x^2 - 4x)$, adding 4 inside the parentheses actually adds $9 \times 4 = 36$ to the expression. You must add 36 to the right side of the equation, not just 4. The factor in front of the group multiplies everything inside.

Section 12.12

Conics in Astronomy, Engineering, and Design

Conic sections are not abstract curiosities. They appear repeatedly in the physical world because they are the natural outcome of inverse-square laws, reflective properties, and orbital mechanics.

Planetary Orbits — Ellipses

In 1609, Johannes Kepler published his first law of planetary motion: every planet orbits the Sun in an ellipse, with the Sun located at one focus. This discovery, later derived from Newton's law of universal gravitation, means that no planet travels in a perfect circle. Earth's orbit is nearly circular, but the orbits of Mercury, Mars, and comets can be noticeably elongated.

Eccentricity measures how elongated an ellipse is:

Eccentricity

$$e = \frac{c}{a} \quad (0 \leq e < 1 \text{ for an ellipse})$$

When $e = 0$, the ellipse is a perfect circle. As $e \to 1$, the ellipse becomes more elongated and the foci move toward the vertices.

Example 12.12.1
Earth's elliptical orbit
  • ProblemEarth's orbit has semi-major axis $a \approx 149.6$ million km and eccentricity $e \approx 0.017$. Find $c$ and $b$.
  • Solve$$c = ea = 0.017 \times 149.6 \approx 2.54 \text{ million km}$$ $$b = \sqrt{a^2 - c^2} = \sqrt{149.6^2 - 2.54^2} \approx 149.58 \text{ million km}$$
  • InterpretEarth's orbit is very close to circular — the Sun is only about 2.5 million km off-center from an orbit roughly 150 million km in radius. The eccentricity $e \approx 0.017$ confirms this near-circularity.

Parabolic Reflectors

Parabolas have a remarkable reflective property: any ray traveling parallel to the axis of a parabola, upon hitting the curve, reflects directly toward the focus. Conversely, any ray from the focus reflects outward parallel to the axis. This property makes parabolas essential in satellite dishes, radio telescopes, reflecting telescopes, automobile headlights, and solar cookers.

Hyperbolas in Navigation and Engineering

The difference-of-distances definition of a hyperbola is the basis of LORAN (long-range navigation), a historical radio navigation system. Two fixed transmitters broadcast signals simultaneously. A receiver measures the difference in arrival times, which corresponds to a constant difference in distances — placing the receiver on a hyperbola with the transmitters as foci. Multiple signal pairs allow precise location.

Hyperbolic shapes also appear in the design of cooling towers for power plants. These towers have a hyperboloid shape — generated by rotating a hyperbola around its conjugate axis — which is structurally strong, uses less material than a cylinder, and creates natural airflow.

Whispering galleries use elliptical ceilings to concentrate sound from one focus to the other. The National Statuary Hall in the U.S. Capitol and the Mormon Tabernacle in Salt Lake City are famous examples.

Example 12.12.2
Parabolic arch bridge
  • ProblemA parabolic arch bridge spans 200 feet with the top of the arch 80 feet above the base. Vertex at origin, opening downward. Write the equation and find the height 50 feet from center.
  • ThinkThe rim of the arch passes through $(\pm 100, -80)$ (span 200 ft, arch drops 80 ft). Equation form: $x^2 = 4py$ with $p < 0$.
  • Solve$$100^2 = 4p(-80) \implies 10000 = -320p \implies p = -31.25$$ $$x^2 = -125y \quad\text{or equivalently}\quad y = -\frac{x^2}{125}$$ \text{At } x = 50: \quad y = -\frac{2500}{125} = -20
Answer: The arch is $80 - 20 = \mathbf{60}$ feet above the ground at 50 feet from center.

Section 12.13

Common Conic Section Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Sign Error in the Center
Center of $(x+3)^2 + (y-5)^2 = 36$ written as $(3, 5)$.

Fix: Rewrite $(x+3) = (x - (-3))$. The center uses the values that make each factor zero: $h = -3$, $k = 5$. Always read the sign actively.

Mistake 2 — Not Multiplying by the Leading Factor When Balancing
Completing the square in $4(x^2 - 6x)$: adds 9 inside and only 9 to the right side.

Fix: Adding 9 inside $4(\cdots)$ adds $4 \times 9 = 36$ to the expression. Add 36 to the right side, not just 9.

Mistake 3 — Using the Wrong $c^2$ Formula
Using $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$ for a hyperbola (the ellipse formula).

Fix: For an ellipse, $c < a$ so $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$. For a hyperbola, $c > a$ so $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$. If your $c$ comes out less than $a$ for a hyperbola, you used the wrong formula.

Mistake 4 — Misidentifying the Major Axis of an Ellipse
For $\dfrac{x^2}{9} + \dfrac{y^2}{25} = 1$: major axis identified as horizontal because the "$x$-term comes first."

Fix: The major axis goes with the larger denominator. Since $25 > 9$ and 25 is under the $y$-term, the major axis is vertical.

Mistake 5 — Misidentifying the Direction a Hyperbola Opens
$\dfrac{y^2}{9} - \dfrac{x^2}{4} = 1$ identified as opening left and right.

Fix: The positive term determines the direction. The $y$-term is positive (first) → opens up and down.

Mistake 6 — Forgetting to Square the Radius
Circle with radius 7 written as $(x-2)^2 + (y+3)^2 = 7$.

Fix: Standard circle form has $r^2$ on the right. If the radius is 7, the equation uses $49$. If the equation shows 49, the radius is $\sqrt{49} = 7$, not 49.

Mistake 7 — Forgetting Extraneous Solutions
When solving a system (e.g., line intersects circle), keeping both algebraic solutions without checking them in the original equation.

Fix: Always substitute candidate solutions back into the original equation to verify. Squaring and substituting can introduce extraneous values.

Section 12.14

Preparing for Precalculus Geometry

You have now built the complete algebraic foundation for conic sections. You can write equations, identify features, graph, convert between forms, and apply conic sections to real-world problems. In Precalculus, you will encounter conic sections again — and go considerably further.

Parametric Equations. Conic sections can be described using parametric equations, where both $x$ and $y$ are functions of a third variable (usually $t$ or $\theta$). The unit circle, for instance, becomes $x = \cos\theta$, $y = \sin\theta$. Parametric forms are natural for describing motion along curves.

Polar Form of Conics. In polar coordinates, all four conic sections unify into a single equation involving eccentricity:

Polar Form of Conics (Precalculus Preview)

$$r = \frac{ed}{1 \pm e\cos\theta}$$

The value of $e$ determines the conic: $e < 1$ → ellipse; $e = 1$ → parabola; $e > 1$ → hyperbola. All four conics are members of one continuous family, separated only by eccentricity.

Rotated Conics. When the general form includes an $xy$-term ($Bxy$), the conic is rotated relative to the coordinate axes. Precalculus covers rotation of axes to eliminate this term and identify the resulting conic.

Calculus Applications. In Calculus, you will find areas, arc lengths, and volumes related to conic sections, and you will use parametric and polar equations to describe and analyze curved paths. The ellipse you studied algebraically here reappears as the provable consequence of Newton's law of universal gravitation.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1Conic sections (circle, parabola, ellipse, hyperbola) are the curves formed by intersecting a plane with a double cone. Each is defined by a distance condition and described by an algebraic equation in standard form.
  • Sec 12.2Distance formula: $d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$. Midpoint formula: $M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$. Both are essential tools throughout the chapter.
  • Sec 12.3 – 12.4A circle is the set of points equidistant from a center. Standard form: $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$. Center $(h, k)$, radius $r$. General form $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ is less useful for graphing.
  • Sec 12.5Complete the square separately for $x$-terms and $y$-terms to convert general form to standard form. When a leading coefficient is not 1, divide first. Add the completed values to both sides, multiplied by any factor in front.
  • Sec 12.6A parabola is equidistant from a focus and directrix. Standard forms use $4p$, where $p$ is the vertex-to-focus distance. Sign of $p$ determines opening direction. Parabolic shapes focus parallel rays at the focus.
  • Sec 12.7 – 12.8An ellipse has a constant sum of distances to two foci. Larger denominator ($a^2$) identifies the major axis direction. $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$ (foci inside, $c < a$). Applications include planetary orbits and whispering galleries.
  • Sec 12.9 – 12.10A hyperbola has a constant absolute difference of distances. Two branches open toward the positive term's axis. $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ (foci outside, $c > a$). Asymptotes pass through the center; use the central rectangle to graph them.
  • Sec 12.11From $Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$: $A = C$ → circle; same sign, $A \neq C$ → ellipse; opposite signs → hyperbola; one coefficient is zero → parabola. Complete the square to convert to standard form and find features.
  • Sec 12.12Conics model planetary orbits (Kepler's first law, ellipses), parabolic reflectors (satellite dishes, telescopes), navigation systems (hyperbolas, LORAN), architectural arches (parabolas), and elliptical whispering galleries. Eccentricity $e = c/a$ measures elongation.
  • Sec 12.13 – 12.14Common mistakes involve sign errors in centers, unbalanced completing-the-square with leading coefficients, swapped ellipse/hyperbola formulas, and misidentifying major axis direction or hyperbola opening. Precalculus extends conics to parametric, polar, and rotated forms.

Key Terms Review

conic section
A curve formed by the intersection of a plane with a double cone. Includes circles, parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas.
focus / foci
Fixed point(s) used in the geometric definition of parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas.
directrix
A fixed line used together with the focus to define a parabola.
circle
The set of all points equidistant from a fixed center. Equation: $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$.
radius
The fixed distance from the center of a circle to any point on it.
parabola
The set of all points equidistant from a focus and a directrix.
vertex (parabola)
The turning point of a parabola; the midpoint between focus and directrix.
p (parabola)
The distance from the vertex to the focus; also the distance from the vertex to the directrix.
ellipse
The set of all points for which the sum of distances to two foci is constant ($= 2a$).
major axis
The longer axis of an ellipse, running through both foci and both vertices.
minor axis
The shorter axis of an ellipse, perpendicular to the major axis at the center.
co-vertex
An endpoint of the minor axis of an ellipse.
hyperbola
The set of all points for which the absolute difference of distances to two foci is constant ($= 2a$).
transverse axis
The axis of a hyperbola connecting its two vertices; lies along the direction the hyperbola opens.
asymptote
A line that a branch of a hyperbola approaches but never reaches; each hyperbola has two.
eccentricity
$e = c/a$; measures how elongated an ellipse is. $e = 0$ is a circle; $e \to 1$ is a very elongated ellipse.
standard form
A form of the conic equation that displays center (or vertex), $a$, $b$, $r$, and $p$ directly.
general form
$Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$; the conic type is identified by comparing $A$ and $C$.
completing the square
An algebraic technique for rewriting a quadratic expression as a perfect square trinomial; used to convert general form to standard form.

Bridge Note — Looking Ahead to Chapter 13
Chapter 13: Trigonometric Foundations

You are now prepared to move into Trigonometric Foundations. Trigonometry and conic sections are deeply connected in four important ways.

The unit circle. In Section 12.3, you wrote the standard equation of any circle as $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$. The unit circle is the special case $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ — a circle centered at the origin with radius 1. Chapter 13 introduces this circle as the foundation for defining sine and cosine for all angles.

Parametric equations of conics. The ellipse $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ becomes $x = a\cos\theta$, $y = b\sin\theta$ in parametric form. For a circle, this simplifies to $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$. Trigonometric functions allow you to trace each conic curve as a point moves through angle values.

Polar equations of conics. All four conic sections unify into a single polar equation involving eccentricity — an elegant formula you cannot fully appreciate without both this chapter's coordinate geometry and Chapter 13's trigonometry.

Kepler's laws and calculus. In the MGU Calculus course, you will prove — using calculus, vectors, and trigonometry — why gravitational attraction causes planets to follow elliptical orbits. The ellipse you studied algebraically here reappears as the mathematically inevitable consequence of an inverse-square force law.

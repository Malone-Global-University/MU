Chapter 3 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Quadratic functions appeared in Algebra I as the second major function family after linear functions. You learned to graph parabolas, identify the vertex, find x-intercepts, and use the quadratic formula to solve equations. That foundation was solid, but it was also introductory.

In Algebra II, the goal is fluency. You should not only be able to solve a quadratic equation — you should understand why different equation forms exist, when each form is most useful, how the shape of a parabola is connected to its equation, and what it means when a quadratic equation has no real solutions. You should be able to move between a graph, a table, and an equation with confidence, and you should be able to use quadratic functions to model real situations involving area, projectile motion, revenue, and optimization.

This chapter also serves as a bridge. Once you understand that some quadratic equations have no real solutions, you will be ready to ask: what kinds of numbers do those solutions involve? That question will open the door to complex numbers in Chapter 4, and it will set the stage for the study of polynomial functions in Chapter 5.

Opening Question

A company produces a product and knows from market research that its weekly revenue depends on the price it charges per unit. The revenue function is \( R(p) = -50p^2 + 800p \), where \( p \) is the price in dollars and \( R(p) \) is the weekly revenue in dollars.

At what price does the company earn the most revenue? What is that maximum revenue? And below what price does the company earn nothing at all? These questions require everything this chapter covers: recognizing a quadratic function, identifying its vertex, understanding what the zeros mean, and choosing the right form or method for the situation.

Why This Chapter Matters

Quadratic functions are not only a topic in algebra. They appear throughout mathematics, science, engineering, economics, and design. In physics, the height of a projectile under gravity is a quadratic function of time. In economics, profit and revenue functions are often modeled as quadratics. In geometry, area calculations lead to quadratic expressions. In engineering, structural arches and satellite dishes follow parabolic shapes. In calculus, understanding quadratics deeply makes the transition into derivatives and optimization far easier.

Beyond applications, quadratic functions introduce a fundamental idea that runs through all of Algebra II: a single mathematical object — a function — can be written in multiple forms, and each form reveals something different about the function's behavior. Learning to move between forms is one of the most transferable skills in algebra.

Section 3.1

Reviewing Quadratic Functions

What a Quadratic Function Is

A quadratic function is a function that can be written in the form \( f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \), where \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) are real numbers and \( a \neq 0 \). The condition \( a \neq 0 \) is essential: if \( a = 0 \), the \( x^2 \) term disappears and the function becomes linear, not quadratic.

The graph of a quadratic function is always a parabola — a smooth, symmetric, U-shaped curve. Parabolas open upward when \( a > 0 \) and downward when \( a < 0 \).

Three Critical Features of Every Parabola

Vertex: the turning point — either the lowest point (parabola opens up) or the highest point (parabola opens down).

Axis of symmetry: the vertical line \( x = h \) passing through the vertex, dividing the parabola into two mirror-image halves.

Direction of opening: determined entirely by the sign of \( a \). Positive \( a \) → opens up. Negative \( a \) → opens down.

Diagram Instruction

Show the parabola opening upward and the line of symmetry as a dashed vertical line through the vertex.

The same function can look like any of these:

One Function, Three Forms

\( f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 3 \)    (standard form)

\( f(x) = (x - 2)^2 - 1 \)    (vertex form)

\( f(x) = (x - 1)(x - 3) \)    (factored form)

All three equations describe exactly the same function and the same parabola. Each form makes different information easy to read.

Section 3.2

Standard Form

The standard form of a quadratic function is \( f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \), where \( a \neq 0 \). This form shows the y-intercept immediately — because \( f(0) = c \), the y-intercept is always the point \( (0, c) \). Standard form also makes the coefficients \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) available for the quadratic formula.

What standard form does not show directly is the vertex. To find it, use the vertex formula: \( x = -\dfrac{b}{2a} \), then substitute to find \( y \).

Example 3.2.1
Finding vertex, intercepts, and axis of symmetry from standard form
  • ProblemGiven \( f(x) = 2x^2 - 12x + 10 \), find the vertex, axis of symmetry, y-intercept, and x-intercepts.
  • ThinkThe function is in standard form with \( a = 2 \), \( b = -12 \), \( c = 10 \). Since \( a > 0 \), the parabola opens upward. I will find the vertex using \( x = -\dfrac{b}{2a} \), find the y-intercept from \( c \), and find the x-intercepts by setting \( f(x) = 0 \) and factoring.
  • Solve x-coord of vertex: \( x = -\dfrac{-12}{2(2)} = \dfrac{12}{4} = 3 \) y-coord: \( f(3) = 2(9) - 12(3) + 10 = 18 - 36 + 10 = -8 \)   →   Vertex: \( (3, -8) \) Axis of symmetry: \( x = 3 \) y-intercept: \( c = 10 \)   →   \( (0, 10) \) x-intercepts: \( 2x^2 - 12x + 10 = 0 \; \Rightarrow \; x^2 - 6x + 5 = 0 \; \Rightarrow \; (x-1)(x-5) = 0 \) x-intercepts: \( (1, 0) \) and \( (5, 0) \)
  • CheckThe vertex must lie halfway between the x-intercepts. Midpoint of \( x = 1 \) and \( x = 5 \): \( \dfrac{1+5}{2} = 3 \). ✓
Answer: Vertex \( (3, -8) \); axis of symmetry \( x = 3 \); y-intercept \( (0, 10) \); x-intercepts \( (1, 0) \) and \( (5, 0) \).

Section 3.3

Vertex Form

The vertex form of a quadratic function is \( f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k \), where \( (h, k) \) is the vertex of the parabola. The vertex is read directly from the equation without any calculation. Vertex form is ideal for graphing and for understanding transformations.

Student Warning

Vertex form contains \( (x - h)^2 \), not \( (x + h)^2 \). If the function is \( f(x) = 2(x - 3)^2 + 5 \), the vertex is at \( (3, 5) \), not \( (-3, 5) \). The vertex is where the squared quantity equals zero: \( (x - 3)^2 = 0 \) gives \( x = 3 \). The subtraction shifts the graph to the right — which feels counterintuitive but is always correct.

Given \( f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k \)How to Find It
Vertex\( (h, k) \) — read directly
Axis of symmetry\( x = h \)
DirectionUp if \( a > 0 \); down if \( a < 0 \)
WidthNarrower if \( |a| > 1 \); wider if \( 0 < |a| < 1 \)
Minimum/Maximum\( k \) is the minimum if \( a > 0 \); maximum if \( a < 0 \)
Example 3.3.1
Converting vertex form to standard form
  • ProblemConvert \( f(x) = 3(x - 2)^2 - 7 \) to standard form.
  • ThinkExpand \( (x-2)^2 \), multiply through by 3, and collect constant terms.
  • Solve \( f(x) = 3(x - 2)^2 - 7 \) \( = 3(x^2 - 4x + 4) - 7 \) \( = 3x^2 - 12x + 12 - 7 \) \( = 3x^2 - 12x + 5 \)
  • CheckVertex from vertex form: \( (2, -7) \). From standard form: \( x = -\dfrac{-12}{6} = 2 \); \( f(2) = 12 - 24 + 5 = -7 \). ✓
Answer: \( f(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 5 \)
Example 3.3.2
Writing an equation from a graph using vertex form
  • ProblemA parabola has vertex \( (-1, 4) \), opens downward, and passes through the point \( (1, -4) \). Write the equation in vertex form.
  • ThinkStart with \( f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k = a(x + 1)^2 + 4 \). Use the given point to solve for \( a \).
  • Solve Substitute \( (1, -4) \): \( -4 = a(1 + 1)^2 + 4 = 4a + 4 \) \( -8 = 4a \;  \Rightarrow \;  a = -2 \)
  • Check\( f(1) = -2(2)^2 + 4 = -8 + 4 = -4 \). ✓
Answer: \( f(x) = -2(x + 1)^2 + 4 \)

Section 3.4

Factored Form

The factored form of a quadratic function is \( f(x) = a(x - r_1)(x - r_2) \), where \( r_1 \) and \( r_2 \) are the zeros of the function — the values of \( x \) where \( f(x) = 0 \) — and \( a \) is the leading coefficient. This form makes the x-intercepts immediately visible.

Because a parabola is symmetric, the axis of symmetry always passes through the midpoint of the two zeros. The x-coordinate of the vertex is therefore \( h = \dfrac{r_1 + r_2}{2} \).

Example 3.4.1
Reading information from factored form
  • ProblemGiven \( f(x) = -2(x - 1)(x - 5) \), find the zeros, vertex, axis of symmetry, and y-intercept.
  • ThinkThe zeros are directly visible. I will find the axis as the midpoint of the zeros, compute the vertex by substitution, and find the y-intercept at \( f(0) \).
  • Solve Zeros: \( x = 1 \) and \( x = 5 \)   →   x-intercepts \( (1, 0) \) and \( (5, 0) \) Axis of symmetry: \( x = \dfrac{1 + 5}{2} = 3 \) Vertex: \( f(3) = -2(3-1)(3-5) = -2(2)(-2) = 8 \)   →   \( (3, 8) \) y-intercept: \( f(0) = -2(-1)(-5) = -10 \)   →   \( (0, -10) \)
  • CheckSince \( a = -2 < 0 \), the parabola opens downward and the vertex \( (3, 8) \) is a maximum. ✓
Answer: Zeros at \( (1, 0) \) and \( (5, 0) \); vertex \( (3, 8) \); axis \( x = 3 \); y-intercept \( (0, -10) \); opens downward.
Example 3.4.2
Converting factored form to standard form
  • ProblemWrite \( f(x) = 3(x + 2)(x - 4) \) in standard form.
  • Solve \( f(x) = 3(x + 2)(x - 4) = 3(x^2 - 2x - 8) = 3x^2 - 6x - 24 \)
Answer: \( f(x) = 3x^2 - 6x - 24 \)

Section 3.5

Transformations of Parabolas

The simplest quadratic function is the parent function \( y = x^2 \) — a parabola with vertex at the origin, opening upward, symmetric about the y-axis. Every other quadratic function is a transformation of this parent.

TransformationEquationEffect on Graph
Vertical shift\( y = x^2 + k \)Up if \( k > 0 \); down if \( k < 0 \). Vertex moves to \( (0, k) \).
Horizontal shift\( y = (x - h)^2 \)Right if \( h > 0 \); left if \( h < 0 \). Vertex moves to \( (h, 0) \).
Reflection\( y = -x^2 \)Flips parabola over x-axis (opens downward).
Vertical stretch\( y = ax^2 \), \( |a| > 1 \)Narrower than parent function.
Vertical compression\( y = ax^2 \), \( 0 < |a| < 1 \)Wider than parent function.
Student Warning — Horizontal Shifts Feel Backward

In \( y = (x - 3)^2 \), the minus sign appears to shift left, but the graph shifts right. Reason: the vertex occurs where the squared quantity equals zero. \( (x - 3)^2 = 0 \) gives \( x = 3 \) — three units to the right. Always solve for where the squared expression equals zero to find the vertex x-coordinate.

Example 3.5.1
Describing and identifying transformations
  • ProblemDescribe the transformations applied to \( y = x^2 \) to obtain \( f(x) = -3(x + 2)^2 + 5 \), and identify the vertex.
  • ThinkCompare to \( f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k \). Here \( a = -3 \), \( h = -2 \), \( k = 5 \).
  • Solve \( a = -3 \): opens downward (reflection); vertically stretched by factor 3 (narrower). \( h = -2 \): horizontal shift 2 units to the left. \( k = 5 \): vertical shift 5 units up. Vertex: \( (-2, 5) \).
Answer: Starting from \( y = x^2 \): reflect over x-axis and stretch by factor 3, shift 2 left, shift 5 up. Vertex \( (-2, 5) \).

Show with arrows the horizontal shift (2 left) and vertical shift (5 up), and indicate that the parabola is reflected and narrower.

Section 3.6

Completing the Square

To convert from standard form to vertex form, you use a technique called completing the square. The idea: any quadratic expression \( x^2 + bx \) can be made into a perfect square trinomial by adding the right constant. A perfect square trinomial factors as the square of a binomial: \( (x + m)^2 = x^2 + 2mx + m^2 \). The constant \( m^2 \) is always the square of half the coefficient of \( x \).

How to Complete the Square

To complete the square on \( x^2 + bx \):

1. Take half the coefficient of \( x \):   \( \dfrac{b}{2} \)

2. Square it:   \( \left(\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2 \)

3. Add and subtract this value to maintain equality.

Example 3.6.1
Completing the square — leading coefficient 1
  • ProblemWrite \( f(x) = x^2 - 6x + 2 \) in vertex form.
  • ThinkComplete the square on the \( x^2 - 6x \) part. Add and subtract \( \left(\dfrac{-6}{2}\right)^2 = 9 \).
  • Solve \( f(x) = x^2 - 6x + 2 \) \( = (x^2 - 6x + 9) - 9 + 2 \) \( = (x - 3)^2 - 7 \)
  • CheckExpand: \( (x-3)^2 - 7 = x^2 - 6x + 9 - 7 = x^2 - 6x + 2 \). ✓
Answer: \( f(x) = (x - 3)^2 - 7 \); vertex \( (3, -7) \).
Example 3.6.2
Completing the square — leading coefficient not 1
  • ProblemWrite \( f(x) = 2x^2 + 8x - 3 \) in vertex form.
  • ThinkWhen \( a \neq 1 \), factor \( a \) out of the variable terms first, then complete the square inside.
  • Solve Step 1: Factor 2 out of the first two terms only. \( f(x) = 2(x^2 + 4x) - 3 \) Step 2: Half of 4 is 2; \( 2^2 = 4 \). Add and subtract 4 inside the parentheses. \( = 2(x^2 + 4x + 4 - 4) - 3 \) \( = 2\bigl[(x + 2)^2 - 4\bigr] - 3 \) Step 3: Distribute the 2. \( = 2(x + 2)^2 - 8 - 3 = 2(x + 2)^2 - 11 \)
  • CheckExpand: \( 2(x^2 + 4x + 4) - 11 = 2x^2 + 8x + 8 - 11 = 2x^2 + 8x - 3 \). ✓
Answer: \( f(x) = 2(x + 2)^2 - 11 \); vertex \( (-2, -11) \).
Student Warning

When you factor \( a \) out of the variable terms, do not factor it out of the constant \( c \). The constant stays outside the factored expression. Factoring \( a \) from all three terms produces an incorrect result. Only the terms containing \( x \) are factored.

Section 3.7

Deriving the Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula is one of the most important results in algebra. It comes directly from completing the square on the general standard form equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \). Understanding the derivation — not just memorizing the result — reveals what the formula means and why it always works.

Full Derivation — Completing the Square on \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \)
Step 1: Move the constant.   \( ax^2 + bx = -c \)
Step 2: Divide by \( a \).   \( x^2 + \dfrac{b}{a}x = -\dfrac{c}{a} \)
Step 3: Add \( \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} \) to both sides to complete the square.
\( x^2 + \dfrac{b}{a}x + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} = -\dfrac{c}{a} + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} \)
Step 4: Write left side as a squared binomial.   \( \left(x + \dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \dfrac{b^2 - 4ac}{4a^2} \)
Step 5: Take the square root of both sides.   \( x + \dfrac{b}{2a} = \pm\dfrac{\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)
Step 6: Solve for \( x \).   \[\boxed{x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}}\]
What the Formula Reveals

Notice that \( -\dfrac{b}{2a} \) is the x-coordinate of the vertex. The \( \pm\sqrt{\ldots} \) part measures how far the zeros are from the vertex. When the expression under the radical is zero, the vertex touches the x-axis. When it is negative, there are no real solutions — the parabola does not cross the x-axis.

Example 3.7.1
Using the quadratic formula
  • ProblemSolve \( 3x^2 - 5x - 2 = 0 \) using the quadratic formula.
  • ThinkIdentify \( a = 3 \), \( b = -5 \), \( c = -2 \). Substitute into the formula.
  • Solve \( x = \dfrac{-(-5) \pm \sqrt{(-5)^2 - 4(3)(-2)}}{2(3)} = \dfrac{5 \pm \sqrt{25 + 24}}{6} = \dfrac{5 \pm \sqrt{49}}{6} = \dfrac{5 \pm 7}{6} \) \( x = \dfrac{12}{6} = 2 \quad \text{or} \quad x = \dfrac{-2}{6} = -\dfrac{1}{3} \)
  • Check\( f(2) = 12 - 10 - 2 = 0 \) ✓    \( f(-\frac{1}{3}) = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{5}{3} - 2 = 2 - 2 = 0 \) ✓
Answer: \( x = 2 \) or \( x = -\dfrac{1}{3} \)

Section 3.8

The Discriminant

The expression under the square root in the quadratic formula has a name: \( \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \), called the discriminant. It distinguishes among three different situations — predicting the type and number of solutions without requiring you to solve the full equation.

\( \Delta > 0 \)TwoTwo distinct real solutionsParabola crosses x-axis at two points
\( \Delta = 0 \)OneOne real solution (repeated)Vertex touches x-axis exactly once
\( \Delta < 0 \)None (in reals)Two nonreal (complex) solutionsParabola does not cross x-axis

Example 3.8.1
Using the discriminant to classify solutions
  • ProblemWithout solving, determine the number and type of solutions for each equation: (a) \( 2x^2 - 3x + 1 = 0 \)   (b) \( x^2 - 4x + 4 = 0 \)   (c) \( x^2 + 2x + 5 = 0 \)
  • Solve (a) \( \Delta = (-3)^2 - 4(2)(1) = 9 - 8 = 1 > 0 \)    →    Two distinct real solutions. (b) \( \Delta = (-4)^2 - 4(1)(4) = 16 - 16 = 0 \)    →    One repeated real solution (vertex on x-axis). (c) \( \Delta = 2^2 - 4(1)(5) = 4 - 20 = -16 < 0 \)    →    No real solutions (two complex solutions).
Answer: (a) Two real solutions. (b) One real solution. (c) No real solutions — complex.

Section 3.9

Real and Nonreal Solutions

When the discriminant is negative, the square root in the quadratic formula produces \( \sqrt{\text{negative number}} \). In the real number system, square roots of negative numbers do not exist. This is not a computation error — it is a genuine limitation of the real numbers.

Does this mean the equation has no solutions at all? No — it means it has no real solutions. In Chapter 4, you will learn that \( \sqrt{-16} = 4i \), where \( i = \sqrt{-1} \) is the imaginary unit. The solutions are complex numbers, not real numbers.

For now, the key skill is recognizing this situation: when the discriminant is negative, report that there are no real solutions. Do not write a nonsense expression involving \( \sqrt{\text{negative number}} \) as though it were a real answer.

Example 3.9.1
Recognizing nonreal solutions
  • ProblemSolve \( x^2 + 4x + 8 = 0 \). Describe the nature of the solutions.
  • ThinkCompute the discriminant first.
  • Solve \( \Delta = 4^2 - 4(1)(8) = 16 - 32 = -16 \) The discriminant is negative. No real solutions exist. Preview of Chapter 4: \( x = \dfrac{-4 \pm \sqrt{-16}}{2} = \dfrac{-4 \pm 4i}{2} = -2 \pm 2i \)
Answer: No real solutions. The solutions are complex: \( x = -2 + 2i \) and \( x = -2 - 2i \). These will be studied fully in Chapter 4.

Section 3.10

Maximum and Minimum Problems

Because the vertex is either the highest or the lowest point on a parabola, it represents the maximum or minimum value of the quadratic function. This makes quadratic functions the natural model for any situation involving optimization — finding the best possible output under a given constraint.

When \( a > 0 \): the vertex is a minimum.    When \( a < 0 \): the vertex is a maximum.

Example 3.10.1
Maximum revenue
  • ProblemA company's weekly revenue is modeled by \( R(p) = -50p^2 + 800p \), where \( p \) is the price per unit in dollars. At what price is revenue maximized, and what is the maximum revenue?
  • ThinkSince \( a = -50 < 0 \), the parabola opens downward and the vertex is a maximum. I will find the vertex using the standard form vertex formula.
  • Solve \( p = -\dfrac{800}{2(-50)} = -\dfrac{800}{-100} = 8 \) \( R(8) = -50(64) + 800(8) = -3200 + 6400 = 3200 \)
  • Check\( R(7) = -50(49) + 5600 = 3150 < 3200 \) ✓    \( R(9) = -50(81) + 7200 = 3150 < 3200 \) ✓
Answer: Maximum revenue of $3,200 is achieved at a price of $8.00 per unit.
Example 3.10.2
Maximum area with fixed fencing
  • ProblemA farmer has 200 meters of fencing to enclose a rectangular garden along a barn wall. The barn provides one side; only three sides need fencing. Find the dimensions that maximize the area.
  • ThinkLet \( w \) = width (the two sides perpendicular to the barn). Then length along barn = \( 200 - 2w \). Area: \( A(w) = w(200 - 2w) = 200w - 2w^2 \). This is a downward parabola — find the vertex.
  • Solve \( w = -\dfrac{200}{2(-2)} = 50 \) \( A(50) = 200(50) - 2(2500) = 10000 - 5000 = 5000 \text{ m}^2 \) Length along barn: \( 200 - 2(50) = 100 \) m
  • Check\( A(49) = 49(102) = 4998 < 5000 \) ✓
Answer: Dimensions 50 m × 100 m produce the maximum area of 5,000 m².

Section 3.11

Quadratic Models

When a real-world relationship produces a parabolic shape — output increases, reaches a peak, then decreases (or decreases to a minimum and rises) — a quadratic function is the appropriate model. Quadratic models appear in physics (projectile motion under gravity), economics (revenue and profit), geometry (area with a fixed perimeter), biology (short-interval population growth), and engineering (parabolic arch and cable shapes).

Example 3.11.1
Projectile motion model
  • ProblemA rock is thrown upward from a cliff. Its height above the ground (in meters) after \( t \) seconds is \( h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 19.6t + 44.1 \). (a) What is the maximum height? (b) When does the rock reach the ground?
  • Think(a) Maximum occurs at the vertex. (b) Rock reaches ground when \( h(t) = 0 \).
  • Solve (a) \( t = -\dfrac{19.6}{2(-4.9)} = 2 \) s    \( h(2) = -19.6 + 39.2 + 44.1 = 63.7 \text{ m} \) (b) Set \( h(t) = 0 \). Divide by \( -4.9 \): \( t^2 - 4t - 9 = 0 \) \( t = \dfrac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 + 36}}{2} = \dfrac{4 \pm \sqrt{52}}{2} \approx \dfrac{4 \pm 7.21}{2} \) Positive solution: \( t \approx 5.61 \) s    (discard the negative time value)
Answer: (a) Maximum height of 63.7 meters at \( t = 2 \) seconds. (b) Rock hits ground at approximately \( t \approx 5.61 \) seconds.
Student Tip — Domain Restrictions

In projectile problems, always check that your time values are non-negative. The model is only valid for times after the object is launched. Discard any negative time solution without apology — it does not represent a physical event.

Section 3.13

Common Advanced Quadratic Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Misreading the vertex from vertex form
Vertex of \( f(x) = (x + 3)^2 - 5 \) is reported as \( (3, -5) \)

Correct: \( (x + 3) = (x - (-3)) \), so \( h = -3 \). The vertex is \( (-3, -5) \). Always solve \( (x - h) = 0 \) to find \( h \).

Mistake 2 — Forgetting to balance when completing the square
Writing \( x^2 + 6x + 9 \) after adding 9, without subtracting 9 elsewhere

Correct: \( x^2 + 6x = (x^2 + 6x + 9) - 9 = (x + 3)^2 - 9 \). Every addition must be balanced by an equal subtraction.

Mistake 3 — Not factoring out \( a \) before completing the square
Completing the square on \( 2x^2 + 8x \) without first factoring out 2

Correct: \( 2x^2 + 8x = 2(x^2 + 4x) = 2(x^2 + 4x + 4 - 4) = 2(x + 2)^2 - 8 \). Factor out \( a \) from variable terms first; the constant stays outside.

Mistake 4 — Dropping the ± symbol in the quadratic formula
Writing only \( x = \dfrac{-b + \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \) and reporting one answer

Correct: The ± sign produces two solutions. Compute both \( \dfrac{-b + \sqrt{\ldots}}{2a} \) and \( \dfrac{-b - \sqrt{\ldots}}{2a} \) and report each.

Mistake 5 — Accepting a negative time solution in projectile problems
Reporting both solutions of a time equation, including a negative time value

Correct: Physical models restrict the domain. In projectile motion, \( t \geq 0 \). Discard any negative time solution — it is not physically meaningful.

Mistake 6 — Saying "no solutions" when discriminant is negative
"The equation \( x^2 + 4x + 5 = 0 \) has no solutions."

Correct: It has no real solutions. It has two complex (nonreal) solutions. Always say "no real solutions" — the distinction matters, and Chapter 4 will find those solutions.

Mistake 7 — Finding only the vertex x-coordinate
Reporting the "vertex" as \( x = 3 \) without computing \( f(3) \)

Correct: The vertex is an ordered pair \( (h, k) \). Always compute both coordinates: find \( x = -b/(2a) \), then substitute to find \( y = f(x) \).

Mistake 8 — Sign errors when applying the quadratic formula
For \( -3x^2 + 5x - 2 = 0 \), writing \( -b = 5 \) instead of \( -b = -(5) = -5 \)

Correct: Write the formula substituting values explicitly, including all signs, before simplifying. \( a = -3 \), \( b = 5 \), so \( -b = -5 \).

Section 3.14

Preparing for Complex Numbers

This chapter explored quadratic functions from multiple directions: graph shape, equation forms, transformations, completing the square, the quadratic formula, the discriminant, optimization, and modeling. You learned that some quadratic equations have two real solutions, some have one, and some have none in the real number system.

This last case — the case of a negative discriminant — raises an urgent mathematical question: if the equation has no real solutions, what kind of solutions does it have? Mathematics does not simply leave that question unanswered. It expands the number system.

Consider the equation \( x^2 + 1 = 0 \). Solving algebraically gives \( x = \pm\sqrt{-1} \). In the real numbers, \( \sqrt{-1} \) does not exist. But mathematicians defined a new number, called \( i \), to be exactly this value: \( i = \sqrt{-1} \). With this definition, \( x = i \) and \( x = -i \) are both solutions. These are complex numbers — a new number system that contains the real numbers as a subset.

Chapter 4 will build this system from the ground up: defining \( i \), performing all four operations with complex numbers, interpreting them geometrically, and revisiting quadratic equations to find all their solutions — real or complex. The bridge is direct: every quadratic equation that this chapter left "unsolvable in the reals" will be solved fully once you have complex numbers.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 3.1A quadratic function \( f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \) (\( a \neq 0 \)) has a parabolic graph. Key features: vertex, axis of symmetry, direction of opening (sign of \( a \)), and intercepts.
  • Sec 3.2Standard form \( ax^2 + bx + c \) reveals the y-intercept directly as \( c \). The vertex x-coordinate is \( x = -b/(2a) \). Standard form is required for the quadratic formula.
  • Sec 3.3Vertex form \( a(x-h)^2 + k \) reveals the vertex \( (h, k) \) directly. Watch the sign of \( h \). Best for graphing and transformations.
  • Sec 3.4Factored form \( a(x-r_1)(x-r_2) \) reveals the zeros directly. The vertex lies at the midpoint of the zeros: \( x = (r_1 + r_2)/2 \).
  • Sec 3.5All quadratic functions are transformations of the parent \( y = x^2 \). The parameters \( a \), \( h \), and \( k \) in vertex form control stretch/reflection, horizontal shift, and vertical shift respectively.
  • Sec 3.6Completing the square converts standard form to vertex form by adding and subtracting \( (b/2)^2 \). When \( a \neq 1 \), factor \( a \) from the variable terms first.
  • Sec 3.7The quadratic formula \( x = (-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac})/(2a) \) is derived by completing the square on the general form. It solves any quadratic equation.
  • Sec 3.8The discriminant \( \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \) predicts solutions: \( \Delta > 0 \) (two real), \( \Delta = 0 \) (one real), \( \Delta < 0 \) (no real solutions).
  • Sec 3.9A negative discriminant means no real solutions — not "no solutions." The equation has complex (nonreal) solutions, which Chapter 4 studies fully.
  • Sec 3.10The vertex gives the maximum (\( a < 0 \)) or minimum (\( a > 0 \)) value of any quadratic model. Optimization problems find the input and output at the vertex.
  • Sec 3.11Quadratic models describe projectile motion, revenue, area, and many real-world relationships. Always define variables, build the function, use the vertex or zeros, and check the domain.
  • Sec 3.12No single form is always best. Standard form for formula use, vertex form for graphing and optimization, factored form for zeros. Flexibility across all three is the core skill.
  • Sec 3.13Eight common errors: misreading vertex sign, unbalanced completing the square, forgetting to factor \( a \), dropping ±, ignoring domain restrictions, misidentifying "no solutions," reporting only x-coordinate of vertex, sign errors in formula.
  • Sec 3.14A negative discriminant signals that solutions involve \( \sqrt{-1} \), opening the door to complex numbers. Chapter 4 builds the full complex number system from this need.

Key Terms Review

quadratic function
A function of the form \( f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \), \( a \neq 0 \). Its graph is a parabola.
parabola
The smooth, symmetric U-shaped curve that is the graph of every quadratic function.
vertex
The highest or lowest point on a parabola. The turning point where the function changes direction.
axis of symmetry
The vertical line \( x = h \) passing through the vertex, dividing the parabola into two mirror halves.
standard form
\( f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \). Shows the y-intercept as \( c \). Required for the quadratic formula.
vertex form
\( f(x) = a(x-h)^2 + k \). Vertex \( (h, k) \) is immediately visible. Best for graphing and transformations.
factored form
\( f(x) = a(x-r_1)(x-r_2) \). Zeros \( r_1 \) and \( r_2 \) are immediately visible.
completing the square
A method of rewriting a quadratic expression to create a perfect square trinomial, used to convert standard form to vertex form and to derive the quadratic formula.
perfect square trinomial
A trinomial that factors as the square of a binomial: \( x^2 + 2mx + m^2 = (x + m)^2 \).
quadratic formula
\( x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \). Solves any quadratic equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \).
discriminant
\( \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \). Determines the number and type of solutions: positive (two real), zero (one real), negative (no real).
zero of a function
A value of \( x \) for which \( f(x) = 0 \). Corresponds to an x-intercept on the graph.
optimization
Finding the maximum or minimum value of a function. For quadratics, the vertex provides the optimal value.
nonreal solution
A solution that involves the square root of a negative number. Occurs when the discriminant is negative. Chapter 4 studies these as complex numbers.

Bridge Note — Looking Ahead
Chapter 4: Complex Numbers

In this chapter, you encountered quadratic equations with a negative discriminant — equations that produce a square root of a negative number in the quadratic formula. In the real number system, these equations have no solutions.

But mathematics does not stop there. Chapter 4 introduces the complex number system, which begins with a single powerful definition: \( i = \sqrt{-1} \). With this one extension, every quadratic equation — regardless of its discriminant — has solutions. The equation \( x^2 + 2x + 5 = 0 \) has solutions \( x = -1 + 2i \) and \( x = -1 - 2i \). These numbers are not on the real number line, but they are genuine, well-defined mathematical objects with clear algebraic rules.

Complex numbers are not a curiosity — they appear in electrical engineering, signal processing, quantum mechanics, control systems, and advanced mathematics. Chapter 4 will build this system from the ground up: defining \( i \), performing all four operations, interpreting complex numbers geometrically on the complex plane, and revisiting quadratic equations to find all their solutions. The quadratic formula already contains the structure of complex numbers. Chapter 4 reveals what that structure means.

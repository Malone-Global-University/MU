Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 8 extends the idea of radicals from expressions into functions, graphs, equations, and models. In Algebra I, you learned to simplify square roots, work with cube roots, and apply radicals to geometry through the Pythagorean theorem. In this chapter, those ideas grow into a broader framework that connects roots, powers, and inverse relationships.

The central insight of this chapter is that radicals and rational exponents are two notations for the same mathematical idea. A square root is a one-half power. A cube root is a one-third power. Recognizing this equivalence allows you to apply exponent rules to radicals, graph radical functions as transformations, solve equations involving roots, and interpret radical models in science, geometry, and engineering.

This chapter also introduces a critical habit: checking for extraneous solutions. When solving radical equations, the algebraic process can introduce false answers that must be identified and rejected. This kind of careful verification is not just a technique for radical equations — it reflects the broader mathematical discipline of asking whether an answer actually satisfies the original conditions.

Opening Question

A structural engineer is designing a cable support for a suspension bridge. The natural frequency at which the cable vibrates depends on the tension in the cable and the cable's mass per unit length. The formula involves a square root. The engineer needs to know: if the tension in a cable is quadrupled, what happens to the frequency? To answer this question, you need to understand how radical functions behave, how they respond to changes in their inputs, and how to reason about their outputs without computing every value numerically. By the end of this chapter, you will have exactly those tools.

Section 8.1

Reviewing Radicals

What a Radical Expression Is

A radical expression is any expression that contains a root symbol, called the radical sign: \(\sqrt{\phantom{x}}\). The number or expression under the radical sign is called the radicand. The small number written above and to the left of the radical sign is called the index, indicating which root is being taken.

When no index is written, the index is understood to be 2, meaning a square root.

\[\sqrt[n]{a}\]

In this expression, \(n\) is the index and \(a\) is the radicand.

Square Roots

The square root of a number \(a\) is the number whose square equals \(a\): \(\sqrt{a} = b\) means \(b^2 = a\). Every positive real number has two square roots — one positive and one negative — but the symbol \(\sqrt{a}\) refers specifically to the principal square root, meaning the non-negative square root.

\[\sqrt{25} = 5 \qquad \text{(not } -5\text{)}\]
Student Warning

The expression \(-\sqrt{81}\) is not the same as \(\sqrt{-81}\). The first applies a negative sign to the principal square root of 81, giving \(-9\). The second asks for the square root of a negative number, which is not a real number.

Cube Roots

The cube root of a number \(a\) is the number whose cube equals \(a\). Unlike square roots, cube roots are defined for all real numbers, including negatives, because \((-2)^3 = -8\).

\[\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2\]
Key Distinction

Even-indexed radicals (square root, fourth root, …) require non-negative radicands for a real result. Odd-indexed radicals (cube root, fifth root, …) accept any real number as the radicand.

Example 8.1.1
Evaluating Square and Cube Roots
  • ProblemEvaluate (a) \(\sqrt{144}\)   (b) \(\sqrt[3]{27}\)   (c) \(-\sqrt{81}\)   (d) \(\sqrt[3]{-125}\)
  • ThinkIdentify the root and check whether the radicand is a perfect square or cube. Apply the definition directly.
  • Solve (a) \(\sqrt{144} = 12\), because \(12^2 = 144\).
    (b) \(\sqrt[3]{27} = 3\), because \(3^3 = 27\).
    (c) \(-\sqrt{81} = -9\). The negative sign is outside the radical; the principal root is 9, then the sign is applied.
    (d) \(\sqrt[3]{-125} = -5\), because \((-5)^3 = -125\).
  • CheckVerify by squaring or cubing each result.
Answer: 12  ·  3  ·  −9  ·  −5
Section 8.2

nth Roots

The nth root of a number \(a\), written \(\sqrt[n]{a}\), is the number \(b\) such that \(b^n = a\). This extends naturally to any positive integer index greater than 1.

\[\sqrt[n]{a} = b \quad \text{means} \quad b^n = a\]
ExpressionReads asResultCheck
\(\sqrt[4]{16}\)fourth root of 162\(2^4 = 16\) ✓
\(\sqrt[5]{32}\)fifth root of 322\(2^5 = 32\) ✓
\(\sqrt[4]{81}\)fourth root of 813\(3^4 = 81\) ✓
\(\sqrt[6]{64}\)sixth root of 642\(2^6 = 64\) ✓
Even vs. Odd Index — The Essential Distinction

Even index — Radicand must be non-negative. \(\sqrt[n]{-a}\) is not real. Principal root is non-negative. \(\sqrt[n]{a^n} = |a|\).

Odd index — Radicand can be any real number. Negative radicands give negative roots. \(\sqrt[n]{a^n} = a\) for all real \(a\).

Example 8.2.1
Evaluating nth Roots
  • ProblemEvaluate each expression, if it is real: (a) \(\sqrt[4]{625}\)   (b) \(\sqrt[5]{-32}\)   (c) \(\sqrt[4]{-16}\)
  • ThinkCheck whether the index is even or odd, and whether the radicand allows a real result.
  • Solve (a) \(\sqrt[4]{625} = 5\), because \(5^4 = 625\).
    (b) \(\sqrt[5]{-32} = -2\), because \((-2)^5 = -32\). Odd index permits a negative radicand.
    (c) \(\sqrt[4]{-16}\) is not a real number. The index is even and the radicand is negative.
Answer: 5  ·  −2  ·  not a real number

Section 8.3

Rational Exponents

Defining \(a^{1/n}\)

Apply the power rule: if \(\left(8^{1/3}\right)^3 = 8^{(1/3)\cdot 3} = 8^1 = 8\), then \(8^{1/3}\) is a number whose cube equals 8. That is exactly the definition of a cube root. Therefore:

\[a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}\]

Defining \(a^{m/n}\)

The rational exponent \(m/n\) combines a root and a power. The denominator is the root index; the numerator is the power. There are two equivalent interpretations:

\[a^{m/n} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m = \sqrt[n]{a^m}\]
Practical Tip — Root First

In practice, take the root first, then apply the power. This keeps numbers smaller and reduces arithmetic errors. For example, \(64^{3/2}\): take \(\sqrt{64} = 8\), then cube: \(8^3 = 512\). Much easier than squaring 64 first.

Example 8.3.1
Evaluating Rational Exponents
  • ProblemEvaluate (a) \(27^{1/3}\)   (b) \(64^{3/2}\)   (c) \(16^{-3/4}\)
  • ThinkIdentify root (denominator) and power (numerator). Take the root first.
  • Solve (a) \(27^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{27} = 3\)

    (b) \(64^{3/2} = \left(\sqrt{64}\right)^3 = 8^3 = 512\)

    (c) \(16^{-3/4} = \dfrac{1}{16^{3/4}} = \dfrac{1}{\left(\sqrt[4]{16}\right)^3} = \dfrac{1}{2^3} = \dfrac{1}{8}\)
  • Check(a) \(3^3 = 27\) ✓   (b) \(512^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{512})^2 = 8^2 = 64\) ✓   (c) \((1/8)^{-4/3} = 8^{4/3} = 2^4 = 16\) ✓
Answer: 3  ·  512  ·  1/8
Example 8.3.2
Using Exponent Rules with Rational Exponents
  • ProblemSimplify with positive rational exponents: (a) \(x^{1/2} \cdot x^{1/3}\)   (b) \(\dfrac{y^{3/4}}{y^{1/4}}\)   (c) \(\left(z^{2/3}\right)^{3/2}\)
  • ThinkApply standard exponent rules — add, subtract, or multiply the fractional exponents.
  • Solve (a) \(x^{1/2} \cdot x^{1/3} = x^{3/6 + 2/6} = x^{5/6}\)

    (b) \(\dfrac{y^{3/4}}{y^{1/4}} = y^{3/4 - 1/4} = y^{2/4} = y^{1/2}\)

    (c) \(\left(z^{2/3}\right)^{3/2} = z^{(2/3)(3/2)} = z^{6/6} = z\)
Answer: \(x^{5/6}\)  ·  \(y^{1/2}\)  ·  \(z\)
Section 8.4

Converting Between Radical and Exponential Form

Conversion Rules

\(\sqrt[n]{a^m} = a^{m/n}\)    The index becomes the denominator; the power becomes the numerator.

\(a^{m/n} = \sqrt[n]{a^m}\)    The denominator becomes the index; the numerator becomes the power.

Example 8.4.1
Converting from Radical to Exponential Form
  • ProblemWrite using a rational exponent: (a) \(\sqrt[5]{x^3}\)   (b) \(\sqrt{y^7}\)   (c) \(\sqrt[4]{a^3 b^5}\)
  • Solve (a) \(\sqrt[5]{x^3} = x^{3/5}\)
    (b) \(\sqrt{y^7} = y^{7/2}\)
    (c) \(\sqrt[4]{a^3 b^5} = a^{3/4} \cdot b^{5/4}\)
Answer: \(x^{3/5}\)  ·  \(y^{7/2}\)  ·  \(a^{3/4}b^{5/4}\)
Example 8.4.2
Converting from Exponential to Radical Form
  • ProblemWrite using a radical: (a) \(x^{2/7}\)   (b) \((3a)^{3/4}\)   (c) \(5^{1/2}\)
  • Solve (a) \(x^{2/7} = \sqrt[7]{x^2}\)
    (b) \((3a)^{3/4} = \sqrt[4]{(3a)^3} = \sqrt[4]{27a^3}\)
    (c) \(5^{1/2} = \sqrt{5}\)
Answer: \(\sqrt[7]{x^2}\)  ·  \(\sqrt[4]{27a^3}\)  ·  \(\sqrt{5}\)
Student Warning

When converting \((3a)^{3/4}\), the exponent applies to the entire product \(3a\). Write the entire expression under the radical before simplifying: \(\sqrt[4]{(3a)^3}\), not \(\sqrt[4]{3} \cdot a^3\).

Section 8.5

Simplifying Radical Expressions

Product and Quotient Properties of Radicals

\(\sqrt[n]{a \cdot b} = \sqrt[n]{a} \cdot \sqrt[n]{b}\)    The radical of a product equals the product of the radicals.

\(\sqrt[n]{\dfrac{a}{b}} = \dfrac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}}\)    The radical of a quotient equals the quotient of the radicals. \((b \neq 0)\)

Strategy: factor the radicand to expose perfect nth power factors, then extract them.

Example 8.5.1
Simplifying Square Roots
  • ProblemSimplify (a) \(\sqrt{72}\)   (b) \(\sqrt{180}\)   (c) \(\sqrt{50x^4 y^3}\)
  • ThinkFactor each radicand to find the largest perfect square factor. Apply the product rule.
  • Solve (a) \(\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \cdot 2} = 6\sqrt{2}\)

    (b) \(\sqrt{180} = \sqrt{36 \cdot 5} = 6\sqrt{5}\)

    (c) \(\sqrt{50x^4 y^3} = \sqrt{25 \cdot 2 \cdot x^4 \cdot y^2 \cdot y} = 5x^2 y\sqrt{2y}\)
  • Check(c) \((5x^2y)^2 \cdot 2y = 25x^4y^2 \cdot 2y = 50x^4y^3\) ✓
Answer: \(6\sqrt{2}\)  ·  \(6\sqrt{5}\)  ·  \(5x^2 y\sqrt{2y}\)
Example 8.5.2
Simplifying Cube Roots
  • ProblemSimplify (a) \(\sqrt[3]{54}\)   (b) \(\sqrt[3]{16x^5}\)
  • Solve (a) \(\sqrt[3]{54} = \sqrt[3]{27 \cdot 2} = 3\sqrt[3]{2}\)

    (b) \(\sqrt[3]{16x^5} = \sqrt[3]{8 \cdot 2 \cdot x^3 \cdot x^2} = 2x\sqrt[3]{2x^2}\)
  • Check(b) \((2x)^3 \cdot 2x^2 = 8x^3 \cdot 2x^2 = 16x^5\) ✓
Answer: \(3\sqrt[3]{2}\)  ·  \(2x\sqrt[3]{2x^2}\)
Example 8.5.3
Rationalizing the Denominator
  • ProblemRationalize the denominator of \(\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{3}}\)
  • ThinkMultiply numerator and denominator by \(\sqrt{3}\) to eliminate the radical below.
  • Solve \[\frac{6}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{6\sqrt{3}}{3} = 2\sqrt{3}\]
Answer: \(2\sqrt{3}\)
Section 8.6

Operations with Radicals

Adding and Subtracting — Like Radicals

Radical expressions can be added or subtracted only when they have the same index and the same radicand. Such expressions are called like radicals. Combine them by adding or subtracting their coefficients. If expressions don't appear to match, simplify first — hidden like radicals often appear after simplification.

Example 8.6.1
Adding and Subtracting Radicals
  • ProblemSimplify (a) \(5\sqrt{3} + 2\sqrt{3} - \sqrt{3}\)   (b) \(\sqrt{50} + \sqrt{18}\)   (c) \(3\sqrt[3]{16} - \sqrt[3]{54}\)
  • ThinkFor (a), combine like radicals directly. For (b) and (c), simplify first to reveal like radicals.
  • Solve (a) \((5+2-1)\sqrt{3} = 6\sqrt{3}\)

    (b) \(\sqrt{50}+\sqrt{18} = 5\sqrt{2} + 3\sqrt{2} = 8\sqrt{2}\)

    (c) \(3\sqrt[3]{8\cdot2} - \sqrt[3]{27\cdot2} = 6\sqrt[3]{2} - 3\sqrt[3]{2} = 3\sqrt[3]{2}\)
Answer: \(6\sqrt{3}\)  ·  \(8\sqrt{2}\)  ·  \(3\sqrt[3]{2}\)
Student Warning

\(\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}\) cannot be combined. The radicands differ, so the expression is already in simplest form. Do not write \(\sqrt{5}\).

Multiplying Radicals

To multiply radicals with the same index, multiply the radicands. Multiply coefficients separately from radicands. Binomial radical products use the distributive property or FOIL.

Example 8.6.2
Multiplying Radical Expressions
  • ProblemExpand and simplify (a) \(\sqrt{3}(\sqrt{6}+4)\)   (b) \((\sqrt{5}+2)(\sqrt{5}-3)\)   (c) \((2\sqrt{3}+\sqrt{7})^2\)
  • ThinkDistribute or FOIL. Remember \(\sqrt{a}\cdot\sqrt{a} = a\).
  • Solve (a) \(\sqrt{3}(\sqrt{6}+4) = \sqrt{18}+4\sqrt{3} = 3\sqrt{2}+4\sqrt{3}\)

    (b) \((\sqrt{5}+2)(\sqrt{5}-3) = 5 - 3\sqrt{5} + 2\sqrt{5} - 6 = -1-\sqrt{5}\)

    (c) \((2\sqrt{3})^2 + 2(2\sqrt{3})(\sqrt{7}) + (\sqrt{7})^2 = 12 + 4\sqrt{21} + 7 = 19 + 4\sqrt{21}\)
Answer: \(3\sqrt{2}+4\sqrt{3}\)  ·  \(-1-\sqrt{5}\)  ·  \(19+4\sqrt{21}\)

Conjugate Pairs and Rationalizing Binomial Denominators

The expressions \(a+b\) and \(a-b\) are conjugates. Their product eliminates the middle terms: \((a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2\). When one term is a square root, this product is rational. Use the conjugate to rationalize binomial radical denominators.

Example 8.6.3
Rationalizing a Binomial Denominator
  • ProblemRationalize: \(\dfrac{4}{\sqrt{7}-1}\)
  • ThinkMultiply by the conjugate \(\sqrt{7}+1\).
  • Solve \[\frac{4}{\sqrt{7}-1} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{7}+1}{\sqrt{7}+1} = \frac{4(\sqrt{7}+1)}{7-1} = \frac{4\sqrt{7}+4}{6} = \frac{2\sqrt{7}+2}{3}\]
Answer: \(\dfrac{2\sqrt{7}+2}{3}\)

Section 8.7

Radical Functions

A radical function is a function defined by a rule that contains a radical expression with a variable under the radical sign. The domain — the set of all allowable inputs — depends on the index.

For even-indexed radical functions, set the radicand \(\geq 0\) and solve the inequality. For odd-indexed radical functions, the domain is all real numbers.

Example 8.7.1
Finding the Domain of Radical Functions
  • ProblemState the domain of (a) \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-3}\)   (b) \(g(x)=\sqrt{2x+8}\)   (c) \(h(x)=\sqrt[3]{x+5}\)
  • ThinkEven roots: radicand ≥ 0. Odd roots: all reals.
  • Solve (a) \(x-3 \geq 0 \Rightarrow x \geq 3\). Domain: \([3,\infty)\)
    (b) \(2x+8 \geq 0 \Rightarrow x \geq -4\). Domain: \([-4,\infty)\)
    (c) Odd index; domain: \((-\infty,\infty)\)
Answer: \([3,\infty)\)  ·  \([-4,\infty)\)  ·  \((-\infty,\infty)\)
Inverse Relationships

Radical functions are inverses of power functions. The function \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) undoes squaring. The function \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\) undoes cubing. This inverse relationship will deepen in Chapters 9 and 10 with exponential and logarithmic functions.

Section 8.10

Transformations of Radical Functions

General Transformation Form

\(f(x) = a\sqrt[n]{x-h}+k\)

a — Vertical stretch (\(|a|>1\)) or compression (\(0<|a|<1\)); if \(a<0\), reflects over the x-axis.

h — Horizontal shift: graph moves h units right (left if \(h<0\)).

k — Vertical shift: graph moves k units up (down if \(k<0\)).

For even-indexed radicals, the starting point shifts to \((h,k)\). For odd-indexed radicals, the center/inflection point shifts to \((h,k)\).

Example 8.10.1
Identifying Transformations
  • ProblemDescribe transformations and state domain and range for (a) \(f(x)=-\sqrt{x}+3\)   (b) \(g(x)=2\sqrt{x+1}-4\)   (c) \(h(x)=-\sqrt[3]{x-5}\)
  • Solve (a) \(a=-1\), \(h=0\), \(k=3\): reflected over x-axis, shifted 3 up. Domain: \([0,\infty)\). Range: \((-\infty,3]\).

    (b) \(a=2\), \(h=-1\), \(k=-4\): stretched by factor 2, shifted 1 left and 4 down. Domain: \([-1,\infty)\). Range: \([-4,\infty)\).

    (c) \(a=-1\), \(h=5\), \(k=0\): reflected over x-axis, shifted 5 right. Domain: \((-\infty,\infty)\). Range: \((-\infty,\infty)\).
Transformations and domains/ranges as described above.

Section 8.11

Solving Radical Equations

General Procedure for Solving Radical Equations

1. Isolate the radical on one side of the equation.

2. Raise both sides to the power equal to the index of the radical.

3. Solve the resulting equation (linear, quadratic, or other).

4. Check every solution in the original equation. Reject extraneous solutions.

Example 8.11.1
Solving a Basic Radical Equation
  • ProblemSolve \(\sqrt{x+3}=7\)
  • ThinkThe radical is already isolated. Square both sides.
  • Solve \[\left(\sqrt{x+3}\right)^2 = 7^2 \implies x+3=49 \implies x=46\]
  • Check\(\sqrt{46+3} = \sqrt{49} = 7\) ✓
Answer: \(x = 46\)
Example 8.11.2
Isolating Before Squaring
  • ProblemSolve \(\sqrt{3x-2}-4=0\)
  • Solve \[\sqrt{3x-2}=4 \implies 3x-2=16 \implies x=6\]
  • Check\(\sqrt{3(6)-2}-4 = \sqrt{16}-4 = 0\) ✓
Answer: \(x = 6\)
Example 8.11.3
Solving a Cube Root Equation
  • ProblemSolve \(\sqrt[3]{2x-1}=4\)
  • ThinkCube both sides to eliminate the cube root. Odd-index equations do not produce extraneous solutions, but checking is good practice.
  • Solve \[2x-1=4^3=64 \implies 2x=65 \implies x=\frac{65}{2}\]
  • Check\(\sqrt[3]{2 \cdot \frac{65}{2}-1} = \sqrt[3]{64} = 4\) ✓
Answer: \(x = \dfrac{65}{2}\)
Example 8.11.4
Radical Equation Leading to a Quadratic
  • ProblemSolve \(\sqrt{x+5}=x-1\)
  • ThinkSquare both sides. This produces a quadratic with two possible solutions — check both.
  • Solve \[x+5 = (x-1)^2 = x^2-2x+1\] \[0 = x^2-3x-4 = (x-4)(x+1)\] \[x=4 \quad \text{or} \quad x=-1\]
  • Check \(x=4\): \(\sqrt{9}=3\) and \(4-1=3\) ✓
    \(x=-1\): \(\sqrt{4}=2\) and \(-1-1=-2\) ✗   Extraneous!
Answer: \(x = 4\) only. (\(x=-1\) is extraneous.)
Example 8.11.5
Equation with Two Radicals
  • ProblemSolve \(\sqrt{x+7}=\sqrt{x}+1\)
  • ThinkSquare both sides; the right side expands as a binomial. A radical will remain — isolate it and square again.
  • Solve \[x+7 = (\sqrt{x}+1)^2 = x+2\sqrt{x}+1\] \[6 = 2\sqrt{x} \implies \sqrt{x}=3 \implies x=9\]
  • Check\(\sqrt{16}=4\) and \(\sqrt{9}+1=4\) ✓
Answer: \(x = 9\)
Section 8.12

Extraneous Solutions

An extraneous solution is a value that satisfies an equation obtained by algebraic manipulation but does not satisfy the original equation. Extraneous solutions arise in radical equations because squaring both sides is not a reversible operation.

If \(A=B\), then \(A^2=B^2\). But \(A^2=B^2\) does not guarantee \(A=B\) — it also allows \(A=-B\). Squaring can introduce false solutions from the case where one side was actually the negative of the other.

Example 8.12.1
No Valid Solution
  • ProblemSolve \(\sqrt{x-4}+8=5\)
  • Solve\(\sqrt{x-4} = -3\)
  • CheckA principal square root is always non-negative. It cannot equal \(-3\). Stop here.
Answer: No solution. Solution set is \(\emptyset\).
Student Warning — Recognize This Pattern Immediately

If isolating the radical gives \(\sqrt{\text{something}} = \text{negative number}\), there is no real solution. Do not proceed to square both sides. Squaring would give a numerical answer, but that answer would be entirely extraneous.

Summary — Handling Extraneous Solutions

1. Solve to get all candidate solutions.

2. Substitute each candidate into the original equation (not the squared version).

3. Accept those that make the original true; reject those that do not.

4. If all candidates are rejected: no solution.

Section 8.13

Radical Models in Geometry and Science

Radicals in Geometry

Key Geometric Formulas Involving Radicals

Pythagorean Theorem — For a right triangle with legs \(a\), \(b\) and hypotenuse \(c\): \(\;c = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}\)

Distance Formula — Between points \((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\): \(\;d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\)

Square area/side — If area is \(A\): side length \(s = \sqrt{A}\)

Circle area/radius — If area is \(A\): radius \(r = \sqrt{A/\pi}\)

Example 8.13.1
Geometry — Diagonal of a Rectangular Park
  • ProblemA rectangular park is 150 m long and 80 m wide. How far is it diagonally across?
  • ThinkThe diagonal is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs 150 and 80.
  • Solve \[d = \sqrt{150^2+80^2} = \sqrt{22500+6400} = \sqrt{28900} = 170\]
  • Check\(170^2 = 28900 = 22500+6400\) ✓
Answer: The diagonal is 170 meters.

Radicals in Physics — The Pendulum

Period of a Simple Pendulum

The time \(T\) (in seconds) for one complete swing of a pendulum of length \(L\) (in meters):

\[T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\]

where \(g \approx 9.8\) m/s² near Earth's surface. Because of the square root, quadrupling the length only doubles the period: \(\sqrt{4L}=2\sqrt{L}\).

Example 8.13.2
Pendulum Period
  • ProblemA clock pendulum is 0.25 m long. What is its period? Use \(g=9.8\) m/s², \(\pi \approx 3.14159\).
  • Solve \[T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.25}{9.8}} = 2(3.14159)\sqrt{0.02551} \approx 2(3.14159)(0.1597) \approx 1.00 \text{ sec}\]
  • CheckA 25 cm pendulum swinging once per second is exactly how many old mechanical clocks were calibrated. ✓
Answer: Period \(\approx\) 1.00 second.
Example 8.13.3
Solving a Science Formula for a Variable
  • ProblemA pendulum has a period of 2 seconds. Find its required length. Use \(g=9.8\) m/s².
  • ThinkSubstitute \(T=2\) and isolate \(L\) by isolating the radical, then squaring both sides.
  • Solve \[2 = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{9.8}} \implies 1=\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{9.8}} \implies \frac{1}{\pi}=\sqrt{\frac{L}{9.8}}\] \[\frac{1}{\pi^2}=\frac{L}{9.8} \implies L=\frac{9.8}{\pi^2} \approx \frac{9.8}{9.8696} \approx 0.993 \text{ m}\]
Answer: Length \(\approx\) 0.993 meters (about 99.3 cm).

Section 8.14

Common Radical Function Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Distributing a Root Across a Sum
\(\sqrt{a+b} = \sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}\)

Counterexample: \(\sqrt{9+16}=\sqrt{25}=5\), but \(\sqrt{9}+\sqrt{16}=3+4=7 \neq 5\). The radical of a sum cannot be split across the terms.

Mistake 2 — Forgetting to Check for Extraneous Solutions
Accepting every solution of the squared equation without checking in the original

Squaring is not reversible. Always substitute candidate solutions back into the original radical equation and reject those that fail.

Mistake 3 — Ignoring the Domain Restriction of Even-Indexed Radicals
Treating \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-5}\) as defined for all x

The domain is \(x \geq 5\). Even-indexed radical functions require non-negative radicands. Always determine the domain before graphing or solving.

Mistake 4 — Confusing \(-\sqrt{x}\) and \(\sqrt{-x}\)
\(-\sqrt{x} = \sqrt{-x}\)

\(-\sqrt{x}\) is the negative of the principal square root of \(x\) (defined for \(x \geq 0\)). The expression \(\sqrt{-x}\) requires \(x \leq 0\) and gives a non-negative output. These are different functions with different domains.

Mistake 5 — Dropping Absolute Values with Even-Indexed Roots
\(\sqrt{x^2}=x\) for all x

Correct: \(\sqrt{x^2}=|x|\). The principal square root is non-negative, so \(\sqrt{(-3)^2}=\sqrt{9}=3\), not \(-3\). For odd roots: \(\sqrt[3]{x^3}=x\) for all real \(x\).

Mistake 6 — Not Simplifying Before Combining
Concluding that \(\sqrt{50}+\sqrt{18}\) cannot be simplified

Simplify first: \(\sqrt{50}=5\sqrt{2}\), \(\sqrt{18}=3\sqrt{2}\), so their sum is \(8\sqrt{2}\). Like radicals often appear only after simplifying.

Mistake 7 — Inverting the Rational Exponent Rule
\(a^{m/n}=\sqrt[m]{a^n}\)

Correct: \(a^{m/n}=\sqrt[n]{a^m}\). The denominator is the index (root) and the numerator is the power. Swapping them gives a completely different — and wrong — expression.

Mistake 8 — Stopping After One Squaring When Two Are Needed
In \(\sqrt{x+7}=\sqrt{x}+1\), squaring once and then treating remaining radicals as numbers

After the first squaring, a radical may remain. Isolate it and square again. Continue until no radicals remain. Always check the final result.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1A radical expression contains a radicand under a radical sign with an index indicating which root is taken. The principal square root is non-negative. Cube roots accept all real numbers. Perfect square and cube recognition aids simplification.
  • Sec 8.2The nth root of a extends to any positive integer index. Even-indexed roots require non-negative radicands; odd-indexed roots accept all reals. For even n, \(\sqrt[n]{a^n}=|a|\); for odd n, \(\sqrt[n]{a^n}=a\).
  • Sec 8.3Rational exponents connect roots and powers: \(a^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a}\) and \(a^{m/n}=(\sqrt[n]{a})^m\). The denominator is the root; the numerator is the power. All standard exponent rules apply to fractional exponents. Take the root first for easier arithmetic.
  • Sec 8.4Radical notation and rational exponent notation are interchangeable. Convert by matching the index to the denominator and the power of the radicand to the numerator. Choose whichever form is more convenient for the task.
  • Sec 8.5Simplify radicals using product and quotient properties. Factor out perfect nth power factors from the radicand. Rationalize denominators by multiplying by an appropriate radical or conjugate.
  • Sec 8.6Add and subtract only like radicals (same index, same radicand). Multiply radicals using the product property. Use the distributive property or FOIL for binomial products. Multiply conjugate pairs to rationalize binomial denominators.
  • Sec 8.7A radical function contains a variable under a radical sign. Domain: for even-indexed functions, set radicand ≥ 0; for odd-indexed functions, all reals. Radical functions are inverses of power functions with appropriate domain restrictions.
  • Sec 8.8The parent function \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) begins at the origin, curves up-right, with domain and range \([0,\infty)\). It is concave down — the rate of growth decreases as x increases. Shifted versions begin at a different starting point.
  • Sec 8.9The parent function \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\) passes through the origin as an S-shaped curve with domain and range \((-\infty,\infty)\). It is an odd function with point symmetry about the origin. Odd-indexed radicals produce curves defined for all inputs.
  • Sec 8.10Transformations take the form \(f(x)=a\sqrt[n]{x-h}+k\). The value \(a\) controls stretch and reflection; \(h\) shifts horizontally; \(k\) shifts vertically. The starting or center point moves to \((h,k)\).
  • Sec 8.11Solve radical equations by isolating the radical, then raising both sides to the power equal to the index. The result may be linear or quadratic. Check every solution in the original equation before accepting it.
  • Sec 8.12Extraneous solutions arise because squaring is irreversible — it can introduce the case \(A=-B\) as well as \(A=B\). The only remedy is to verify every candidate in the original equation. If isolating gives \(\sqrt{\cdot}=\text{negative}\), there is no solution.
  • Sec 8.13Radical models appear in geometry (Pythagorean theorem, distance formula, area formulas), physics (pendulum period \(T=2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\)), and engineering. Solving these models for a variable requires isolating the radical and squaring, with careful checking.
  • Sec 8.14Eight common mistakes: splitting a root across a sum; skipping extraneous solution checks; ignoring domain restrictions; confusing \(-\sqrt{x}\) with \(\sqrt{-x}\); dropping absolute values; not simplifying before combining; inverting the rational exponent rule; stopping after one squaring when two are needed.

Key Terms Review

radical expression
An expression containing a root symbol with a radicand under it and an index indicating which root is taken.
radicand
The expression placed under the radical sign.
index
The integer written above and to the left of the radical sign, indicating which root is being taken. Omitted index defaults to 2 (square root).
nth root
The nth root of a number a is the number b such that b raised to the nth power equals a.
principal root
The non-negative real nth root for even indices. The radical symbol always refers to the principal root.
rational exponent
An exponent written as a fraction m/n: the denominator is the root index and the numerator is the power. \(a^{m/n}=\sqrt[n]{a^m}\).
irrational number
A real number that cannot be expressed as a ratio of integers. Many simplified radical expressions, like \(\sqrt{2}\), are irrational.
like radicals
Radicals with the same index and the same radicand. Only like radicals can be combined by addition or subtraction.
conjugate radical pair
Expressions of the form \(a+b\) and \(a-b\) where at least one term contains a square root. Their product \(a^2-b^2\) contains no radicals.
radical function
A function whose rule contains a variable under a radical sign.
domain restriction
A limitation on allowable inputs. For even-indexed radical functions, the radicand must be non-negative.
radical equation
An equation in which the variable appears under a radical sign.
extraneous solution
A value that satisfies an equation obtained by algebraic steps but does not satisfy the original equation. Must be identified and rejected.
square root function
The parent function \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) with domain and range \([0,\infty)\). Starts at the origin and curves up and to the right.
cube root function
The parent function \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\) with domain and range \((-\infty,\infty)\). S-shaped curve through the origin.
transformation
A shift, stretch, compression, or reflection applied to a parent function via the parameters a, h, and k.
inverse function
A function that reverses the input-output relationship of another. Radical functions are inverses of power functions with appropriate domain restrictions.

Bridge Note — Chapter 9
From Radical Functions to Exponential Functions

Chapter 8 developed your ability to work with expressions and functions that involve roots — which are, at their core, fractional powers. You have seen that the notation \(a^{m/n}\) connects roots and powers in a single unified language, and that this language obeys the same algebraic laws as integer exponents.

In Chapter 9, you will study exponential functions of the form \(f(x) = a \cdot b^x\), where the variable appears in the exponent rather than the base. These functions describe repeated multiplicative change: population growth, compound interest, radioactive decay, and many other real-world phenomena.

The connection runs deeper than it may first appear. The function \(f(x)=2^x\) is defined not only for integer values of x, but for all real numbers including fractions and irrationals. When \(x=1/2\), then \(2^{1/2}=\sqrt{2}\). When \(x=2/3\), then \(2^{2/3}=\sqrt[3]{4}\). The rational exponent work of this chapter is precisely what makes exponential functions continuous and defined at every real input.

In Chapter 10, logarithmic functions will appear as the inverses of exponential functions — just as radical functions are the inverses of power functions. The theme of inverse relationships that runs through Chapter 8 will continue throughout the rest of this textbook. Carry forward: rational exponents unify roots and powers; domains must be checked; inverse relationships organize entire families of functions; and verifying solutions is never optional.

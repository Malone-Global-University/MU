Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 13 introduces trigonometry — the study of triangle ratios and periodic relationships — as a natural extension of the algebraic thinking developed throughout this course. Trigonometry is one of the oldest and most useful branches of mathematics, connecting geometry, algebra, and the behavior of repeating patterns in nature and engineering.

Students begin with the most concrete entry point: right triangles. A right triangle has predictable, measurable relationships among its sides and angles, and trigonometry gives names and structure to those relationships. From there, the chapter extends those ideas outward — first to all angles, then to the coordinate plane, and finally to the graphs of functions that repeat themselves in predictable cycles.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to define and evaluate the three primary trigonometric ratios, solve problems involving right triangles, convert between degrees and radians, locate values on the unit circle, and sketch and interpret the graphs of sine, cosine, and tangent. These skills form the foundation for Precalculus and Trigonometry, where the study of periodic functions becomes a central theme. This chapter prepares students for that work without becoming a full trigonometry course. The emphasis remains on building understanding from right triangles first, then extending to functions and graphs.

Opening Question

A 50-foot cable is anchored to the ground and attached to the top of a vertical support pole. If the cable makes a 62° angle with the ground, how tall is the pole? Without a way to directly measure a height that may be unreachable or dangerous to climb, how can you calculate it? Trigonometry provides an exact method — and by the end of this chapter, you will know how to solve it.

Why This Chapter Matters. Trigonometry appears in nearly every quantitative field. Engineering and construction use triangle ratios to design structures, calculate forces, and measure distances. Physics uses sine and cosine functions to model waves, oscillations, pendulums, and alternating electrical current. Navigation and surveying use angle measurements to determine positions and distances across terrain. Computer graphics and animation rely on trigonometric functions to simulate motion and create realistic visual effects. Music and acoustics describe sound waves using periodic functions. Even finance and economics sometimes use cyclical models to represent repeating patterns in data. Understanding how periodic functions behave provides a powerful lens for interpreting patterns in the world.

Section 13.1

What Trigonometry Studies

Trigonometry begins with a simple observation: in any right triangle, the ratios between pairs of sides are determined entirely by the angles of the triangle, not by the size of the triangle. To see why this is true, imagine two right triangles with the same angle measures but different sizes. Even though one triangle might be twice as large as the other, the ratio of the opposite side to the hypotenuse will be identical in both. This consistency is what makes trigonometry so powerful. Once you know an angle, you know the ratios of the sides. Once you know the ratios, you can find missing side lengths.

This idea expands significantly beyond triangles. Trigonometric ratios can be extended to any angle — not just angles inside a right triangle — and the resulting functions repeat their values in predictable cycles. This is why trigonometry is also the mathematics of waves, vibrations, orbits, and oscillations.

Algebra II introduces the foundational layer of this subject. The full study of trigonometric identities, laws of sines and cosines, inverse functions, and parametric equations belongs to Precalculus and Trigonometry. Here, the goal is to build firm roots: understand the ratios, learn the key angle values, grasp the unit circle, and read the graphs of sine, cosine, and tangent.

Section 13.2

Right Triangles

A right triangle is any triangle that contains exactly one 90° angle. Because the angles of any triangle must sum to 180°, the other two angles in a right triangle must each be less than 90° and must together add to exactly 90°. Those two non-right angles are called acute angles.

When working with trigonometry, three sides must be correctly identified relative to a chosen angle. The hypotenuse is always the side directly across from the 90° angle — it is the longest side. The opposite side is directly across from the angle you are working with. The adjacent side forms the angle you are working with and is not the hypotenuse. These labels shift depending on which angle you are using.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a right triangle with the right angle at the lower right corner. Label the vertex at the lower left as angle θ. The hypotenuse runs from the lower left to the upper right. Label the vertical side (across from θ) as "opposite." Label the horizontal bottom side (next to θ) as "adjacent." Label the longest side (from lower left to upper right) as "hypotenuse." Place a small square at the right angle corner.

The Pythagorean Theorem

If \(a\) and \(b\) are the two legs and \(c\) is the hypotenuse of a right triangle:

\[ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \]

If two sides are known, the third can always be found using this relationship.

Example 13.2.1
Identifying sides of a right triangle
  • ProblemIn a right triangle, angle \(\theta\) is at the bottom left. The horizontal leg has length 5, the vertical leg has length 12, and the hypotenuse has length 13. Identify the opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse relative to \(\theta\).
  • ThinkThe hypotenuse is always across from the right angle. The adjacent side forms the angle \(\theta\) without being the hypotenuse. The opposite side is across from \(\theta\).
  • AnswerHypotenuse = 13  ·  Adjacent = 5 (horizontal leg)  ·  Opposite = 12 (vertical leg)
Example 13.2.2
Using the Pythagorean Theorem
  • ProblemA right triangle has legs of length 8 and 15. Find the hypotenuse.
  • Solve\[ 8^2 + 15^2 = c^2 \implies 64 + 225 = 289 \implies c = \sqrt{289} = 17 \]
  • Check\(8^2 + 15^2 = 289 = 17^2\). ✓
Answer: The hypotenuse is 17.
Section 13.3

Sine, Cosine, and Tangent

The three primary trigonometric ratios each compare two sides of a right triangle relative to a specific angle \(\theta\). These ratios hold true for any right triangle containing the same angle, regardless of size.

SOH-CAH-TOA

\[ \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \qquad \cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \qquad \tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} \]

SOH: Sine = Opposite / Hypotenuse    CAH: Cosine = Adjacent / Hypotenuse    TOA: Tangent = Opposite / Adjacent

Example 13.3.1
Writing the three trigonometric ratios
  • ProblemA right triangle has angle \(\theta\) with opposite = 7, adjacent = 24, hypotenuse = 25. Write all three ratios.
  • CheckVerify: \(7^2 + 24^2 = 49 + 576 = 625 = 25^2\). ✓
  • Solve\[ \sin(\theta) = \frac{7}{25} \qquad \cos(\theta) = \frac{24}{25} \qquad \tan(\theta) = \frac{7}{24} \]
Answer: sin(θ) = 7/25, cos(θ) = 24/25, tan(θ) = 7/24
Example 13.3.2
Evaluating trigonometric ratios with a calculator
  • ProblemFind \(\sin(35°)\), \(\cos(35°)\), and \(\tan(35°)\) to four decimal places.
  • ThinkUse a scientific calculator. Confirm the calculator is in degree mode before computing.
  • Solve\[ \sin(35°) \approx 0.5736 \qquad \cos(35°) \approx 0.8192 \qquad \tan(35°) \approx 0.7002 \]
Answer: sin(35°) ≈ 0.5736, cos(35°) ≈ 0.8192, tan(35°) ≈ 0.7002
Student Warning — Calculator Mode

One of the most common early errors is leaving a calculator in radian mode when working with degree problems. Before computing any trigonometric value, check the mode setting. A quick check: \(\sin(90°)\) should equal exactly 1 in degree mode.

Section 13.4

Solving Right Triangles

When at least one side and one acute angle of a right triangle are known, trigonometric ratios can be used to find the remaining sides. When two sides are known, inverse trigonometric functions can find missing angles. The procedure: identify the known angle, the known side, and the unknown side; choose the ratio that connects them; solve.

Note on Inverse Functions

The notation \(\sin^{-1}(x)\) does not mean \(\frac{1}{\sin(x)}\). It means the inverse sine function — the function that returns an angle whose sine is \(x\). On most calculators this is labeled \(\sin^{-1}\) or arcsin. The same applies to \(\cos^{-1}\) and \(\tan^{-1}\).

Example 13.4.1
Finding a missing side
  • ProblemA right triangle has a 40° angle and a hypotenuse of 20. Find the side opposite the 40° angle.
  • ThinkKnown: hypotenuse = 20. Unknown: opposite. The ratio connecting opposite to hypotenuse is sine.
  • Solve\[ \sin(40°) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{20} \implies \text{opposite} = 20 \cdot \sin(40°) \approx 20 \cdot 0.6428 \approx 12.86 \]
  • CheckThe adjacent side: \(20 \cdot \cos(40°) \approx 15.32\). Pythagorean check: \(12.86^2 + 15.32^2 \approx 399.7 \approx 20^2\). ✓
Answer: The opposite side is approximately 12.86.
Example 13.4.2
Finding a missing angle
  • ProblemIn a right triangle, the opposite side is 9 and the hypotenuse is 15. Find angle \(\theta\).
  • ThinkThe ratio of opposite to hypotenuse is sine. To find the angle from a ratio, apply the inverse sine function.
  • Solve\[ \sin(\theta) = \frac{9}{15} = 0.6 \implies \theta = \sin^{-1}(0.6) \approx 36.87° \]
  • Check\(\sin(36.87°) \approx 0.6\). ✓
Answer: θ ≈ 36.87°
Example 13.4.3
Fully solving a right triangle
  • ProblemA right triangle has a 55° angle and an adjacent side of 10. Find the opposite leg and the hypotenuse.
  • ThinkUse tangent for the opposite leg (opposite/adjacent). Use cosine for the hypotenuse (adjacent/hypotenuse).
  • Solve\[ \tan(55°) = \frac{\text{opp}}{10} \implies \text{opp} = 10 \cdot \tan(55°) \approx 14.28 \] \[ \cos(55°) = \frac{10}{\text{hyp}} \implies \text{hyp} = \frac{10}{\cos(55°)} \approx 17.43 \]
  • Check\(10^2 + 14.28^2 \approx 303.9 \approx 17.43^2\). ✓
Answer: Opposite leg ≈ 14.28, hypotenuse ≈ 17.43.

Section 13.5

Angles of Elevation and Depression

Two common real-world applications involve angles measured from a horizontal reference line. The angle of elevation is measured upward from horizontal toward an object higher than the observer. The angle of depression is measured downward from horizontal toward an object lower than the observer.

A key geometric fact: when two horizontal lines are parallel, the angle of elevation from the lower level and the angle of depression from the upper level to the same two points are equal — they are alternate interior angles formed by the same transversal.

Diagram Instruction

Draw two horizontal lines at different heights. At the lower level, draw a person looking upward at an object above; label the angle between horizontal and the line of sight as the angle of elevation. At the upper level, draw a person looking downward at an object below; label that angle as the angle of depression. Note the two angles are equal when the horizontal lines are parallel.

Example 13.5.1
Angle of elevation — building height
  • ProblemA person stands 40 meters from the base of a building. The angle of elevation to the top is 65°. How tall is the building?
  • ThinkThe 40 m distance is the adjacent side. The building height is the opposite side. Use tangent.
  • Solve\[ \tan(65°) = \frac{\text{height}}{40} \implies \text{height} = 40 \cdot \tan(65°) \approx 40 \cdot 2.1445 \approx 85.78 \text{ m} \]
Answer: The building is approximately 85.78 meters tall.
Example 13.5.2
Angle of depression — lifeguard tower
  • ProblemA lifeguard in a tower 12 feet above the ground spots a swimmer at an angle of depression of 20°. How far is the swimmer from the base of the tower?
  • ThinkThe tower height (12 ft) is the opposite side. The horizontal distance is the adjacent side. Use tangent.
  • Solve\[ \tan(20°) = \frac{12}{\text{distance}} \implies \text{distance} = \frac{12}{\tan(20°)} \approx \frac{12}{0.3640} \approx 32.97 \text{ ft} \]
Answer: The swimmer is approximately 32.97 feet from the base.
Student Tip — Draw First

Draw a diagram for every angle-of-elevation or angle-of-depression problem. Establish clearly what side is opposite, what side is adjacent, and where the right angle is before choosing any ratio. Rushing without a diagram is the single most common source of error in these problems.

Section 13.6

Special Right Triangles

Two types of right triangles have angle measures that produce exact, simple side ratios. They appear so frequently in mathematics, physics, and engineering that their relationships should be derived and internalized.

The 45°–45°–90° Triangle

An isosceles right triangle where the two legs are equal. If each leg is \(s\), the Pythagorean theorem gives the hypotenuse \(s\sqrt{2}\).

45°–45°–90° Ratios

\[ \text{leg} : \text{leg} : \text{hypotenuse} = 1 : 1 : \sqrt{2} \]

If each leg = \(s\), then hypotenuse = \(s\sqrt{2}\).

The 30°–60°–90° Triangle

Derived by cutting an equilateral triangle in half along its altitude. The short leg (opposite 30°) is half the hypotenuse.

30°–60°–90° Ratios

\[ \text{short leg} : \text{long leg} : \text{hyp} = 1 : \sqrt{3} : 2 \]

If short leg = \(s\), then long leg = \(s\sqrt{3}\) and hypotenuse = \(2s\).

Example 13.6.1
Using the 45°–45°–90° Triangle
  • ProblemA square garden has a diagonal path of length 10 feet. Find the length of each side.
  • ThinkThe diagonal of a square forms two 45°–45°–90° triangles. The diagonal is the hypotenuse: \(s\sqrt{2} = 10\).
  • Solve\[ s = \frac{10}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{10\sqrt{2}}{2} = 5\sqrt{2} \approx 7.07 \text{ ft} \]
Answer: Each side is \(5\sqrt{2} \approx 7.07\) feet.
Example 13.6.2
Using the 30°–60°–90° Triangle
  • ProblemA right triangle has a 30° angle and a hypotenuse of 16. Find both legs.
  • ThinkShort leg = \(\frac{1}{2}\) × hypotenuse. Long leg = short leg × \(\sqrt{3}\).
  • Solve\[ \text{short leg} = \frac{1}{2}(16) = 8 \qquad \text{long leg} = 8\sqrt{3} \approx 13.86 \]
  • Check\(8^2 + (8\sqrt{3})^2 = 64 + 192 = 256 = 16^2\). ✓
Answer: Short leg = 8, long leg = \(8\sqrt{3} \approx 13.86\).
Student Warning

Do not confuse which leg is opposite 30° and which is opposite 60°. The shorter leg is always opposite the smaller angle (30°). The longer leg is always opposite the larger acute angle (60°).

Section 13.7

Degrees and Radians

So far, angles have been measured in degrees, where a full rotation equals 360°. In higher mathematics, a different unit called the radian is standard. A radian is the angle formed when an arc of length equal to the radius of a circle is cut off on the circle's circumference.

Because the entire circumference of a circle with radius \(r\) is \(2\pi r\), the total angle in a full rotation equals \(2\pi\) radians. This gives the fundamental conversion:

Degree–Radian Conversion

\[ 180° = \pi \text{ radians} \]

Degrees → Radians: multiply by \(\dfrac{\pi}{180}\)     Radians → Degrees: multiply by \(\dfrac{180}{\pi}\)

Example 13.7.1
Converting degrees to radians
  • ProblemConvert 120° to radians.
  • Solve\[ 120° \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{120\pi}{180} = \frac{2\pi}{3} \]
Answer: \(120° = \dfrac{2\pi}{3}\) radians.
Example 13.7.2
Converting radians to degrees
  • ProblemConvert \(\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\) radians to degrees.
  • Solve\[ \frac{5\pi}{6} \times \frac{180}{\pi} = \frac{5 \times 180}{6} = 150° \]
Answer: \(\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\) radians = 150°.

Section 13.8

Introduction to the Unit Circle

The unit circle is a circle with radius 1 centered at the origin. It is the central organizing tool for extending trigonometric values beyond right triangles. When an angle \(\theta\) is measured from the positive x-axis and the terminal ray intersects the unit circle, the coordinates of that intersection point are exactly \((\cos\theta,\, \sin\theta)\).

Unit Circle Definition

At any point \(P\) on the unit circle at angle \(\theta\):

\[ P = (\cos\theta,\, \sin\theta) \]

The x-coordinate always equals \(\cos\theta\). The y-coordinate always equals \(\sin\theta\).

Diagram Instruction

Draw a unit circle (radius 1) centered at the origin. Draw a ray from the origin at angle θ from the positive x-axis. Mark the intersection point as P. Draw a vertical dashed line from P to the x-axis. Label the horizontal distance as cos(θ) and the vertical distance as sin(θ). Label P as (cos θ, sin θ). Also mark the four axis-intercept points: (1,0) at 0°, (0,1) at 90°, (−1,0) at 180°, (0,−1) at 270°.

For angles in the first quadrant, this definition matches the right-triangle definition exactly — the hypotenuse is the radius (= 1), the horizontal leg is the cosine, and the vertical leg is the sine. The unit circle's advantage is that it extends trigonometry to angles greater than 90°, negative angles, and multiple rotations.

Section 13.10

Graphs of Sine and Cosine

The sine and cosine functions can be graphed by placing angle measure on the horizontal axis and function value on the vertical axis. These graphs reveal the periodic, cyclical behavior of the functions.

Diagram Instruction — Graph of y = sin(x)

Draw a coordinate plane. Label the horizontal axis from 0 to 2π, marked at π/2, π, 3π/2, and 2π. The vertical axis runs from −1 to 1. Draw the smooth S-shaped curve: starting at (0, 0), rising to a maximum of 1 at π/2, returning to 0 at π, falling to a minimum of −1 at 3π/2, and returning to 0 at 2π. Label the maximum, minimum, and all x-intercepts.

Properties of y = sin(x) and y = cos(x)

Sine: starts at origin (0, 0); max at \(\frac{\pi}{2}\); returns to 0 at \(\pi\); min at \(\frac{3\pi}{2}\); x-intercepts at multiples of \(\pi\).

Cosine: starts at maximum (0, 1); crosses 0 at \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) and \(\frac{3\pi}{2}\); min at \(\pi\); x-intercepts at odd multiples of \(\frac{\pi}{2}\).

Both functions share: Domain = all reals  ·  Range = \([-1,\, 1]\)  ·  Period = \(2\pi\)  ·  Amplitude = 1  ·  Midline: \(y = 0\).

The cosine graph is the sine graph shifted left by \(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\) — identical shape, different starting position.

Section 13.11

Amplitude, Period, and Midline

Real-world periodic phenomena do not always have amplitude 1, period \(2\pi\), or midline \(y = 0\). The general forms of sine and cosine allow these features to be adjusted.

General Form

\[ y = a\sin(bx) + k \qquad \text{and} \qquad y = a\cos(bx) + k \]

Amplitude = \(|a|\) — maximum distance from the midline to a peak or valley.

Period = \(\dfrac{2\pi}{|b|}\) — horizontal length of one complete cycle.

Midline = \(y = k\) — the horizontal center line of oscillation.

If \(a < 0\), the graph is reflected vertically (flipped upside down). Amplitude remains \(|a|\).

Example 13.11.1
Identifying features from an equation
  • ProblemIdentify the amplitude, period, and midline of \(y = 3\sin(2x) - 1\).
  • ThinkMatch to \(y = a\sin(bx) + k\): here \(a = 3\), \(b = 2\), \(k = -1\).
  • Solve\[ \text{Amplitude} = |3| = 3 \qquad \text{Period} = \frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi \qquad \text{Midline}: y = -1 \]
  • AnswerThe graph oscillates between \(y = -4\) (min) and \(y = 2\) (max), centered on \(y = -1\), completing one cycle every \(\pi\) units.
Answer: Amplitude = 3, Period = π, Midline y = −1.
Example 13.11.2
Sketching a transformed cosine function
  • ProblemIdentify features and sketch \(y = 2\cos\!\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right) + 1\).
  • Think\(a = 2\), \(b = \frac{1}{2}\), \(k = 1\).
  • Solve\[ \text{Amplitude} = 2 \qquad \text{Period} = \frac{2\pi}{1/2} = 4\pi \qquad \text{Midline}: y = 1 \] Max = \(1 + 2 = 3\), Min = \(1 - 2 = -1\). One cycle spans \(4\pi\) horizontal units, starting at the maximum.
Answer: Amplitude = 2, Period = 4π, Midline y = 1.

Example 13.11.3
Writing an equation from graph features
  • ProblemA sine function has amplitude 5, period \(3\pi\), and midline \(y = 2\). Write its equation.
  • ThinkUse \(y = a\sin(bx) + k\). Find \(b\) from the period: \(\dfrac{2\pi}{b} = 3\pi\).
  • Solve\[ a = 5,\quad k = 2,\quad b = \frac{2\pi}{3\pi} = \frac{2}{3} \] \[ y = 5\sin\!\left(\frac{2}{3}x\right) + 2 \]

Section 13.12

Tangent as a Function

The tangent function is defined as the ratio of sine to cosine:

Tangent Definition and Key Properties

\[ \tan(\theta) = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)} \]

Wherever \(\cos(\theta) = 0\), tangent is undefined — vertical asymptotes occur at all odd multiples of \(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\).

Domain: all reals except \(\dfrac{\pi}{2} + n\pi\)  ·  Range: all real numbers  ·  Period: \(\pi\) (repeats every half-rotation)  ·  No amplitude (no maximum or minimum).

Diagram Instruction — Graph of y = tan(x)

Draw a coordinate plane with horizontal axis from −π/2 to 5π/2. Draw vertical dashed lines at x = π/2 and x = 3π/2 as vertical asymptotes. In each interval between asymptotes, sketch the tangent curve: it rises from negative infinity near the left asymptote, passes through (0, 0), crosses through (π/4, 1), and rises steeply toward the right asymptote. The pattern repeats every π units. Each branch has the same rising S-like shape.

Example 13.12.1
Evaluating tangent using sine and cosine
  • ProblemEvaluate \(\tan(150°)\).
  • ThinkUse \(\tan = \sin/\cos\). From Section 13.9: \(\sin(150°) = \frac{1}{2}\), \(\cos(150°) = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\).
  • Solve\[ \tan(150°) = \frac{1/2}{-\sqrt{3}/2} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{-2}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{3}} = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} \]
Answer: \(\tan(150°) = -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\)
Section 13.13

Trigonometric Models

Periodic phenomena occur throughout science, nature, technology, and daily life. Any situation that repeats in regular intervals can potentially be modeled by a sine or cosine function. Given the maximum value, the minimum value, and the period of a periodic situation, the following procedure identifies the model.

Setting Up a Trigonometric Model

Step 1 — Amplitude: \(a = \dfrac{\text{max} - \text{min}}{2}\)

Step 2 — Midline: \(k = \dfrac{\text{max} + \text{min}}{2}\)

Step 3 — Find b from period: \(b = \dfrac{2\pi}{\text{period}}\)

Step 4 — Choose sine or cosine based on the starting value. If the function starts at the midline moving upward, use sine. If it starts at the maximum, use cosine.

Example 13.13.1
Daily temperature model
  • ProblemA city's average monthly temperature reaches a maximum of 95°F in July (month 7) and a minimum of 35°F in January (month 1). Write a cosine model for temperature \(T\) as a function of month \(m\), then estimate April (month 4).
  • ThinkPeriod = 12 months. Max = 95°F, min = 35°F. Maximum occurs at month 7, so shift the cosine peak there.
  • Solve\[ a = \frac{95-35}{2} = 30 \qquad k = \frac{95+35}{2} = 65 \qquad b = \frac{2\pi}{12} = \frac{\pi}{6} \] \[ T(m) = 30\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{6}(m - 7)\right) + 65 \] April: \(T(4) = 30\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{6}(4-7)\right) + 65 = 30\cos\!\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) + 65 = 30(0) + 65 = 65°F\)
  • CheckApril is a transitional month midway between winter and summer, so the midline value (65°F) is a reasonable answer. ✓
Answer: \(T(m) = 30\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{6}(m-7)\right) + 65\). April temperature ≈ 65°F.
Example 13.13.2
Tidal height model
  • ProblemA harbor's tidal depth ranges from a low of 6 feet to a high of 14 feet. The period from high tide to high tide is 12.5 hours. Write a model for depth \(H(t)\) assuming the cycle begins at high tide.
  • Solve\[ a = \frac{14-6}{2} = 4 \qquad k = \frac{14+6}{2} = 10 \qquad b = \frac{2\pi}{12.5} = \frac{4\pi}{25} \] Starting at high tide (maximum), use cosine: \[ H(t) = 4\cos\!\left(\frac{4\pi}{25}t\right) + 10 \]
Answer: \(H(t) = 4\cos\!\left(\dfrac{4\pi}{25}t\right) + 10\), where \(t\) is hours after high tide.
Always Define Your Variable

In modeling problems, always state what your variable represents and what units you are using. Writing "where \(t\) is measured in hours" is part of a complete mathematical answer, not an optional detail.

Section 13.14

Common Trigonometry Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Calculator in the Wrong Mode
sin(30) in radian mode ≈ −0.988

Correct: sin(30°) = 0.5. Always verify the mode setting first. A quick check: sin(90°) should equal exactly 1 in degree mode.

Mistake 2 — Confusing Opposite, Adjacent, and Hypotenuse
Using sine when opposite and hypotenuse are known, then writing opposite/adjacent

Correct: Always label all three sides explicitly before choosing a ratio. The hypotenuse is always across from the right angle. The opposite and adjacent depend on which angle you are using.

Mistake 3 — Treating sin⁻¹ as a Reciprocal
sin⁻¹(0.5) = 1/sin(0.5) ≈ 1.919

Correct: sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30°. The inverse sine function returns an angle whose sine is 0.5. It is not the same as 1/sin(0.5).

Mistake 4 — Wrong Sign in Non-First-Quadrant Angles
cos(210°) = √3/2 (positive first-quadrant value used without sign adjustment)

Correct: 210° is in Quadrant III where cosine is negative. cos(210°) = −√3/2. Always apply the ASTC sign rule after finding the reference-angle value.

Mistake 5 — Swapping Amplitude and Period Formula
In y = a·sin(bx), saying "the period is a" or "the amplitude is 2π/b"

Correct: Amplitude = |a| (the coefficient in front). Period = 2π/|b| (depends on b, the coefficient inside). These are not interchangeable.

Mistake 6 — Claiming Tangent Has an Amplitude
"The amplitude of y = tan(x) is 1."

Correct: Tangent has no amplitude. Its range is all real numbers — it grows without bound in both directions and has no maximum or minimum.

Mistake 7 — Backwards Degree–Radian Conversion
90° to radians: 90 × 180/π (multiplying by the reciprocal of the correct factor)

Correct: Degrees to radians uses π/180. So 90 × π/180 = π/2 radians. Remember: to go to radians, multiply by π/180.

Mistake 8 — Evaluating tan(90°) as a Large Number
tan(90°) = 1,000,000 (just a very large number on the calculator)

Correct: tan(90°) is undefined because cos(90°) = 0, and division by zero is undefined. The calculator error or overflow message is correct — this value does not exist.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1Trigonometry studies ratios of sides in right triangles. These ratios are consistent across all similar triangles and extend to periodic functions that model repeating behavior. Algebra II builds the foundational layer before Precalculus deepens the study.
  • Sec 13.2A right triangle has one 90° angle and two acute angles summing to 90°. Sides are labeled relative to the chosen angle: hypotenuse (across from 90°), opposite, and adjacent. The Pythagorean theorem connects all three sides.
  • Sec 13.3The three primary ratios are sine (opp/hyp), cosine (adj/hyp), and tangent (opp/adj), remembered as SOH-CAH-TOA. These ratios depend only on the angle, not the triangle's size. A calculator in degree mode evaluates them numerically.
  • Sec 13.4Missing sides are found by setting up a trigonometric equation and solving. Missing angles are found using inverse functions (sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹). The Pythagorean theorem provides an additional check.
  • Sec 13.5Angles of elevation are measured upward from horizontal; angles of depression are measured downward. Drawing a clear diagram with labeled sides before choosing a ratio prevents most errors in these problems.
  • Sec 13.6The 45°–45°–90° triangle has sides in ratio 1:1:√2. The 30°–60°–90° triangle has sides in ratio 1:√3:2. These produce exact trig values at 30°, 45°, and 60° that should be derived, not simply memorized in isolation.
  • Sec 13.7A radian is defined from arc length equal to the radius. Since one full rotation = 2π radians = 360°, the conversion is 180° = π. Degrees-to-radians: multiply by π/180. Radians-to-degrees: multiply by 180/π.
  • Sec 13.8The unit circle has radius 1 centered at the origin. Every point on it at angle θ has coordinates (cos θ, sin θ). This extends trigonometry beyond right triangles to any angle, including angles greater than 90°.
  • Sec 13.9Reference angles (the acute angle to the nearest x-axis) allow trig values to be found for any angle. The ASTC rule determines signs: All positive in QI; Sine positive in QII; Tangent positive in QIII; Cosine positive in QIV.
  • Sec 13.10The graphs of y = sin(x) and y = cos(x) are smooth wave curves with amplitude 1, period 2π, and midline y = 0. Cosine is the sine graph shifted left by π/2. Both have domain all reals and range [−1, 1].
  • Sec 13.11The general form y = a·sin(bx) + k has amplitude |a|, period 2π/|b|, and midline y = k. If a < 0, the graph is reflected. These parameters allow sine and cosine to model any amplitude, speed, and center of oscillation.
  • Sec 13.12Tangent is defined as sin/cos. It has period π, vertical asymptotes where cosine equals zero, range of all reals, and no amplitude. Its graph consists of repeating rising branches separated by asymptotes.
  • Sec 13.13Periodic real-world situations can be modeled by sine or cosine. Given max, min, and period: amplitude = (max − min)/2; midline = (max + min)/2; b = 2π/period. Choose sine or cosine based on the starting position.
  • Sec 13.14Eight recurring errors: wrong calculator mode; mislabeling sides; treating sin⁻¹ as a reciprocal; dropping signs in non-QI angles; swapping amplitude and period; claiming tangent has amplitude; backwards conversion; mishandling tan(90°).

Key Terms Review

trigonometry
The branch of mathematics that studies relationships between angles and sides in triangles, and the behavior of periodic functions.
hypotenuse
The side of a right triangle that is across from the 90° angle; always the longest side.
opposite side
The side of a right triangle directly across from the angle being considered (not the 90° angle).
adjacent side
The side of a right triangle that forms the angle being considered and is not the hypotenuse.
sine
The ratio opposite/hypotenuse for a given angle in a right triangle. Written sin(θ).
cosine
The ratio adjacent/hypotenuse for a given angle in a right triangle. Written cos(θ).
tangent
The ratio opposite/adjacent, also equal to sin/cos. Written tan(θ).
SOH-CAH-TOA
A memory device: Sine = Opp/Hyp, Cosine = Adj/Hyp, Tangent = Opp/Adj.
inverse trig functions
sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹: functions that return an angle when given a ratio. Also called arcsin, arccos, arctan.
angle of elevation
The angle measured upward from horizontal to a line of sight toward a higher object.
angle of depression
The angle measured downward from horizontal to a line of sight toward a lower object.
special right triangle
A 45°–45°–90° or 30°–60°–90° triangle with side ratios that produce exact trig values.
radian
A unit of angle measure; the angle formed when arc length equals the radius. 2π radians = 360°.
unit circle
A circle of radius 1 centered at the origin. Points on it at angle θ have coordinates (cos θ, sin θ).
reference angle
The acute angle between the terminal side of an angle and the nearest x-axis. Always between 0° and 90°.
ASTC
"All Students Take Calculus": QI all positive, QII sine positive, QIII tangent positive, QIV cosine positive.
periodic function
A function that repeats its output values at regular horizontal intervals called periods.
amplitude
The maximum distance a periodic function reaches above or below its midline. Equal to |a| in y = a·sin(bx) + k.
period
The horizontal length of one complete cycle of a periodic function. For y = a·sin(bx) + k, period = 2π/|b|.
midline
The horizontal line y = k centered between the maximum and minimum of a periodic function.
vertical asymptote
A vertical line where a function's value grows without bound; tangent has asymptotes at odd multiples of π/2.
trigonometric model
A sine or cosine function used to represent a real-world periodic situation, with amplitude, period, and midline derived from the data.

Chapter 14: Probability, Statistics, and Algebraic Decision-Making

Chapter 13 introduced the mathematical language of periodic change — trigonometric functions that describe situations repeating in regular cycles. Chapter 14 turns in a different direction. Rather than studying repeating patterns, it studies uncertainty and data: how to count outcomes, measure likelihood, and make mathematical decisions when the future is not fully predictable.

The larger project of Algebra II has been learning to choose and use mathematical models for the world. Exponential functions model growth and decay. Logarithmic functions reverse exponential behavior. Trigonometric functions model cycles. Chapter 14 adds the tools of probability and statistical reasoning — models for situations where the answer is not certain, but where mathematics can still tell us what is likely, what is reasonable, and what conclusions the evidence can and cannot support. Students should understand that every chapter of this course has added a new class of model; Chapter 14 will be the last addition before Precalculus.

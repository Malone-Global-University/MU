Chapter 2 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Mathematics rarely describes a situation with a single equation. Real problems come with multiple conditions operating at the same time: a budget and a production limit, a mixture of two chemicals with a known ratio, three unknowns connected by three separate rules. Chapter 2 develops the tools needed to handle exactly these situations.

This chapter begins by revisiting two-variable systems of linear equations from Algebra I, then extends that work in two important directions. First, it moves into three-variable systems, where solutions become points in three-dimensional space. Second, it introduces matrices — a powerful organizational structure that allows systems of any size to be written, manipulated, and solved in a compact, systematic way.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to solve systems by graphing, substitution, elimination, and matrix methods. They should understand what it means for a system to have one solution, no solution, or infinitely many solutions, and they should recognize systems as mathematical models of real-world constraints, not merely as puzzles to be solved.

Opening Question

A small company manufactures three products. Each product requires time on a cutting machine, a welding machine, and a painting machine. The company knows how many hours each machine has available per day, and how many hours each product requires on each machine. How many units of each product should the company produce to use all available machine time exactly?

This is a real problem. Answering it requires setting up three equations with three unknowns and solving the system. By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to do exactly that — including a matrix-based method that professionals and computers use when the systems become even larger.

Why This Chapter Matters

Systems of equations appear throughout science, engineering, business, economics, computer science, and everyday decision-making. Nutritionists balance systems of dietary constraints. Engineers design circuits that must satisfy voltage and current conditions simultaneously. Economists model supply and demand. Programmers use matrix operations in graphics, machine learning, and data processing.

Matrices in particular are one of the most important tools in modern mathematics. They are the language of linear algebra, which appears in virtually every technical field. This chapter gives students their first systematic introduction to matrix thinking — a foundation they will build on in Precalculus, Calculus, Statistics, Physics, Computer Science, and Engineering.

Section 2.1

Systems as Multiple Conditions

Most situations in the real world are not governed by a single rule. A person planning a budget must satisfy both a spending limit and a savings goal at the same time. A chemist mixing two solutions must reach a specific concentration while using a fixed total volume. A factory scheduling production must respect limits on time, material, and workforce simultaneously.

Each of these situations involves more than one equation, and all of the equations must be satisfied at once. This is what a system of equations describes: a set of two or more equations that must all be true simultaneously for the same set of variables.

What a Solution Means

For a system of two equations in two variables \(x\) and \(y\), a solution is an ordered pair \((x, y)\) that satisfies every equation in the system at the same time. For a system of three equations in three variables, a solution is an ordered triple \((x, y, z)\) that satisfies every equation. The collection of all solutions is called the solution set.

Systems as Intersections

Geometrically, a linear equation in two variables describes a line. The solution to a two-variable system is the point or points where those lines intersect. If two lines cross at exactly one point, the system has one unique solution. If the lines are parallel and never meet, the system has no solution. If the lines coincide, the system has infinitely many solutions.

Writing a System

A system of equations is written with equations stacked vertically and a large brace on the left to signal that all equations belong together. For example:

\[\begin{cases} 2x + y = 7 \\ x - y = 2 \end{cases}\]

This system asks: find values of \(x\) and \(y\) such that both equations are true simultaneously.

Section 2.2

Reviewing Two-Variable Systems

Before extending to larger systems and matrix methods, it is worth reviewing the three main strategies for solving two-variable systems: graphing, substitution, and elimination. All three were introduced in Algebra I. Here we revisit them briefly and sharpen the techniques in preparation for more complex work.

Method 1: Graphing

Graph each equation on the same coordinate plane and identify the point of intersection. Graphing gives a visual picture of the system and makes it easy to recognize special cases, but is difficult when the intersection does not land on integer coordinates.

Example 2.2.1
Solving a system by graphing
  • ProblemSolve by graphing: \(\begin{cases} y = 2x - 1 \\ y = -x + 5 \end{cases}\)
  • ThinkBoth equations are in slope-intercept form. Graph each line and find where they cross.
  • Solve For \(y = 2x - 1\): \(y\)-intercept at \((0, -1)\), slope \(2\). Plot \((0,-1)\) and \((1,1)\).

    For \(y = -x + 5\): \(y\)-intercept at \((0,5)\), slope \(-1\). Plot \((0,5)\) and \((1,4)\).

    Diagram instruction: Draw a coordinate plane. Graph \(y = 2x-1\) rising steeply through \((0,-1)\) and \((1,1)\). Graph \(y = -x+5\) falling through \((0,5)\) and \((1,4)\). Mark the intersection point at \((2,3)\).
  • Check\(3 = 2(2)-1 = 3\) ✓    \(3 = -(2)+5 = 3\) ✓
Answer: The solution is \((2, 3)\).

Method 2: Substitution

Isolate one variable in one equation, then substitute that expression into the other equation. Substitution works especially well when one variable is already isolated or has a coefficient of 1.

Example 2.2.2
Solving a system by substitution
  • ProblemSolve by substitution: \(\begin{cases} x + 3y = 11 \\ 2x - y = 1 \end{cases}\)
  • ThinkSolve the first equation for \(x\), then substitute into the second.
  • Solve Step 1: \(x = 11 - 3y\)

    Step 2: \(2(11-3y) - y = 1 \implies 22 - 6y - y = 1 \implies 22 - 7y = 1 \implies y = 3\)

    Step 3: \(x = 11 - 3(3) = 2\)
  • Check\(2 + 3(3) = 11\) ✓    \(2(2) - 3 = 1\) ✓
Answer: \((2, 3)\)

Method 3: Elimination

Add or subtract multiples of the equations to eliminate one variable. Elimination is most efficient when the coefficients of one variable can be made equal by multiplying.

Example 2.2.3
Solving a system by elimination
  • ProblemSolve by elimination: \(\begin{cases} 3x + 2y = 16 \\ 5x - 2y = 24 \end{cases}\)
  • ThinkThe coefficients of \(y\) are \(+2\) and \(-2\). Adding the equations eliminates \(y\) immediately.
  • Solve \((3x+2y)+(5x-2y) = 16+24 \implies 8x = 40 \implies x = 5\)

    Substitute: \(3(5)+2y=16 \implies 2y = 1 \implies y = \tfrac{1}{2}\)
  • Check\(3(5)+2(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 15+1 = 16\) ✓    \(5(5)-2(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 25-1 = 24\) ✓
Answer: \(\left(5,\ \dfrac{1}{2}\right)\)
Choosing a Method

All three methods find the same solution. Graphing is best for visualizing; substitution is best when a variable is isolated; elimination is best with integer coefficients that can be matched. Later in this chapter, matrix row operations extend elimination to any size system.

  2. Solve by substitution: \(\begin{cases} y = 2x-3 \\ 3x+y=12 \end{cases}\)
  3. Solve by elimination: \(\begin{cases} 2x+5y=16 \\ 2x-3y=0 \end{cases}\)
  4. Solve by any method: \(\begin{cases} 4x-y=9 \\ 2x+3y=7 \end{cases}\)
Section 2.3

Systems in Three Variables

When a problem involves three unknown quantities, we need three equations. A system of three equations in three variables describes conditions that must be satisfied simultaneously by values of \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\).

A single linear equation in three variables — such as \(2x + y - z = 5\) — describes a flat plane in three-dimensional space. Three such planes can intersect at exactly one point (unique solution), share a line or plane (infinitely many solutions), or have no point in common (no solution). For most of this section, we focus on systems with a unique solution.

Example 2.3.1
Setting up a system from a word problem
  • ProblemA math quiz has 10 questions worth 100 points total. Questions are worth 5, 10, or 15 points each. There are twice as many 5-point questions as 15-point questions. Set up the system.
  • DefineLet \(a\) = number of 5-point questions, \(b\) = 10-point, \(c\) = 15-point.
  • Solve Total questions: \(a + b + c = 10\)
    Total points: \(5a + 10b + 15c = 100\)
    Twice as many 5-pt as 15-pt: \(a = 2c\) \[\begin{cases} a + b + c = 10 \\ 5a + 10b + 15c = 100 \\ a = 2c \end{cases}\]
The three equations capture all given conditions. This system will be solved in Section 2.4.
Practice 2.3Setting Up Three-Variable Systems
  1. A theater sells adult, student, and child tickets. On Saturday, 200 tickets were sold for $1,450 total. Adult tickets cost $10, student tickets $7, and child tickets $4. The number of adult tickets sold was equal to the combined number of student and child tickets. Write the system of three equations. (Do not solve yet.)
  2. Three numbers have a sum of 24. The second number is twice the first. The third number is 6 more than the first. Write the system.
Section 2.4

Solving by Substitution

Substitution works for three-variable systems the same way it works for two-variable systems, though the process takes more steps. The key is to isolate one variable in one equation and substitute that expression into the remaining two equations, reducing the system step by step.

Example 2.4.1
Three-variable system by substitution (using Example 2.3.1)
  • ProblemSolve: \(\begin{cases} a + b + c = 10 \\ 5a + 10b + 15c = 100 \\ a = 2c \end{cases}\)
  • ThinkEquation (iii) already gives \(a\) in terms of \(c\). Substitute \(a = 2c\) into the other two equations.
  • Solve Into (i): \(2c + b + c = 10 \implies b + 3c = 10\)   (iv)

    Into (ii): \(5(2c) + 10b + 15c = 100 \implies 10b + 25c = 100 \implies 2b + 5c = 20\)   (v)

    Multiply (iv) by 2: \(2b + 6c = 20\)   (vi)
    Subtract (v) from (vi): \(c = 0\)

    Back-substitute: \(b + 3(0) = 10 \implies b = 10\);   \(a = 2(0) = 0\)
  • Check\(0+10+0=10\) ✓    \(5(0)+10(10)+15(0)=100\) ✓    \(0=2(0)\) ✓
Answer: \(a = 0\), \(b = 10\), \(c = 0\). All 10 questions are 10-point questions.
Example 2.4.2
A clean three-variable system by substitution
  • ProblemSolve: \(\begin{cases} x + y + z = 6 \\ 2x - y + z = 3 \\ x + 2y - z = 2 \end{cases}\)
  • ThinkSolve equation (i) for \(x\): \(x = 6 - y - z\), then substitute into the other two equations.
  • Solve Into (ii): \(2(6-y-z)-y+z = 3 \implies 12-3y-z = 3 \implies 3y+z = 9\)   (iv)

    Into (iii): \((6-y-z)+2y-z = 2 \implies 6+y-2z = 2 \implies y-2z = -4\)   (v)

    From (v): \(y = 2z-4\). Into (iv): \(3(2z-4)+z = 9 \implies 7z = 21 \implies z = 3\)

    \(y = 2(3)-4 = 2\);   \(x = 6-2-3 = 1\)
  • Check\(1+2+3=6\) ✓    \(2(1)-2+3=3\) ✓    \(1+2(2)-3=2\) ✓
Answer: \((x, y, z) = (1, 2, 3)\)
Section 2.5

Solving by Elimination

The elimination method for three-variable systems extends naturally from the two-variable version. The idea is to systematically eliminate one variable at a time by adding or subtracting multiples of equations. Choose a variable to eliminate first; work with two different pairs of equations to produce two new equations in two unknowns; then solve that smaller system.

Example 2.5.1
Three-variable system by elimination
  • ProblemSolve: \(\begin{cases} 2x + y - z = 5 \\ x - y + 2z = 1 \\ 3x + 2y + z = 12 \end{cases}\)
  • ThinkEliminate \(z\) from two pairs of equations. Use (i)+(iii) since \(-z+z=0\); use \(2\cdot\text{(i)}+\text{(ii)}\) to eliminate \(z\) from the second pair.
  • Solve Pair 1 — multiply (i) by 2, add to (ii):
    \(4x+2y-2z+x-y+2z=10+1 \implies 5x+y=11\)   (iv)

    Pair 2 — add (i) and (iii):
    \(2x+y-z+3x+2y+z=5+12 \implies 5x+3y=17\)   (v)

    Subtract (iv) from (v): \(2y=6 \implies y=3\)
    Into (iv): \(5x+3=11 \implies x=\tfrac{8}{5}\)
    Into (i): \(2(\tfrac{8}{5})+3-z=5 \implies z=\tfrac{6}{5}\)
  • Check (i): \(\tfrac{16}{5}+3-\tfrac{6}{5}=\tfrac{25}{5}=5\) ✓   (ii): \(\tfrac{8}{5}-3+\tfrac{12}{5}=\tfrac{5}{5}=1\) ✓   (iii): \(\tfrac{24}{5}+6+\tfrac{6}{5}=\tfrac{60}{5}=12\) ✓
Answer: \(\left(\dfrac{8}{5},\ 3,\ \dfrac{6}{5}\right)\)
Tip — Fractional Answers Are Normal

Fractions are a completely normal outcome of elimination. A fractional intermediate result does not mean you made an error. Always verify by substituting back into all three original equations.

  2. Solve by elimination: \(\begin{cases} 2x+y-z=3 \\ x-2y+3z=1 \\ 3x+y+2z=11 \end{cases}\)
Section 2.6

Dependent and Inconsistent Systems

Not every system of equations has exactly one solution. When you recognize these special cases early, you can avoid wasted computation.

Inconsistent Systems

An inconsistent system has no solution. When solving, you will reach a contradiction — a false statement such as \(0 = 4\). Geometrically, for two variables the lines are parallel; for three variables the planes share no common point.

Example 2.6.1
Inconsistent system
  • ProblemSolve: \(\begin{cases} x + 2y = 5 \\ x + 2y = 9 \end{cases}\)
  • SolveSubtract the first equation from the second: \(0 = 4\). This is a contradiction.
Answer: No solution. The lines are parallel and never intersect.

Dependent Systems

A dependent system has infinitely many solutions. When solving, you will reach an identity — a statement such as \(0 = 0\) that is always true. Geometrically, for two variables the lines coincide.

Example 2.6.2
Dependent system
  • ProblemSolve: \(\begin{cases} 2x - y = 4 \\ 4x - 2y = 8 \end{cases}\)
  • SolveMultiply the first equation by 2: \(4x - 2y = 8\). This is identical to the second equation. Subtracting gives \(0 = 0\).
Answer: Infinitely many solutions — all points on the line \(2x - y = 4\).
Result While SolvingInterpretationSolutions
One unique solution foundConsistent and independentExactly one
Contradiction (e.g., \(0=5\))InconsistentNone
Identity (e.g., \(0=0\))Consistent and dependentInfinitely many
Student Warning

Do not stop working when you see a contradiction or identity without recording your conclusion. Write a clear statement about what it means. Identifying the system type and explaining why is part of the complete answer.

Section 2.7

Matrices as Data Structures

Solving systems by substitution or elimination works, but it is slow and error-prone for large systems. The next several sections introduce a systematic method using matrices — a way of organizing numbers in rows and columns that makes computation more efficient and less cluttered with variable names.

A matrix (plural: matrices) is a rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns and enclosed in square brackets. Each number in the matrix is called an entry. The entry in row \(i\) and column \(j\) is written as \(a_{ij}\).

Dimensions of a Matrix

The dimensions of a matrix describe its size: the number of rows by the number of columns, written as \(m \times n\) (read "m by n"). A matrix with 3 rows and 3 columns is a \(3 \times 3\) matrix. A matrix with 2 rows and 4 columns is a \(2 \times 4\) matrix.

\[B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & -4 \\ 3 & -1 & 5 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(2 × 4 matrix)}\]

Matrices provide a way to separate the structure of a system from the variable names. The important information — the coefficients and right-hand side values — is captured by the matrix while the variable labels are temporarily set aside. This makes computation cleaner and more systematic.

Section 2.8

Matrix Addition and Scalar Multiplication

Matrix Addition

Two matrices can be added only if they have the same dimensions. Addition is performed entry by entry: each entry of the sum is the sum of the corresponding entries.

Example 2.8.1
Matrix addition
  • ProblemCompute \(A + B\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix}3&-1\\0&5\end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix}2&4\\-3&1\end{bmatrix}\).
  • Solve\[A + B = \begin{bmatrix}3+2 & -1+4\\0+(-3) & 5+1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}5&3\\-3&6\end{bmatrix}\]
Answer: \(\begin{bmatrix}5&3\\-3&6\end{bmatrix}\)

Scalar Multiplication

A scalar is a single number. When you multiply a matrix by a scalar, you multiply every entry of the matrix by that number.

Example 2.8.2
Scalar multiplication
  • ProblemCompute \(3A\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix}2&-1\\0&4\end{bmatrix}\).
  • Solve\[3A = \begin{bmatrix}3(2)&3(-1)\\3(0)&3(4)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6&-3\\0&12\end{bmatrix}\]
Answer: \(\begin{bmatrix}6&-3\\0&12\end{bmatrix}\)
Properties of Matrix Addition

Commutative: \(A + B = B + A\)

Associative: \((A + B) + C = A + (B + C)\)

Additive identity: The zero matrix \(O\) of all zeros satisfies \(A + O = A\)

Additive inverse: \(A + (-A) = O\), where \(-A\) negates all entries

Student Warning

Matrix addition is only defined for matrices of exactly the same dimensions. A \(2 \times 3\) matrix and a \(3 \times 2\) matrix cannot be added — even though both have six entries. Always check dimensions before adding or subtracting.

Section 2.9

Matrix Multiplication

Matrix multiplication is the most powerful — and most initially surprising — operation on matrices. It is not performed entry by entry. Instead, it combines rows of the first matrix with columns of the second in a specific pattern called a dot product.

Two matrices can be multiplied — \(AB\) — only when the number of columns of \(A\) equals the number of rows of \(B\). If \(A\) is \(m \times n\) and \(B\) is \(n \times p\), then the product \(AB\) is an \(m \times p\) matrix.

The entry in row \(i\) and column \(j\) of \(AB\) is computed by taking the entries of row \(i\) of \(A\) and the entries of column \(j\) of \(B\), multiplying them together pair by pair, and summing all the results.

Example 2.9.1
Matrix multiplication (2 × 2)
  • ProblemCompute \(AB\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix}2&3\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}\), \(B = \begin{bmatrix}4&0\\-2&5\end{bmatrix}\).
  • ThinkBoth are \(2 \times 2\), so the product is \(2 \times 2\). Compute each entry as a dot product of a row of \(A\) with a column of \(B\).
  • Solve Row 1, Col 1: \((2)(4)+(3)(-2) = 8-6 = 2\)
    Row 1, Col 2: \((2)(0)+(3)(5) = 0+15 = 15\)
    Row 2, Col 1: \((1)(4)+(-1)(-2) = 4+2 = 6\)
    Row 2, Col 2: \((1)(0)+(-1)(5) = 0-5 = -5\) \[AB = \begin{bmatrix}2&15\\6&-5\end{bmatrix}\]
Answer: \(\begin{bmatrix}2&15\\6&-5\end{bmatrix}\)
Example 2.9.2
Non-square matrix multiplication
  • ProblemCompute \(AB\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix}1&2&3\end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix}4\\-1\\2\end{bmatrix}\).
  • Think\(A\) is \(1\times3\) and \(B\) is \(3\times1\). The product \(AB\) is \(1\times1\) — a single number.
  • Solve\[AB = \begin{bmatrix}(1)(4)+(2)(-1)+(3)(2)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4-2+6\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}8\end{bmatrix}\]
Answer: \(8\)
Critical Warning — Matrix Multiplication Is Not Commutative

Unlike ordinary multiplication, \(AB \neq BA\) in general. The order of the matrices matters and must never be reversed without explicit justification. Even when both \(AB\) and \(BA\) are defined and have the same dimensions, they are usually not equal. This is one of the most important properties distinguishing matrix arithmetic from real-number arithmetic.

Section 2.10

Augmented Matrices

An augmented matrix is a matrix that captures all the information in a system of linear equations: the coefficients and the constants, organized by row. Each row represents one equation. Each column of the coefficient matrix represents one variable. The vertical bar separates the coefficient matrix (left) from the constant column (right).

Writing an Augmented Matrix

For the system:

\[\begin{cases} 2x + y - z = 5 \\ x - y + 2z = 1 \\ 3x + 2y + z = 12 \end{cases}\]

the augmented matrix is:

\[\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 5 \\ 1 & -1 & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 2 & 1 & 12 \end{array}\right]\]

Student Warning — Zero Placeholders

When a variable is missing from an equation, its coefficient is zero. Write a zero in that column. The equation \(3x + 5z = 7\) with no \(y\) term becomes the row \(\begin{bmatrix}3 & 0 & 5 & | & 7\end{bmatrix}\). Forgetting the zero misaligns every subsequent column.

Example 2.10.1
Reading a system from an augmented matrix
  • ProblemWrite the system of equations represented by: \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&-3&8\\0&1&-2\end{array}\right]\)
  • SolveRow 1: \(x - 3y = 8\)    Row 2: \(y = -2\)
  • Back-subFrom row 2: \(y = -2\). Into row 1: \(x - 3(-2) = 8 \implies x = 2\).
Answer: \((x, y) = (2, -2)\)

  1. \(\begin{cases}3x+y=8\\x-4y=2\end{cases}\)
  2. \(\begin{cases}x+y+z=5\\2x-y=3\\y+3z=7\end{cases}\)
  3. \(\begin{cases}4x-z=1\\2x+y+z=9\\3y-2z=0\end{cases}\)
  4. \(\begin{cases}5x-2y+z=0\\x+3z=4\\2y-z=1\end{cases}\)
Section 2.11

Row Operations

The key to solving a system using matrices is to transform the augmented matrix into a simpler form using operations that preserve the solution of the system. These are called row operations. There are exactly three legal row operations, each corresponding to a legal manipulation of equations.

Row Operation 1 — Swap Two Rows

Swapping two rows reorders the equations. The solution is unchanged.

Notation: \(R_1 \leftrightarrow R_2\)

Row Operation 2 — Multiply a Row by a Nonzero Constant

Multiplying every entry in a row by a nonzero number is equivalent to multiplying both sides of an equation by that constant.

Notation: \(kR_i \to R_i\)

Row Operation 3 — Add a Multiple of One Row to Another

Adding a multiple of one row to another is equivalent to the elimination step of combining two equations. This is the most frequently used operation.

Notation: \(R_j + kR_i \to R_j\)   (row \(j\) is replaced by row \(j\) plus \(k\) times row \(i\))

Row Echelon Form

The goal of row operations is to reach row echelon form (REF). A matrix is in row echelon form when: each row that is not all zeros has a leading 1 (called a pivot) as its first nonzero entry; the pivot in each row is to the right of the pivot in the row above; and rows of all zeros, if any, appear at the bottom. The result looks like a descending staircase:

\[\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & * & * & * \\ 0 & 1 & * & * \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & * \end{array}\right]\]

where ✱ can be any number

Example 2.11.1
Applying row operations step by step
  • ProblemTransform toward row echelon form: \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}2&4&10\\3&1&7\end{array}\right]\)
  • Step 1Create a pivot of 1 in row 1.   \(\tfrac{1}{2}R_1 \to R_1\):   \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&2&5\\3&1&7\end{array}\right]\)
  • Step 2Eliminate the 3 below the pivot.   \(R_2-3R_1\to R_2\):   \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&2&5\\0&-5&-8\end{array}\right]\)
  • Step 3Create pivot in row 2.   \(-\tfrac{1}{5}R_2\to R_2\):   \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&2&5\\0&1&\tfrac{8}{5}\end{array}\right]\)
  • Back-sub\(y = \tfrac{8}{5}\);   \(x + 2\!\left(\tfrac{8}{5}\right) = 5 \implies x = \tfrac{9}{5}\)
Answer: \(\left(\dfrac{9}{5},\ \dfrac{8}{5}\right)\)
Section 2.12

Solving Systems with Matrices

The complete method — write an augmented matrix, apply row operations to reach row echelon form, then use back substitution — is called Gaussian elimination (after mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss). It extends cleanly to any number of variables.

Example 2.12.1
Gaussian elimination — two variables
  • ProblemSolve: \(\begin{cases}x+2y=7\\3x-y=7\end{cases}\)
  • Augment\(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&2&7\\3&-1&7\end{array}\right]\)
  • Step 1\(R_2 - 3R_1 \to R_2\):  \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&2&7\\0&-7&-14\end{array}\right]\)
  • Step 2\(-\tfrac{1}{7}R_2 \to R_2\):  \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&2&7\\0&1&2\end{array}\right]\)
  • Back-sub\(y = 2\);   \(x + 2(2) = 7 \implies x = 3\)
  • Check\(3 + 2(2) = 7\) ✓    \(3(3) - 2 = 7\) ✓
Answer: \((3, 2)\)
Example 2.12.2
Gaussian elimination — three variables
  • ProblemSolve: \(\begin{cases}x+y+z=6\\2x-y+z=3\\x+2y-z=2\end{cases}\)
  • Augment\(\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\2&-1&1&3\\1&2&-1&2\end{array}\right]\)
  • Step 1 \(R_2 - 2R_1 \to R_2\): row 2 becomes \([0, -3, -1\,|\,-9]\)
    \(R_3 - R_1 \to R_3\): row 3 becomes \([0, 1, -2\,|\,-4]\) \[\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\0&-3&-1&-9\\0&1&-2&-4\end{array}\right]\]
  • Step 2Swap \(R_2 \leftrightarrow R_3\) to avoid fractions: \[\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\0&1&-2&-4\\0&-3&-1&-9\end{array}\right]\]
  • Step 3 \(R_3 + 3R_2 \to R_3\): row 3 becomes \([0, 0, -7\,|\,-21]\)
    \(-\tfrac{1}{7}R_3 \to R_3\): row 3 becomes \([0, 0, 1\,|\,3]\) \[\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\0&1&-2&-4\\0&0&1&3\end{array}\right]\]
  • Back-sub \(z = 3\);   \(y - 2(3) = -4 \implies y = 2\);   \(x + 2 + 3 = 6 \implies x = 1\)
  • Check\(1+2+3=6\) ✓    \(2(1)-2+3=3\) ✓    \(1+2(2)-3=2\) ✓
Answer: \((x,y,z) = (1, 2, 3)\)
Recognizing Special Cases in Matrix Form

No solution: A row of all zeros on the left with a nonzero entry on the right — such as \(\begin{bmatrix}0&0&0\,|\,5\end{bmatrix}\) — reads as \(0=5\), a contradiction. Stop and write "no solution."

Infinitely many solutions: A row of all zeros on both sides — such as \(\begin{bmatrix}0&0&0\,|\,0\end{bmatrix}\) — reads as \(0=0\). The system is dependent.

  2. Solve by Gaussian elimination: \(\begin{cases}x+2y+z=4\\2x-y+3z=7\\3x+y-z=2\end{cases}\)
  3. Apply the indicated row operations to the matrix \(M = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}2&6&-4&8\\1&3&1&5\\-1&0&2&3\end{array}\right]\) in order: (a) \(\tfrac{1}{2}R_1\to R_1\)   (b) \(R_2 - R_1 \to R_2\)   (c) continue until reaching row echelon form.

Section 2.13

Systems in Business, Science, and Engineering

One of the most important reasons to study systems of equations is that they appear naturally in real-world problems. This section presents three extended applications, each showing how the same matrix tools apply across very different contexts.

Application 1: Nutritional Planning

A dietitian designs a meal using three foods — rice, chicken, and broccoli — to meet nutritional targets of 600 calories, 50 g of protein, and 80 g of carbohydrates.

FoodCalories/servingProtein (g)Carbohydrates (g)
Rice200445
Chicken150250
Broccoli50410

Let \(r\), \(c\), and \(b\) = servings of rice, chicken, and broccoli. The system:

\[\begin{cases} 200r + 150c + 50b = 600 \\ 4r + 25c + 4b = 50 \\ 45r + 0c + 10b = 80 \end{cases} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}200&150&50&600\\4&25&4&50\\45&0&10&80\end{array}\right]\]

Applying row operations (beginning with \(\tfrac{1}{50}R_1\), then elimination) produces an echelon form that can be solved by back substitution. Students should carry the row reduction through to completion in practice.

Application 2: Chemical Mixing

Example 2.13.1
Mixing two solutions to reach a target concentration
  • ProblemA chemist wants 10 liters of a 40% acid solution by mixing a 25% solution and a 60% solution. Find how many liters of each to use.
  • DefineLet \(x\) = liters of 25% solution, \(y\) = liters of 60% solution.
  • System\[\begin{cases}x+y=10\\0.25x+0.60y=4\end{cases} \longrightarrow \left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&1&10\\0.25&0.60&4\end{array}\right]\]
  • Solve \(R_2 - 0.25R_1 \to R_2\):  \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1&1&10\\0&0.35&1.5\end{array}\right]\)

    \(\tfrac{1}{0.35}R_2\to R_2\):   \(y \approx 4.29\);   \(x = 10-4.29 \approx 5.71\)
  • Check\(0.25(5.71)+0.60(4.29) = 1.43+2.57 = 4.00\) ✓
Answer: Mix approximately 5.71 liters of the 25% solution with 4.29 liters of the 60% solution.

Application 3: Break-Even Analysis

Company A has fixed costs of $4,000 and variable costs of $6 per unit. Company B has fixed costs of $1,500 and variable costs of $11 per unit. Let \(u\) = units produced and \(C\) = total cost. The system is:

\[\begin{cases}C = 6u + 4000 \\ C = 11u + 1500\end{cases}\]

Setting the expressions equal: \(6u + 4000 = 11u + 1500 \implies 2500 = 5u \implies u = 500\). Total cost at break-even: \(C = 6(500)+4000 = \$7{,}000\). Below 500 units, Company A costs more; above 500 units, Company B costs more.

Section 2.14

Common Systems and Matrix Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting to check the solution in all original equations

A small error in the middle of a multi-step process can produce a value that satisfies some equations but not all. Always substitute back into every original equation — not just the last one used.

Mistake 2 — Sign confusion in row operations
R₂ − 2R₁ when R₁ = [1, −3, 5]: student writes 1 − 2(−3) = 1 − 6 = −5

Correct: \(1 - 2(-3) = 1+6 = 7\). Subtracting a negative number means adding. Write each row operation component by component and check signs carefully.

Mistake 3 — Adding matrices of different dimensions
Adding a 2×3 matrix and a 3×2 matrix because "both have 6 entries"

Matrix addition is only defined when both matrices have exactly the same dimensions. Always check dimensions before adding or subtracting.

Mistake 4 — Reversing row and column order for entries
Reading a₁₂ as the entry in column 1, row 2

The entry \(a_{ij}\) is in row \(i\), column \(j\) — row first, column second. Remember: \(i\) comes before \(j\) in the alphabet, just as row comes before column.

Mistake 5 — Assuming matrix multiplication is commutative
Swapping AB to BA because "multiplication is commutative"

For matrices, \(AB \neq BA\) in general. The order matters and cannot be reversed without explicit justification. This is one of the most important properties distinguishing matrix arithmetic from scalar arithmetic.

Mistake 6 — Misreading a contradiction row as a zero solution
Seeing [0 0 0 | 2] and writing z = 0

A row of the form \([0\ 0\ 0\ |\ c]\) with \(c \neq 0\) reads as \(0 = c\), which is a contradiction. The system has no solution. Stop and write that conclusion clearly.

Mistake 7 — Omitting zero placeholders in augmented matrices
Writing [2, −1, 7] for the equation 2x − z = 7 (missing the y column)

Every variable must have a column entry, even when the coefficient is zero. The equation \(2x - z = 7\) becomes \([2, 0, -1\ |\ 7]\). Skipping the zero misaligns every column that follows.

Chapter 2 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 2.1A system of equations describes multiple conditions that must all be satisfied simultaneously. The solution set is the collection of all values that satisfy every equation at once.
  • Sec 2.2Two-variable linear systems can be solved by graphing (visual), substitution (one variable isolated), or elimination (matching coefficients). All three methods produce the same solution.
  • Sec 2.3Three-variable systems require three equations. Each equation describes a plane; the solution is the point where all three planes meet. Setting up the system correctly from a word problem is the essential first step.
  • Sec 2.4Substitution reduces a three-variable system to a two-variable system by isolating one variable and substituting. Back substitution then recovers all three values. Always verify in all original equations.
  • Sec 2.5Elimination removes one variable from two pairs of equations, producing a two-equation system. Fractional answers are normal. Checking in the original equations is mandatory.
  • Sec 2.6A contradiction (e.g., \(0=4\)) means no solution — the system is inconsistent. An identity (e.g., \(0=0\)) means infinitely many solutions — the system is dependent. Recognizing these outcomes early saves computation.
  • Sec 2.7A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers with defined dimensions \(m \times n\). Entries are labeled by row and column. Matrices allow a system to be organized without repeating variable names.
  • Sec 2.8Matrix addition and subtraction require matching dimensions and work entry by entry. Scalar multiplication multiplies every entry by a single number. These operations satisfy commutative, associative, and identity properties.
  • Sec 2.9Matrix multiplication requires the columns of the first matrix to match the rows of the second. Products are computed as dot products of rows and columns. Matrix multiplication is not commutative: \(AB \neq BA\) in general.
  • Sec 2.10An augmented matrix captures a system's coefficients and constants in a single organized array. Each row is an equation. Missing variables become zero entries. The coefficient matrix and constant column are separated by a vertical bar.
  • Sec 2.11Three row operations transform an augmented matrix without changing its solution: swapping rows, scaling a row by a nonzero constant, and adding a multiple of one row to another. The goal is row echelon form with a staircase of pivot 1s.
  • Sec 2.12Gaussian elimination applies row operations to reach row echelon form, then uses back substitution to find the solution. Contradiction rows mean no solution; all-zero rows signal a dependent system.
  • Sec 2.13Systems model real situations including nutritional planning, traffic flow, chemical mixing, and break-even analysis. The same matrix tools apply regardless of the application domain.
  • Sec 2.14Seven common errors: not checking all equations; sign confusion in row operations; adding incompatible matrices; entry index reversal; assuming commutativity; misreading contradiction rows; omitting zero placeholders.

Key Terms Review

augmented matrix
A matrix formed by placing the constant column of a system next to the coefficient matrix, separated by a vertical bar.
back substitution
The process of working upward through equations in row echelon form to find each variable's value.
coefficient matrix
The matrix formed by the coefficients of the variables in a system, without the constant column.
consistent system
A system of equations that has at least one solution (either unique or infinitely many).
dependent system
A consistent system with infinitely many solutions, identified by a row of all zeros in reduced form (an identity).
dimensions
The size of a matrix expressed as (rows) × (columns), such as \(3 \times 4\).
elimination
A method of solving systems by adding or subtracting multiples of equations to remove one variable at a time.
entry
A single number in a matrix; the entry in row \(i\) and column \(j\) is written \(a_{ij}\).
Gaussian elimination
The method of using row operations to put an augmented matrix into row echelon form, then solving by back substitution.
inconsistent system
A system of equations with no solution, identified by a contradiction (such as \(0=5\)) during solving.
matrix
A rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns and enclosed in square brackets.
matrix multiplication
The operation of combining two matrices by computing dot products of rows of the first with columns of the second. Not commutative.
ordered triple
A solution \((x, y, z)\) to a system of three equations in three unknowns.
pivot
The leading 1 in each row of a matrix in row echelon form; determines the staircase pattern.
row echelon form
A matrix form with leading 1s (pivots) descending in a staircase pattern and zeros below each pivot.
row operations
Three legal transformations: swapping rows, multiplying a row by a nonzero constant, and adding a multiple of one row to another.
scalar multiplication
The operation of multiplying every entry of a matrix by a single number (scalar).
solution set
The complete collection of all values that satisfy every equation in a system simultaneously.
substitution
A method of solving a system by isolating one variable and substituting its expression into the remaining equations.
system of equations
Two or more equations involving the same variables that must all be satisfied simultaneously.

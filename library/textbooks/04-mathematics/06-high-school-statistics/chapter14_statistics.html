Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter closes the book by drawing together every skill, concept, and habit of mind you have built across the previous thirteen chapters. You have learned to ask statistical questions, collect data responsibly, organize and display data with graphs and tables, summarize distributions numerically, model relationships with lines and curves, reason about probability, work with random variables and distributions, use normal models, understand sampling variation, estimate with confidence intervals, and test hypotheses. Now you will learn how those pieces connect into a single, coherent process: the complete statistical investigation.

This chapter is not simply a review. It introduces new thinking about how to design a full investigation, choose tools wisely, evaluate evidence honestly, communicate uncertainty, recognize misuse of data, and apply statistical reasoning to real decisions in science, public policy, finance, and technology. It also prepares you for the kind of statistical thinking you will encounter in college courses, careers, and civic life.

Opening Question

A city school district is debating whether to extend the school day by forty-five minutes. Supporters argue that longer school days improve test scores. Opponents argue that students are already fatigued. A local newspaper reports that "studies show longer school days raise achievement." How would you investigate this question fairly? What data would you need? What conclusions could you responsibly draw — and what conclusions would go too far? This chapter gives you the tools to think through every stage of that kind of investigation.

Section 14.1

The Full Statistical Investigation Process

Statistics is not a collection of isolated tools. It is a process — a structured way of asking a question, gathering evidence, examining that evidence, drawing conclusions, and communicating what was found. Understanding the full process is more valuable than knowing any single formula.

The statistical investigation process can be organized into seven stages. Each stage matters. A perfectly executed analysis cannot rescue a badly designed study, and a brilliantly collected data set is wasted if the conclusions are overstated or dishonestly communicated.

Stage 1
Ask
Form a statistical question that anticipates variability and specifies the population of interest.
Stage 2
Plan
Decide who or what to study, how to select them, what to measure, and how to minimize bias.
Stage 3
Collect
Gather data according to the plan. Record measurements consistently and accurately.
Stage 4
Organize
Structure, clean, and prepare the data. Handle missing values and errors. Document decisions.
Stage 5
Analyze
Construct graphs, compute summaries, fit models, and run inference procedures.
Stage 6
Interpret
Explain what the analysis reveals in the context of the original question, with attention to variability.
Stage 7
Communicate
Share findings honestly: what was studied, what was found, what it means, and what the limitations are.

Section 14.2

Asking a Statistical Question

The first and most important step in any investigation is asking a question that statistics can actually answer. A statistical question is a question that can be answered with data, anticipates variability in the answers, and specifies the population or context of interest.

Not a Statistical QuestionStatistical Question
How old is the oldest student in this class?How old are students in this school, on average, and how much does age vary by grade?
Did our school's soccer team win last year?What is the relationship between practice hours and win rates across high school soccer teams in our region?
What is the boiling point of water at sea level?How does the boiling point of water change with elevation, and how much does measured boiling point vary across different thermometers?
Characteristics of a Strong Statistical Question

A strong statistical question specifies what is being measured, who is being measured, and what the investigation hopes to learn. It is open enough to require data but focused enough to be answerable. It does not assume the answer before collecting data, and it is honest about the limitations of what data can reveal.

Example 14.2.1
Evaluating and revising a statistical question
  • ProblemA student writes this question: "Does eating breakfast help students do better in school?" Evaluate it and revise it into a strong statistical question.
  • ThinkThe question is well-intentioned but too vague. "Better in school" is not measurable. "Eating breakfast" is not defined. The population is unspecified.
  • RepresentA well-formed statistical question should specify: the population, the explanatory variable (breakfast frequency), and the response variable (a measurable academic outcome).
  • Analyze"Breakfast" needs to be defined — how many days per week? "Better in school" needs a measurable variable — GPA? test scores? The population should be stated — which school? which grade level?
  • InterpretRevised: "Among high school students at Jefferson High, is there a difference in quarterly GPA between students who report eating breakfast on most school days and students who report rarely eating breakfast?"
Answer: The revised question specifies the population, explanatory variable, response variable, and comparison. It anticipates variability in GPA and can be addressed with data.

Section 14.3

Planning Data Collection

Once a statistical question is clear, the next task is planning how to collect data that can actually answer it. Poor data collection is the most common reason that statistical conclusions go wrong — not calculation errors, not graph mistakes, but fundamental flaws in how data were gathered.

Four Elements of a Data Collection Plan

Who or what will be measured — the population of interest and how individuals will be identified.

How individuals will be selected — census, simple random sample, stratified, cluster, or systematic sample. Review Chapter 2 for full descriptions.

What will be measured and how — operationalize variables: translate an abstract concept into a concrete, consistent measurement.

How bias will be minimized — identify and address voluntary response, convenience sampling, leading questions, and measurement inconsistency.

Example 14.3.1
Evaluating a data collection plan
  • ProblemA student wants to study whether extracurricular participation is associated with higher GPAs. She plans to survey students who are at the library on Tuesday and Thursday afternoons.
  • ThinkConsider who will be in this sample and whether they represent all students at the school.
  • AnalyzeLibrary-goers on those days are not a random sample. They may be students who study more or who have fewer extracurricular commitments — which are precisely the variables being studied. Students heavily involved in sports or clubs may be busy at those times. This is a convenience sample with serious bias.
  • InterpretA better plan would use the school roster to select a simple random sample of students from all grade levels, then contact those students through a standardized survey sent to school email accounts.
Answer: The library sample is biased. Use a random sample from the full school roster instead.

Section 14.4

Organizing and Cleaning Data

Raw data is rarely ready for analysis. Real data sets contain errors, inconsistencies, missing values, and formatting problems. Data cleaning means reviewing a data set to identify and correct errors, make decisions about missing values, standardize variable formats, and check that the data are consistent with what was intended to be collected.

Data ProblemDescriptionHow to Handle
Missing valuesA respondent skipped a question or a sensor failed to recordExclude with documentation, use average substitution, or record missingness as a variable
Impossible valuesAge of 147 years; test score of 110 on a 100-point testInvestigate the source; mark as missing if the correct value cannot be recovered
Inconsistent categories"CA," "California," "calif." all mean the same thingStandardize to one canonical entry before analysis
Wrong variable typesA numeric column contains text entries like "N/A"Identify and recode before applying mathematical operations
Duplicate recordsThe same individual appears more than onceIdentify and remove duplicates; investigate cause
Why Documentation Matters

Every decision made during data cleaning should be recorded. If values are excluded, note how many and why. If a variable is recoded, document both the original and new coding. This allows others to understand, verify, and reproduce the analysis — a requirement of honest statistical practice.

Example 14.4.1
Identifying and handling data problems
  • ProblemA class collects survey data from 30 students. Issues found: three students left "hours of sleep" blank; one student reported 25 hours of sleep; the GPA column contains "3.7," "3,7," and "B+"; the "favorite subject" column contains "math," "Math," "MATH," and "mathematics."
  • ThinkEach issue requires a decision based on the goals of the analysis and the size of the data set.
  • Analyze Missing sleep: Analyze the 27 complete cases; note 3 non-responses in the report.
    25 hours of sleep: Impossible — check the original survey. If unrecoverable, mark as missing.
    "3,7": Use of comma as decimal separator — recode as 3.7. "B+": Not a GPA value — convert using an equivalence scale and document, or exclude with explanation.
    "math" / "Math" etc.: All mean the same thing — standardize to one entry (e.g., "Mathematics").
  • InterpretGood data cleaning balances completeness with accuracy. Each decision is documented so the analysis can be reproduced and evaluated by others.
Answer: Handle each type of error systematically, document every decision, and report cleaning choices in the methods section of the report.

Section 14.5

Choosing Displays and Summaries

Variable Type and GoalAppropriate DisplayRecommended Summary
One categorical variableBar graph, pie chart, frequency tableCounts and proportions
Two categorical variablesTwo-way table, segmented bar graphConditional distributions
One quantitative variable — symmetricDot plot, histogram, box plotMean and standard deviation
One quantitative variable — skewed or outliersDot plot, histogram, box plotMedian and IQR
Two quantitative variablesScatter plot (with possible model)Correlation r; slope and intercept
Quantitative variable across groupsSide-by-side box plots; back-to-back stem-and-leafMedian and IQR per group
What to Look For in Every Display

For quantitative data: always describe shape (symmetric, skewed left, skewed right, uniform), center (typical value), spread (range of values), and any unusual features (clusters, gaps, outliers). For scatter plots: describe direction, form, strength, and any outliers. For categorical data: compare proportions, not just raw counts — especially when group sizes differ.

Example 14.5.1
Choosing a display and summary
  • ProblemA researcher collects hours per week students exercise, along with whether they play a school sport (yes or no). She wants to display the data and choose a good numerical summary.
  • ThinkThere are two variables: one quantitative (hours of exercise) and one categorical (plays a sport: yes/no). The goal is to compare the exercise distributions between groups.
  • Choose a DisplaySide-by-side box plots allow visual comparison of center, spread, and shape for both groups. Back-to-back stem-and-leaf plots would also work for a smaller sample.
  • Choose a SummaryBox plots display five-number summaries. If the distributions are skewed or contain outliers, median and IQR are the appropriate numerical summaries for each group — computed separately.
Answer: Side-by-side box plots for the display; median and IQR for each group as numerical summaries.

Section 14.6

Choosing a Model

A statistical model is a mathematical description of a pattern in data. Models allow us to summarize a relationship, make predictions, and reason about what might happen under different conditions. But choosing the wrong model can lead to misleading summaries and poor predictions.

Model TypeEquation FormWhen to Use
Linearŷ = a + bxStraight-line pattern with roughly constant spread in the scatter plot
Quadraticŷ = ax² + bx + cCurved pattern opening upward or downward; data peak or valley
Exponential growthŷ = a · bˣ, b > 1Data increase at an increasing rate; multiplicative growth per unit
Exponential decayŷ = a · bˣ, 0 < b < 1Data decrease at a decreasing rate; approaches an asymptote
Logarithmicŷ = a + b · ln(x)Rapid early growth that levels off; diminishing returns
How to Choose a Model

1. Draw a scatter plot or graph of the data.

2. Describe the shape: linear? curved? accelerating? approaching a limit?

3. Try models whose shape matches what you observe.

4. Check fit using residual plots (see Section 14.7).

5. Consider context: does the model make sense for this type of variable in this situation?

Example 14.6.1
Choosing between a linear and exponential model
  • ProblemA student plants bacteria and records colony size (thousands of cells) every two hours: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 at hours 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10. Choose an appropriate model.
  • ThinkDoes y increase by a constant amount, or does it double each time?
  • AnalyzeThe increases are 1, 2, 4, 8, 16 — they are growing, not constant. Each time x increases by 2, y doubles. A linear model would predict equal additive increases; this data shows multiplicative growth.
  • Calculate The growth factor per 2-hour interval: 2 Growth factor per hour: √2 ≈ 1.414 Model: ŷ = (√2)ˣ Prediction at x = 12 hours: (√2)¹² = 2⁶ = 64 thousand cells
  • InterpretThe colony doubles every two hours. An exponential model fits because the data show constant multiplicative growth, not constant additive growth.
Answer: Exponential growth model. At x = 12 hours, the model predicts 64 thousand cells.

Section 14.7

Evaluating Model Fit

Choosing a model is only the beginning. Once a model is fit to data, you must evaluate whether it fits well. The primary tool for evaluating model fit is the residual plot.

Recall that a residual is the difference between an observed value and the value predicted by the model: residual = observed y − predicted ŷ. A residual plot displays residuals on the vertical axis and the explanatory variable (or predicted values) on the horizontal axis.

Residual Plot PatternWhat It MeansWhat to Do
Random scatter around zero, constant spreadModel fits wellProceed with the model
Curved pattern (U-shape or arc)Wrong model shape — linear fit to nonlinear dataTry a different model (quadratic, exponential)
Fan shape (spread increases with x)Non-constant variance across range of xConsider transforming the data
One extreme outlier, rest near zeroOne data point inconsistent with the patternInvestigate the unusual point; consider its influence
Student Warning — R² Is Not Enough

A high R² value does not guarantee a model fits well. A curved model might have a high R² but still fit poorly because the pattern is not the right shape. Always examine the residual plot alongside R². Extrapolation — using a model to predict far outside the range of observed data — is also unreliable even when R² is high within that range.

Example 14.7.1
Interpreting a residual plot
  • ProblemA student fits a linear model to car age vs. resale value. The residual plot shows residuals that are positive for young cars, negative for middle-aged cars, and positive again for old cars — a smile-shaped curve.
  • ThinkA curved pattern in the residual plot reveals that the model shape is wrong.
  • AnalyzeThe curved pattern means the linear model is systematically wrong: it overestimates value at intermediate ages and underestimates at young and old ages. The true relationship is not linear — cars lose value rapidly early and more slowly later.
  • InterpretThe residual plot is telling the investigator to try a different model. An exponential decay model should be fit, and its residual plot examined to see if it is now randomly scattered around zero.
Answer: The curved residual plot indicates a linear model is inappropriate. Replace it with an exponential decay model and recheck fit.
Practice 14.7Evaluating Model Fit
  1. A residual plot shows residuals all close to zero for small values of x, but large and positive for large values of x. What does this tell you about the model?
  2. A residual plot for a fitted exponential model shows residuals scattered randomly above and below zero with no pattern. What does this tell you?
  3. A student fits a linear model with r = 0.72. A classmate says R² = 0.518 is too low. A second classmate says r = 0.72 is strong enough. Write a paragraph explaining how to evaluate model fit more completely, using both the residual plot and R².
  4. Explain why extrapolation is risky, using a specific example from everyday life.
Section 14.8

Drawing Conclusions from Evidence

A statistical conclusion is a careful, evidence-based statement that answers the original question, uses the language of the analysis, acknowledges variability and uncertainty, avoids claiming more certainty than the data can support, and states the limits on what can be generalized.

One of the most important conclusions in all of statistics is also one of the most frequently violated: association does not prove causation. Two variables can be strongly correlated without either causing the other. Confounding variables, common causes, coincidence, and selection bias can all create the appearance of a causal relationship. True causal conclusions require randomized controlled experiments, ruling out confounders, replication, and a plausible mechanism.

Weak ConclusionStrong Conclusion
Students who eat breakfast do better in school.Among 87 surveyed students at Jefferson High, those who reported eating breakfast on at least four school days per week had a median GPA of 3.4, compared to 2.9 for students eating breakfast fewer than two days per week. This association cannot establish causation, as many other variables may differ between groups.
The new fertilizer works.In this randomized experiment, tomato plants assigned to the new fertilizer produced an average of 4.2 more tomatoes per plant (95% CI: 1.8 to 6.6) than control plants. This difference is statistically significant at α = 0.05.
Example 14.8.1
Writing a statistical conclusion
  • ProblemA randomized experiment assigns 60 students to a mindfulness program or control. After 8 weeks, the mindfulness group has mean stress score 4.1 vs. 6.3 for the control group. p-value = 0.002 at α = 0.05.
  • ThinkAddress the question, state what the data show, acknowledge the design, report the p-value, state the decision, and note limitations.
  • InterpretThe data provide strong evidence that the mindfulness program reduced self-reported stress scores. The mindfulness group's mean (4.1) was 2.2 points lower than the control (6.3). The p-value of 0.002 is far below α = 0.05, so the null hypothesis of no difference is rejected. Because participants were randomly assigned, a causal connection is supported — within this sample. However, the study used college students, so conclusions may not generalize to high school students without further research.
Answer: Reject the null hypothesis. The mindfulness program caused a statistically significant reduction in stress scores in this sample (p = 0.002). Generalization requires caution.
Practice 14.8Drawing Conclusions

  2. An observational study finds r = –0.81 between fast-food restaurants per capita and average life expectancy in counties. Is it appropriate to conclude that fast food causes shorter lives? Explain what other explanation might account for this correlation.
  3. A randomized experiment with 200 participants finds no statistically significant difference in weight loss between a new diet and a standard diet (p = 0.21). Write a complete conclusion including what the data showed and what can and cannot be concluded.
Section 14.9

Explaining Uncertainty

Statistical conclusions are not certain. They are estimates, decisions under uncertainty, or summaries of patterns — all of which involve variability and the possibility of error. A key mark of statistical literacy is the ability to explain uncertainty clearly and honestly.

How to Communicate Uncertainty

Confidence intervals — give a range of plausible values for a population parameter. "We estimate the mean commute time to be 28 minutes, with a 95% CI of 24 to 32 minutes."

Margin of error — state the precision of a poll or survey result. "42% support the measure, margin of error ±3 percentage points."

p-values and significance levels — convey how unusual the observed result would be if the null hypothesis were true; a p-value is not the probability that the null is correct.

Sample size limitations — small samples produce wide intervals and less reliable estimates.

Scope of generalization — state which population the conclusions apply to and which they do not.

Student Warning — Misinterpreting a Confidence Interval

A 95% confidence interval does not mean "there is a 95% chance the true parameter is in this interval." The confidence is in the method, not in any one interval. A 95% CI was constructed using a method that captures the true parameter in 95% of all possible repeated samples. Any specific interval either contains the true value or it does not.

Example 14.9.1
Communicating uncertainty in a poll result
  • ProblemA polling organization surveys 1,000 registered voters and finds 54% support Candidate A, with a margin of error of ±3.1 percentage points at the 95% confidence level. Explain what this means in plain language.
  • ThinkThe margin of error describes the precision of the estimate. The confidence level describes the reliability of the method.
  • InterpretBased on this poll, we estimate that between 50.9% and 57.1% of all registered voters support Candidate A. The margin of error means that if many different polls of 1,000 voters were conducted, about 95% of them would produce an interval containing the true proportion. This particular estimate could be off by up to 3.1 points in either direction due to random sampling variation.
  • CheckNote that the margin of error applies only to random sampling error. Bias from non-response, poorly worded questions, or voters who change their minds is not captured by the margin of error.
Answer: We are 95% confident the true proportion is between 50.9% and 57.1%. The margin of error reflects sampling variability only — not all sources of uncertainty.
Practice 14.9Uncertainty

  2. A hypothesis test produces p = 0.04. A student concludes: "There is a 4% probability that the null hypothesis is true." Explain the error in this interpretation and state the correct interpretation.
  3. A study of 18 patients produces a very wide confidence interval. A study of 180 patients produces a much narrower interval. Explain why sample size affects interval width.
  4. A national poll reports 61% support a new infrastructure bill, margin of error ±2.5%. Explain what the margin of error means and identify one source of uncertainty it does not capture.

Section 14.10

Communicating with Graphs and Tables

Statistical findings must be communicated clearly to be useful. A researcher who understands every step of an investigation but cannot explain it to others has not completed the work. Good statistical communication uses graphs, tables, clear writing, and honest representation of uncertainty.

Principles of Effective Statistical Communication

1. Lead with the question. The reader should know what question is being answered before data are presented.

2. Label everything. Every graph needs a title, labeled axes (with units), and a sensible scale. Every table needs a clear header for every column and row.

3. Describe what the display shows. Do not assume the reader will extract the correct conclusion from the graph alone. Write sentences describing shape, center, spread, and any unusual features.

4. Report summaries alongside graphs. A graph shows shape; a numerical summary gives precision. Both together tell a complete story.

5. Acknowledge limitations. Good statistical writing includes a sentence noting what the investigation cannot conclude.

6. Avoid misleading presentations. A truncated y-axis, inconsistent scales, or an unreadable pie chart distorts understanding as surely as a wrong calculation does.

Example 14.10.1
Writing a statistical report summary
  • ProblemA student collects sleep data from 120 students separated into student-athletes and non-athletes. Side-by-side box plots show: Athletes — min 5, Q1 6.5, median 7, Q3 7.5, max 9. Non-athletes — min 5.5, Q1 7, median 7.5, Q3 8.5, max 10. Write the graph description and a summary paragraph.
  • Graph DescriptionThe side-by-side box plots display nightly sleep hours for 120 students at Jefferson High, separated into student-athletes (n = 58) and non-athletes (n = 62). Both distributions are roughly symmetric with no extreme outliers. The median sleep time for non-athletes (7.5 hours) is 0.5 hours higher than for student-athletes (7.0 hours). Non-athletes show greater spread (IQR = 1.5 hours) compared to student-athletes (IQR = 1.0 hours). Both groups include students sleeping as few as 5 to 5.5 hours.
  • SummaryOn average, non-athlete students in this sample reported slightly more sleep per night than student-athletes. Because this is an observational study based on one school, we cannot conclude that playing sports causes less sleep. Additional investigation with a larger and more diverse sample would be needed to generalize these findings.
Answer: Graph description addresses shape, center, spread, and comparison. Summary paragraph interprets in context and acknowledges limitations.

Section 14.11

Data Ethics and Misuse of Statistics

Statistics is powerful. It can inform policy decisions, guide medical treatment, allocate resources, and shape public understanding of important questions. Because of this power, statistical work carries ethical responsibilities that go beyond mathematical accuracy.

Data ethics is the field of study concerned with responsible collection, use, analysis, and reporting of data. It addresses questions such as: Who has the right to collect this data? How should privacy be protected? Are the conclusions being reported honestly and in context?

Form of MisuseDescriptionWhy It Is a Problem

Ethical Statistical Practice

Ethical practice includes: collecting data only with proper consent; protecting respondent identity; reporting all results, not just favorable ones; acknowledging limitations and sample sizes; refusing to manipulate graphs; being willing to say "the data do not support a conclusion" when they do not; and considering who is affected by decisions made on the basis of statistical analysis.

Example 14.11.1
Identifying statistical misuse
  • ProblemAn advertisement: "In our study, people who took our supplement every day for six months had a 40% lower rate of common colds than the national average." Identify at least three concerns.
  • ThinkConsider the source of the study, the design, the comparison group, and what is not said.
  • Analyze 1. No comparison group: The claim compares supplement users to "the national average," not to a control group in the same conditions. Participants may differ in many ways that affect cold rates.
    2. No mention of randomization: If participants chose to take the supplement, the study is observational and cannot establish causation.
    3. Conflict of interest: The company conducted its own study — more susceptible to cherry-picking and reporting bias.
    4. Missing details: Sample size, duration, definition of "cold," and statistical significance are all absent.
  • InterpretThis advertisement uses the appearance of scientific evidence without providing the information needed to evaluate whether that evidence is trustworthy.
Answer: At least four concerns — no control group, no randomization, conflict of interest, and missing critical details.

Section 14.12

Capstone Project Design

A capstone statistical investigation is a full, original study that moves through all seven stages of the investigation process. This section describes how to design and carry out such a project.

A Capstone Report Should Include

1. Introduction: The statistical question, its importance, and the population of interest.

2. Data collection: Sampling method, variables measured, and steps taken to reduce bias.

3. Data organization: Sample size, data cleaning decisions, and summary of the cleaned data.

4. Analysis: Appropriate graphs and numerical summaries with written descriptions.

5. Inference (if applicable): Confidence intervals, margins of error, or hypothesis test results, interpreted in context.

6. Conclusion: Answer the original question. Acknowledge uncertainty. Discuss limitations. Note what follow-up study might address.

Accessible Capstone Project Categories

School-related data (grades, attendance, sleep, extracurriculars) · Health and fitness (exercise, screen time, nutrition) · Local community data (weather, prices, demographics) · Public data sets (sports statistics, government data) · Environmental data (temperature records, habitat observations)

Example 14.12.1
Capstone project plan
  • AskIs there a positive association between average nightly sleep and average quiz score among students at Jefferson High?
  • PlanUse a voluntary survey distributed to 80 randomly selected 10th-grade students. The survey asks about average nightly sleep (to the nearest half-hour) and average quiz score in math class. Responses collected anonymously.
  • CollectSurvey distributed during homeroom with school permission. Paper responses collected.
  • OrganizeData entered into spreadsheet. Missing values (students who don't know their quiz average) excluded with documentation. Impossible values checked.
  • AnalyzeScatter plot with sleep on x-axis and quiz score on y-axis. Compute r; fit a linear model if the pattern is linear. Examine residual plot to check fit. Report slope in context.
  • InterpretDescribe direction, form, and strength of association. Note whether the linear model fits well. Discuss whether a causal claim is appropriate given the observational design.
Answer: A complete plan specifying the question, population, sampling method, variables, analysis tools, and interpretation approach.

Section 14.13

Statistics in Public Policy, Science, Finance, and Technology

Statistical reasoning appears in virtually every professional and civic domain. Understanding where statistics is used and what it can and cannot tell us is an essential component of data literacy.

Public policy. Government agencies use statistical surveys, administrative data, and randomized evaluations to assess the effects of policies. The United States Census Bureau counts the population every ten years, and that count directly affects political representation and federal funding. Public health agencies use statistical models to track disease spread and evaluate vaccines. Environmental regulators use statistical sampling to monitor pollution levels.

Science. Scientific research depends on statistical methods at every stage. Experiments are designed with random assignment and control groups. Results are evaluated using p-values and confidence intervals. Reproducibility — the ability to repeat a study and get similar results — is a major concern in modern science, partly because p-hacking and small sample sizes have produced many published findings that do not hold up.

Finance. Financial analysts use statistical models to estimate investment risk, forecast prices, and evaluate financial products. Insurance companies use statistical models of life expectancy and accident rates to set premiums. Credit scoring uses statistical models to predict the likelihood that a borrower will repay a loan.

Technology. Machine learning and artificial intelligence systems are built on statistical foundations. A spam filter learns from data about which emails are spam and uses that model to classify new ones. A recommendation algorithm uses data about user behavior to predict preferences. In each case, the quality of the training data, the appropriateness of the model, and the care taken to avoid bias all determine how well the system works and how equitably it treats different users.

Section 14.14

Common Data-Based Decision Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Statistical Significance with Practical Importance
"p = 0.001 means this difference is important." Correct: A result can be statistically significant without being practically meaningful. A study of 100,000 participants might find a mean weight loss of 0.2 pounds with p = 0.001 — real but trivially small. Always ask: is the difference large enough to matter in the real world?
Mistake 2 — Treating an Observational Study as an Experiment
"People who do X have better outcome Y, so X causes Y." Correct: Observational studies find associations, not causes. Confounding variables, self-selection, and reverse causation can all explain an association without any direct causal link.
Mistake 3 — Generalizing Beyond the Sample
"Our study of 50 students proves all teenagers prefer X." Correct: Conclusions are valid for the population from which the sample was drawn. A study of 50 students from one school does not generalize to all teenagers without further research.
Mistake 4 — Ignoring Variability
"The average income in both cities is $45,000, so they are the same." Correct: Two communities with the same mean income may have very different distributions of wealth. Always consider variability alongside center — the same mean can describe very different distributions.
Mistake 5 — Trusting a Model Outside Its Range (Extrapolation)
"The linear model fit well between x = 10 and x = 50, so I'll use it to predict x = 500." Correct: Extrapolation is unreliable. A model's accuracy degrades outside the range of observed data. Predictions far beyond the data should be treated with great skepticism.
Mistake 6 — Ignoring Missing Data
"I excluded the missing responses and proceeded with the analysis." Correct: If data are missing systematically — not at random — the missing data may be informative. Surveys in which non-response is related to the variable being measured will produce biased estimates if missingness is simply ignored.
Mistake 7 — Presenting Misleading Graphs
Bar graph with y-axis starting at 80 makes a 4-point difference look enormous. Correct: The visual design of a graph should represent the data as accurately as the numbers do. A truncated y-axis, inappropriate scaling, or inconsistent bin widths can lead viewers to draw incorrect conclusions.
Mistake 8 — Using the Wrong Summary for the Shape
"I'll use the mean and standard deviation — I always do." Correct: The mean is pulled toward the tail in a skewed distribution and toward outliers. Use median and IQR when the distribution is not roughly symmetric.
Mistake 9 — Drawing Conclusions from One Study Alone
"One study proved that X causes Y. It's settled." Correct: No single study is definitive. Science and public policy should be based on the accumulated weight of evidence from multiple independent studies, ideally using different methods and populations.
Mistake 10 — Failing to State Limitations
"This study completely resolves the question." Correct: Every statistical investigation has limitations. A study that does not acknowledge them is not trustworthy. Responsible reporting includes an honest statement of what the study cannot tell us.

Section 14.15

Readiness for College Statistics and Data Science

This textbook has introduced you to the complete toolkit of high school statistics. As you look ahead to college courses, careers, and civic life, this section describes what comes next and how your preparation in this course connects to future learning.

College introductory statistics builds directly on this book. You will revisit sampling distributions, confidence intervals, and hypothesis testing with greater mathematical depth. You will encounter chi-square tests, one-sample and two-sample t-tests, paired tests, ANOVA, and regression inference. The logic and vocabulary you have developed here will be familiar — the machinery becomes more precise, but the reasoning is the same.

Probability and mathematical statistics (typically taken after Calculus I) derives sampling distributions from first principles using calculus, proves theorems about expected value and variance, and provides the rigorous mathematical foundation for everything from the Central Limit Theorem to maximum likelihood estimation.

Data science extends statistical reasoning into computing. You will learn programming languages (Python, R), work with large data sets, apply machine learning models, and use statistical inference to evaluate whether a model has learned a real pattern or is simply fitting noise. The critical thinking skills from this course — evaluating model fit, identifying bias, communicating uncertainty, and practicing data ethics — are just as central to data science as to classical statistics.

The most important things you have learned in this course are not specific formulas or calculator sequences. They are habits of mind. Ask how the data were collected before trusting a conclusion. Look at the distribution before computing a summary. Consider variability before making a decision. State uncertainty honestly. Ask whether an observed association could be explained by confounding. Recognize when a model is being stretched beyond its appropriate range. Question graphs and numbers that seem designed to mislead. Protect the people whose data you use.

Statistics is not finished when the textbook ends. The questions that will matter most in the coming decades — about artificial intelligence, climate change, public health, economic inequality, and technological risk — will all require rigorous, honest, and ethical use of data. You are prepared to begin.

Chapter 14 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 14.1The statistical investigation process has seven stages: Ask, Plan, Collect, Organize, Analyze, Interpret, and Communicate. Every stage matters. A flawed plan cannot be rescued by sophisticated analysis.
  • Sec 14.2A statistical question anticipates variability, can be answered with data, and specifies the population of interest. Weak questions are too vague, too narrow, or already answered by a known fact.
  • Sec 14.3A data collection plan specifies who will be measured, how they will be selected, what will be measured, and how bias will be minimized. Poor collection cannot be fixed by analysis.
  • Sec 14.4Data cleaning addresses missing values, impossible values, inconsistent categories, wrong variable types, and duplicates. All cleaning decisions should be documented. In real investigations, cleaning often takes more time than the analysis itself.
  • Sec 14.5Displays and summaries should match the variable type and the distribution shape. Compare proportions rather than raw counts when group sizes differ. Describe shape, center, spread, and unusual features in every quantitative display.
  • Sec 14.6Model selection requires matching the model shape to the pattern in the data. Look at a scatter plot or graph first, then try models whose shape matches. Check fit with residual plots.
  • Sec 14.7A well-fitting model produces a residual plot with random scatter around zero. Curved patterns indicate the wrong model shape. Fan shapes indicate non-constant variance. R² alone is not enough to confirm a good fit.
  • Sec 14.8Statistical conclusions should be specific, grounded in evidence, and honest about uncertainty. Association does not prove causation. Conclusions are only as broad as the data allow — scope of inference is determined by the study design and the sample.
  • Sec 14.9Uncertainty is communicated through confidence intervals, margins of error, p-values, and honest statements of limitations. A 95% CI reflects the reliability of the method, not the probability that any one interval contains the true value.
  • Sec 14.10Effective statistical communication labels everything, describes what displays show, reports numerical summaries alongside graphs, acknowledges limitations, and avoids presentations that distort the message visually.
  • Sec 14.11Statistical misuse includes cherry-picking, misleading graphs, overgeneralization, p-hacking, suppressing uncertainty, and algorithmic bias. Ethical practice requires honest reporting, protection of privacy, and acknowledgment of limitations.
  • Sec 14.12A capstone project follows all seven stages and produces a report including an introduction, methods, analysis (graphs and summaries), inference where applicable, and a conclusion that acknowledges uncertainty and limitations.
  • Sec 14.13Statistical reasoning appears in public policy, science, finance, and technology. Statistical literacy — the ability to evaluate evidence, communicate uncertainty, and recognize misuse — is an essential skill for civic life and most professional fields.
  • Sec 14.14Ten recurring errors in data-based decisions: confusing significance with importance; treating observational studies as experiments; overgeneralizing; ignoring variability; extrapolating; ignoring missing data; misleading graphs; wrong summary for the shape; drawing conclusions from one study; failing to state limitations.
  • Sec 14.15College statistics, probability, and data science all build on the reasoning developed in this course. The most important outcomes are habits of mind: question how data were collected, consider variability, state uncertainty honestly, and protect the people whose data you use.

Key Terms Review

statistical investigation process
The seven-stage process of Ask, Plan, Collect, Organize, Analyze, Interpret, and Communicate.
statistical question
A question that anticipates variability, can be answered with data, and specifies the population of interest.
data cleaning
Reviewing a data set to identify and address errors, missing values, inconsistencies, and impossible values before analysis.
model selection
Choosing a mathematical model (linear, quadratic, exponential, etc.) whose shape matches the pattern in the data.
residual plot
A graph of residuals against the explanatory variable or predicted values, used to assess whether a model fits the data well.
model fit
How well a statistical model describes the pattern in data, evaluated using residual plots, r², and contextual reasonableness.
conclusion in context
A statement that answers the original question using the language of the analysis, while acknowledging variability, limitations, and the scope of generalization.
uncertainty
The range of possible variation in an estimate or decision, communicated through confidence intervals, margins of error, or p-values.
data ethics
The principles governing responsible collection, use, analysis, and reporting of data, including consent, privacy, honest reporting, and protection from misuse.
statistical significance
A result is statistically significant if the observed data are unlikely under the null hypothesis at the stated significance level. Not the same as practical importance.
practical significance
An effect large enough to matter in the real world, as judged by the context of the investigation. Distinct from statistical significance.
overgeneralization
Drawing conclusions about a broader population than the data can support.
reproducibility
The ability to repeat an investigation and obtain similar results — a key standard of reliable scientific evidence.
data literacy
The ability to read, understand, interpret, and critically evaluate data and statistical arguments.
p-hacking
Running many statistical tests until a significant result is found, then reporting only that result. Inflates the false positive rate.
extrapolation
Using a model to predict values far outside the range of observed data. Unreliable and should be treated with caution.
confounding variable
A variable that is related to both the explanatory variable and the response variable, which can create the appearance of a causal relationship.
scope of inference
The population to which a study's conclusions can legitimately be generalized, determined by the sampling method and study design.
cherry-picking
Reporting only the findings that support a desired conclusion while ignoring findings that do not. A form of statistical misuse.
margin of error
The maximum expected difference between a sample statistic and the true population parameter, due to random sampling variation.

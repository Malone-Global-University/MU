Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Numbers alone rarely reveal what data are trying to say. A list of one hundred test scores, the daily high temperatures for an entire year, or the finishing times of runners in a race may each be perfectly accurate as raw numbers — and yet each list is nearly impossible to interpret until it is organized, arranged, and displayed in a form that makes its patterns visible. That is exactly what this chapter is about.

Chapter 4 teaches you how to display quantitative data — data that take numerical values and that can be measured or counted along a scale. You will learn to construct and interpret dot plots, stem-and-leaf plots, histograms, box plots, and cumulative frequency displays. More importantly, you will learn how to read these graphs: how to describe the shape of a distribution, identify the center and spread, notice clusters and gaps, and recognize values that stand apart from the rest. These skills are not cosmetic. They are the foundation for every analysis that follows in this book.

This chapter also connects graphical thinking to geometric reasoning. The shape of a distribution — whether it is roughly symmetric, stretched to one side, or spread broadly and evenly — carries statistical meaning. Learning to see that shape, and to describe it precisely, is one of the most important skills a statistics student can develop.

Opening Question

A school records the number of minutes students spend on homework each night. Some students report 10 minutes; others report 180 minutes. Most cluster somewhere in between. How would you display this data so that a viewer could immediately see where most students fall, how spread out the values are, and whether any values seem unusually high or low? By the end of this chapter, you will have several tools to answer that question — and you will know when each tool is most appropriate.

Why This Chapter Matters

Data displays are not decoration. They are analytical tools. A well-constructed graph lets you see patterns that arithmetic alone hides. Scientists use histograms to detect anomalous measurements. Doctors use distribution plots to identify patients whose values fall far outside the typical range. Economists use displays to identify income inequality. Public health researchers use distributions to track how disease spreads across a population. Engineers use displays to monitor whether a manufacturing process is staying within acceptable bounds.

In every case, the display does the same fundamental job: it transforms a list of numbers into a visual picture of variation. Learning to construct these displays correctly, and to read them critically, is among the most practical skills that statistics offers.

Section 4.1

What Quantitative Data Measure

In Chapter 3, you worked with categorical data — data that sort individuals into named groups or categories, such as preferred music genre, political affiliation, or brand of phone. Categorical data answer the question "which kind?" or "which group?" and are displayed with bar graphs, pie charts, two-way tables, and segmented bars.

Quantitative data are different. They answer the question "how much?" or "how many?" They take numerical values along a meaningful scale, and arithmetic operations on those values are meaningful. The temperature outside is quantitative. So is a student's GPA, the distance a car travels on a tank of gas, the number of push-ups a person completes in one minute, or the time it takes water to boil.

Within quantitative data, statisticians distinguish two types. Discrete quantitative data take values from a countable set — whole numbers or specific integers. The number of children in a family is discrete: a family can have 0, 1, 2, or 3 children, but not 2.7. Continuous quantitative data can take any value within a range, including decimals and fractions. Height, weight, time, and temperature are typically treated as continuous because they can be measured to any degree of precision your instrument allows.

This distinction matters for display choices and for later analysis, but at the high school level the more important distinction is simply: does the variable take numerical values along a scale? If yes, it is quantitative, and the displays in this chapter apply.

Interpreting Quantitative Data in Context

Always identify the units when describing quantitative data. "The typical value is about 35" means nothing without knowing whether the unit is seconds, dollars, miles per hour, or milligrams. Units anchor quantitative data to the real-world situation they describe.

Section 4.2

Frequency Tables for Quantitative Data

Before constructing any graph, you often begin by organizing quantitative data into a frequency table. Unlike a categorical frequency table — which simply lists group names and their counts — a quantitative frequency table must first group the numerical values into intervals called class intervals or bins. Each class interval spans a range of values, and the table records how many data values fall within each range.

To build a frequency table, you must decide on two things: where the intervals begin and end, and how wide each interval should be. These choices affect how the resulting table and graph will look. Good choices reveal the shape of the data; poor choices can hide important features or create a misleading picture.

Steps for Building a Frequency Table for Quantitative Data

Step 1: Find the minimum and maximum values in the data set.

Step 2: Calculate the range: range = maximum − minimum.

Step 3: Choose a number of class intervals — typically between 5 and 15 for most data sets. More data generally support more intervals.

Step 4: Divide the range by the number of intervals to estimate the interval width. Round to a convenient number.

Step 5: Set the left boundary of the first interval at or just below the minimum. Build each subsequent interval by adding the width.

Step 6: Tally the data values and record the frequency (count) in each interval. Add a relative frequency column (proportion or percent) if useful.

Student Warning — Boundary Conventions

When a data value falls exactly on a class boundary, you must have a consistent rule for which interval it belongs to. The most common convention is to include the left boundary and exclude the right boundary: [20, 40) includes 20 but not 40; 40 belongs to [40, 60). Always state your convention, especially when values fall exactly on boundaries.

Section 4.3

Dot Plots

A dot plot is one of the simplest displays for quantitative data. To construct a dot plot, you draw a number line that spans the range of the data, then place one dot directly above the number line for each data value. When values repeat, the dots stack vertically. The result is a picture of the distribution: you can immediately see where data values cluster, where there are gaps, and whether any values stand far apart from the others.

Dot plots are especially effective for small to moderate data sets — roughly 5 to 50 values. They preserve every data value (unlike a histogram, which groups values into intervals) and make it easy to count exact frequencies. For very large data sets, dot plots become cluttered and histograms or box plots are more practical.

Display Instruction — Dot Plot

Draw a horizontal number line. Mark a scale that begins slightly below the minimum data value and extends slightly above the maximum, using consistent, evenly spaced tick marks. For each data value, place a dot directly above the corresponding position on the number line. Stack dots vertically when values repeat, keeping them neatly aligned. Label the axis with the variable name and units. Give the plot a descriptive title.

  • Represent Quiz Scores (out of 10) · · · · · · · · · ───────────────────── 5 6 7 8 9 10

The score 5 appears once and stands somewhat apart from the others — it may be worth investigating whether that student was absent for a key review session.
Answer: The distribution clusters between 7 and 9. A score of 5 appears once and is noticeably lower than the rest.
Dot Plots and Individual Values

One advantage of a dot plot over a histogram is that it preserves every individual value. You can count exactly how many times each value appears, identify which specific values are repeated, and trace any individual data point. This makes dot plots especially useful when you want to describe the data in detail rather than just its overall shape.

Section 4.4

Stem-and-Leaf Plots

A stem-and-leaf plot (sometimes called a stemplot) is a display that organizes quantitative data by splitting each value into two parts: a stem — the leading digit or digits — and a leaf — the final digit. Stems are listed vertically in order from least to greatest, and the leaves for each stem are written horizontally in a row to the right of the stem. The result is a kind of sideways histogram that also preserves the original data values.

For two-digit numbers, the tens digit is typically the stem and the ones digit is the leaf. For three-digit numbers, the hundreds digit (or the hundreds and tens together) becomes the stem. The key is to make the stem meaningful — it should capture the largest portion of each value while the leaf shows the fine detail.

Steps for Constructing a Stem-and-Leaf Plot

Step 1: Identify the stems — typically the leading digit(s). List them in order from least to greatest in a vertical column.

Step 2: For each data value, write the leaf (final digit) in the row corresponding to its stem. Initially, leaves may be written in the order the data appear.

Step 3: Reorder the leaves in each row from least to greatest. This makes the display easier to read.

Step 4: Add a title and a key. The key explains how to read one value. Example: 4 | 7 = 47 minutes.

Example 4.4.1
Constructing a stem-and-leaf plot for test scores
  • ProblemFifteen students earn the following scores on an exam: 72, 85, 91, 68, 77, 83, 95, 74, 88, 61, 79, 82, 90, 76, 84. Construct a stem-and-leaf plot.
  • ThinkThe scores range from 61 to 95. The tens digits (6, 7, 8, 9) will be the stems. The ones digits will be the leaves.
  • Represent Stem | Leaves 6 | 1 8 7 | 2 4 6 7 9 8 | 2 3 4 5 8 9 | 0 1 5 Key: 7 | 4 = 74 points
  • CheckCounting leaves: 2 + 5 + 5 + 3 = 15 values. ✓ The minimum leaf visible is 61 and the maximum is 95. ✓
  • InterpretThe distribution is roughly symmetric, centered in the 70s and 80s. Most scores fall between 72 and 88. The lowest scores (61, 68) and highest (91, 95) each appear in only one or two cases.
Answer: The stemplot shows a roughly mound-shaped distribution centered in the high 70s to mid-80s range.

Back-to-Back Stem-and-Leaf Plots

When you want to compare two groups, a back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot places the stems in the center column and the leaves for one group on the left and the leaves for the other group on the right. This layout makes direct visual comparison easy: you can see whether the two distributions have similar or different centers, similar or different spreads, and whether one group is shifted higher or lower than the other.

  • Represent Athletes Stem Non-Athletes 9 8 5 4 2 | 5 | 3 2 1 0 | 6 | 6 8 | 7 | 0 1 2 4 5 6 8 | 8 | 0 Key: 5|8 (Athletes) = 58 bpm 6|8 (Non-athletes) = 68 bpm

Student Warning — Ordering Leaves

The leaves in each row of a stem-and-leaf plot must be ordered from least to greatest — or, in a back-to-back plot, ordered away from the stem. If leaves are left in the order data were collected, the plot cannot be easily read for shape or values. Always order the leaves before declaring the plot finished.

Section 4.5

Histograms

A histogram is the most widely used display for quantitative data. It looks similar to a bar graph, but there are important differences. In a bar graph for categorical data, the bars represent named categories, and there are gaps between bars because the categories are distinct groups. In a histogram, the bars represent class intervals along a continuous numerical scale, and the bars touch one another because the intervals are adjacent on the number line — there is no natural gap between them.

The height of each bar represents the frequency (or relative frequency) of data values in that interval. The width of each bar represents the width of the class interval. A critical point that many students miss: in a histogram, it is the area of each bar — not just its height — that is proportional to the number of data values in that interval. When all bars have equal width (the usual case at this level), height and area carry the same information. But this geometric interpretation of area becomes very important in Chapter 11 when you study normal distributions, where areas under a curve represent probabilities.

Key Differences: Bar Graph vs. Histogram

Bar graph — bars represent categories; gaps between bars; order may be arbitrary; height = frequency of category.

Histogram — bars represent class intervals on a number line; bars touch; order is fixed by the scale; height (or area) = frequency of values in interval.

Do not use a bar graph to display quantitative data. Do not put gaps between histogram bars.

Display Instruction — Histogram

Draw a horizontal axis labeled with the measurement variable and units. Mark the class interval boundaries as tick marks along the axis. Draw a vertical axis labeled "Frequency" (or "Count") with an appropriate scale. For each interval, draw a rectangle whose left and right sides align with the interval boundaries and whose height equals the frequency. Bars should touch — there are no gaps. Give the histogram a descriptive title.

Reading a Histogram

When reading a histogram, look for four things: (1) the location of the tallest bar — that is roughly where the center lies; (2) how wide the bars are spread — that tells you about the spread; (3) whether the histogram is roughly symmetric, or whether one tail is longer; and (4) whether any bars are isolated by a gap — those may indicate unusual values.

Section 4.6

Choosing Class Intervals

One of the most consequential decisions in constructing a histogram is the choice of class interval width. A poor choice can dramatically distort the picture of the data — not through dishonesty, but simply by grouping data in a way that hides variation or creates false patterns.

If you choose intervals that are too wide, the histogram will have very few bars and will look flat and uninformative. All the variation within each interval is hidden. You lose the ability to see where values cluster within a broad range, and important features like bimodal patterns (two peaks) may disappear entirely.

If you choose intervals that are too narrow, the histogram will have many thin bars, most of which may contain only one or zero values. The display looks jagged and noisy rather than smooth. The overall shape of the distribution is hard to discern because every small fluctuation in the data creates a visible spike or gap.

A good choice of interval width produces a histogram with enough bars to show the shape of the distribution — typically somewhere between 5 and 15 bars for most classroom data sets — while smoothing out small random fluctuations. There is no single formula, but a common starting rule is to aim for roughly the square root of the number of data values as the number of bars. From there, adjust using judgment and by trying a few alternatives.

Example 4.6.1
The effect of interval width on a histogram's appearance
  • ProblemThe ages (in years) of 30 museum visitors are collected. The minimum is 8 and the maximum is 72. Compare histograms using interval widths of 5, 10, and 20. Describe what each reveals and hides.
  • ThinkWith width 5, there are about (72−8)/5 ≈ 13 bars. With width 10, about 6–7 bars. With width 20, about 3–4 bars.
  • Analyze
    • Width = 5 (many narrow bars): The display is detailed but jagged. Small random variations between adjacent bars look like real patterns. It may be difficult to see the overall shape.
    • Width = 10 (moderate): The shape is clear — perhaps a concentration in the 20–40 range, with fewer visitors at young and old extremes. This is likely the most informative choice for 30 data values.
    • Width = 20 (very wide): The histogram has only 3–4 bars. All variation within each 20-year range is hidden. You cannot tell whether most visitors in the 20–40 range are in their 20s or 30s.
  • InterpretThe width-10 histogram is the best choice here. It reveals the shape without excessive noise and uses a manageable number of bars for 30 data values.
Answer: Width = 10 gives the clearest picture for this data set. Wider intervals hide variation; narrower intervals produce jagged, noisy displays.
Data Ethics Note — Interval Width and Misleading Histograms

Someone who wants to make a distribution look more spread out, more concentrated, or more uniform than it really is can manipulate the interval width of a histogram to achieve that appearance — without altering the data. Be skeptical of histograms where the interval width is very unusual or not clearly labeled. Always check the axis scale and interval width before drawing conclusions from any histogram you did not construct yourself.

Section 4.7

Relative Frequency Histograms

A relative frequency histogram is identical to a regular histogram in construction, except that the vertical axis shows relative frequencies — proportions or percentages — rather than raw counts. The height of each bar tells you what fraction (or what percent) of all data values fall in that interval.

Relative frequency histograms are especially valuable when you want to compare two distributions that have different total numbers of data values. If one class has 20 students and another has 35, a frequency histogram will make the larger class look more prominent simply because its bars are taller. A relative frequency histogram removes this size effect and lets you compare the shapes and centers of the two distributions directly.

Relative frequency histograms also connect naturally to probability. If you treat the relative frequency as the probability that a randomly selected value from the data set falls in a given interval, the histogram begins to look like a probability model. This connection will become very important in Chapter 11 when you study normal distributions.

Section 4.8

Box Plots

A box plot (also called a box-and-whisker plot) is a display that summarizes a quantitative distribution using five key values, called the five-number summary: the minimum, the first quartile (Q1), the median, the third quartile (Q3), and the maximum. These five numbers divide the data into four roughly equal-sized groups: the bottom 25%, the lower middle 25%, the upper middle 25%, and the top 25%.

In a box plot, a rectangular box is drawn from Q1 to Q3. A line inside the box marks the median. Two lines — called whiskers — extend from the box outward to the minimum and maximum values (or, when outliers are marked separately, to the furthest non-outlier values). The box spans the middle half of the data; its width is called the interquartile range (IQR). The whiskers show how far the lower and upper quarters of data extend. Outliers, when present, are typically plotted as individual dots beyond the whiskers.

Box plots are especially powerful for comparing two or more distributions side by side, because multiple box plots can be drawn on the same axis. At a glance, you can compare centers (where each median line falls), spreads (how wide each box is, how long the whiskers are), and whether the distributions are symmetric or skewed.

Steps for Constructing a Box Plot

Step 1: Arrange the data from least to greatest.

Step 2: Find the five-number summary: minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum. (Quartiles will be reviewed in detail in Chapter 5.)

Step 3: Draw a number line scaled to span the data range.

Step 4: Draw a rectangular box from Q1 to Q3. Mark the median with a vertical line inside the box.

Step 5: Draw whiskers from the left side of the box to the minimum, and from the right side of the box to the maximum. (In the version with outlier identification, draw whiskers only to the last non-outlier value and plot outliers separately. This rule will be covered in Chapter 5 Section 5.9.)

Step 6: Label the number line axis with the variable name and units.

What Box Plots Show and What They Hide

A box plot efficiently shows the five-number summary and the spread of the distribution. However, it hides information that a histogram or dot plot preserves: you cannot see individual values, you cannot detect bimodal patterns (two distinct peaks), and you cannot see clusters within each quartile. A box plot tells you about spread and center but not about the fine-grained shape inside the box. Use a histogram or dot plot when shape matters most; use a box plot when comparison matters most.

Section 4.9

Cumulative Frequency Displays

A cumulative frequency display (also called an ogive, pronounced "OH-jive") is a graph that shows the running total of frequencies — or relative frequencies — as you move from left to right across the data range. At each class boundary, the graph plots the cumulative frequency up to that point. The resulting curve always rises from left to right and eventually reaches either the total number of data values (for a cumulative frequency ogive) or 100% (for a cumulative relative frequency ogive).

Ogives are useful for reading off percentiles — the value below which a given percentage of the data falls. To find the percentile value for 60%, for example, you draw a horizontal line at the 60% mark on the vertical axis, then drop a vertical line down to the horizontal axis to read off the corresponding data value. Ogives are also useful for estimating the median (the value at 50%) and the quartiles (at 25% and 75%) directly from the graph.

Display Instruction — Cumulative Frequency Ogive

From your frequency table, add a cumulative frequency column by summing the frequencies from top to bottom: each row's cumulative frequency is the count of all values up to and including that interval. Divide by total n to get cumulative relative frequencies (proportions). Plot points at each class upper boundary with the cumulative relative frequency on the vertical axis. Connect the points with straight line segments. The ogive begins at the lower boundary of the first interval at 0% and ends at the upper boundary of the last interval at 100%.

Section 4.10

Shape: Symmetric, Skewed, and Uniform

The single most informative thing you can say about a distribution's graph is its shape. Shape tells you how data values are arranged across the range — whether they pile up in the middle, lean toward one end, or spread evenly from bottom to top. Different shapes carry different statistical implications, and shape determines which summary statistics (mean, median, standard deviation, IQR) are most appropriate to use — a distinction that Chapter 5 will develop in detail.

Symmetric Distributions

A distribution is symmetric if the left half of the graph is roughly a mirror image of the right half. If you were to fold the histogram along the tallest bar, the two sides would align approximately. In a perfectly symmetric, mound-shaped distribution, the mean, median, and mode all coincide at the center. Many naturally occurring measurements — heights of adults in a population, errors in precision instruments, scores on well-designed tests — tend to produce symmetric or nearly symmetric distributions.

Skewed Distributions

A distribution is skewed when one tail is longer than the other. The direction of skew is the direction of the longer tail — not the direction of the bulk of the data, which often confuses students.

In a right-skewed (or positively skewed) distribution, the right tail is longer. Most values cluster toward the left side, but a few unusually large values pull the distribution's tail to the right. Examples include incomes in a population (most people earn moderate incomes, but a small number of very high earners create a long right tail), real estate prices, and the wait times at an emergency room.

In a left-skewed (or negatively skewed) distribution, the left tail is longer. Most values cluster toward the right side, but a few unusually small values pull the tail to the left. Examples include scores on a very easy test (where most students score near the top but a few score very low) or ages at retirement (most people retire in their 60s or 70s, but a few retire very early).

Uniform Distributions

In a uniform distribution, data values are spread roughly evenly across the entire range. The histogram looks approximately flat — no bar is dramatically taller than the others. A perfectly uniform distribution would have equal frequency in every class interval. In practice, truly uniform distributions are uncommon with real data, but some situations produce approximately uniform distributions: the outcome of rolling a fair die many times, random number generation, or certain waiting times.

Shape and the Mean vs. Median

Shape directly affects the relationship between the mean and median. In a right-skewed distribution, a few large values pull the mean to the right (higher) while the median remains more centrally placed. This is why income data is typically described using the median rather than the mean — a small number of very high incomes can push the mean far above what a "typical" person earns. You will study this connection quantitatively in Chapter 5.

Section 4.11

Center, Spread, Clusters, Gaps, and Outliers

Describing a distribution completely requires more than identifying its shape. A full description of a distribution should always address five features: center, spread, clusters, gaps, and outliers. These five features are what statistical graphs are designed to reveal, and they are what you should systematically look for every time you examine a quantitative display.

Center

The center of a distribution is a value that represents where the data are concentrated — a typical value. From a graph, you can estimate the center visually as the value that divides the distribution into two roughly equal areas, or the value at which the distribution peaks. The center is not always at the arithmetic middle of the range; it is the value where the bulk of the data lies.

Spread

The spread of a distribution describes how variable the data are — how widely the values are dispersed from the center. A narrow distribution with bars concentrated over a small range has little spread. A distribution whose bars are spread broadly across a wide range has a lot of spread. Spread is related to the concept of variability: high spread means values differ substantially from one another; low spread means they tend to be similar. From a graph, you can estimate spread visually by looking at how wide the distribution is from the leftmost to the rightmost data values and whether the bars are tightly bunched or widely spread.

Clusters and Gaps

A cluster is a concentration of data values within a narrow portion of the range — a place where many observations fall close together. A gap is an interval within the range where no (or very few) data values appear. Clusters and gaps are often the most statistically interesting features of a distribution because they may signal that the population is composed of distinct subgroups, or that a measuring instrument has a limited number of possible values.

For example, a distribution of speeds recorded at a highway checkpoint might cluster around 65 mph (the speed limit) and around 80 mph (those exceeding the limit), with a gap between the two clusters. This bimodal pattern — two distinct clusters — would suggest two different types of drivers, and would be completely hidden by a simple average.

Outliers

An outlier is a data value that falls far apart from the bulk of the distribution — either much larger or much smaller than most other values. Outliers appear visually as isolated dots in a dot plot, isolated leaves separated from the main body of a stemplot, isolated bars separated by gaps in a histogram, or as individual dots plotted beyond the whiskers of a box plot.

Outliers deserve careful attention. They may represent genuine extremes in the data (a student who is unusually talented or unusually unprepared), recording errors or measurement mistakes, or members of a different population than the one you intended to study. Before dismissing or removing an outlier, always investigate its source. You will learn a formal rule for identifying outliers (the 1.5 IQR rule) in Chapter 5.

SOCS — A Framework for Describing Distributions

Use the acronym SOCS to remember what to address when describing a distribution from a graph:

S — Shape (symmetric, right-skewed, left-skewed, uniform, bimodal)

O — Outliers (any values that stand noticeably apart from the rest?)

C — Center (where does the distribution peak or balance? What is the typical value?)

S — Spread (how wide is the distribution? Are values tightly clustered or broadly dispersed?)

Always interpret SOCS in context. Use the variable name and units in your description.

Section 4.12

Comparing Distributions Visually

Statistics often asks us to compare groups — not just to describe one distribution, but to ask whether two or more distributions differ in their centers, spreads, shapes, or whether one group consistently produces higher or lower values than another. Visual comparison is frequently the first and most powerful step in this analysis.

Three displays are especially useful for comparing distributions: side-by-side box plots (placed on the same numerical axis), back-to-back stem-and-leaf plots (already seen in Section 4.4), and histograms with a common scale (placed one above the other or overlaid with transparency). Each has strengths and weaknesses. Box plots show five-number summaries and medians clearly but hide shape within each quartile. Back-to-back stemplots preserve every value but only work well for small data sets. Histograms show overall shape and density but can be harder to compare when data sets differ in size (use relative frequency histograms to solve this problem).

When comparing two distributions, always address the same SOCS features for each group and use explicit comparison language: "Group A has a higher center than Group B," "Group B is more spread out," "Group A appears symmetric while Group B is right-skewed."

Section 4.13

Misleading Displays of Quantitative Data

Graphs of quantitative data can be manipulated — deliberately or accidentally — to create a false impression. Unlike calculation errors, which can often be checked by recomputing, misleading graphs require the viewer to notice that something about the display has been distorted. Critical reading of statistical graphs is a skill that protects you from being misled by data presented in media, advertising, political campaigns, and business reports.

Truncated Vertical Axis

One of the most common manipulations is starting the vertical axis at a value other than zero. If a histogram or time plot shows only the range from, say, 85 to 100, small differences between bars look enormous. A difference that is actually 3 units on a 0-to-100 scale fills nearly a third of the visible graph area when the axis starts at 88. This technique is frequently used in news graphics and product advertisements to make modest changes appear dramatic.

Inconsistent Class Intervals

In a histogram, all bars must have equal width — unless you explicitly label each bar's width and show proportional areas. When bars have different widths but equal heights, the wider bars represent more data values, but the graph makes every interval look equally common. This creates a severely distorted picture of the distribution. Always check that class intervals are equal-width (or that area is correctly proportional) before trusting a histogram.

Missing Labels and Missing Context

A graph without labeled axes, missing units, or no title forces you to guess what the data represent and what scale the values are on. This omission may be innocent carelessness, or it may be a way to hide information that would make an interpretation less impressive. Always ask: What variable is shown? What are the units? What is the scale? Who collected this data and why?

Data Ethics — Graphs in Media and Policy

Misleading graphs appear regularly in news articles, social media posts, campaign materials, and corporate earnings presentations. When examining any graph you did not construct yourself, ask: Does the vertical axis start at zero? Are all class intervals equal? Are there labels, units, and a source? What impression is the graph trying to create — and is that impression accurate? Developing the habit of critical graph-reading is one of the most practically useful outcomes of a statistics course.

Section 4.14

Common Quantitative Graph Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Drawing gaps between histogram bars
Drawing a histogram with gaps between the bars, as if it were a bar graph for categorical data.

Correct approach: In a histogram, bars represent adjacent intervals on a continuous number line. They touch because there is no natural gap between, say, 40–50 and 50–60. A gap in a histogram signals an interval with zero frequency — a real gap in the data. Drawing gaps where none exist misrepresents the data.

Mistake 2 — Confusing direction of skew
"The distribution is left-skewed because most of the data is on the left side."

Correct approach: The direction of skew is determined by the tail, not by where most data lie. A right-skewed distribution has most data on the left and a long tail extending to the right. A left-skewed distribution has most data on the right and a long tail extending to the left. "Skewed right" means the tail points right.

Mistake 3 — Describing shape without context
"The histogram is shaped like a hill." (No further description.)

Correct approach: A complete description of shape connects the graph to the data. "The distribution of homework times is approximately symmetric and mound-shaped, centered near 50 minutes, with most students spending between 35 and 65 minutes." Use the variable name, units, and context in every description.

Mistake 4 — Using a bar graph for quantitative data
Constructing a bar graph with one bar for each individual value of a quantitative variable (e.g., separate bars for every score from 50 to 100).

Correct approach: When the number of possible values is large, group quantitative data into class intervals and use a histogram. Constructing one bar per value produces a cluttered, unreadable display that does not reveal the distribution's shape.

Mistake 5 — Concluding from shape without addressing outliers
"The data are right-skewed." (Description ends without noting the isolated value at 95 that is separated by a large gap.)

Correct approach: A complete description notes both the overall shape and any specific outliers or unusual features. Outliers can affect both the apparent shape and later calculations. They deserve to be named and investigated, not ignored.

Mistake 6 — Treating the center as the only important feature
"Both distributions have the same center, so they are basically the same."

Correct approach: Two distributions with identical centers can look very different — one may be tightly concentrated and the other extremely spread out. Always report spread, shape, and unusual features in addition to center. A center alone does not describe a distribution.

Chapter Summary

Key Terms Review

quantitative data
Data that take numerical values along a meaningful scale and can be measured or counted. Arithmetic operations on the values are meaningful.
discrete data
Quantitative data that take values from a countable set, typically whole numbers. Example: number of children in a family.
continuous data
Quantitative data that can take any value within a range, including decimals. Example: height, time, temperature.
class interval
A range of values that defines one row of a frequency table or one bar of a histogram. Also called a bin.
frequency table
A table that lists class intervals and the count (frequency) of data values that fall within each interval.

The proportion or percentage of data values in a given interval or category: relative frequency = frequency ÷ total n.

stem-and-leaf plot
A display that splits each value into a stem (leading digit[s]) and a leaf (final digit), preserving original values while showing shape.
histogram
A display for quantitative data using touching rectangular bars, where bar height represents the frequency of values in each class interval.
relative frequency histogram
A histogram with proportions (not counts) on the vertical axis. Used to compare distributions of different sizes.
box plot
A display of the five-number summary (minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum) using a box from Q1 to Q3, a median line, and whiskers.
five-number summary
The minimum, first quartile (Q1), median, third quartile (Q3), and maximum of a data set.
ogive
A cumulative frequency (or relative frequency) graph that plots running totals against class upper boundaries. Used to estimate percentiles.
symmetric distribution
A distribution whose left half is roughly a mirror image of the right half. Mean, median, and mode coincide in a perfectly symmetric distribution.
skewed right
A distribution with a long right tail. Most values cluster toward the lower end, but a few large values extend the right tail. Also called positively skewed.
skewed left
A distribution with a long left tail. Most values cluster toward the upper end, but a few small values extend the left tail. Also called negatively skewed.
outlier
A data value that falls far apart from the bulk of the distribution. May represent a genuine extreme, a data error, or a member of a different population.
SOCS
An acronym for the four features to address when describing a distribution: Shape, Outliers, Center, and Spread.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 5: Summarizing Quantitative Data

Chapter 4 gave you the visual tools to see a distribution — to look at its shape, estimate its center, observe its spread, and identify unusual values. Chapter 5 will give you the numerical tools to measure those same features precisely. You will learn to calculate the mean, median, mode, range, interquartile range, variance, and standard deviation. You will also learn to compute the five-number summary formally and to find percentiles and z-scores. Most importantly, you will learn when to use each measure — and the answer will depend directly on what you learned about shape in Chapter 4. For a symmetric distribution, the mean and standard deviation are appropriate; for a skewed distribution or one with outliers, the median and IQR provide more honest summaries. The graphs you have learned to read in Chapter 4 will guide every calculation you make in Chapter 5.

Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 6 showed you how to model the relationship between two quantitative variables using a straight line. A linear model is a powerful tool, and it fits an enormous range of real situations surprisingly well. But the world does not always grow at a fixed rate. Populations double. Medications wear off. Projectiles rise and fall. Sound fades. Light dims. None of these patterns produces a straight line when graphed, and forcing a linear model onto curved data gives answers that can mislead everyone who reads them.

This chapter extends your statistical toolkit beyond linear regression. You will study four major families of nonlinear models — quadratic, exponential, logarithmic, and power — at a level that connects naturally to your work in Algebra I and Geometry. You will learn to look at a scatter plot and ask: What kind of pattern does this data seem to follow? What model fits the shape? How do I check whether that model actually works? And how do I interpret the model's numbers in plain language that describes the real situation?

You will also be introduced to data transformation — the technique of converting variable values using logarithms or other operations to reveal hidden patterns or linearize curved relationships. Transformation connects Chapter 7 to your future work in Algebra II, Precalculus, and college-level statistics and data science.

This chapter is about judgment, not just calculation. Statistics does not require you to find a single perfect model. It requires you to find a reasonable model, check its fit carefully using residual plots, and describe its meaning and limitations honestly.

Opening Question

A researcher records the number of bacteria in a laboratory dish every hour for eight hours. At hour zero, there are 100 bacteria. By hour eight, there are more than 25,000. When she plots these values on a coordinate plane, the pattern curves sharply upward — nothing like a straight line. Should she use a linear model anyway? What happens if she does? What model might describe her data better, and how would she know? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer these questions fully.

Why this chapter matters. Every scientific model, financial projection, public health forecast, and engineering calculation rests on a choice of model type. When you assume linear growth where the real relationship is exponential, your predictions can be drastically wrong — not slightly off, but off by orders of magnitude. Learning to distinguish among model types, and to evaluate fit using residual plots and context, is one of the most practical skills in applied statistics. It connects directly to biology, physics, chemistry, environmental science, economics, finance, medicine, engineering, and data science — fields that all depend on choosing the right model for the data.

Section 7.1

Why Not All Data Are Linear

A linear model assumes that the relationship between two variables changes at a roughly constant rate. In Algebra I, you learned that slope measures this rate. A line with slope 3 means that for every one-unit increase in x, the value of y increases by exactly 3 units — the same fixed amount every time, whether x is small or large.

This assumption works well in many situations. If a car travels at a constant speed, the relationship between time and distance is linear. If a store charges a fixed price per item, cost scales linearly with quantity. But many real situations do not work this way at all.

Consider a few concrete examples. A savings account earning compound interest grows faster and faster over time — not by the same dollar amount each year, but by a fixed percentage of whatever is currently in the account. The more money is in the account, the larger each year's gain becomes. A radioactive substance loses mass by a fixed percentage of whatever remains, so the rate of loss slows as the quantity decreases. A ball thrown upward rises, reaches a peak, and then falls — a pattern that traces a symmetric curve, not a line. The loudness of sound fades quickly as you first move away from its source and then more slowly as you continue moving — again, not a constant rate.

In each of these cases, forcing a straight line onto the data would produce a model that misleads more than it informs. The line might pass near the middle of the data while badly missing both ends. The slope would have no meaningful interpretation because the actual rate of change is not constant. And predictions based on the linear model would grow worse and worse as x moves away from the center of the data.

Recognizing When a Line Is Not Enough

A linear model is probably not the right choice when you observe any of the following signals:

SignalWhat It Suggests
The scatter plot curves upward or downward rather than following a straight path.A nonlinear model is likely needed.
The residual plot from a linear model shows a curved pattern rather than random scatter.The linear model is systematically missing the true shape of the data.
The data changes by a roughly constant percentage rather than a constant amount per step.An exponential model may be appropriate.
The data rises and then falls (or falls and then rises) in a symmetric way.A quadratic model may be appropriate.
Growth is rapid at first and then levels off without ever turning back down.A logarithmic model may be appropriate.
The subject matter involves acceleration, decay, scaling laws, or geometric patterns.A nonlinear model matches the physics or biology of the situation.
Student Warning

A common mistake is to fit a linear model and report its equation without ever examining the scatter plot or residual plot. Always look at the graph first. A curved scatter plot is a clear signal that a different model type may be needed — and the scatter plot is the first check you should always make before any modeling decision.

Section 7.2

Reviewing Linear Models

Before moving to nonlinear models, it is important to revisit what a linear model looks like and what its parts mean. This review gives you a clear baseline for comparison.

A linear model takes the form:

The Linear Model

ŷ = a + bx

In this equation, x is the explanatory variable (the input), ŷ is the predicted value of the response variable (the output), b is the slope (the predicted change in y for each one-unit increase in x), and a is the intercept (the predicted value of y when x = 0).

The graph of a linear model is a straight line. Residuals from a good linear fit should be small and scattered randomly above and below zero, with no visible pattern when plotted against x. If the residual plot shows a curved pattern — one where the residuals tend to be positive at the extremes and negative in the middle, or vice versa — this is a signal that the linear model is not capturing the true shape of the data.

Graph Instruction — What a Residual Plot Should Look Like

Draw a horizontal axis labeled with the explanatory variable x. Draw a vertical axis labeled "Residual (y − ŷ)." Mark a dashed horizontal line at residual = 0. Plot each residual as a point directly above or below its corresponding x-value. A good-fitting model produces points that scatter randomly above and below the zero line with no obvious pattern — some positive, some negative, arranged without structure. A curved or wave-shaped cluster of points is a warning sign that the model type is wrong.

Example 7.2.1
Reviewing a linear model and its residuals
  • ProblemA student measures how far a toy car travels (in centimeters) when released from ramps of different heights (in centimeters). She fits the linear model ŷ = 5 + 12x. Identify the slope and intercept, and give each a meaning in context. Then explain what a curved residual plot would mean here.
  • ThinkIdentify the model form, then connect each coefficient to the context. Consider what curved residuals would indicate about the model.
  • RepresentLinear model: ŷ = 5 + 12x, where x = ramp height (cm) and ŷ = predicted distance traveled (cm).
  • Interpret Slope = 12: For each additional centimeter of ramp height, the car is predicted to travel 12 more centimeters. Intercept = 5: When the ramp height is 0 cm, the model predicts the car travels 5 cm. This may not be physically meaningful — a ramp of zero height cannot launch a car.
  • CheckIf x = 10 cm: ŷ = 5 + 12(10) = 125 cm. Does this seem reasonable? That depends on the actual data. The intercept should be interpreted with caution whenever x = 0 falls outside the range of the data or has no physical meaning.
  • ResidualsIf the residual plot from this model showed a U-shape — negative residuals for small and large ramp heights, positive in the middle — this would mean the linear model is under-predicting at the extremes and over-predicting in the middle. The true relationship may curve, and a different model should be considered.
Answer: Slope = 12 cm per cm of height; intercept = 5 cm (interpret cautiously). A curved residual plot signals that the linear model misses the true pattern.
Key Idea — Residuals as Diagnostics

Residuals are not just leftovers from a calculation — they are diagnostic tools. A random residual plot confirms good model fit. A curved or patterned residual plot reveals what the model is missing. Every time you fit a model, examine the residual plot before drawing conclusions.

Section 7.3

Quadratic Models

A quadratic model takes the form:

Quadratic Model

ŷ = ax² + bx + c

The graph is a parabola — a symmetric curve that opens upward when a > 0 and downward when a < 0. The vertex is the highest point (maximum) if the parabola opens downward, or the lowest point (minimum) if it opens upward. The x-coordinate of the vertex is found at x = −b / (2a).

You encountered parabolas in Algebra I and Geometry. In statistics, the same shape describes certain data patterns. The height of a projectile over time follows a downward-opening parabola — it rises to a peak and then falls. The braking distance of a car as speed increases follows an upward-opening curve, because doubling speed more than doubles stopping distance. The profit of a business as a function of price might peak at an optimal price point and fall on either side, tracing a downward-opening parabola.

The key difference between a quadratic model and a linear model is that a quadratic has a rate of change that is not constant — it increases or decreases continuously as x changes. This is why the parabola curves rather than running straight.

Parameters in a Quadratic Model

ParameterRoleInterpretation
aLeading coefficientControls direction (up if a > 0, down if a < 0) and width. Larger |a| means a narrower, steeper curve.
bLinear term coefficientShifts the vertex horizontally. Together with a, determines the location of the vertex.
cConstant termThe value of ŷ when x = 0. Often the starting or initial value in context.
VertexMaximum or minimumLocated at x = −b/(2a). Represents the peak height, optimal price, maximum range, or minimum cost depending on context.
Example 7.3.1
Interpreting a quadratic model for a falling object
  • ProblemA ball is dropped from a building. Its height above the ground (in meters) after t seconds is modeled by ŷ = 80 − 5t². Identify the model type, interpret the parameters, and find when the ball hits the ground.
  • ThinkThe equation has the form ŷ = at² + c (no linear term, so b = 0). The leading coefficient is negative, so the parabola opens downward — height decreases as time increases.
  • RepresentQuadratic model: ŷ = −5t² + 80, where t = seconds elapsed and ŷ = height in meters.
  • Calculate At t = 0: ŷ = 80 − 5(0) = 80 m (starting height) At t = 2: ŷ = 80 − 5(4) = 80 − 20 = 60 m At t = 4: ŷ = 80 − 5(16) = 80 − 80 = 0 m (reaches the ground)
  • CheckThe ball starts at 80 m and reaches 0 m at t = 4 s. Height decreases — consistent with a falling object. The decrease accelerates: in the first 2 s the ball falls 20 m; in the next 2 s it falls 60 m. This matches the physics of free fall, where speed increases over time.
  • InterpretThe initial height is 80 meters. The coefficient −5 on t² reflects the acceleration due to gravity. The ball does not fall at a constant rate; it falls faster and faster. It hits the ground after exactly 4 seconds.
Answer: ŷ = 80 − 5t². The ball starts at 80 m and hits the ground at t = 4 seconds. The negative leading coefficient confirms the parabola opens downward (height decreases).
Tip — Direction of Opening Matters

Before interpreting a quadratic model, always identify the sign of the leading coefficient a. If a > 0, the parabola opens upward and the vertex is a minimum — the model reaches its lowest predicted value at the vertex. If a < 0, the parabola opens downward and the vertex is a maximum — the model reaches its highest predicted value at the vertex. The context will tell you which makes sense.

Section 7.4

Exponential Growth Models

An exponential growth model takes the form:

Exponential Growth Model

ŷ = a · bx  where b > 1 and a > 0

The key feature: the quantity is multiplied by a constant factor b for each one-unit increase in x. This is completely different from a linear model, where a constant amount is added. If b = 2, the quantity doubles with each step. If b = 1.05, it grows by 5% per step. Growth accelerates — the more there is, the more it increases each period.

In a linear model, the difference between successive y-values is constant. In an exponential growth model, the ratio between successive y-values is constant. This distinction is the heart of what makes exponential growth so different from linear growth — and why it accelerates.

Connecting to Percentage Growth

If a quantity grows by a percentage r per period, then the growth factor is b = 1 + r. For example, 3% annual growth means b = 1.03. A quantity doubling each period means b = 2 (which corresponds to 100% growth per period). The initial value a is the quantity when x = 0.

Example 7.4.1
Bacteria population — exponential growth
  • ProblemA laboratory dish starts with 200 bacteria. The count doubles every hour. Write an exponential growth model and predict the population after 3 hours and after 6 hours.
  • ThinkDoubling means the growth factor is b = 2. The initial value is a = 200. The explanatory variable x represents hours elapsed.
  • RepresentExponential growth model: ŷ = 200 · 2x, where x = hours elapsed.
  • Calculate At x = 0: ŷ = 200 · 1 = 200 bacteria At x = 3: ŷ = 200 · 2³ = 200 · 8 = 1,600 bacteria At x = 6: ŷ = 200 · 2⁶ = 200 · 64 = 12,800 bacteria
  • CheckEach hour the count doubles: 200 → 400 → 800 → 1,600 → 3,200 → 6,400 → 12,800. The ratio between consecutive values is always 2. This confirms the exponential pattern.
  • InterpretThe bacteria population starts at 200 and doubles each hour. After 6 hours, the model predicts 12,800 — 64 times the starting value. This dramatic growth illustrates why exponential models cannot be extrapolated far without caution; real populations eventually hit resource limits that slow growth.
Answer: ŷ = 200 · 2x. After 3 hours: 1,600 bacteria. After 6 hours: 12,800 bacteria.
Data Ethics Note

Exponential growth models are commonly used in public health to describe the early stages of disease spread. However, unchecked exponential growth always eventually slows because of resource limits, acquired immunity, behavior changes, or medical interventions. Using an exponential model to make long-range predictions during an epidemic — without checking whether the growth rate has already changed — can dramatically overstate the scale of future harm. Responsible modeling always states the time period for which a model is expected to apply.

Section 7.5

Exponential Decay Models

An exponential decay model has the same form as an exponential growth model — ŷ = a · bx — but here the growth factor satisfies 0 < b < 1. Because b is less than 1, each step multiplies the quantity by a fraction, so the quantity decreases. The decrease is fast at first and slows over time, approaching but never quite reaching zero.

The shape of an exponential decay curve is the mirror image of a growth curve: it starts high on the left, drops steeply, and then gradually levels off to the right. This leveling-off shape is a distinctive visual signature that distinguishes exponential decay from a straight line declining at a constant rate.

Connecting to Percentage Decrease

If a quantity decreases by a percentage r per period, then b = 1 − r.

A drug losing 20% of its concentration per hour: b = 0.80

A radioactive substance losing 10% of its mass per year: b = 0.90

A car losing 18% of its value per year: b = 0.82

Half-Life

A special case of exponential decay is half-life — the time it takes for a quantity to reduce to half its value. Radioactive materials and some medications are routinely described by their half-lives in physics, environmental science, and medicine. If the half-life is h time units, then after each period of length h, the remaining amount is multiplied by 1/2.

Example 7.5.1
Medication concentration — exponential decay
  • ProblemA patient takes a medication. The drug's concentration in the bloodstream begins at 400 mg/L and decreases by 25% each hour. Write a model and find the concentration after 4 hours.
  • ThinkA 25% decrease each hour means 75% of the concentration remains each hour. So b = 1 − 0.25 = 0.75. The initial value is a = 400 mg/L.
  • RepresentExponential decay model: ŷ = 400 · (0.75)x, where x = hours elapsed.
  • Calculate At x = 1: ŷ = 400 · 0.75 = 300 mg/L At x = 2: ŷ = 400 · (0.75)² = 400 · 0.5625 = 225 mg/L At x = 4: ŷ = 400 · (0.75)⁴ = 400 · 0.3164 ≈ 126.6 mg/L
  • CheckEach hour retains 75% of the previous value: 400 → 300 → 225 → 168.75 → 126.6. The ratio of consecutive values is consistently 0.75. The values decrease and slow — consistent with exponential decay.
  • InterpretAfter 4 hours, roughly 31.6% of the original dose remains — less than a third. Medical professionals use this kind of model to schedule dosing intervals: if the drug is only effective above a certain concentration threshold, they need to know how quickly it drops below that level.
Answer: ŷ = 400 · (0.75)x. After 4 hours, the concentration is approximately 126.6 mg/L.
Tip — Growth Factor vs. Growth Rate

The growth factor b and the growth rate r are related but different. The growth factor b is the number you multiply by each step. The growth rate r is the percentage change expressed as a decimal. For growth: b = 1 + r. For decay: b = 1 − r. If b = 0.82, then r = 0.18, meaning the quantity decreases by 18% per period. Always distinguish between the two when writing interpretations.

Section 7.6

Logarithmic Models as an Introduction

A logarithmic model takes the form:

Logarithmic Model

ŷ = a + b · ln(x)  where x > 0

The function ln(x) is the natural logarithm of x — the inverse of the natural exponential function. The logarithmic function grows quickly at first but slows as x increases. The graph curves upward steeply at small values of x and then flattens out, never reaching a fixed ceiling but adding smaller and smaller amounts with each step.

If you have studied logarithms in Algebra II or Precalculus, you already know that the natural logarithm is the inverse of the exponential function ex. If you have not yet studied logarithms formally, the key idea for this chapter is the shape: logarithmic growth is rapid early and slow late, which is the opposite of exponential growth.

Logarithmic models appear in situations where something improves or grows quickly at first but gains slow down with continued input. A new student practicing a skill improves rapidly in the first few sessions but continues to gain at a diminishing rate. A biological population grows quickly when resources are abundant and food is easy to find, but slows as competition increases. The decibel scale for sound and the Richter scale for earthquake magnitude are both logarithmic — each step on the scale represents a tenfold increase in physical intensity, but our perception of loudness or shaking grows more slowly.

Example 7.6.1
A typing practice learning curve
  • ProblemA student practices typing. Her speed (in words per minute) after x weeks of daily practice is approximately modeled by ŷ = 10 + 18 · ln(x). Find her predicted speed after 1, 4, 20, and 100 weeks. Describe the rate of improvement.
  • ThinkThis is a logarithmic model. Improvements are large early and smaller later. Use a calculator or the fact that ln(1) = 0, ln(4) ≈ 1.386, ln(20) ≈ 2.996, ln(100) ≈ 4.605.
  • RepresentLogarithmic model: ŷ = 10 + 18 · ln(x), where x = weeks of practice.
  • Calculate x = 1 week: ŷ = 10 + 18(0) = 10 wpm x = 4 weeks: ŷ = 10 + 18(1.386) ≈ 10 + 25.0 = 35 wpm x = 20 weeks: ŷ = 10 + 18(2.996) ≈ 10 + 53.9 = 64 wpm x = 100 weeks:ŷ = 10 + 18(4.605) ≈ 10 + 82.9 = 93 wpm
  • CheckFrom week 1 to week 4 (3 more weeks), speed jumps 25 wpm. From week 4 to week 20 (16 more weeks), it gains 29 wpm. From week 20 to week 100 (80 more weeks), it gains only 29 more wpm. Gains are large early, small late — consistent with the logarithmic shape.
  • InterpretThe student improves rapidly in the first few weeks and continues to improve, but each additional week of practice produces a smaller gain than the week before. After 100 weeks, the model predicts about 93 wpm. This pattern — fast early improvement slowing over time — matches the experience of many learners.
Answer: Predicted speeds: 10, 35, 64, and 93 wpm. Each block of time produces smaller gains — the signature of logarithmic growth.
Student Warning — No True Maximum

Unlike an exponential decay curve that approaches zero, or a bounded model that levels off at a ceiling, the logarithmic model never stops growing — it always increases, just more and more slowly. If a real-world situation has a true ceiling (a maximum possible skill level, a saturation concentration, or a fixed carrying capacity), the logarithmic model eventually becomes unrealistic. Always state the range of x for which your model is meant to apply.

Section 7.7

Power Models as an Introduction

A power model takes the form:

Power Model

ŷ = a · xb  where a > 0 and b is any real number

The behavior of the power model depends entirely on the value of b. When b > 1, the curve rises faster than a line (accelerating growth). When 0 < b < 1, the curve rises but decelerates (like a square-root function). When b < 0, the curve decreases and approaches zero as x grows.

Power models appear frequently in physics, biology, and engineering, often arising from geometric or physical relationships. When a quantity depends on area, it scales with the square. When it depends on volume, it scales with the cube. These geometric relationships produce power models naturally.

Value of bShapeExample Context
b = 2Parabolic — rises quicklyArea of a circle as radius increases; braking distance as speed increases
b = 3Cubic — rises very steeplyVolume of a sphere; weight scaling with length for fish or other animals
b = 0.5Square root — rises then levelsSpeed of a falling object as a function of height; wave speed and depth
b = 1.5Between linear and quadraticKepler's Third Law: orbital period vs. distance from Sun
b < 0Decreasing and approaching zeroGravity weakening with distance; light intensity diminishing with distance
Example 7.7.1
Weight of a fish — power model
  • ProblemFor a species of freshwater fish, the weight w (in grams) is modeled by ŷ = 0.012 · L3.1, where L is the fish's length in centimeters. Find the predicted weights for fish of lengths 20 cm and 40 cm. Does doubling the length double the weight?
  • ThinkThis is a power model with b = 3.1. Weight grows much faster than length because the exponent is greater than 1.
  • RepresentPower model: ŷ = 0.012 · L3.1
  • Calculate At L = 20 cm: ŷ = 0.012 · (20)³·¹ ≈ 0.012 · 9,189 ≈ 110 g At L = 40 cm: ŷ = 0.012 · (40)³·¹ ≈ 0.012 · 78,088 ≈ 937 g
  • CheckThe 40 cm fish is twice the length of the 20 cm fish. Is it twice the weight? 937 / 110 ≈ 8.5 — the 40 cm fish is about 8.5 times heavier, not 2 times heavier. This makes intuitive sense: a fish twice as long is roughly twice as long, twice as wide, and twice as deep, so it occupies roughly 2³ = 8 times the volume.
  • InterpretDoubling the length of a fish increases its predicted weight by a factor of about 8.5 — not 2. This is the signature of a power model with exponent near 3: the relationship is geometric, not linear. Biologists and fisheries managers use this kind of model to estimate fish populations from length measurements alone.
Answer: 20 cm fish: ≈ 110 g. 40 cm fish: ≈ 937 g. Doubling length multiplies weight by ≈ 8.5, not 2.

Section 7.8

Transforming Data to Reveal Patterns

When a scatter plot curves, a straight-line model will not fit the data well. However, if you apply a mathematical operation to the values of x, the values of y, or both, the relationship may become approximately linear. A linearized relationship is much easier to analyze with the tools you already have. This technique is called data transformation or linearization.

The most common transformation in statistics is taking the natural logarithm. Logarithms convert multiplicative relationships (like exponential growth) into additive relationships (like linear growth), which is exactly why they are so useful here.

Linearizing Exponential Relationships

Suppose the true relationship is y = a · bx. Taking the natural logarithm of both sides gives:

Linearizing an Exponential Model

Original: y = a · bx

Take ln of both sides: ln(y) = ln(a) + x · ln(b)

This is the equation of a straight line in x and ln(y). If you plot x on the horizontal axis and ln(y) on the vertical axis and the points approximately follow a straight line, this is evidence that the original data follow an exponential relationship.

Linearizing Power Relationships

Suppose the true relationship is y = a · xb. Taking the natural logarithm of both sides gives:

Linearizing a Power Model

Original: y = a · xb

Take ln of both sides: ln(y) = ln(a) + b · ln(x)

This is the equation of a straight line in ln(x) and ln(y). If you plot ln(x) against ln(y) and the points approximately follow a line, this is evidence that the original data follow a power relationship. The slope of the line in this transformed plot equals the exponent b.

Asking the Right Questions

When you look at a scatter plot, work through these questions in order. They help you narrow the field of candidates before committing to a model.

  1. 1Does the data go up, go down, or both? A pattern that goes up and then comes back down eliminates everything except a downward-opening quadratic (or a similar curve with a peak).

Always bring subject-matter knowledge into the model-selection process.

Section 7.10

Interpreting Parameters in Context

Every model has parameters — the constants and coefficients that define its specific shape and scale. Two exponential models can have the same algebraic form but behave very differently because their parameters are different. Interpreting parameters in context means connecting the numbers in the equation to the real-world situation they describe. A parameter without a context interpretation is statistically incomplete.

Exponential Parameters in Context

In the model ŷ = a · bx, the parameter a is the initial value — the quantity when x = 0. The parameter b is the growth or decay factor — the multiplier applied per unit of x. To find the growth rate in percentage terms, compute r = b − 1 for growth (so if b = 1.08, the rate is 8% per period) or r = 1 − b for decay (so if b = 0.90, the rate is 10% decrease per period).

Quadratic Parameters in Context

In the model ŷ = ax² + bx + c, the parameter c is the initial value (ŷ when x = 0). The vertex represents the maximum or minimum of the model, which often has a specific physical meaning: the peak height of a projectile, the time at which a disease reaches peak incidence, or the price that maximizes revenue. The sign of a determines whether the vertex is a maximum (a < 0) or minimum (a > 0).

  • ProblemA car's resale value is modeled by V̂ = 28,000 · (0.82)t, where V is value in dollars and t is years since purchase. Identify and interpret each parameter. Then find the value after 5 years.

Section 7.11

Residual Plots

From Chapter 6, recall that a residual is the difference between an observed data value and the value predicted by a model:

Residual Definition

residual = y − ŷ

A positive residual means the model under-predicted the actual value. A negative residual means the model over-predicted. A good model produces residuals that are small and show no systematic pattern.

A residual plot graphs the residuals on the vertical axis against the explanatory variable x on the horizontal axis. For a well-fitting model, residuals should be approximately centered at zero with no obvious pattern — just random scatter above and below the zero line. If the residual plot shows a curved pattern, a fan shape (widening spread), or a wave, the model is not capturing the true structure of the data, and a different model should be considered.

Graph Instruction — Constructing and Reading a Residual Plot

Draw a horizontal axis labeled with the explanatory variable x. Draw a vertical axis labeled "Residual (y − ŷ)." Draw a dashed horizontal line at residual = 0. Plot each point directly above or below its corresponding x-value at the height of its residual. Then examine the pattern:

Random scatter above and below zero: Good model fit. The model captures the main structure of the data.

Curved pattern (U-shape or inverted-U): The model type is probably wrong. A linear model fitted to curved data shows this pattern. Try a nonlinear model.

Fan shape (increasing spread): The variability of the data increases as x grows — a condition called heteroscedasticity. The model may still be right in form, but its precision varies across the range of x.

Cluster or wave pattern: The model is missing a systematic structure — possibly a seasonal cycle, group effect, or trend within a trend.

Example 7.11.1
Using a residual plot to detect the wrong model type
  • ProblemA student fits a linear model to population data recorded over 10 years. The residual plot shows a clear pattern: residuals are positive for years 1–3, negative for years 4–7, and positive again for years 8–10. What does this pattern mean? What should the student do?
  • ThinkA pattern where residuals start positive, dip negative, and return to positive traces a U-shape — the classic signature of a linear model applied to curved data.
  • AnalyzeThe U-shape means the linear model over-predicts in the early and late years and under-predicts in the middle years. Equivalently, the data values in the middle are lower than the line but the data at both ends are higher — the data curve upward at both ends and sag in the middle. Wait — or is it the reverse? Positive residuals at the edges mean the actual values are above the line at the edges, and negative residuals in the middle mean actual values are below the line in the middle. This suggests the data curve like a U — not the linear model's straight path.
  • InterpretThe U-shaped residual plot strongly suggests the data follow a curved pattern — possibly exponential or quadratic — rather than a straight line. The linear model is systematically wrong, not just noisy.
  • ConclusionThe student should look at the original scatter plot again and consider exponential, quadratic, or logarithmic models depending on whether the pattern shows consistent multiplication, a peak, or rapid-early-slow-late growth.
Answer: The U-shaped residual pattern indicates the linear model is not appropriate. The student should consider a nonlinear model — the residual plot identifies the problem but does not specify which nonlinear model to use.
Student Warning — Residual Plots Do Not Choose the Model

A residual plot tells you whether your current model is adequate. It does not tell you which model to use instead. To choose the right nonlinear model, you must also look at the original scatter plot, consider the context and subject matter, and possibly try several candidates. The residual plot is the final check, not the first step.

Section 7.12

Choosing a Reasonable Model

Choosing a model is not guesswork. It is a structured process that combines visual analysis, contextual knowledge, and statistical checking. The following six-step process will guide you through model selection in a systematic way.

Six-Step Model Selection Process

Step 1 — Look at the scatter plot. What does the overall shape suggest? Linear? Curved upward? Leveling off? A peak?

Step 2 — Consider the context. What does the subject matter tell you? Does this kind of quantity typically grow exponentially, decay, peak, or level off?

Step 3 — Fit candidate models. Using a graphing calculator or statistical software, fit the models suggested by Steps 1 and 2. Compare how well each fits visually.

Step 4 — Examine residual plots. For each candidate model, examine the residual plot. A good-fitting model produces random residual scatter. A curved or patterned residual plot indicates a poor fit.

Step 5 — Consider the limitations. Does the model make sense throughout the range of x? Does it predict reasonable values outside the observed data? Are there conditions under which the model would break down?

Step 6 — State the model and its limitations. Report the model equation, interpret the parameters in context, and describe known limitations — especially about extrapolation.

  • SituationA savings account starts with $1,000 and earns 6% interest per year, compounded annually. A student has recorded the balance each year for 10 years and must choose between a linear model and an exponential model.

  • Scatter PlotThe data curve upward. A straight line would pass through the middle of the data but under-predict in the later years and over-predict in the earlier years — consistent with a curve, not a line.

Section 7.13

Nonlinear Models in Science, Finance, and Technology

Nonlinear models are not abstract mathematics. They are the foundation of practical work in many fields that students will encounter in college courses and careers. This section connects the models studied in this chapter to a broad range of applications.

Biology and Medicine

Bacterial population growth in early stages follows exponential growth. Drug concentration in the bloodstream decays exponentially, making dosing schedules a direct application of the exponential decay model. Many ecological populations grow logistically — a pattern where exponential growth is modified by resource limits and eventually levels off. Tumor growth can also be modeled as exponential in early stages. Epidemiologists use exponential and logistic models to predict the spread of infectious disease and evaluate the effect of interventions.

Physics and Environmental Science

The distance a free-falling object travels is quadratic in time: d = ½gt². The intensity of light decreases with the square of the distance from the source — an inverse power model. Orbital periods of planets follow Kepler's power law. Radioactive decay and the half-lives of pollutants in ecosystems follow exponential decay, making these models essential for environmental monitoring, nuclear waste management, and carbon dating in archaeology.

Finance and Economics

Compound interest, investment growth, and inflation are all exponential. Loan balances decrease exponentially (with compounding) as regular payments are made. Some financial risks — including insurance premiums for catastrophic events — are modeled with power functions. Demand curves in economics are often modeled as power functions of price.

Technology

The growth of computer processing power was described for several decades by Moore's Law, an approximately exponential doubling of transistor count every two years. Network effects — where a platform becomes more valuable as more users join — can grow super-linearly and sometimes approximately exponentially. Signal attenuation in wireless communications follows logarithmic or power models depending on the medium and frequency.

Section 7.14

Common Nonlinear Modeling Mistakes

Nonlinear modeling introduces new opportunities for error that go beyond simple arithmetic mistakes. These are conceptual errors — misunderstandings about model types, model fit, or the responsible use of models for prediction. Learning to recognize and avoid them is as important as learning to build the models themselves.

Mistake 1 — Assuming All Curved Data Are Exponential
"The scatter plot curves upward, so I'll use an exponential model."

Not every upward curve is exponential. A quadratic also curves upward. A power model can curve upward. A logarithmic model curves upward and then levels off. Before labeling any curved data as exponential, examine the residual plot of the exponential model and compare it to other candidates. Check whether consecutive y-values have a constant ratio (exponential) or whether the curve has a peak (quadratic) or leveling (logarithmic).

Mistake 2 — Reporting a Model Without Checking Residuals
"The model looks close to the data on the scatter plot, so it fits."

A model can appear visually close to the data while still producing a systematic residual pattern that reveals important misfit. Always examine the residual plot after fitting a model. A model that looks "close enough" on a scatter plot may still fail the residual check — and a failed residual check means the model type is wrong, not just imprecise.

Mistake 3 — Extrapolating Nonlinear Models Too Far
"The exponential model fits well for 10 years, so I'll use it to predict 50 years from now."

Nonlinear models — especially exponential ones — can produce extreme values very quickly when extrapolated beyond the range of the observed data. Real populations cannot grow without limit. Real drug concentrations cannot fall below zero. Exponential growth always eventually slows because of resource limits, saturation, or physical constraints. Always state the range of x for which the model is reasonable, and treat predictions far outside that range with great skepticism.

Mistake 4 — Ignoring Units and Context When Interpreting Parameters
"The growth factor is 1.08."

This statement is incomplete. A growth factor of 1.08 means very different things depending on whether x is measured in days, months, years, or decades. Always state the units of x when interpreting a growth or decay factor. "The quantity grows by 8% per year" is a complete interpretation; "the growth factor is 1.08" is not.

Mistake 5 — Using an Exponential Model When Growth Eventually Slows
"The early data fit an exponential model, so the exponential model describes the full pattern."

Early-stage growth in biology, social media adoption, and technology often appears exponential, but real systems eventually run into limits — resource constraints, market saturation, or physical boundaries. Using an exponential model to predict long-term behavior in such systems overstates growth. Recognizing when to switch from an exponential to a bounded or logistic model is an important judgment skill in applied statistics.

Mistake 6 — Confusing Correlation r with Model Type Choice
"The correlation r = 0.91, so the linear model fits well."

A high correlation coefficient r does not guarantee that a linear model is appropriate. If the data are curved, a linear model might still show a moderately high r while missing the true shape. Correlation measures linear association only — it does not evaluate model type. Always base model selection on the scatter plot shape, the residual plot, and the context of the data — not on r alone.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Linear models assume a constant rate of change. When data curve, have a constant ratio between consecutive values, peak and fall, or level off, a linear model is not appropriate. Look at the scatter plot first, and examine residual plots to confirm.
  • Sec 7.2A linear model ŷ = a + bx serves as the baseline. Residuals from a good linear fit are randomly scattered around zero. A curved residual pattern signals that a nonlinear model is needed.
  • Sec 7.3Quadratic models ŷ = ax² + bx + c produce parabolas. When a < 0 the parabola opens downward with a maximum vertex; when a > 0 it opens upward with a minimum vertex. The vertex occurs at x = −b/(2a).
  • Sec 7.4Exponential growth models ŷ = a · bx with b > 1 describe quantities that multiply by a constant factor per step. The initial value is a; the growth factor is b; the growth rate is r = b − 1. Consecutive ratios are constant.
  • Sec 7.5Exponential decay models have 0 < b < 1. The decay rate is r = 1 − b. The curve decreases and approaches but never reaches zero. Half-life is a special case: after each half-life period, the quantity is multiplied by 0.5.
  • Sec 7.6Logarithmic models ŷ = a + b · ln(x) describe rapid early growth that slows over time. The model has no true maximum — it always grows, but more and more slowly. Useful for learning curves, diminishing returns, and sensory perception scales.
  • Sec 7.7Power models ŷ = a · xb produce a variety of curve shapes depending on b. Common in physics, biology, and engineering where geometric or physical scaling laws produce natural power relationships.
  • Sec 7.8Data transformation uses logarithms to linearize curved relationships. Taking ln(y) linearizes exponential data; taking ln(x) and ln(y) linearizes power data. The slope of the transformed line identifies the growth factor or exponent.
  • Sec 7.9Comparing model shapes requires asking whether the data go up or down, whether the rate accelerates or decelerates, whether there is a peak, and whether the pattern levels off. Context guides model choice alongside visual inspection.
  • Sec 7.10Parameters must be interpreted in context. In exponential models: a is the initial value, b is the factor, and r = b − 1 or 1 − b is the rate. In quadratic models: c is the initial value, and the vertex gives the maximum or minimum and when it occurs.
  • Sec 7.11Residual plots (y − ŷ vs. x) diagnose model fit. Random scatter near zero indicates good fit. A curved pattern indicates the model type is wrong. Residual plots do not specify which model to use — they only identify failures.
  • Sec 7.12Six-step model selection: (1) examine scatter plot; (2) consider context; (3) fit candidates; (4) check residual plots; (5) consider limitations; (6) state the model and its limitations. Choosing a model requires judgment, not just computation.
  • Sec 7.13Nonlinear models appear throughout biology, medicine, physics, environmental science, finance, economics, and technology. Recognizing which model applies in which field connects statistical modeling to real-world scientific and professional practice.
  • Sec 7.14Six major mistakes: assuming all curves are exponential; skipping residual checks; extrapolating too far; omitting units from parameter interpretations; applying exponential models beyond their valid range; confusing correlation r with model type suitability.

Key Terms Review

nonlinear model
A statistical model whose equation produces a curved graph rather than a straight line. Includes quadratic, exponential, logarithmic, and power models.
quadratic model
A model of the form ŷ = ax² + bx + c. Its graph is a parabola with a maximum (a < 0) or minimum (a > 0) at the vertex.
exponential growth
A model ŷ = a · bx with b > 1. The quantity is multiplied by a constant factor b per unit of x. Consecutive ratios are constant.
exponential decay
A model ŷ = a · bx with 0 < b < 1. The quantity decreases by a constant percentage per unit of x and approaches but never reaches zero.
logarithmic model
A model ŷ = a + b · ln(x). Describes quantities that grow quickly at first and then more slowly, without a fixed maximum.
power model
A model ŷ = a · xb. The shape depends on b. Common in physics and biology where geometric scaling produces natural power relationships.
growth factor
The base b in an exponential model. The multiplier applied to the quantity per unit of x. Growth rate r = b − 1 for growth; r = 1 − b for decay.
half-life
The time required for an exponentially decaying quantity to reduce to half its current value. After each half-life, the quantity is multiplied by 0.5.
residual
The difference y − ŷ between an observed data value and the value predicted by a model. Positive residuals indicate under-prediction; negative indicate over-prediction.
residual plot
A graph of residuals on the vertical axis versus x on the horizontal axis. Random scatter near zero indicates good model fit. Curved patterns indicate the wrong model type.
data transformation
Converting variable values using a mathematical operation — such as taking logarithms — to reveal patterns or linearize a nonlinear relationship.
linearization
Transforming variables so that a nonlinear relationship becomes approximately linear. Taking ln(y) linearizes exponential data; taking ln(x) and ln(y) linearizes power data.
parameters
The constants and coefficients in a model equation — such as a, b, and c — that determine its specific shape, scale, and behavior.
extrapolation
Using a model to predict values far outside the range of the observed data. Carries much greater risk of error than interpolation and must be described with appropriate limitations.

Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 8 introduced the foundations of probability: sample spaces, events, the complement rule, and the basic logic of how probability measures uncertainty. You learned that probability is not guessing — it is structured reasoning about long-run patterns and degrees of likelihood.

Chapter 9 builds on that foundation by asking a more precise question: What happens to a probability when we already know something? If you know that a randomly selected student is a junior, does that change the probability that the student is enrolled in a science course? If a card has already been drawn from a deck without being replaced, does that change the probability of the next draw? If a medical test comes back positive, what is the probability that the patient actually has the disease?

All of these questions involve conditional probability — probability that depends on information. Conditional probability is one of the most powerful and most misunderstood ideas in all of statistics. It appears in medical testing, legal reasoning, weather forecasting, insurance pricing, machine learning, and everyday decision-making.

The second half of this chapter introduces counting methods — systematic ways to count complex collections of outcomes without listing every case individually. Counting is the foundation for calculating probabilities in situations where outcomes are numerous. You will learn the Fundamental Counting Principle, permutations, combinations, and how to decide which method fits a given situation.

Section 9.1

Why Conditional Probability Matters

In Chapter 8, probability was calculated with respect to a full sample space. When you rolled a standard die, the probability of rolling a 4 was 1/6 because all six outcomes were equally likely and none were ruled out. But what if someone told you that the die had already landed on an even number? Now only three outcomes remain possible: 2, 4, and 6. The probability that the result was 4 is no longer 1/6 — it is 1/3. The condition changed the probability.

Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that some other event or condition is already known to be true. The condition shrinks the sample space from the full set of outcomes down to the set of outcomes consistent with the given information.

Why This Matters Outside the Classroom

FieldConditional Probability Application
Medical TestingWhat is the probability a patient has a disease given that the test is positive? This differs sharply from the test's accuracy rate alone.
Legal ReasoningWhat is the probability of guilt given the presented evidence? Courts have used — and misused — conditional probability in high-profile cases.
Weather Forecasting"70% chance of rain given that a cold front is approaching" is a conditional probability statement built from historical data.
Machine LearningRecommendation algorithms predict what you will click next given what you have already clicked — conditional probability at massive scale.
Insurance PricingPremiums are set based on the probability of a claim given a policyholder's age, driving history, location, and other conditions.
The Right Question

Conditional probability teaches you to ask the right question: Probability of what, given what? Failing to specify the condition — or accidentally swapping which event is the condition — leads to wrong answers with serious real-world consequences.

Section 9.2

Conditional Probability from Tables

The clearest way to understand conditional probability is to start with a two-way table. Two-way tables organize categorical data into rows and columns so that joint and marginal counts are easy to read. To calculate a conditional probability from a table: (1) identify the condition — the row or column that restricts the sample space; (2) find the total for that row or column — this becomes the new, restricted sample space; (3) find the count that satisfies both the condition and the event; (4) divide.

Example 9.2.1
Finding a conditional probability from a two-way table
  • ProblemUsing the enrollment table (Sophomores: 40 Art, 60 Music; Juniors: 50 Art, 50 Music; Total: 200), find the probability that a randomly selected student takes Music, given that the student is a junior.
  • ThinkThe condition is "given that the student is a junior." This restricts the sample space to the Junior row only. Among the 100 juniors, find those who take Music.
  • RepresentFrom the table: 100 juniors total; 50 take Music.
  • CalculateP(Music | Junior) = 50/100 = 0.50
  • CheckAmong all 200 students, P(Music) = 110/200 = 0.55. The conditional probability of 0.50 differs from 0.55, which makes sense — knowing a student is a junior does change the probability.
  • InterpretThere is a 50% probability that a randomly selected student takes Music, given that the student is a junior.
Answer: P(Music | Junior) = 0.50
Example 9.2.2
Two different conditional probabilities from the same table
  • ProblemFind (a) P(Art | Sophomore) and (b) P(Sophomore | Art). These are different questions.
  • Part (a)Condition: Sophomore row. Total: 100. Art sophomores: 40. P(Art | Sophomore) = 40/100 = 0.40
  • Part (b)Condition: Art column. Total Art students: 90. Sophomores in Art: 40. P(Sophomore | Art) = 40/90 ≈ 0.444
  • InterpretPart (a) asks "Among sophomores, what fraction takes Art?" Part (b) asks "Among Art students, what fraction are sophomores?" These are genuinely different questions with different answers.
Answers: P(Art | Sophomore) = 0.40    P(Sophomore | Art) ≈ 0.444
Student Warning — The Order of the Bar Matters

P(A | B) and P(B | A) are almost never equal. Swapping the event and the condition is one of the most consequential errors in probabilistic reasoning. Always re-read the problem to identify which event is the condition (after the vertical bar) and which is the event of interest (before the vertical bar).

Section 9.3

Conditional Probability Notation

Once you understand the table method, you can generalize it into a formula that works in any setting. Recall that P(A ∩ B) means the probability that both A and B occur. The conditional probability formula is:

Conditional Probability Formula

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

To find the probability of A given B, divide the joint probability of both A and B by the probability of B alone. When we condition on B, we restrict the sample space to B. The formula scales the joint probability so it is expressed as a fraction of B's probability rather than the full sample space.

Example 9.3.1
Using the conditional probability formula
  • ProblemA survey of 500 adults: 300 own a car, 220 own a bicycle, 180 own both. Find P(Car | Bicycle).
  • ThinkWe want P(Car | Bicycle). We need P(Car ∩ Bicycle) and P(Bicycle).
  • RepresentP(Car ∩ Bicycle) = 180/500 = 0.36 P(Bicycle) = 220/500 = 0.44
  • CalculateP(Car | Bicycle) = 0.36 / 0.44 ≈ 0.818
  • CheckP(Car) = 300/500 = 0.60. Since P(Car | Bicycle) ≈ 0.818 > 0.60, knowing someone owns a bicycle makes it more likely they also own a car — plausible given the data.
  • InterpretAmong adults who own a bicycle, approximately 81.8% also own a car.
Answer: P(Car | Bicycle) ≈ 0.818
Rearranging the Formula

The conditional probability formula can be rearranged to give a formula for joint probability: P(A ∩ B) = P(B) · P(A | B). This is the General Multiplication Rule, developed fully in Section 9.5.

Section 9.4

Independent and Dependent Events

Definition of Independence

Events A and B are independent if knowing that one occurred does not change the probability of the other:

P(A | B) = P(A)    equivalently    P(B | A) = P(B)

Events are dependent if knowing that one occurred does change the probability of the other: P(A | B) ≠ P(A).

Example 9.4.1
Testing independence from a two-way table
  • ProblemUsing the enrollment table, determine whether "takes Music" and "is a Junior" are independent.
  • ThinkIf independent, then P(Music | Junior) must equal P(Music).
  • CalculateP(Music) = 110/200 = 0.55 P(Music | Junior) = 50/100 = 0.50
  • Check0.50 ≠ 0.55, so the events are not independent.
  • InterpretKnowing a student is a junior does change the probability of taking Music. The events are dependent — though only weakly so. Juniors are slightly less likely to take Music compared to the overall student population.
Answer: Events are dependent. P(Music | Junior) = 0.50 ≠ P(Music) = 0.55.
Student Warning — Independence vs. Mutual Exclusivity

Students often confuse independence with mutual exclusivity. These are opposite ideas in most practical cases. Mutually exclusive events cannot both occur: P(A ∩ B) = 0. Independent events can both occur; knowing one happened does not change the probability of the other. If two events are mutually exclusive and each has positive probability, they are not independent — because if one occurs, the probability of the other drops to zero, which is a change.

Example 9.4.2
With and without replacement
  • ProblemA bag contains 5 red marbles and 3 blue marbles. (a) Two marbles drawn with replacement — what is P(both red)? (b) Two marbles drawn without replacement — what is P(both red)?
  • Part (a) — With ReplacementEach draw is independent. The bag is restored before each draw. P(both red) = (5/8) × (5/8) = 25/64 ≈ 0.391
  • Part (b) — Without ReplacementThe first draw changes what remains. If the first marble was red, only 4 red and 3 blue remain (7 total). P(first red) = 5/8 P(second red | first red) = 4/7 P(both red) = (5/8) × (4/7) = 20/56 = 5/14 ≈ 0.357
  • InterpretWithout replacement, the events are dependent — drawing one red marble reduces the probability of drawing a second. The probability decreases from 0.391 to 0.357.
Answers: (a) 25/64 ≈ 0.391    (b) 5/14 ≈ 0.357

Section 9.5

Multiplication Rule

Rearranging the conditional probability formula gives the General Multiplication Rule, which finds the probability that both A and B occur:

Multiplication Rules

General Rule (always valid):

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B | A) = P(B) · P(A | B)

Simplified Rule (independent events only):

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B)

Do not use the simplified rule when events are dependent. It is a special case of the general rule, valid only when P(B | A) = P(B).

Example 9.5.1
Applying the General Multiplication Rule — cards without replacement
  • ProblemFrom a deck of 52 cards, two are drawn without replacement. What is the probability that both are aces?
  • ThinkThese events are dependent — drawing one ace reduces the aces remaining. Use the General Multiplication Rule.
  • RepresentLet A₁ = first card is an ace, A₂ = second card is an ace.
  • CalculateP(A₁) = 4/52 P(A₂ | A₁) = 3/51 (3 aces remain among 51 cards) P(A₁ ∩ A₂) = (4/52) × (3/51) = 12/2652 = 1/221 ≈ 0.00452
  • CheckAbout 0.45% — small but reasonable for drawing two specific cards in a row.
  • InterpretThe probability of drawing two aces in a row without replacement is approximately 0.45%.
Answer: P(both aces) = 1/221 ≈ 0.00452
Example 9.5.2
Applying the Simplified Rule — independent components
  • ProblemEach computer component independently has a 3% defect rate. If three components are tested, what is the probability that all three are defective?
  • ThinkComponents are tested independently. Use the simplified Multiplication Rule.
  • CalculateP(all three defective) = (0.03)³ = 0.000027
  • InterpretThe probability that all three components are defective is about 0.0027% — extremely small, as expected since each individual defect is already rare.
Answer: P(all three defective) = 0.000027
Section 9.6

Addition Rule

In Chapter 8, you learned that for mutually exclusive events, P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B). But when events can both occur — when they share outcomes — simply adding probabilities overcounts the overlap. The General Addition Rule corrects for this:

General Addition Rule

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

The term P(A ∩ B) is subtracted because outcomes in both A and B were counted twice — once in P(A) and once in P(B). For mutually exclusive events, P(A ∩ B) = 0, so the rule reduces to simple addition.

Example 9.6.1
Applying the General Addition Rule
  • ProblemIn a group of 100 students, 60 play a sport, 45 play a musical instrument, and 20 do both. What is the probability a randomly selected student plays a sport or an instrument?
  • ThinkThese events are not mutually exclusive — 20 students do both. Use the General Addition Rule.
  • RepresentLet S = plays a sport, M = plays an instrument. P(S) = 0.60, P(M) = 0.45, P(S ∩ M) = 0.20.
  • CalculateP(S ∪ M) = 0.60 + 0.45 − 0.20 = 0.85
  • CheckUsing a Venn diagram: 40 sport only + 25 instrument only + 20 both = 85 students. 85/100 = 0.85. ✓
  • InterpretThe probability that a randomly selected student plays a sport or an instrument (or both) is 85%.
Answer: P(S ∪ M) = 0.85
Example 9.6.2
Heart or face card — deck of cards
  • ProblemA single card is drawn from a standard 52-card deck. Find P(heart or face card).
  • ThinkNot mutually exclusive — some face cards are hearts (J♥, Q♥, K♥). Use the General Addition Rule.
  • Represent13 hearts, 12 face cards, 3 face cards that are hearts.
  • CalculateP(H ∪ F) = 13/52 + 12/52 − 3/52 = 22/52 = 11/26 ≈ 0.423
  • InterpretThe probability of drawing a heart or a face card is approximately 42.3%.
Answer: P(heart or face card) = 11/26 ≈ 0.423
Section 9.7

Tree Diagrams with Conditional Probabilities

A tree diagram organizes outcomes of a multi-stage process where probabilities at each stage may depend on earlier stages. To build a tree diagram: (1) draw a starting node; (2) for each first-stage outcome, draw a labeled branch showing the probability; (3) from each branch endpoint, draw branches for the next stage using conditional probabilities; (4) multiply along branches to find joint probabilities; (5) add branch-end probabilities for "or" questions.

Display Instruction — Two-Stage Tree Diagram

Draw a tree diagram with two stages. Stage 1 splits into two branches: "Junior" (probability 0.50) and "Sophomore" (probability 0.50). From "Junior," draw two branches: "Art" (0.50) and "Music" (0.50). From "Sophomore," draw two branches: "Art" (0.40) and "Music" (0.60). Label each complete path with its joint probability (probability of the branch times probability of the sub-branch). Verify that the four joint probabilities sum to 1.

Example 9.7.1
Tree diagram for medical testing — base rate reasoning
  • ProblemA disease affects 1% of a population. A test is 90% sensitive (correctly identifies 90% with the disease) and 95% specific (correctly identifies 95% without it). A randomly selected person tests positive. What is the probability they actually have the disease?
  • ThinkBuild a two-stage tree: (1) does the person have the disease? (2) does the test return positive? Multiply along branches for joint probabilities.
  • RepresentStage 1: P(Disease) = 0.01, P(No Disease) = 0.99. Stage 2 — if Disease: P(Positive | D) = 0.90, P(Negative | D) = 0.10. If No Disease: P(Positive | D') = 0.05, P(Negative | D') = 0.95.
  • CalculateJoint probabilities from each complete path: Disease AND Positive: 0.01 × 0.90 = 0.0090 Disease AND Negative: 0.01 × 0.10 = 0.0010 No Disease AND Positive: 0.99 × 0.05 = 0.0495 No Disease AND Negative: 0.99 × 0.95 = 0.9405 Total: = 1.0000 P(Positive) = 0.0090 + 0.0495 = 0.0585 P(Disease | Positive) = 0.0090 / 0.0585 ≈ 0.154
  • CheckMany students expect this to be near 90%. But the disease affects only 1% of people, so most positive tests come from the vast pool of healthy people who happen to test positive (false positives).
  • InterpretEven with a positive test result, there is only about a 15.4% probability that this person actually has the disease. This is called the positive predictive value — and it depends critically on how common the disease is in the population.
Answer: P(Disease | Positive) ≈ 0.154, or about 15.4%
Data Ethics Note — Medical Testing and False Positives

This result explains why mass screening programs for rare diseases can be misleading. A patient who receives a positive result may experience serious anxiety, additional testing, or unnecessary treatment when the actual probability of disease is still quite low. Medical professionals must understand conditional probability and base rates to communicate test results responsibly. Reporting only sensitivity and specificity, without considering prevalence, can lead patients and providers to overestimate how alarming a positive test truly is.

Section 9.8

Fundamental Counting Principle

Fundamental Counting Principle

If a first event can occur in m ways, and for each of those ways a second event can independently occur in n ways, then the two events together can occur in m × n ways.

This extends to any number of stages: when choices at each stage are independent, the total number of outcomes is the product of the number of ways at each stage.

Example 9.8.1
Counting meal combinations
  • ProblemA cafeteria offers 4 main dishes, 3 sides, and 2 beverages. How many different meals (one of each) are possible?
  • Calculate4 × 3 × 2 = 24 different meal combinations
Answer: 24 meal combinations
Example 9.8.2
Counting passwords — with and without repetition
  • Problem(a) A 4-character password uses any letter (26) or digit (10), 36 total per character, with repetition allowed. How many passwords? (b) A 3-digit lock combination uses digits 0–9 with no repetition. How many combinations?
  • Part (a)36 × 36 × 36 × 36 = 36⁴ = 1,679,616 passwords
  • Part (b)First digit: 10 choices. Second digit: 9. Third digit: 8. 10 × 9 × 8 = 720 combinations
  • InterpretLonger passwords with larger character sets are exponentially more secure. Without repetition, the count drops substantially from 10³ = 1000 to 720.
Answers: (a) 1,679,616    (b) 720
Section 9.9

Factorials

Factorial Definition

The factorial of a positive integer n, written n!, is the product of all positive integers from n down to 1:

n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ··· × 2 × 1

By definition: 0! = 1

This convention is necessary for counting formulas to work correctly — without it, combination and permutation formulas would divide by zero.

Counting arrangements of a full set

Section 9.10

Permutations

Permutations — Order Matters

A permutation is an arrangement of a selection of items in which order matters. The number of permutations of r items chosen from n distinct items is:

P(n, r) = n! / (n−r)!

This equals n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ··· × (n−r+1) — the product of r descending factors starting at n.

Example 9.10.1
Race placements — ordered positions
  • ProblemEight athletes compete. How many ways can first, second, and third place be awarded?
  • ThinkOrder matters — first, second, and third are distinct. Use P(8, 3).
  • CalculateP(8, 3) = 8! / (8−3)! = 8! / 5! = 8 × 7 × 6 = 336
  • InterpretThere are 336 different ways to award the three medals among 8 athletes.
Answer: P(8, 3) = 336
Example 9.10.2
Arranging letters — no repetition
  • ProblemHow many 3-letter arrangements can be made from the letters of MATH if no letter is repeated?
  • ThinkOrder matters — MAT ≠ TMA. Use P(4, 3).
  • CalculateP(4, 3) = 4! / 1! = 24 / 1 = 24
Answer: P(4, 3) = 24 arrangements
Section 9.11

Combinations

Combinations — Order Does Not Matter

A combination is a selection of items in which order does not matter. The number of combinations of r items from n distinct items is:

C(n, r) = n! / [r! × (n−r)!]

Also written as ⁿCᵣ or "n choose r." Since each selection of r items can be arranged in r! ways, C(n,r) = P(n,r) / r!.

Example 9.11.1
Counting committee selections
  • ProblemA class of 10 students must select a committee of 3. How many different committees are possible?
  • ThinkOrder does not matter — {Alice, Bob, Chen} is the same committee regardless of order. Use C(10, 3).
  • CalculateC(10, 3) = 10! / [3! × 7!] = (10 × 9 × 8) / (3 × 2 × 1) = 720 / 6 = 120
Answer: C(10, 3) = 120 committees
Example 9.11.2
Five-card hands from a standard deck
  • ProblemHow many different 5-card hands are possible from a 52-card deck?
  • ThinkOrder of cards in a hand does not matter. Use C(52, 5).
  • CalculateC(52, 5) = 52! / [5! × 47!] = (52 × 51 × 50 × 49 × 48) / (5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1) = 311,875,200 / 120 = 2,598,960
  • InterpretThere are over 2.5 million possible 5-card hands. This is why certain poker hands are exceedingly rare — favorable outcomes are tiny fractions of this total.
Answer: C(52, 5) = 2,598,960 hands
Symmetry Property of Combinations

C(n, r) = C(n, n−r). Choosing 3 items from 10 gives the same count as choosing 7 items from 10, because selecting 3 is equivalent to deciding which 7 are not selected. Use this to simplify calculations: C(52, 48) = C(52, 4).

Section 9.12

Choosing Between Permutations and Combinations

The hardest part of permutation and combination problems is deciding which formula to use. The answer turns on one question: Does the order of selection matter?

Example 9.12.1
Identifying permutation versus combination in context
  • Situation (a)A club must choose a president, vice president, and treasurer from 15 members. → Permutation — these are distinct roles. The same 3 people in different roles produce different outcomes.P(15, 3) = 15 × 14 × 13 = 2,730
  • Situation (b)A book club selects 4 books to read from a list of 12. → Combination — a selection with no ordered ranking.C(12, 4) = 12! / [4! × 8!] = 495
  • Situation (c)A playlist is created by choosing 5 songs from 20 and arranging them in order. → Permutation — the sequence matters.P(20, 5) = 20 × 19 × 18 × 17 × 16 = 1,860,480
  • Situation (d)A school board selects a 3-member subcommittee from 8 board members. → Combination — no ranked positions.C(8, 3) = 56
Example 9.12.2
Computing both — comparing ordered and unordered selection
  • ProblemFrom a group of 6 people, select 3. (a) If assigned to leader, recorder, presenter: how many outcomes? (b) If they simply form a group with no roles: how many outcomes?
  • Part (a)P(6, 3) = 6! / 3! = 6 × 5 × 4 = 120
  • Part (b)C(6, 3) = 6! / [3! × 3!] = 120 / 6 = 20
  • InterpretAssigning distinct roles multiplies the count by 3! = 6. The relationship P(n,r) = r! × C(n,r) reflects exactly this: each unordered group can be arranged in r! different ordered ways.
Answers: (a) 120 with assigned roles    (b) 20 without roles
Section 9.13

Counting and Probability Applications

Once you know how to count, you can calculate probabilities in complex settings using the classical probability formula: P(A) = (number of outcomes in A) / (total number of equally likely outcomes). The challenge is counting both the favorable outcomes and the total outcomes carefully.

Example 9.13.1
Probability of exactly 2 aces in a 5-card hand
  • ProblemWhat is the probability that a randomly dealt 5-card hand contains exactly 2 aces?
  • ThinkCount total 5-card hands and favorable hands using combinations. For exactly 2 aces: choose 2 aces from 4, and choose 3 non-aces from the remaining 48.
  • CalculateTotal hands: C(52, 5) = 2,598,960 Favorable: C(4, 2) × C(48, 3) = 6 × 17,296 = 103,776 P(exactly 2 aces) = 103,776 / 2,598,960 ≈ 0.0399
  • InterpretThe probability of being dealt exactly 2 aces in a 5-card hand is approximately 4.0%.
Answer: P(exactly 2 aces) ≈ 0.0399 or about 4.0%
Example 9.13.2
Conditional probability with counting — committee selection
  • ProblemA committee of 4 is selected randomly from 6 seniors and 4 juniors. Given that at least 1 senior is on the committee, what is the probability that all 4 members are seniors?
  • ThinkUse complementary counting to find "at least 1 senior," then apply the conditional probability formula.
  • CalculateTotal committees: C(10, 4) = 210 All-senior committees: C(6, 4) = 15 All-junior committees: C(4, 4) = 1 At least 1 senior: 210 − 1 = 209 P(all seniors | at least 1 senior) = 15/209 ≈ 0.0718
  • InterpretGiven that at least one senior is on the committee, there is approximately a 7.2% probability that all four members are seniors.
Answer: P(all seniors | at least 1 senior) = 15/209 ≈ 0.072

Section 9.14

Common Conditional Probability and Counting Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Swapping the Condition and the Event
Interpreting P(Positive | Disease) = 0.90 as P(Disease | Positive) = 0.90

P(A | B) and P(B | A) are almost never equal. A 90% sensitive test does not mean a positive result has a 90% probability of disease — prevalence matters. Always identify which event is the condition and which is the event of interest.

Mistake 2 — Confusing Independence with Mutual Exclusivity
"Events A and B can't both happen, so they must be independent."

Mutually exclusive events with positive probability are actually dependent — if one occurs, the other cannot, which is a change in probability. Independence means P(A | B) = P(A), not that they share no outcomes.

Mistake 3 — Using the Simplified Multiplication Rule for Dependent Events
P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B) when drawing cards or marbles without replacement

The simplified rule P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B) is valid only for independent events. Without replacement, each draw changes what remains. Always use the General Multiplication Rule: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B | A).

Mistake 4 — Adding Probabilities Without Subtracting the Overlap
P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) = 0.60 + 0.45 = 1.05

A probability greater than 1 is impossible. When events can overlap, use P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B). The simple addition rule applies only to mutually exclusive events.

Mistake 5 — Using Permutations When Combinations Are Needed
Computing P(10, 3) = 720 for "choose 3 committee members from 10"

A committee has no ranked positions — Alice-Bob-Chen is the same committee as Chen-Bob-Alice. Using P(10, 3) overcounts by 3! = 6. The correct answer is C(10, 3) = 120. Ask: does the order of selection create a different outcome?

Mistake 6 — Using Combinations When Permutations Are Needed
Computing C(8, 3) = 56 for "award gold, silver, and bronze to 3 of 8 athletes"

Gold, silver, and bronze are distinct — the same 3 athletes in different medal positions produce different outcomes. The correct answer is P(8, 3) = 336. When distinct roles, ranks, or positions are assigned, use permutations.

Mistake 7 — Treating 0! as 0
C(n, n) = n! / (n! × 0!) → dividing by zero because "0! = 0"

By definition, 0! = 1. This convention is not a computation result — it is the value needed to make counting formulas work. Memorize it: C(n, n) = 1, because there is exactly one way to choose all n items from n.

Mistake 8 — Ignoring Dependency in the Counting Principle
Multiplying 10 × 10 × 10 for a 3-digit lock combination when no digit repeats

The Fundamental Counting Principle assumes independent choices. When earlier selections restrict later options (as in no-repetition problems), the choices are not independent. Use 10 × 9 × 8 = 720, not 10³ = 1000.

Mistake 9 — Ignoring the Base Rate
Concluding that a 90%-accurate test means a positive result is 90% likely to indicate disease

Base rate (prevalence) matters enormously. When a disease affects only 1% of people, even a 90% sensitive test produces many false positives from the large healthy population. P(Disease | Positive) can be very low even with accurate tests for rare conditions.

Mistake 10 — Mishandling "At Least One" Calculations
Computing P(at least one event) by adding individual probabilities

Adding individual cases quickly becomes complex and risks double-counting. Use the complement: P(at least one) = 1 − P(none). This is almost always simpler and avoids enumeration errors.

Chapter 9 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 9.1Conditional probability asks: given that we know something, what is the probability of something else? The condition restricts the sample space and can substantially change probability. This applies in medicine, law, forecasting, machine learning, and insurance.
  • Sec 9.2Two-way tables are the clearest way to compute conditional probability. Identify the condition (a row or column), use that total as the restricted sample space, and divide the joint count by that total. P(A | B) ≠ P(B | A) in general.
  • Sec 9.3The conditional probability formula is P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B). It can be rearranged to P(A ∩ B) = P(B) · P(A | B), which is the foundation of the Multiplication Rule.
  • Sec 9.4Events are independent if P(A | B) = P(A) — knowing one occurred does not change the probability of the other. Events are dependent otherwise. Independence and mutual exclusivity are different: mutually exclusive events with positive probability are dependent.
  • Sec 9.5General Multiplication Rule: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B | A). For independent events only: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B). Use the general rule whenever events are drawn without replacement or otherwise dependent.
  • Sec 9.6General Addition Rule: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B). The overlap P(A ∩ B) must be subtracted because it is counted twice in P(A) + P(B). For mutually exclusive events, P(A ∩ B) = 0 and the rule simplifies.
  • Sec 9.7Tree diagrams organize multi-stage probability problems. Multiply along branches for joint probabilities; add branch-end probabilities for "or" questions. The medical testing example shows that base rates matter: a rare disease produces many false positives even with an accurate test.
  • Sec 9.8The Fundamental Counting Principle: when choices are independent across stages, multiply the number of ways at each stage. When earlier choices restrict later ones, adjust the counts accordingly.
  • Sec 9.9n! = n × (n−1) × ··· × 1, with 0! = 1 by definition. Factorials count the number of ways to arrange n distinct objects. They grow rapidly: 10! exceeds 3.6 million.
  • Sec 9.10Permutations count ordered arrangements: P(n, r) = n! / (n−r)!. Use when distinct positions, roles, or ranks are assigned — first/second/third, president/vice president, sequence of songs on a playlist.
  • Sec 9.11Combinations count unordered selections: C(n, r) = n! / [r!(n−r)!]. Use when members of a group, committee, or selection are interchangeable — no roles assigned. Symmetry: C(n, r) = C(n, n−r).
  • Sec 9.12The key question: does order matter? Permutations when yes (arrangements, rankings, sequences); combinations when no (groups, teams, selections). P(n,r) = r! × C(n,r) — permutations count each unordered group r! times.
  • Sec 9.13Counting enables probability calculation: P(A) = favorable outcomes / total equally likely outcomes. Combine counting methods with conditional probability for complex problems. Use complement counting for "at least one" situations.
  • Sec 9.14Ten recurring errors: swapping P(A|B) and P(B|A); confusing independence with mutual exclusivity; using the simplified multiplication rule for dependent events; omitting the overlap in the Addition Rule; using permutations vs. combinations incorrectly; treating 0! as 0; ignoring base rates.

Key Terms Review

conditional probability
The probability of an event given that another event is known to have occurred. The condition restricts the sample space.
P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)
joint probability
The probability that two events both occur, written P(A ∩ B). Found in the overlapping cell of a two-way table divided by the grand total.
independent events
Two events where knowing that one occurred does not change the probability of the other. P(A | B) = P(A).
dependent events
Two events where knowing that one occurred does change the probability of the other. P(A | B) ≠ P(A).
General Multiplication Rule
Gives the joint probability of two events for any situation, including dependent events.
P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B | A)
Simplified Multiplication Rule
Valid only when events are independent. Simplifies joint probability calculation.
P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B)
General Addition Rule
Gives the probability that at least one of two events occurs, subtracting the overlap to avoid double-counting.
P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)
mutually exclusive
Events that cannot both occur: P(A ∩ B) = 0. For mutually exclusive events, the Addition Rule simplifies to P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B).
tree diagram
A branching diagram that organizes outcomes and conditional probabilities across multiple stages. Multiply along branches; add across endpoints.
Fundamental Counting Principle
When choices are independent across stages, the total number of outcomes equals the product of the number of ways at each stage.
factorial
n! = n × (n−1) × ··· × 1, the product of all positive integers from n down to 1. By definition, 0! = 1.
permutation
An arrangement of items in which order matters. Use when distinct positions or roles are assigned.
P(n, r) = n! / (n−r)!
combination
A selection of items in which order does not matter. Use when members are interchangeable, with no assigned roles.
C(n, r) = n! / [r!(n−r)!]
positive predictive value
P(disease | positive test). Depends on the test's sensitivity, specificity, and the disease's prevalence (base rate) in the population.
base rate
The prevalence of a condition in a population. A critical factor in interpreting conditional probabilities from test results or other evidence.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 10: Random Variables and Probability Distributions

Conditional probability and counting form the direct foundation of Chapter 10. In Chapter 10, you will assign numerical values to uncertain outcomes and use probability to study their long-run behavior — their expected value, their variability, and their distribution across all possible outcomes.

The binomial distribution — one of the most important probability models in all of statistics — depends directly on the combination counting you practiced here. When you ask "what is the probability of exactly k successes in n independent trials?", the C(n, k) formula counts the number of ways those successes can be arranged among the n trials.

In Chapter 12 and Chapter 13, the conditional reasoning from this chapter reappears in inference. The p-value — the heart of hypothesis testing — is a conditional probability: the probability of observing evidence at least as strong as what was observed, given that the null hypothesis is true.

In college probability and statistics, these ideas deepen into Bayes' Theorem (a general formula for updating probabilities given evidence), multivariate distributions, and stochastic processes. In data science and machine learning, conditional probability drives classification algorithms, recommendation engines, spam filters, and language models. In medicine and public health, the base rate reasoning from Example 9.7.1 — the positive predictive value problem — is an essential tool for interpreting screening results responsibly.

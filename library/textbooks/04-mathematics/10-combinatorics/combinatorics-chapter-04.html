Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter develops the mathematics of unordered selection. In Chapter 3, you studied arrangements — situations where the order of objects matters and different orderings count as different outcomes. This chapter focuses on the opposite situation: choosing a group of objects where the order of selection is irrelevant, and only the composition of the group matters.

This distinction — order matters versus order does not matter — is one of the most important in all of combinatorics. Confusing it is also one of the most common sources of counting errors. The goal of this chapter is to build the concept of a combination from the ground up, connect it clearly to permutations, develop the binomial coefficient as a central piece of mathematical notation, and apply combinatorial selection to committee problems, restricted choices, probability, and data analysis.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to recognize when a problem involves an unordered selection, apply the combination formula correctly, handle restrictions and required or forbidden elements, use complementary counting, and interpret your results in context.

Opening Question

A university committee on curriculum must be formed from a department of twelve faculty members. The committee will have five members. No one has been assigned a special role — every committee member has the same status.

How many different committees are possible? Before reading further, consider: does it matter which order the five faculty members were chosen? If Professor Adesanya is chosen first and Professor Nakamura is chosen fifth, is that a different committee from one in which Professor Nakamura is chosen first? The answer is no. A committee is a group, not a sequence. This is the defining feature of a combination.

Why This Chapter Matters

Permutations count arrangements. Combinations count groups. Each has its domain, and both are essential. Committees, teams, data subsets, sample selections in probability, card hands in game theory, encryption key components in cryptography, feature selections in machine learning, subgraphs in graph theory — all of these are modeled by combinations.

The combination formula also produces the binomial coefficients, which appear in Pascal's Triangle, polynomial algebra, probability theory, the Binomial Theorem, and countless other mathematical structures. Understanding combinations opens a major thread running through the rest of this textbook and into higher mathematics.

Section 4.1

What a Selection Is

A selection is a choice of objects from a set. The essential feature of a selection is that you are identifying which objects are chosen — not specifying any order among them. Compare these two situations carefully.

Feature Situation A — Arrangement Situation B — Selection
Scenario Three students photographed in labeled positions: left, center, right Three students chosen to represent the class at a conference
Does order matter? Yes — swapping positions produces a different photograph No — the group of three representatives is the same group regardless of selection order
What is counted? Arrangements — sequences of objects in positions Groups — subsets of objects with no internal ordering
Mathematical structure Permutation Combination

The mathematical structure of Situation B — choosing a group where the order of selection does not affect the identity of the group — is what this chapter develops. A selection of k objects from a set of n distinct objects, where order does not matter, is called a combination.

The Subset Connection

A combination of k objects from a set S of n distinct objects is exactly a subset of size k drawn from S. If S = {A, B, C, D} and you are selecting groups of 2, the distinct groups are:

{A, B} {A, C} {A, D} {B, C} {B, D} {C, D}

There are six such subsets. Notice that {A, B} and {B, A} are the same subset — they contain the same two elements. Combinations count distinct subsets, not distinct ordered pairs. This is the conceptual foundation of everything that follows.

Section 4.2

Why Order Does Not Matter

When order does not matter, multiple arrangements collapse into a single selection. Understanding this collapse is the key to deriving the combination formula correctly.

Suppose you are choosing a group of 3 people from 5: call them A, B, C, D, E. If you were counting ordered arrangements of 3 from 5, you would count P(5,3) = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60 ordered triples. But many of these triples represent the same group. For example, all six of the following ordered triples correspond to the same unordered group {A, B, C}:

(A, B, C) (A, C, B) (B, A, C) (B, C, A) (C, A, B) (C, B, A)

These six ordered arrangements all correspond to the same unordered group {A, B, C}. There are exactly 3! = 6 arrangements of any fixed 3-element group. So the 60 ordered arrangements are organized into groups of 6, each group corresponding to one unordered selection.

The Overcounting Argument

Step 1. Count ordered arrangements of k from n: P(n,k) = n! / (n−k)!

Step 2. Each unordered selection has been counted k! times — once for each ordering of the selected group.

Step 3. Divide: P(n,k) / k! = n! / (k! · (n−k)!)

This quotient is the combination formula. Division by k! is the correction factor that removes the overcounting caused by treating the same group in different orders as different outcomes.

Checking with a Small Case

For choosing 3 from 5: P(5,3) = 60. Each group of 3 is counted 3! = 6 times. Number of groups: 60 / 6 = 10. You can verify this by listing all groups of 3 from {A, B, C, D, E} — there are exactly 10.

Practice 4.2Conceptual Reasoning
  1. Choosing 2 from {P, Q, R, S}: list all ordered pairs, then list all unordered groups. How many ordered pairs are there? How many unordered groups? What is the ratio?
  2. Explain why dividing by k! is the correct correction when moving from P(n,k) to C(n,k). Why k! specifically?
  3. If you choose 4 objects from a set of 7 with order, you get P(7,4) ordered arrangements. How many of these correspond to each single unordered group? How many unordered groups are there?
Section 4.3

Combinations

Definition — Combination

The number of ways to choose k objects from a set of n distinct objects, without regard to order, is denoted C(n,k) and equals:

Combination Formula
C(n,k) = n! / (k! · (n−k)!)
Applies when objects are distinct, repetition is not allowed, and order does not matter.

Boundary Cases

Choosing 0 objects: C(n,0) = n! / (0! · n!) = 1/1 = 1. There is exactly one way to choose nothing — the empty selection.

Choosing all n objects: C(n,n) = n! / (n! · 0!) = 1. There is exactly one way to choose every object.

Choosing 1 object: C(n,1) = n! / (1!(n−1)!) = n. Each of the n objects can be the sole chosen element.

Quick Verification Check

After computing a combination, check the boundary behavior: C(n,0) should equal 1, C(n,n) should equal 1, and C(n,1) should equal n. If any of these fail for your formula, something has gone wrong.

Example 4.1
Choosing a Curriculum Committee
  • ProblemA department has 12 faculty members. A 5-person curriculum committee will be formed. All members have equal status. How many different committees are possible?
  • ThinkNo positions are assigned. The committee is a group, not a ranked list. Order does not matter. This is a combination problem: choosing 5 from 12 without regard to order.
  • RepresentLet the 12 faculty members be the objects. Select a subset of size 5. The number of such subsets is C(12,5).
  • Count C(12,5) = 12! / (5! · 7!) = (12 × 11 × 10 × 9 × 8) / (5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1) = 95040 / 120 = 792
  • CheckThe answer should be at most C(12,6) = 924 (the maximum is achieved near the middle selection size). The answer 792 is slightly less, which is reasonable for k = 5 from n = 12.
  • InterpretThere are 792 different possible curriculum committees.
Answer: C(12,5) = 792 committees.
Example 4.2
A Small Case Verified by Listing
  • ProblemFour students — Ana, Bo, Cal, Dani — are being considered for a 2-person study pair. No roles are assigned. How many study pairs are possible?
  • ThinkOrder does not matter. {Ana, Bo} and {Bo, Ana} are the same pair.
  • Count C(4,2) = 4! / (2! · 2!) = 24/4 = 6
  • VerifyThe six pairs: {A,B}, {A,C}, {A,D}, {B,C}, {B,D}, {C,D}. Count by listing: 6 pairs.
  • InterpretThe formula and the listing agree. There are 6 possible study pairs.
Answer: C(4,2) = 6 study pairs. Verified by complete listing.

Section 4.4

Binomial Coefficients

The expression C(n,k) is so important that it has its own notation and its own name: the binomial coefficient. It is written with n on top and k on the bottom inside large parentheses, read as "n choose k." The term "binomial coefficient" comes from the fact that these numbers appear as the coefficients when a binomial expression (x + y)ⁿ is expanded — a connection developed fully in Chapter 5.

Binomial Coefficient Notation
C(n,k) = "n choose k" = n! / (k! · (n−k)!)
The notation C(n,k) and the "n choose k" phrasing are standard in combinatorics, probability, algebra, and computer science. Become fluent in reading and writing both.
Why "Binomial"?

A binomial is an expression with two terms, like (x + y). When you expand (x + y)ⁿ by multiplying the binomial by itself n times, each term in the result has a coefficient. Those coefficients are exactly the binomial coefficients C(n,0), C(n,1), …, C(n,n). Chapter 5 will show why this is true — and it is a combination argument.

Section 4.5

Reading C(n,k)

When you encounter C(n,k), read it as a question: "In how many ways can I choose k things from n things, if order does not matter?" The top number, n, is the size of the set you are selecting from. The bottom number, k, is the size of the group you are selecting. The restriction 0 ≤ k ≤ n must hold. If k < 0 or k > n, the binomial coefficient is defined to be 0.

ExpressionReadingValue
C(8,3)Choose 3 from 856
C(10,0)Choose 0 from 101
C(7,7)Choose all 7 from 71
C(6,1)Choose 1 from 66
C(5,2)Choose 2 from 510
C(5,3)Choose 3 from 510 — same as C(5,2)!

The Symmetry Property

Notice that C(5,2) = C(5,3) = 10. This is not a coincidence. In general: C(n,k) = C(n, n−k).

Why. Choosing k objects to include is the same as choosing the remaining n − k objects to exclude. Every selection of k objects corresponds to exactly one exclusion of n − k objects from the same set. The two counts must be equal.

This symmetry is also useful computationally: if k is large, it may be easier to compute C(n, n−k) instead.

Example 4.3
Using the Symmetry Property
  • ProblemCompute C(20,17).
  • ThinkC(20,17) = C(20, 20−17) = C(20,3). Computing C(20,3) is far easier than expanding 20! / (17! · 3!) directly with large factorials.
  • Count C(20,3) = (20 × 19 × 18) / (3 × 2 × 1) = 6840 / 6 = 1140
  • InterpretC(20,17) = 1140. There are 1,140 ways to choose 17 objects from 20 — the same as choosing which 3 to leave out.
Answer: C(20,17) = C(20,3) = 1,140.
Student Warning — Symmetry Works Within n

The symmetry property is C(n,k) = C(n, n−k). The n stays fixed; only the bottom index changes. Do not confuse this with writing C(k,n), which is undefined when k < n. C(10,7) = C(10,3) = 120, not C(7,10).

Section 4.6

Choosing Groups from a Set

Many real selection problems involve choosing a group from a population where everyone is equally eligible and no role distinctions exist. These are combination problems in their most natural form.

Example 4.4
Choosing a Hand of Cards
  • ProblemA standard deck has 52 cards. A hand of 5 cards is dealt. How many distinct hands are possible?
  • ThinkA hand is a set of 5 cards. The order in which cards are dealt does not change the hand. This is a combination.
  • Count C(52,5) = (52 × 51 × 50 × 49 × 48) / (5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1) = 311,875,200 / 120 = 2,598,960
  • InterpretThere are 2,598,960 distinct 5-card hands in a standard deck. This large number is why card games have so much variety.
Answer: C(52,5) = 2,598,960 distinct hands.
Example 4.5
Choosing Projects to Fund
  • ProblemA grant committee must choose 4 projects to fund from a pool of 10 submitted proposals. Each funded project receives the same amount. How many ways can the committee choose?
  • ThinkAll funded projects are treated equally — no ranking is applied. This is a combination.
  • Count C(10,4) = (10 × 9 × 8 × 7) / (4 × 3 × 2 × 1) = 5040/24 = 210
  • InterpretThere are 210 ways to choose 4 projects from 10 for equal funding.
Answer: C(10,4) = 210.

Section 4.7

Combinations with Restrictions

Restrictions change the structure of a selection problem. A restriction is a condition that must be satisfied by every valid selection. Before choosing a formula, you must represent the restriction explicitly.

There are two general strategies for handling restrictions: direct counting (reorganize the problem to count only valid selections) and complementary counting (count all selections, then subtract the invalid ones). This section introduces direct counting through composition requirements.

Strategy — Composition Restrictions

When a selection must contain exactly a certain number from each subgroup (for example, exactly 2 from Group A and 3 from Group B), count each subgroup independently and multiply using the product rule. The two subgroups are chosen in independent steps.

Count from Group A × Count from Group B

Example 4.6
Restriction on Group Composition
  • ProblemA library has 8 novels and 6 nonfiction books. A reading group will select 4 books: exactly 2 novels and 2 nonfiction. How many selections are possible?
  • ThinkThe selection must satisfy a composition requirement. The novels and nonfiction are chosen independently in separate groups. Use the product rule: count the novel choices, count the nonfiction choices, then multiply.
  • RepresentChoose 2 novels from 8: C(8,2). Choose 2 nonfiction from 6: C(6,2). The two choices are independent steps.
  • Count C(8,2) × C(6,2) = 28 × 15 = 420
  • CheckThe total unrestricted selections of 4 from 14 is C(14,4) = 1001. The restricted count of 420 is less than 1001. Reasonable.
  • InterpretThere are 420 ways to select exactly 2 novels and 2 nonfiction books.
Answer: C(8,2) × C(6,2) = 28 × 15 = 420.
Student Warning — Add vs. Multiply

Use multiplication when combining independent choices at separate steps. Use addition when counting mutually exclusive cases. Choosing 2 novels AND 2 nonfiction is a two-step independent process: multiply. Choosing either all novels OR all nonfiction are two exclusive alternatives: add. Getting this wrong is one of the most common errors in restricted combination problems.

Example 4.7
Restriction Using Complementary Counting
  • ProblemA committee of 4 is formed from a group of 5 men and 7 women. The committee must include at least 1 man. How many such committees are possible?
  • Think"At least 1 man" is most efficiently handled by complementary counting: subtract the committees with no men from the total. The only excluded case is all-women committees, which is easy to count.
  • Count — total (no restriction) C(12,4) = 495
  • Count — excluded (all women) C(7,4) = 35
  • Count — valid 495 − 35 = 460
  • CheckThe 35 all-women committees plus the 460 valid committees should total 495. Indeed 460 + 35 = 495. Correct.
  • InterpretThere are 460 committees containing at least one man.
Answer: 495 − 35 = 460 valid committees.
Section 4.8

Complementary Selection Problems

Complementary counting is one of the most efficient strategies in combinatorics. The core idea is simple:

Complementary Counting Principle
(Valid selections) = (Total selections) − (Invalid selections)
Use this when the excluded cases are simpler to count than the valid cases. The phrase "at least one" is a frequent signal.
Example 4.8
At Least Two of a Type
  • ProblemA team of 5 is chosen from 6 engineers and 4 scientists. How many teams contain at least 2 scientists?
  • Think"At least 2 scientists" means 2, 3, or 4 scientists. The complement is "at most 1 scientist" — meaning 0 or 1 scientists. The complement is simpler to count.
  • Count — totalC(10,5) = 252
  • Count — 0 scientistsC(6,5) × C(4,0) = 6 × 1 = 6
  • Count — 1 scientistC(6,4) × C(4,1) = 15 × 4 = 60
  • Count — at most 16 + 60 = 66
  • Count — at least 2252 − 66 = 186
  • Check — direct count 2 scientists: C(4,2)·C(6,3) = 6·20 = 120 3 scientists: C(4,3)·C(6,2) = 4·15 = 60 4 scientists: C(4,4)·C(6,1) = 1·6 = 6 Total: 186 ✓
  • InterpretThere are 186 valid teams containing at least 2 scientists.
Answer: 186 teams.
When to Use the Complement

Use complementary counting when the excluded cases are simpler than the valid cases. "At least 1" excludes only "0," which is usually one term. "At least 2" excludes "0 or 1," which is two terms but still often simpler than listing many valid compositions. When both direct and complementary counting seem equally complex, try both and choose the shorter path.

Section 4.9

Committee Problems

Committee problems are a central application of combinations. A committee is a group, not a ranked list. Every committee member is equal unless the problem specifies otherwise. When a problem assigns roles within a committee (chair, secretary, treasurer), the roles add a permutation layer on top of the combination selection.

Example 4.9
Committee with One Designated Role
  • ProblemA company has 10 employees. A committee of 5 will be formed, and then one member will be designated as chair. How many outcomes are possible?
  • ThinkThis is a two-step process: first choose the group of 5, then designate one as chair. The two steps are connected — the chair must come from the committee.
  • Count — Method 1 C(10,5) × 5 = 252 × 5 = 1260 (Choose the group, then choose the chair from within it.)
  • Count — Method 2 10 × C(9,4) = 10 × 126 = 1260 (Choose the chair first from all 10, then fill the remaining 4 spots from the other 9.)
  • CheckBoth methods give 1260. Agreement confirms the calculation.
  • InterpretThere are 1,260 ways to form a 5-person committee with one designated chair.
Answer: 1,260 committee-with-chair outcomes.
Example 4.10
Committee Restriction — Two People Who Refuse to Serve Together
  • ProblemFrom a group of 8 people including two rivals — P and Q — who refuse to serve together, how many 3-person committees are possible?
  • ThinkUse complementary counting: total committees minus committees containing both P and Q.
  • Count — totalC(8,3) = 56
  • Count — both P and Q includedIf both P and Q are in the committee, the third member can be any of the remaining 6: C(6,1) = 6
  • Count — valid56 − 6 = 50
  • InterpretThere are 50 valid committees in which P and Q do not both serve.
Answer: 56 − 6 = 50 valid committees.
Section 4.10

Selection with Required Elements

A required element is an object that must appear in every valid selection. When some objects are required, they are effectively pre-selected. The remaining choices are made from what is left.

Strategy — Required Elements

If a selection of k objects from n must include r specific required objects, those r objects are already chosen. Fill the remaining k − r positions from the n − r non-required objects.

Number of valid selections: C(n − r, k − r)

Example 4.11
Required Club Members
  • ProblemA hiking club has 15 members. A trail team of 5 must include both the club president and the safety officer. How many trail teams are possible?
  • ThinkTwo members are required. They occupy 2 of the 5 spots automatically. The remaining 3 spots are filled from the other 13 members.
  • Count C(15 − 2, 5 − 2) = C(13,3) = (13 × 12 × 11) / 6 = 286
  • InterpretThere are 286 possible trail teams that include both required members.
Answer: C(13,3) = 286 trail teams.
Section 4.11

Selection with Forbidden Elements

A forbidden element is an object that cannot appear in any valid selection. When some objects are forbidden, exclude them entirely from the pool before counting.

Strategy — Forbidden Elements

If f objects are forbidden, treat them as if they do not exist. Count selections from the remaining n − f objects only.

Number of valid selections of k from n with f forbidden: C(n − f, k)

Example 4.12
Forbidden Team Members
  • ProblemA robotics team has 12 members. Two members are disqualified. A team of 4 must be chosen from eligible members only. How many teams are possible?
  • ThinkTwo members are forbidden. Remove them. Count from the remaining 10.
  • CountC(10,4) = 210
  • InterpretThere are 210 valid teams.
Answer: C(10,4) = 210.
Example 4.13
Mixed Required and Forbidden
  • ProblemFrom 9 candidates, 1 person is required (the project lead must be on the team) and 2 are forbidden (scheduling conflicts). A team of 4 must be formed. How many teams are possible?
  • ThinkRemove the 2 forbidden members. The required member is already placed. From the 6 remaining eligible candidates (9 total − 1 required − 2 forbidden = 6 others), choose the final 3 spots.
  • CountC(6,3) = 20
  • InterpretThere are 20 valid teams satisfying both constraints.
Answer: C(6,3) = 20.

Section 4.12

Combinations in Probability and Data Problems

Combinations are fundamental to probability. When a sample is drawn uniformly at random from a population, the probability of any particular event is:

Probability with Equally Likely Outcomes
P(event) = (favorable outcomes) / (total equally likely outcomes)
When the sample is unordered — as most random samples are — both the numerator and denominator involve combinations.
Example 4.14
Probability of Exactly 3 Hearts
  • ProblemA hand of 5 cards is dealt from a standard 52-card deck. What is the probability that the hand contains exactly 3 hearts?
  • ThinkThe sample space is all 5-card hands. The event is "exactly 3 hearts." Count favorable outcomes using combinations: 3 hearts from the 13 in the deck, and 2 non-hearts from the remaining 39.
  • Total outcomesC(52,5) = 2,598,960
  • Favorable outcomes C(13,3) × C(39,2) = 286 × 741 = 211,926
  • Probability P = 211,926 / 2,598,960 ≈ 0.0815
  • InterpretThere is approximately an 8.15% probability that a random 5-card hand contains exactly 3 hearts.
Answer: P ≈ 0.0815 (about 8.15%).
Example 4.15
Selecting a Data Sample by Region
  • ProblemA database contains 20 customer records: 8 from Eastern, 7 from Western, 5 from Central regions. An analyst randomly selects 3 records for review. What is the probability that all 3 are from the same region?
  • ThinkCount all-Eastern, all-Western, and all-Central selections. These cases are mutually exclusive, so add them.
  • TotalC(20,3) = 1140
  • Favorable C(8,3) + C(7,3) + C(5,3) = 56 + 35 + 10 = 101
  • ProbabilityP = 101 / 1140 ≈ 0.0886
  • InterpretThere is approximately an 8.86% probability that all three reviewed records come from the same region.
Answer: P ≈ 0.0886 (about 8.86%).
MGU Library Connection — Combinations in Computing and Data Science
  • In data analysis and machine learning, combinations appear when selecting features or variable subsets. If a dataset has n features and a model tests all subsets of k, there are C(n,k) such subsets — growing very rapidly for large n.
  • This explosive growth explains why exhaustive feature search is computationally prohibitive and motivates smarter selection algorithms.
  • The role of combinatorics in feature selection and algorithmic search is developed in the MGU Data Science Foundations and Machine Learning Mathematics chapters.
  • Cryptography students will find combinations in key-space analysis and error-correcting code design — see MGU Cryptography and Coding Theory chapters.
Section 4.13

Common Combination Mistakes

The following mistakes appear frequently in student work. Each one involves a genuine conceptual misunderstanding, not merely a computation slip. Recognizing and understanding them now will prevent wasted effort on every selection problem you encounter.

Mistake 1 — Using a Permutation When a Combination Is Correct
C(10,3) asked. Student computes P(10,3) = 720.

Why it happens: the student multiplied correctly but failed to recognize that committees are unordered groups. Ask: does the order of selection change the outcome? If no, use C(n,k). A committee of Alice, Bo, and Cal is the same regardless of who was chosen first.

Mistake 2 — Using a Combination When a Permutation Is Correct
3 prizes (1st, 2nd, 3rd) from 10 contestants. Student computes C(10,3) = 120 instead of P(10,3) = 720.

Why it happens: student recognized "choose 3 from 10" but ignored that the prizes are ranked. Ask: does the assignment of roles matter? If yes, the positions are distinct — use a permutation.

Mistake 3 — Adding Instead of Multiplying Independent Choices
2 novels from 8 AND 2 nonfiction from 6. Student writes C(8,2) + C(6,2) = 43.

Why it happens: student treated two simultaneous independent steps as alternatives. Independent choices at separate steps multiply. Use addition only for mutually exclusive alternatives (Case A or Case B, not Case A and Case B).

Mistake 4 — Skipping the Restriction Entirely
Problem requires at least 1 from each group. Student counts all selections without applying the restriction.

Why it happens: the restriction was read but not modeled. Before computing, write out what the restriction requires. For "at least 1 from each group," plan your strategy — direct casework or complementary counting — before reaching for a formula.

Mistake 5 — Double-Counting Across Cases
Casework counts Case A and Case B, but the same outcome belongs to both.

Why it happens: cases were not defined carefully. Verify that cases are mutually exclusive (no outcome belongs to two cases) and exhaustive (every outcome belongs to at least one case). Use the sum rule only for mutually exclusive cases.

Mistake 6 — Forgetting Required or Forbidden Elements
Student counts all selections without pre-selecting required members or excluding forbidden ones.

Why it happens: the constraint was stated but not built into the count. Before applying any formula, identify the effective pool size after removing forbidden elements and accounting for required ones.

Mistake 7 — Misreading the Symmetry Property
Student writes C(10,7) = C(7,10), claiming the answer is 0 or undefined.

Why it happens: the symmetry flips the bottom index within the same n. The correct symmetry is C(10,7) = C(10, 10−7) = C(10,3) = 120. The n stays fixed; only the bottom index changes.

Section 4.14

Preparing for Binomial Patterns

This chapter introduced the combination formula C(n,k) as a count of unordered selections. Chapter 5 will show that these same numbers appear as coefficients when a binomial expression (x + y)ⁿ is expanded.

The connection is not accidental. When you expand (x + y)ⁿ by multiplying the binomial by itself n times, the coefficient of the term x^(n−k) · y^k is C(n,k) — because expanding the product involves choosing which k of the n factors contribute a y rather than an x. That choice is a combination.

This means that every skill developed in this chapter — computing C(n,k), using the symmetry property, interpreting the formula as a count — directly supports the work of Chapter 5. Pascal's Triangle, the Binomial Theorem, combinatorial identities, and many important algebraic properties all build from what you have learned here.

Looking Ahead — Chapter 5: Binomial Coefficients and Pascal's Triangle

Chapter 5 will organize the values of C(n,k) into Pascal's Triangle and reveal a remarkable pattern: each entry equals the sum of the two entries above it. This is Pascal's Identity — C(n,k) = C(n−1,k−1) + C(n−1,k) — and it has a beautiful combinatorial proof using the ideas of this chapter. The Binomial Theorem will then show how these numbers arise in algebraic expansion. Every combination skill you have built here carries directly into that material.

Chapter 4 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 4.1A combination is a selection of k objects from n distinct objects where order does not matter. A combination is equivalent to a subset of size k. Contrast with permutations, where position or order matters.
  • Sec 4.2The combination formula arises from dividing the permutation count P(n,k) by k!, the number of orderings of any fixed group of k objects. Division by k! removes the overcounting caused by treating the same group in different orders as distinct.
  • Sec 4.3C(n,k) = n! / (k!(n−k)!). Boundary cases: C(n,0) = C(n,n) = 1, and C(n,1) = n. Always verify boundary cases when working with new combination problems.
  • Sec 4.4The binomial coefficient C(n,k), read "n choose k," is the standard notation for combinations. The name "binomial" reflects the appearance of these numbers as coefficients in the expansion of (x+y)ⁿ.
  • Sec 4.5The symmetry property: C(n,k) = C(n, n−k). Choosing k objects is equivalent to choosing which n−k objects to exclude. Use symmetry to simplify computation when k is close to n.
  • Sec 4.6Group-selection problems — card hands, project choices, juries, representative panels — are the natural domain of combinations. When all selected objects are equal, use C(n,k).
  • Sec 4.7Composition restrictions require choosing independently from each subgroup and multiplying. Independent choices multiply; mutually exclusive alternatives add. Misidentifying this relationship is one of the most common errors.
  • Sec 4.8Complementary counting: valid = total − invalid. Most efficient when the complement is simpler. "At least 1" phrases almost always signal this approach. Always verify by checking that valid + invalid = total.
  • Sec 4.9Committee problems are pure combination problems unless roles are assigned. When roles are added, a permutation layer multiplies the combination count. Two-method verification (select group then role; select role then group) provides a useful check.
  • Sec 4.10Required elements are pre-selected; reduce both the pool and the selection size: choose k − r from n − r remaining objects. Formula: C(n−r, k−r).
  • Sec 4.11Forbidden elements are removed entirely from the pool before counting. Formula: C(n−f, k). For mixed required-and-forbidden problems, apply both adjustments: remove forbidden, place required, count from the remainder.
  • Sec 4.12Combinations appear in probability whenever outcomes are unordered samples. P(event) = (favorable combinations) / (total combinations). This structure governs card probabilities, data sampling, and feature selection in computing.
  • Sec 4.13Seven common mistakes: permutation used instead of combination; combination used instead of permutation; addition where multiplication is needed; restriction skipped; cases double-counted; required or forbidden elements ignored; symmetry misread.
  • Sec 4.14The binomial coefficients C(n,k) will reappear in Chapter 5 as the entries of Pascal's Triangle and as the coefficients of the Binomial Theorem. The counting argument for why they appear in algebraic expansion is a direct application of the ideas of this chapter.

Key Terms Review

combination
A selection of k objects from a set of n distinct objects in which order does not matter. Equivalent to choosing a subset of size k.
binomial coefficient
The number C(n,k), read "n choose k," counting unordered selections of k from n. Named for its role as coefficients in binomial expansion.
selection
A choice of a subset of objects from a larger set, with no ordering imposed on the chosen objects.
symmetry property
C(n,k) = C(n, n−k). Choosing k objects is equivalent to choosing which n−k objects to exclude. The n stays fixed.
required element
An object that must appear in every valid selection. Pre-select it and reduce the remaining pool and selection size accordingly.
forbidden element
An object that cannot appear in any valid selection. Remove it from the pool entirely before counting.
complementary counting
Valid = Total − Invalid. A strategy used when the excluded cases are simpler to count than the valid cases. "At least one" problems are a frequent application.
restriction
A condition that must be satisfied by every valid selection. Must be modeled explicitly before any counting formula is applied.
committee problem
A selection problem in which a group of people is chosen from a larger pool with no role distinctions among members — the natural application of combinations.
overcounting
Including the same outcome more than once in a count. The combination formula corrects the overcounting in P(n,k) by dividing by k!.
correction factor
A divisor applied to remove overcounting. In combinations, the correction factor is k!, the number of orderings of any group of k selected objects.

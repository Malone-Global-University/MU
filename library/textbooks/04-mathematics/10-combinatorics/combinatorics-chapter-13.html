Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter studies combinatorics from a different angle than simple counting. In earlier chapters, the central question was: how many arrangements or selections are there? In this chapter, the question expands: can an arrangement satisfying certain rules even be built, and when are two arrangements really the same thing?

These are the questions of combinatorial design theory and symmetry. They arise naturally in scheduling tournaments fairly, designing scientific experiments so every treatment pair is compared the same number of times, constructing codes, arranging symbols in grids without repetition in any row or column, and counting colorings where rotating or reflecting a figure does not produce a genuinely new pattern.

This chapter introduces the major ideas at an accessible level. Students who continue into abstract algebra, coding theory, experimental design, graph theory, or discrete mathematics will encounter all of these ideas again in greater depth.

Opening Question

A pharmaceutical researcher wants to compare four experimental drugs. She has enough resources to test each pair of drugs together exactly once, and she wants each drug to appear in the same number of tests. She also wants each test group to contain exactly two drugs. Can she build a schedule satisfying all these conditions? If she relabels the drugs, does that produce a genuinely new schedule, or just a renamed version of the same one? These questions require the ideas of this chapter.

Section 13.1

What a Combinatorial Design Is

In earlier chapters, the central problems of counting asked for the number of ways to arrange or select objects from a set. Chapter 3 counted ordered arrangements. Chapter 4 counted unordered selections. Chapter 6 distributed objects into boxes. These techniques answer the question how many?

A combinatorial design asks a different question. It asks whether an arrangement satisfying a collection of balance conditions can exist, and if so, what it looks like. The arrangement itself is the object of study, not merely its count.

To understand why balance conditions matter, consider a sports league with six teams. The league wants every pair of teams to play each other exactly once. It also wants every team to play the same total number of games. Can such a schedule be built? How many games will each team play? How many total games will there be? These are structural questions — not answered by listing all possible pairings, but by reasoning about what conditions any valid schedule must satisfy.

What Makes a Design

A combinatorial design is a collection of subsets, called blocks, chosen from a finite set of objects or points, such that every object and every pair of objects appears in the design with a controlled frequency. The power of this framework is that it connects counting, algebra, and structure.

Section 13.2

Arranging Objects Under Conditions

Condition TypeWhat It RequiresExample

SeparationCertain objects must not appear togetherTwo chemically reactive compounds never in the same trial
UniformityEvery block must have the same sizeEvery test group has exactly 3 participants

The approach to structured arrangements follows a consistent pattern: identify the objects and blocks; state the conditions precisely; derive necessary numerical conditions from algebra; check whether those conditions can be satisfied; then attempt to construct an example. Steps one through four are accessible at this level. The construction step can range from straightforward for small cases to an open research problem for large ones.

Necessary conditions from double counting are especially useful. If a condition must hold for a design to exist, any design with those parameters must satisfy it. But satisfying a necessary condition does not guarantee the design exists — it only rules out immediate impossibility.

Latin Squares

Definition — Latin Square

A Latin square of order n is an n × n grid filled with n distinct symbols such that each symbol appears exactly once in every row and exactly once in every column.

The simplest nontrivial example is a Latin square of order 2, using symbols 1 and 2. A Latin square of order 3 uses symbols 1, 2, and 3 in a 3 × 3 grid. The name comes from the work of Leonhard Euler, who studied them in the eighteenth century using Latin letters.

Cyclic Construction

Place symbols 0, 1, 2, …, n−1 in the first row. For each subsequent row, shift all symbols one position to the right, wrapping around. This works for any n and produces a valid Latin square, though not the only one of order n.

Orthogonal Latin Squares

Two Latin squares of order n are called orthogonal if, when one is superimposed on the other, every ordered pair of symbols appears exactly once across the n² cells. Pairs of orthogonal Latin squares are especially useful in experimental design, where two independent factors each need to be controlled, and in certain constructions of codes and designs.

Section 13.4

Tournament Schedules

A round-robin tournament is a competition in which every pair of participants plays each other exactly once. This is one of the most natural structured combinatorial objects: a schedule for the complete graph Kn, where each edge represents a game.

Round-Robin Facts

For n teams, the total number of games is the number of pairs:

C(n, 2) = n(n−1) / 2

Each team plays n − 1 games. If n is even: n − 1 rounds of n/2 games each, with no team idle. If n is odd: n rounds of (n−1)/2 games each, with one team sitting out each round.

Example 13.4.1
Scheduling a six-team round-robin tournament
  • ProblemSchedule a round-robin tournament for 6 teams (labeled 1–6) into rounds. How many rounds? How many games per round?
  • ThinkSince n = 6 is even, there are n − 1 = 5 rounds, each with n/2 = 3 games. Fix team 6 and rotate the remaining 5 teams through a cycle.
  • Analyze Round 1: (6 vs 1), (2 vs 5), (3 vs 4) Round 2: (6 vs 2), (3 vs 1), (4 vs 5) Round 3: (6 vs 3), (4 vs 2), (5 vs 1) Round 4: (6 vs 4), (5 vs 3), (1 vs 2) Round 5: (6 vs 5), (1 vs 4), (2 vs 3)
  • CheckTotal games: 5 × 3 = 15 = C(6,2). ✓ Each team plays in every round. ✓ Each pair plays exactly once. ✓
A 6-team round-robin schedules into 5 rounds of 3 simultaneous games, with every team active each round.
Section 13.5

Block Designs as an Introduction

BIBD — Balanced Incomplete Block Design

A BIBD consists of a set V of v points and b blocks, each of size k, such that every point appears in exactly r blocks and every pair of distinct points appears together in exactly λ blocks. The five parameters (v, b, r, k, λ) specify the structure completely.

Necessary Condition 1: bk = vr (double count point-block incidences)

Necessary Condition 2: r(k−1) = λ(v−1) (count pairs through a fixed point)

Necessary ≠ Sufficient

Satisfying both necessary conditions does not guarantee a design exists. Some parameter sets pass both conditions but no design with those parameters can be constructed. Always treat the conditions as a filter that rules out impossibility, not a proof of existence.

Example 13.5.1
Checking necessary conditions — the Fano plane
  • ProblemA researcher wants v = 7 treatments in blocks of size k = 3, with every pair appearing in exactly λ = 1 block. Is this feasible?
  • ThinkIdentify the parameters and derive the unknowns b and r using the necessary conditions.
  • Derive From Condition 2: r(k−1) = λ(v−1) → r · 2 = 1 · 6 → r = 3
    From Condition 1: bk = vr → b · 3 = 7 · 3 → b = 7
  • Analyze Parameters: (v, b, r, k, λ) = (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) Condition 1: bk = 21 = vr = 21 ✓ Condition 2: r(k−1) = 3·2 = 6 = λ(v−1) = 1·6 ✓
  • InterpretBoth necessary conditions hold. This design does exist — it is the Fano plane, one of the most important objects in combinatorics. Its 7 blocks are: {1,2,4}, {2,3,5}, {3,4,6}, {4,5,7}, {5,6,1}, {6,7,2}, {7,1,3}.
Answer: Parameters (7, 7, 3, 3, 1) pass both necessary conditions. The design exists as the Fano plane.

Section 13.6

Balanced Designs as an Introduction

The word "balanced" in design theory has a precise meaning: a design is balanced if every pair of points appears together in the same number λ of blocks. Balance ensures that no pair of objects is treated more or less favorably than any other pair.

Why does balance matter? In a clinical trial comparing v treatments, a balanced design ensures that the comparison between any two treatments is based on the same amount of data. No treatment pair gets more information than another, controlling bias and allowing fair statistical analysis.

Some designs have a further property: their blocks can be partitioned into parallel classes, where each parallel class contains every point exactly once. Such a design is called resolvable. The round-robin tournament from Section 13.4 is resolvable — the rounds form parallel classes since every team plays in every round.

Section 13.7

Finite Geometries as a Preview

A finite geometry is a mathematical structure consisting of a finite set of points and a finite collection of lines (subsets of points) satisfying specified incidence conditions: (1) any two distinct points lie on exactly one common line; (2) any two distinct lines meet in exactly one point; (3) there exist at least four points, no three collinear. These conditions are analogues of Euclidean geometry in a finite setting.

The Fano plane from Section 13.5 is the smallest finite projective plane, called PG(2,2). A finite projective plane of order q has exactly q² + q + 1 points and q² + q + 1 lines, with q + 1 points on every line and q + 1 lines through every point.

Finite Projective Plane of Order q

When q is a prime or prime power, a projective plane of order q always exists. Its parameters as a BIBD are:

v = b = q² + q + 1, r = k = q + 1, λ = 1

For q = 2: the Fano plane. For q = 3: 13 points, 13 lines, 4 points per line. Whether finite projective planes of non-prime-power order exist is one of the great open questions in combinatorics.

Section 13.8

Symmetry in Counting

Everything in Chapters 3 through 12 counted labeled arrangements. When permutations counted seating arrangements, each person was considered distinct. When combinations counted committees, the people were distinguishable. But many real counting problems ask for something different: how many essentially different arrangements are there, where two arrangements count as the same if one can be transformed into the other by some natural symmetry?

ProblemNaive CountWhat Symmetry Does
Faces of a cube painted with 2 colors, rotations considered same2⁶ = 64Many paintings are equivalent rotations of each other
Necklace of 6 beads in 2 colors, rotations and flips considered same2⁶ = 64Necklace can be rotated and flipped; many coincide
3 objects at vertices of equilateral triangle, rotations considered same3! = 6Rotations by 120° and 240° identify some arrangements

In each case, symmetry reduces the number of distinct arrangements below the naive count. To count correctly, we need to know: for each symmetry transformation, how many arrangements look the same after that transformation is applied?

Section 13.9

Rotations and Reflections

Symmetries of a Regular n-gon

A regular n-gon has exactly 2n symmetries:

n rotations — by 0°, 360°/n, 2·360°/n, …, (n−1)·360°/n. The 0° rotation is the identity.

n reflections — flip across each of the n axes of symmetry.

The collection of all symmetries of a regular n-gon is called the dihedral group of order 2n.

Section 13.10

Equivalent Arrangements

Two arrangements of objects on a symmetric figure are equivalent if one arrangement can be transformed into the other by applying a symmetry of the figure. The orbit of an arrangement A under a group of symmetries G is the set of all arrangements obtainable from A by applying some symmetry in G.

Two arrangements are equivalent if and only if they belong to the same orbit. The number of truly distinct arrangements equals the number of orbits.

Example 13.10.1
Equivalent colorings of a triangle — direct orbit analysis
  • ProblemColor the vertices of an equilateral triangle using 2 colors (R, B). Two colorings are equivalent if one can be rotated to match the other. How many distinct colorings are there?
  • ThinkWithout symmetry: 2³ = 8 labeled colorings. Rotations by 0°, 120°, 240° may make some equivalent. Group the 8 colorings into orbits.
  • Analyze Orbit 1: {(R,R,R)} — rotating all-red gives all-red.
    Orbit 2: {(R,R,B), (B,R,R), (R,B,R)} — these three are rotations of each other.
    Orbit 3: {(R,B,B), (B,R,B), (B,B,R)} — these three are rotations of each other.
    Orbit 4: {(B,B,B)} — all-blue is fixed by all rotations.
  • CheckTotal arrangements: 1 + 3 + 3 + 1 = 8. ✓ All 8 colorings are accounted for in exactly 4 orbits.
Answer: There are 4 distinct colorings under rotation symmetry — one all-red, one all-blue, one with two reds, one with two blues.
Section 13.11

Group Actions as an Introduction

The mathematical framework formalizing symmetries acting on arrangements is called a group action. A group G is a collection of symmetry transformations that is closed under composition, contains the identity, and contains an inverse for every element.

For counting purposes, the key concept is a fixed arrangement. For a symmetry g ∈ G, an arrangement A is fixed by g if applying g to A leaves A unchanged: g · A = A. Denote by |Fix(g)| the number of arrangements fixed by symmetry g.

Symmetry TypeWhat It Fixes
IdentityEvery arrangement — always contributes the full count |S| to the Burnside sum
Rotation by 360°/nOnly arrangements where all positions in each rotation cycle have the same value
ReflectionOnly arrangements symmetric across the reflection axis — paired positions must match

Section 13.12

Burnside's Lemma as a Preview

Burnside's Lemma

Let G be a group of symmetries acting on a set S of arrangements. The number of truly distinct arrangements (the number of orbits) is:

Distinct arrangements = (1 / |G|) · Σ |Fix(g)|

That is: the average number of arrangements fixed by each symmetry in G equals the number of distinct arrangements.

Procedure: (1) List all symmetries and count |G|. (2) For each symmetry g, count |Fix(g)|. (3) Sum all |Fix(g)| values. (4) Divide by |G|.

Why It Works (Intuition)

Each orbit contributes exactly 1 to the count of distinct arrangements. Burnside's Lemma shows that each orbit contributes exactly |G| to the total sum Σ|Fix(g)|. Dividing by |G| gives the number of orbits — the number of distinct arrangements.

Example 13.12.1
Colorings of a triangle under rotations — Burnside's Lemma
  • ProblemColor the vertices of an equilateral triangle with 2 colors. Using Burnside's Lemma with the 3 rotations as symmetries, find the number of distinct colorings.
  • SetupRotation group: |G| = 3. Total labeled colorings: 2³ = 8.
  • Analyze r₀ (0° — identity): All 8 colorings fixed. |Fix(r₀)| = 8.
    r₁ (120°): Cycles all three vertices (1→2→3→1). Need c₁ = c₂ = c₃. Only 2 arrangements fixed. |Fix(r₁)| = 2.
    r₂ (240°): Same cycle structure. |Fix(r₂)| = 2.
  • Apply Distinct colorings = (1/3)(8 + 2 + 2) = 12/3 = 4
  • CheckMatches the direct orbit analysis in Example 13.10.1. ✓
Answer: 4 distinct 2-colorings of the triangle's vertices under rotation symmetry.
Example 13.12.2
Colorings of a square under full symmetry (rotations and reflections)
  • ProblemColor the vertices of a square with 2 colors (R, B). Count distinct colorings where two are the same if one can be rotated or reflected to match the other.
  • SetupFull symmetry group of the square: |G| = 8 (4 rotations + 4 reflections). Total labeled colorings: 2⁴ = 16.
  • Analyze r₀ (0° identity): All 16 fixed. |Fix| = 16.
    r₁ (90°): Cycles (1→2→3→4→1). Need all equal. |Fix| = 2.
    r₂ (180°): Pairs (1↔3), (2↔4). Need c₁=c₃ and c₂=c₄. Two free choices. |Fix| = 4.
    r₃ (270°): Same as r₁. |Fix| = 2.
    s₁ (horizontal): Pairs (1↔4), (2↔3). Two free choices. |Fix| = 4.
    s₂ (vertical): Pairs (1↔2), (3↔4). Two free choices. |Fix| = 4.
    s₃ (main diagonal, fixes vertices 1 & 3): Pairs (2↔4). Three free choices: c₁, c₃, c₂. |Fix| = 8.
    s₄ (anti-diagonal, fixes vertices 2 & 4): Pairs (1↔3). |Fix| = 8.
  • Apply Sum = 16 + 2 + 4 + 2 + 4 + 4 + 8 + 8 = 48 Distinct = 48 / 8 = 6
  • CheckThe result is a whole number. ✓ Plausible: 16 labeled colorings, 8 symmetries, 6 truly distinct patterns.
Answer: There are 6 genuinely distinct 2-colorings of the square's vertices under full symmetry (rotations and reflections).
Example 13.12.3
Necklace counting — 6 beads, 2 colors
  • ProblemA necklace has 6 beads in a circle, each colored red or blue. Two necklaces are the same if one can be rotated or flipped to match the other. How many distinct necklaces?
  • SetupFull symmetry group of a 6-bead necklace: 6 rotations + 6 reflections, so |G| = 12. Total labeled colorings: 2⁶ = 64.
  • Rotations r₀ (0° identity): |Fix| = 64.
    r₁ (60°): Cycles all 6 positions. Need all equal. |Fix| = 2.
    r₂ (120°): Two 3-cycles (1,3,5) and (2,4,6). Need c₁=c₃=c₅ and c₂=c₄=c₆. Two free choices. |Fix| = 4.
    r₃ (180°): Three 2-cycles (1↔4),(2↔5),(3↔6). Three free choices. |Fix| = 8.
    r₄ (240°): Same as r₂. |Fix| = 4.
    r₅ (300°): Same as r₁. |Fix| = 2.
  • Reflections 3 axes through opposite vertices (fix 2 vertices, swap 2 pairs): 4 free choices. Each gives |Fix| = 16.
    3 axes through opposite edge midpoints (swap 3 pairs): 3 free choices. Each gives |Fix| = 8.
  • Apply Rotations sum: 64 + 2 + 4 + 8 + 4 + 2 = 84 Reflections sum: 3(16) + 3(8) = 48 + 24 = 72 Total: 84 + 72 = 156 Distinct = 156 / 12 = 13
Answer: There are 13 distinct 2-colored necklaces with 6 beads, under rotations and reflections.

Section 13.14

Common Design and Symmetry Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Ignoring symmetry when it applies
Count all 2⁴ = 16 colorings of a square's vertices; that is the answer.

Error: When the problem says rotations or reflections are considered "the same," Burnside's Lemma is required. Naive counts overcount by treating equivalent arrangements as distinct. Correct: apply Burnside's Lemma; the answer is 6, not 16.

Mistake 2 — Applying symmetry when it does not apply
Seating 6 people in 6 specific labeled chairs: divide by 6 for rotational symmetry.

Error: If chairs are labeled and fixed in space, every arrangement is distinct. Burnside's Lemma is only needed when symmetric arrangements are genuinely considered equivalent. Labeled, positioned chairs do not qualify.

Mistake 3 — Miscounting fixed arrangements
For a 120° rotation of a triangle, many colorings where adjacent vertices agree are fixed.

Error: A rotation that cycles all n positions fixes only arrangements where all positions in each cycle have the same value — not all arrangements where adjacent positions happen to agree. Identify cycles explicitly before counting.

Mistake 4 — Concluding existence from necessary conditions
Both bk = vr and r(k−1) = λ(v−1) hold, therefore the BIBD exists.

Error: These are necessary conditions, not sufficient ones. Some parameter sets pass both but no design with those parameters exists. The conditions rule out impossibility; existence requires explicit construction or a separate theorem.

Mistake 5 — Confusing Latin squares with Sudoku
A Latin square requires each symbol once per row, per column, and once per subsquare.

Error: A Latin square requires only the row and column conditions. Sudoku adds a third condition (3×3 subsquare blocks). Every completed Sudoku is a Latin square, but not every Latin square is a valid Sudoku.

Mistake 6 — Treating orthogonal squares as just two valid Latin squares
Two squares are orthogonal if each is individually a valid Latin square.

Error: Orthogonality is a relationship between two squares, not a property of each individually. When superimposed, every ordered pair of symbols must appear exactly once across all n² cells. Two valid Latin squares are not necessarily orthogonal.

Mistake 7 — Forgetting the identity in Burnside's Lemma
Apply Burnside's Lemma using only the non-identity symmetries.

Error: The identity is always in the group. It fixes every arrangement, so its contribution is |S| — always the largest single term in the Burnside sum. Omitting it produces a wrong total and wrong final count.

Mistake 8 — Checking only rows (or only columns) to verify a Latin square
Check that every row is a permutation of the symbols; conclude it is a Latin square.

Error: Both conditions must hold. It is easy to construct a grid where every row is valid but some column repeats a symbol. Always verify rows and columns independently.

The Fano plane is the BIBD (7,7,3,3,1), the smallest balanced design with λ = 1. A resolvable design has blocks partitioned into parallel classes where each point appears exactly once.
  • Sec 13.7A finite projective plane of order q (when q is a prime power) gives a BIBD with v = b = q²+q+1, r = k = q+1, λ = 1. The Fano plane is PG(2,2). Finite geometries provide systematic constructions for BIBDs and error-correcting codes.
  • Sec 13.8–13.10Symmetry reduces the count of distinct arrangements. Two arrangements are equivalent if one can be obtained from the other by a symmetry. The orbit of an arrangement is the set of all equivalent arrangements. The number of orbits equals the number of truly distinct arrangements.
  • Sec 13.9A regular n-gon has 2n symmetries: n rotations and n reflections, forming the dihedral group. An arrangement is fixed by a symmetry g if applying g leaves it unchanged. The identity fixes every arrangement.
  • Sec 13.12Burnside's Lemma: the number of distinct arrangements = (1/|G|) Σ |Fix(g)|. Procedure: list all symmetries, count fixed arrangements for each, sum, divide by |G|. The result is always a whole number. Applied to triangles, squares, and necklaces in three worked examples.
  • Sec 13.13Designs appear in clinical trials (BIBDs), agricultural experiments (Latin squares), error-correcting codes (finite geometries), and tournament and course scheduling (resolvable designs).

Key Terms Review

combinatorial design
An arrangement of objects into blocks satisfying specified balance or regularity conditions on frequency and pair coverage.
Latin square
An n×n grid filled with n symbols such that each symbol appears exactly once in every row and exactly once in every column.
order
The value n in a Latin square — the grid is n×n and uses n distinct symbols.
orthogonal Latin squares
Two Latin squares of the same order where superimposition produces every ordered pair exactly once across all n² cells.
BIBD
Balanced incomplete block design. Parameters (v, b, r, k, λ): v points, b blocks of size k, each point in r blocks, each pair in λ blocks.
necessary conditions
bk = vr and r(k−1) = λ(v−1). Both must hold for a BIBD to exist, but satisfying them does not guarantee existence.
Fano plane
The BIBD with parameters (7,7,3,3,1); the smallest finite projective plane PG(2,2); 7 points and 7 blocks of size 3.
round-robin tournament
A schedule where every pair of participants plays exactly once. For n teams: C(n,2) total games, n−1 rounds when n is even.
resolvable design
A block design whose blocks can be partitioned into parallel classes, each containing every point exactly once.
finite geometry
A geometry with finitely many points and lines satisfying incidence conditions (any two points on one line; any two lines meet in one point).
symmetry
A transformation mapping a figure to itself. For a regular n-gon: n rotations and n reflections, totaling 2n symmetries.
orbit
The set of all arrangements equivalent to a given arrangement under a group of symmetries. Number of orbits = number of distinct arrangements.
fixed arrangement
An arrangement A such that symmetry g maps A to itself: g·A = A. Denoted |Fix(g)| for the count of all such arrangements.
Burnside's Lemma
Distinct arrangements = (1/|G|) Σ |Fix(g)|. The average number of arrangements fixed by each symmetry equals the number of orbits.
dihedral group
The group of symmetries of a regular n-gon, consisting of n rotations and n reflections, with total order 2n.
group action
A formal assignment of symmetry transformations to arrangements so that each symmetry g sends arrangement A to g·A.

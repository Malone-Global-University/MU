Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter connects the combinatorial tools developed in previous chapters to a new kind of mathematical object: the graph. In everyday language, the word "graph" often means a chart showing data over time. In mathematics, a graph is something different — a collection of objects and the relationships between them.

Graphs appear everywhere. A map of airline routes is a graph: airports are objects, and flight paths are relationships. A social network is a graph: people are objects, and friendships are relationships. A circuit board is a graph: components are objects, and wires are relationships. A scheduling problem is a graph: tasks are objects, and conflicts are relationships.

Combinatorics enters graph theory through counting. How many edges can a graph have? How many paths exist between two locations? How many ways can a network be arranged without conflicts? How many trees can be built on a given number of vertices? These are counting questions, and they require the same careful reasoning practiced in every chapter of this book.

Opening Question

A city has seven neighborhoods. The city council wants to build roads so that every neighborhood is directly connected to every other neighborhood by exactly one road. How many roads would need to be built? Before you can answer, you need to know how to represent the problem. The neighborhoods are objects. The roads are relationships between pairs of objects. By the end of Section 12.5, you will see exactly how this connects to combinations — and to a classical graph structure called the complete graph.

Section 12.1

Graphs as Combinatorial Structures

A graph is a finite mathematical structure built from two ingredients: a set of objects and a set of connections between those objects. In graph theory, the objects are called vertices and the connections are called edges.

Definition — Graph

A graph G consists of:

— a nonempty finite set V called the vertex set, whose elements are called vertices, and

— a set E called the edge set, whose elements are pairs of vertices called edges.

In a simple graph, every edge connects two distinct vertices, and no two edges connect the same pair of vertices. Unless stated otherwise, all graphs in this chapter are simple.

There are two standard ways to represent a graph. As a diagram: draw a dot for each vertex and a line between two dots whenever those vertices are connected by an edge. The exact positions of the dots do not matter — only which pairs are connected. As a vertex and edge list: write the vertex set V and edge set E explicitly. The edge connecting vertex u and vertex v is written {u, v} or sometimes uv.

Example 12.1.1
A four-vertex graph — vertex and edge list
  • RepresentLet V = {A, B, C, D} and E = {{A,B}, {B,C}, {C,D}, {A,D}}.
  • DescribeThis graph has 4 vertices and 4 edges. Vertex A connects to B and D. Vertex B connects to A and C. Vertex C connects to B and D. Vertex D connects to C and A.
  • DiagramDraw four dots in a square arrangement and connect adjacent corners. The result is a four-vertex cycle — a square with no diagonals.
Order: 4 vertices. Size: 4 edges. Structure: a 4-cycle.
Graphs and Combinations

Every edge is a 2-element subset of V. The total number of possible 2-element subsets of a set with n vertices is C(n, 2). This means the maximum number of edges in a simple graph on n vertices is C(n, 2) — achieved by the complete graph. This immediately connects graph structure to the combinations studied in Chapter 4.

Labeled vs. Unlabeled Graphs

In this chapter, all vertices are labeled — they have distinct names. Changing which specific vertices are connected gives a different graph, even if the visual shape looks similar. Counting unlabeled graphs (up to isomorphism) requires the symmetry tools of Chapter 13.

Section 12.2

Vertices and Edges

The two most basic counting questions about any graph are how many vertices does it have, and how many edges does it have? The order of a graph G is the number of vertices, written |V| or sometimes n. The size of a graph G is the number of edges, written |E| or sometimes m.

Degree Counting

The degree of a vertex v, written deg(v), is the number of edges incident to v — that is, the number of edges that have v as an endpoint. In a diagram, the degree of a vertex equals the number of lines drawn from that dot.

Regular Graphs

A graph in which every vertex has the same degree k is called a k-regular graph. A cycle Cₙ on n vertices is 2-regular. The complete graph Kₙ is (n − 1)-regular, since every vertex connects to all other n − 1 vertices.

Section 12.4

The Handshaking Lemma

Theorem — The Handshaking Lemma

In any graph G = (V, E):

∑ deg(v) = 2 |E|

The sum of all vertex degrees equals twice the number of edges.

Proof

Every edge {u, v} contributes 1 to deg(u) and 1 to deg(v). So each edge contributes exactly 2 to the total sum of degrees. If there are |E| edges, the total sum is 2|E|. This is an instance of double counting — counting the same quantity two ways: by summing over vertices and by summing over edges.

□

Two important consequences follow immediately. First, the sum of all vertex degrees in any graph is always even. Second — and more striking — in any graph, the number of vertices with odd degree is always even. No matter what graph you draw, you can never have an odd number of odd-degree vertices.

Proof — Even Number of Odd-Degree Vertices

Let Vodd be vertices with odd degree and Veven those with even degree. Then:

∑ deg(Veven) + ∑ deg(Vodd) = 2|E|

The first sum (over even-degree vertices) is even. Since 2|E| is also even, the second sum must be even. A sum of odd numbers is even only when there is an even count of them. Therefore |Vodd| is even.

□
Example 12.4.1
Applying the Handshaking Lemma
  • ProblemA graph has 6 vertices with degrees 1, 2, 3, 3, 4, 5. How many edges does the graph have?
  • ThinkThe Handshaking Lemma says the sum of degrees equals twice the number of edges.
  • Count ∑ deg(v) = 1 + 2 + 3 + 3 + 4 + 5 = 18 2|E| = 18 ⟹ |E| = 9
  • CheckOdd-degree vertices: 1, 3, 3, 5 — four of them. Four is even. Consistent with the Handshaking Lemma.
Answer: The graph has 9 edges.
Example 12.4.2
A degree sequence puzzle
  • ProblemIs there a simple graph with 5 vertices, all having degree 3?
  • CountIf all 5 vertices have degree 3, the sum of degrees is 5 × 3 = 15. The Handshaking Lemma requires this sum to be even. But 15 is odd. Contradiction.
  • InterpretNo such graph exists. The Handshaking Lemma rules it out without any attempt to construct it.
Answer: No. A 3-regular graph must have an even number of vertices.
Section 12.5

Counting Edges in Complete Graphs

The complete graph on n vertices, written Kₙ, is a simple graph in which every pair of distinct vertices is connected by exactly one edge. Each edge in Kₙ corresponds to a 2-element subset of the vertex set. The number of such subsets is C(n, 2).

Edge Count Formula — Complete Graph

|E(Kₙ)| = C(n, 2) = n(n − 1) / 2

Each vertex connects to n − 1 others. Summing over all n vertices gives n(n − 1). Dividing by 2 corrects for counting each edge twice (once from each endpoint).

Example 12.5.1
Counting handshakes at a meeting
  • ProblemTwelve people attend a meeting. Each person shakes hands with every other person exactly once. How many handshakes occur?
  • RepresentPeople are vertices. Handshakes are edges. This is K₁₂.
  • Count |E(K₁₂)| = C(12, 2) = (12 × 11) / 2 = 66
  • CheckEach person shakes 11 hands. Sum: 12 × 11 = 132. Divide by 2 (each handshake counted twice): 132 / 2 = 66. ✓

Section 12.6

Counting Edges in Bipartite Graphs

A bipartite graph is a graph whose vertex set can be divided into two groups — call them A and B — such that every edge connects a vertex in A to a vertex in B. No edge connects two vertices within the same group. Bipartite graphs model situations where two types of objects are connected to each other but not within their own type: students and courses, servers and clients, workers and jobs.

Edge Count Formula — Complete Bipartite Graph

The complete bipartite graph Km,n has m vertices on one side and n on the other, with every vertex on the left connected to every vertex on the right.

|E(Km,n)| = m · n

By the product rule: each of the m left vertices connects to each of the n right vertices.

Identifying Bipartite Graphs

A graph is bipartite if and only if it contains no odd-length cycles. A triangle (K₃) is not bipartite. A four-vertex cycle C₄ is bipartite. All trees are bipartite (they contain no cycles at all).

Example 12.6.1
Expanding a server–client network
  • ProblemA company has 3 servers and 7 clients, each server connected to each client. How many connections are there? If the company adds 2 more servers and 3 more clients, how many connections are in the expanded network?
  • Original K₃,₇ : 3 × 7 = 21 connections
  • ExpandedNow 3 + 2 = 5 servers and 7 + 3 = 10 clients. K₅,₁₀ : 5 × 10 = 50 connections
Original: 21 connections. Expanded: 50 connections — more than double, because both sides grew.

Section 12.7

Paths and Cycles

Definitions — Path and Cycle

A path is a sequence of distinct vertices v₁, v₂, …, vk such that each consecutive pair {vi, vi+1} is an edge. The length of the path is k − 1 (the number of edges).

A cycle is a sequence of distinct vertices v₁, v₂, …, vk, v₁ where each consecutive pair (including the closing pair {vk, v₁}) is an edge. The length of the cycle equals the number of vertices k. A cycle requires at least 3 vertices.

A graph is connected if there is a path between every pair of vertices. A connected graph on n vertices has at least n − 1 edges.

A path visits each vertex at most once. This distinguishes a path from a walk, which may repeat vertices and edges. When counting paths, always clarify whether they are directed (the start and end are distinguished) or undirected (traversal in either direction counts as the same path). The problem's context determines which interpretation applies.

Example 12.7.1
Counting paths of length 2 in P₅
  • ProblemThe path graph P₅ has vertices A – B – C – D – E connected in a line. How many undirected paths of length 2 are there?
  • RepresentA path of length 2 uses two consecutive edges. List adjacent edge pairs: (AB, BC), (BC, CD), (CD, DE).
  • CountUndirected paths: A – B – C, B – C – D, C – D – E. Total: 3.
  • CheckDirected count: each undirected path gives 2 directed paths (e.g., A→B→C and C→B→A). Total directed paths: 6. Divide by 2 for undirected: 3. ✓
Answer: 3 undirected paths of length 2 in P₅.
Section 12.8

Trees and Basic Tree Counting

Definition — Tree

A tree is a connected graph with no cycles. Let T be a tree on n vertices. The following are all equivalent characterizations:

1. T is connected and has no cycles.

2. T is connected and has exactly n − 1 edges.

3. T has no cycles and has exactly n − 1 edges.

4. Any two vertices of T are connected by exactly one path.

5. T is connected, but removing any single edge disconnects it.

Theorem — A Tree on n Vertices Has n − 1 Edges

Proof by induction on n.

Base case (n = 1): A single vertex has 0 edges. And 1 − 1 = 0. ✓

Inductive step: Suppose every tree on k vertices has k − 1 edges. Consider a tree T on k + 1 vertices. Every tree with at least 2 vertices has at least one leaf — a vertex of degree 1. Remove a leaf v and its single incident edge. The resulting graph T′ has k vertices, is still connected, and still has no cycles. So T′ is a tree on k vertices; by the inductive hypothesis it has k − 1 edges. Adding back the removed edge: T has k edges. Since k = (k + 1) − 1, the formula holds.

□

A leaf is a vertex with degree 1. Every tree with at least 2 vertices has at least 2 leaves. A path graph has exactly 2 leaves; more branching trees have more. When a tree is given a designated starting vertex called the root, the tree acquires a natural parent–child hierarchy. Rooted trees are fundamental in computer science: file systems, binary search trees, and parse trees all use this structure.

Cayley's Formula

The number of distinct labeled trees on n vertices is:

nn − 2

For n = 2: 2⁰ = 1. For n = 3: 3¹ = 3. For n = 4: 4² = 16. For n = 5: 5³ = 125.

Section 12.9

Spanning Trees as an Introduction

Definition — Spanning Tree

A spanning tree of a connected graph G is a subgraph that includes all vertices of G and is a tree (connected, no cycles). To form a spanning tree, keep all vertices but remove edges until no cycles remain and the graph stays connected.

Spanning trees arise naturally in network design. Connecting n locations using as few links as possible while keeping everything reachable requires a spanning tree: exactly n − 1 edges. Algorithms for finding minimum-cost spanning trees — such as Kruskal's and Prim's algorithms — are fundamental to internet routing, power grid design, and supply chain optimization.

Since Cayley's Formula counts all labeled trees on n vertices, it also counts spanning trees of Kₙ — the complete graph already contains all possible edges, so every labeled tree on those vertices is a valid spanning tree. For graphs other than Kₙ, counting spanning trees requires the Matrix-Tree Theorem, which is beyond this chapter.

Example 12.9.1
Spanning trees of K₃
  • ProblemFind all spanning trees of K₃ (vertices A, B, C with edges AB, BC, AC). Verify with Cayley's Formula.
  • RepresentA spanning tree must use all 3 vertices and exactly 2 edges (since a tree on 3 vertices has 3 − 1 = 2 edges).
  • Count Edges AB and BC : path A – B – C ✓ Edges AB and AC : path B – A – C ✓ Edges BC and AC : path B – C – A ✓
  • CheckCayley's Formula: n^(n − 2) = 3^(3 − 2) = 3¹ = 3. ✓
Answer: K₃ has exactly 3 spanning trees.

Section 12.10

Graph Coloring as an Introduction

Graph coloring is the problem of assigning labels (called colors) to vertices so that no two adjacent vertices receive the same color. The chromatic number χ(G) of a graph G is the minimum number of colors needed for a valid coloring. Graph coloring has direct applications in scheduling, map coloring, register allocation in compilers, and frequency assignment in wireless networks.

GraphChromatic Number χ(G)Reason
Complete graph KₙnEvery vertex is adjacent to every other; all must differ
Bipartite graph (≥1 edge)2Two-sided structure allows 2-coloring
Tree (≥1 edge)2Trees are bipartite (no odd cycles)
Even cycle Cₙ (n even)2Bipartite; alternate colors around the cycle
Odd cycle Cₙ (n odd)3Not bipartite; third color needed to close the cycle
Chromatic Polynomial — Key Formulas

For the complete graph Kₙ with k available colors:

P(Kₙ, k) = k(k − 1)(k − 2)···(k − n + 1)

For a tree T on n vertices: P(T, k) = k(k − 1)^(n − 1)

For a cycle Cₙ on n vertices: P(Cₙ, k) = (k − 1)^n + (−1)^n (k − 1)

Counting Network Possibilities

Beyond counting properties of a specific graph, combinatorics lets us count how many graphs of a certain type can be formed on a given vertex set. Each simple graph on vertex set V is determined by choosing which 2-element subsets of V to include as edges. There are C(n, 2) possible edges, each independently included or excluded.

Number of simple graphs on n labeled vertices: 2^C(n,2)

Number with exactly m edges: C(C(n,2), m)

Number of bipartite graphs between m and n vertices (any edge subset): 2^(mn)

Section 12.13

Common Graph Counting Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting to divide by 2 when using degree sums
A graph has degree sum 18, so it has 18 edges. Correct: |E| = (sum of degrees) / 2 = 18 / 2 = 9. Each edge contributes 2 to the degree sum.
Mistake 2 — Confusing vertex degree with total edge count
K₈ has 8 edges because each vertex has degree 7. Correct: K₈ has C(8,2) = 28 edges. The degree of each vertex (7) is not the total edge count.
Mistake 3 — Treating directed and undirected paths as equivalent
The path from A to B and the path from B to A are different paths, so I count both. Correct: In undirected graphs, paths A–B and B–A traverse the same edges. Clarify whether paths are directed or undirected before counting.
Mistake 4 — Assuming all trees are paths
Every tree must look like a straight line of vertices. Correct: A tree can branch. A star K₁,ₙ is a tree. A path is a tree, but trees can have any branching structure as long as they are connected and cycle-free.
Mistake 5 — Forgetting that a spanning tree requires all vertices
I found a tree subgraph inside G, so I have a spanning tree. Correct: A spanning tree must include every vertex of G. A tree subgraph that omits vertices is not a spanning tree.
Mistake 6 — Assuming every graph can be 2-colored
Every graph can be colored with 2 colors because there are only 2 choices at each step. Correct: A graph is 2-colorable if and only if it contains no odd cycles. Any triangle (K₃) requires 3 colors. C₅ requires 3 colors.
Mistake 7 — Applying Cayley's Formula to non-complete graphs
Any graph G on n vertices has n^(n−2) spanning trees. Correct: Cayley's Formula counts spanning trees of Kₙ specifically. For other graphs, different methods (like the Matrix-Tree Theorem) are needed.
Mistake 8 — Confusing number of graphs with number of edges
There are 2^C(n,2) edges in a graph on n vertices. Correct: 2^C(n,2) is the number of distinct labeled simple graphs on n vertices — not the edge count of any single graph.
Mistake 9 — Ignoring connectivity when applying tree results
Any graph with n − 1 edges is a tree. Correct: A graph with n − 1 edges is a tree only if it is also connected. A disconnected graph with n − 1 edges is not a tree.
Mistake 10 — Misidentifying bipartite structure
The graph K₃,₃ can be colored with 1 color since it is bipartite. Correct: Bipartite graphs need 2 colors (assuming at least one edge), not 1. The two sides receive different colors. χ(K₃,₃) = 2.
Section 12.14

Preparing for Designs and Finite Structures

Chapter 12 has introduced graphs as finite combinatorial structures built from vertices and edges. You have learned how to count vertices, edges, degrees, paths, cycles, trees, spanning trees, colorings, and network configurations. You have seen how counting formulas — particularly combinations — arise naturally in graph settings, and you have seen the Handshaking Lemma and Cayley's Formula as two of the deepest results in basic graph combinatorics.

In Chapter 13, you will move from graphs to combinatorial designs and finite structures with symmetry. Designs are arrangements of objects satisfying strict balance and regularity conditions. They include Latin squares, tournament schedules, block designs, and systems with finite geometric structure. Graphs and designs are related: a design is often described by a bipartite incidence graph, and many design problems are equivalent to graph coloring or matching problems.

The symmetry tools of Chapter 13 — group actions and Burnside's Lemma — also apply to graphs when you want to count unlabeled graphs (graphs up to isomorphism), which is a much harder and more subtle problem than counting labeled ones. The capstone chapter (Chapter 14) will bring graphs, designs, and all earlier combinatorial tools into a unified modeling framework.

  • Sec 12.4The Handshaking Lemma: ∑ deg(v) = 2|E|. The sum of all degrees is always even. In any graph, the number of odd-degree vertices is always even. Proof uses double counting.
  • Sec 12.5Kₙ has C(n, 2) = n(n−1)/2 edges. Each edge is a 2-element subset of the vertex set. This connects complete graph edge counting directly to combinations.
  • Sec 12.6A bipartite graph has vertices split into two groups with edges only crossing between them. The complete bipartite graph Kₘ,ₙ has mn edges. A graph is bipartite iff it has no odd cycles.
  • Sec 12.7A path is a sequence of distinct vertices connected by edges; its length is the number of edges. A cycle closes back on itself. Connected graphs have paths between all vertex pairs. Minimum edges for connectivity: n − 1.
  • Sec 12.8A tree is a connected acyclic graph. A tree on n vertices has exactly n − 1 edges. Every tree with ≥2 vertices has ≥2 leaves. Cayley's Formula: the number of labeled trees on n vertices is n^(n−2).
  • Sec 12.9A spanning tree of a connected graph G includes all vertices and is a tree. It uses exactly n − 1 edges — the minimum for connectivity. Kₙ has n^(n−2) spanning trees (Cayley's Formula).
  • Sec 12.10Graph coloring assigns colors to vertices so no two adjacent vertices share a color. χ(Kₙ) = n. χ(bipartite) = 2. χ(Cₙ) = 2 for even n, 3 for odd n. Chromatic polynomials count valid colorings exactly.
  • Sec 12.11The number of labeled simple graphs on n vertices is 2^C(n,2). The number with exactly m edges is C(C(n,2), m). The number of bipartite graphs between m and n vertices is 2^(mn).
  • Sec 12.12Graph counting underlies computer networks, file systems, scheduling, routing, social network analysis, circuit design, and cryptographic protocol security.
  • Sec 12.13Ten common mistakes: forgetting to halve degree sums; confusing degree with edge count; direction confusion; assuming trees are paths; missing spanning tree requirements; assuming 2-colorability; misapplying Cayley's Formula; mixing graph counts with edge counts; ignoring connectivity; and misidentifying bipartite chromatic numbers.
  • Sec 12.14Chapter 13 introduces designs, Latin squares, symmetry, and Burnside's Lemma. Symmetry tools extend graph counting to unlabeled graphs. The capstone chapter unifies all combinatorial tools.

Key Terms Review

graph
A mathematical structure of a vertex set V and edge set E of pairs of vertices, modeling relationships between objects.
vertex
A point in a graph; also called a node. The objects being related.
edge
A connection between two vertices. In a simple graph, a 2-element subset of V.
simple graph
A graph with no loops and no multiple edges between the same pair of vertices.
degree
The number of edges incident to a vertex. Written deg(v).
Handshaking Lemma
∑ deg(v) = 2|E|. The sum of all degrees equals twice the number of edges. A double-counting identity.
complete graph Kₙ
A simple graph on n vertices in which every pair is connected. Has C(n,2) edges and is (n−1)-regular.
bipartite graph
A graph whose vertices split into two groups with all edges crossing between groups. Characterised by having no odd cycles.
complete bipartite Kₘ,ₙ
A bipartite graph in which every vertex on one side connects to every vertex on the other. Has mn edges.
path
A sequence of distinct vertices where each consecutive pair is connected by an edge. Length = number of edges.
cycle
A closed path: a sequence of distinct vertices each connected to the next, and the last connected back to the first. Minimum length: 3.
connected graph
A graph in which a path exists between every pair of vertices. A connected graph on n vertices needs at least n − 1 edges.
tree
A connected graph with no cycles. Equivalent: a connected graph with exactly n − 1 edges. Any two vertices connected by exactly one path.
leaf
A vertex of degree 1 in a tree. Every tree with ≥2 vertices has at least 2 leaves.
spanning tree
A subgraph of a connected graph that includes all vertices and is itself a tree. Uses exactly n − 1 edges.
Cayley's Formula
The number of labeled trees on n vertices is n^(n−2). Also counts spanning trees of Kₙ.
graph coloring
An assignment of colors (labels) to vertices so that no two adjacent vertices share the same color.
chromatic number χ(G)
The minimum number of colors needed to properly color graph G.
regular graph
A graph in which every vertex has the same degree k; called k-regular.
subgraph
A graph formed from a subset of vertices and edges of a larger graph.

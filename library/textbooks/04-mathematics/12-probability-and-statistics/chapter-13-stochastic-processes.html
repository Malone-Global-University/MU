Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"Randomness is not merely an imperfection in our knowledge. It is woven into the fabric of how systems change over time."

Every model studied so far in this book has treated randomness as something that happens once. A coin flip produces a single outcome. A continuous random variable takes on a value drawn from a density function. A sample yields data used to estimate a parameter. In each case, randomness occurs and then the story ends.

But many of the most important processes in science, finance, engineering, biology, and computing involve randomness that unfolds over time. A stock price does not simply land on a value; it rises and falls through each trading hour. An epidemic does not reach its size in an instant; it spreads from person to person across days and weeks. Customers do not all arrive at a counter simultaneously; they stream in one by one as time passes.

These are stochastic processes: collections of random variables indexed by time. Chapter 13 develops the mathematical foundations of stochastic processes at a level appropriate for a first calculus-based course. The chapter begins by defining what a stochastic process is, then builds three fundamental classes of models — Bernoulli processes and random walks, Markov chains, and Poisson processes — before connecting them to waiting time distributions, reliability, queueing, and applications across science and computing.

Opening Question

A machine in a factory can be Working, Under Repair, or Failed on any given day. Each day, there is a chance the machine transitions between these states. In the long run, what fraction of days is the machine Working? What fraction is it Failed? By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to answer such questions exactly — and to understand why the answers do not depend on what state the machine is in today.

Section 13.1

What a Stochastic Process Is

A stochastic process asks a different kind of question from a single random variable. Instead of one random outcome, we have an entire trajectory: how does the system evolve? What is the probability of visiting certain states? Where does the process end up in the long run?

Definition — Stochastic Process

A stochastic process is a collection of random variables {X(t) : t ∈ T} defined on a common probability space, where T is an index set representing time.

When T = {0, 1, 2, …} the process is discrete-time. When T = [0, ∞) the process is continuous-time. The set of values each X(t) can take is the state space.

Time IndexState SpaceExample
DiscreteDiscreteMarkov chain — weather states each day
DiscreteContinuousRandom walk on the real line
ContinuousDiscretePoisson process — count of arrivals up to time t
ContinuousContinuousBrownian motion — particle position at each instant
Sample Paths

For a fixed outcome ω in the sample space, the function t ↦ X(t, ω) is called a sample path or realization of the process. When you watch a stock price move through a trading day, you are observing one sample path drawn from a probability model governing all possible paths.

Why Stochastic Processes Matter

Finance
Stock prices, interest rates, option pricing. The Black-Scholes model is built on a stochastic process.
Physics
Brownian motion, diffusion, radioactive decay. Einstein's 1905 paper explained Brownian motion with stochastic arguments.
Biology
Genetic drift, epidemic spread, neural firing. The SIR model is a stochastic process over time.
Computing
Randomized algorithms, MCMC, hidden Markov models, reinforcement learning — all built on stochastic process foundations.

Section 13.2

Random Variables Indexed by Time

For a stochastic process, we need a way to describe how random variables at different times relate to one another. Two key structural properties appear in the most important processes.

Two Key Properties

Stationary increments: The distribution of X(t+s) − X(t) depends only on s, not on where in time t we are. What happens over any interval of length s looks the same statistically.

Independent increments: For non-overlapping intervals [t₁,t₂] and [t₃,t₄], the changes X(t₂)−X(t₁) and X(t₄)−X(t₃) are independent. Knowing what happened in one interval tells you nothing about a separate interval.

Example 13.2.1
Distribution at a fixed time — simple random walk preview
  • SetupSuppose X(0) = 0 and at each step n = 1, 2, 3, …, the value of X(n) increases by 1 or decreases by 1 with equal probability.
  • MeanE[X(n)] = 0 for all n.Each step has expected change 0; the walk is centered at the origin.
  • VarianceVar(X(n)) = n.The spread grows. After 100 steps the standard deviation is √100 = 10; after 10,000 steps, √10,000 = 100.
  • InterpretThe walk does not stay near the origin forever, but its mean stays at 0. Uncertainty grows at rate √n, not n.

Section 13.3

Bernoulli Processes

The Bernoulli process is the simplest discrete-time stochastic process. It is built entirely from the Bernoulli trials studied in Chapter 3: independent coin flips with the same bias at every step.

Definition — Bernoulli Process

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent Bernoulli random variables X₁, X₂, X₃, … each with P(Xₙ = 1) = p and P(Xₙ = 0) = 1 − p = q for some fixed p ∈ (0, 1).

Derived Distributions

QuantityDistributionMeanVariance
Successes in n trials: Sₙ = X₁ + … + XₙBinomial(n, p)npnpq
Time of first success: T = min{n ≥ 1 : Xₙ = 1}Geometric(p)1/pq/p²
Time between the (k−1)-th and k-th successesGeometric(p)1/pq/p²
Example 13.3.1
Basketball free throws as a Bernoulli process
  • SetupA player makes each free throw independently with probability p = 0.75. Attempts form a Bernoulli process.
  • Part aExpected shots until the first made free throw: E[T] = 1/p = 1/0.75 = 4/3 ≈ 1.33 shots.
  • Part bExpected makes in 10 attempts: E[S₁₀] = 10(0.75) = 7.5.
  • Part cProbability of making at least 8 of 10: P(S₁₀ ≥ 8) = C(10,8)(0.75)⁸(0.25)² + C(10,9)(0.75)⁹(0.25)¹ + C(10,10)(0.75)¹⁰ ≈ 0.2816 + 0.1877 + 0.0563 ≈ 0.5256.
About 52.6% chance of making at least 8 of 10 free throws.
Foundation for Everything Ahead

The Bernoulli process is the building block for random walks (sums of ±1 outcomes), the discrete analogue of the Poisson process, and the simplest example of a process with independent, identically distributed components. Every result here will reappear in continuous-time versions later in the chapter.

Section 13.4

Random Walks

A random walk models motion through discrete steps where each step is determined by a random variable. The running sum S₀ = 0, Sₙ = Z₁ + Z₂ + ⋯ + Zₙ with i.i.d. steps Zₙ is the general form. The most studied case is the simple symmetric random walk on the integers, where P(Zₙ = +1) = P(Zₙ = −1) = 1/2.

Simple Symmetric Random Walk — Key Facts

E[Sₙ] = 0    The walk is centered at 0 for all n. Steps average to zero.

Var(Sₙ) = n    Standard deviation is √n. The walk spreads at rate √n, not n.

Sₙ/√n → N(0,1)    By the CLT, properly scaled walks converge to standard normal.

Example 13.4.1
Asymmetric random walk — drift
  • SetupP(Zₙ = +1) = p, P(Zₙ = −1) = q = 1 − p, p ≠ 1/2.
  • MeanE[Zₙ] = p − q = 2p − 1. E[Sₙ] = n(2p − 1).If p > 1/2 the walk drifts right; if p < 1/2 it drifts left.
  • VarianceVar(Sₙ) = 4npq.
The drift term n(2p−1) dominates the random fluctuations for large n when p ≠ 1/2.
Example 13.4.2
Gambler's Ruin — asymmetric case
  • SetupA gambler starts with $10 and wants to reach $50 before going broke, with p = 0.48 per round.
  • FormulaRuin probability formula with k = 10 (start), N = 50 (target): P(reach N before 0 | S₀ = k) = (1 − (q/p)^k) / (1 − (q/p)^N)
  • Compute q/p = 0.52/0.48 ≈ 1.0833 (q/p)^10 ≈ 2.158, (q/p)^50 ≈ 60.34 P = (1 − 2.158) / (1 − 60.34) = −1.158 / −59.34 ≈ 0.0195.
  • InterpretLess than a 2% chance of success. Even a small house advantage over many trials leads to near-certain ruin.
P(success) ≈ 1.95%. A small unfavorable edge is devastating over many trials.
Recurrence of Random Walks

A striking result: the simple symmetric random walk in one dimension is recurrent — it returns to the origin with probability 1. No matter how far it strays, it always comes back. In three or more dimensions, the walk has positive probability of wandering off to infinity and never returning (transient). The boundary is dimension 2, which is also recurrent but barely.

Student Warning — Spread vs. Drift

Do not confuse the expected position E[Sₙ] = n(2p−1) with the spread Var(Sₙ) = 4npq. For a symmetric walk (p = 1/2), the expected position is always 0, but the walk still spreads — the standard deviation grows as √n. "Expected to return to 0" does not mean "stays near 0."

Section 13.5

Markov Chains

The random walk has independent steps. But many real-world processes have a weaker structure: the future depends on the past, but only through the present. Weather tomorrow depends on weather today, not on weather three weeks ago. This is the Markov property.

Definition — The Markov Property

A discrete-time process {Xₙ} with state space S satisfies the Markov property if for all n ≥ 0 and all states i, j, i₀, …, iₙ₋₁ in S:

P(Xₙ₊₁ = j | Xₙ = i, Xₙ₋₁ = iₙ₋₁, …, X₀ = i₀) = P(Xₙ₊₁ = j | Xₙ = i).

A process satisfying the Markov property is called a Markov chain. The transition probability pᵢⱼ = P(Xₙ₊₁ = j | Xₙ = i) gives the probability of moving from state i to state j in one step. For a time-homogeneous chain, these probabilities do not depend on n.

Student Warning — Markov ≠ Independent

The Markov property is weaker than independence. In a Markov chain, X₀ and X₂ are not generally independent — knowing X₀ gives information about X₁, which gives information about X₂. The Markov property says only that given X₁, knowing X₀ adds nothing. Correlation across time is allowed; only long-range memory is excluded.

Example 13.5.1
Two-state weather chain
  • SetupWeather is Sunny (S) or Rainy (R) each day. Transition probabilities: p_SS = 0.8, p_SR = 0.2, p_RS = 0.5, p_RR = 0.5.
  • InterpretIf today is sunny: 80% chance tomorrow is sunny, 20% rainy. If today is rainy: 50% chance tomorrow is sunny.
  • Markov?Yes. Tomorrow's weather depends only on today's weather, not on the weather a week ago.

Three-state machine reliability chain
  • SetupA machine is Working (W), Under Repair (U), or Failed awaiting parts (F) each day.

Section 13.6

Transition Matrices

When the state space S = {1, 2, …, k} is finite, all transition probabilities can be organized into a transition matrix P, where entry pᵢⱼ in row i, column j gives the probability of moving from state i to state j in one step. Every row must sum to 1.

Example 13.6.1
Computing multi-step probabilities via matrix powers
  • MatrixThe weather chain transition matrix is: P = | 0.8 0.2 | | 0.5 0.5 | Row 1 = Sunny, Row 2 = Rainy.
  • Two-stepCompute P² by matrix multiplication: P² = | 0.74 0.26 | | 0.65 0.35 |
  • InterpretThe (1,2) entry of P² is 0.26: if today is Sunny, probability of Rainy in two days is 0.26.
  • Three-step P³ = | 0.722 0.278 | | 0.695 0.305 | The rows are converging — the chain is "forgetting" its starting state.
Theorem
Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations

For a time-homogeneous Markov chain with transition matrix P, the probability of going from state i to state j in exactly n steps is the (i, j) entry of Pⁿ:

P(Xₙ = j | X₀ = i) = (Pⁿ)ᵢⱼ.

The state distribution after n steps is π⁽ⁿ⁾ = π⁽⁰⁾ Pⁿ, where π⁽⁰⁾ is the initial distribution row vector.

Section 13.7

Long-Term Behavior of Markov Chains

As n → ∞, the entries of Pⁿ often converge to limiting values. These represent the long-run fraction of time the chain spends in each state, regardless of starting state. Whether this happens depends on two structural properties.

PropertyDefinitionWhy It Matters
IrreducibleEvery state can be reached from every other state.Ensures the chain does not get trapped in a subset of states.
AperiodicNo state has a period greater than 1. (Return is possible at consecutive times.)Ensures probabilities converge rather than oscillate.
Absorbing stateA state i with pᵢᵢ = 1 — once entered, never left.Absorbing chains require a different analysis (absorption probabilities).
Theorem
Convergence to Stationary Distribution

Let {Xₙ} be an irreducible, aperiodic Markov chain with finite state space. Then:

lim_{n→∞} p_{ij}^{(n)} = πⱼ for all states i and j,

where π = (π₁, π₂, …, πₖ) is the unique probability distribution satisfying πP = π and Σⱼ πⱼ = 1. The chain converges to π regardless of starting state.

Example 13.7.1
Checking irreducibility and aperiodicity — weather chain
  • Irreducible?From Sunny we can reach Rainy (p_SR = 0.2 > 0). From Rainy we can reach Sunny (p_RS = 0.5 > 0). All states communicate. Yes.
  • Aperiodic?p_SS = 0.8 > 0, so the chain can return to Sunny in 1 step. Period of Sunny = gcd{1, 2, 3, …} = 1. Aperiodic. Yes.
  • ConclusionThe weather chain satisfies both conditions. The convergence theorem applies and a unique stationary distribution exists.

Section 13.8

Stationary Distributions as an Introduction

The stationary distribution π satisfies πP = π: if the chain starts in distribution π, it remains there forever. To find π, solve the balance equations πP = π together with the normalization condition Σⱼ πⱼ = 1.

Example 13.8.1
Finding the stationary distribution — weather chain
  • SetupLet π = (π_S, π_R). The equation πP = π gives: π_S = 0.8π_S + 0.5π_R π_R = 0.2π_S + 0.5π_R
  • SolveFrom the first equation: 0.2π_S = 0.5π_R, so π_S = 2.5π_R. Apply normalization: 2.5π_R + π_R = 1 → π_R = 2/7 ≈ 0.286, π_S = 5/7 ≈ 0.714.
  • VerifyCheck πP = π: (5/7)(0.8) + (2/7)(0.5) = 4/7 + 1/7 = 5/7 = π_S. ✓ (5/7)(0.2) + (2/7)(0.5) = 1/7 + 1/7 = 2/7 = π_R. ✓
  • InterpretIn the long run, about 71.4% of days are Sunny and 28.6% Rainy — regardless of whether today is Sunny or Rainy.
Stationary distribution: π_S = 5/7 ≈ 0.714, π_R = 2/7 ≈ 0.286.
Example 13.8.2
Stationary distribution — machine reliability chain
  • Balance equationsLet π = (π_W, π_U, π_F): π_W = 0.90π_W + 0.60π_U + 0.00π_F → 0.10π_W = 0.60π_U → π_W = 6π_U π_F = 0.03π_W + 0.10π_U + 0.60π_F → 0.40π_F = 0.28π_U → π_F = 0.70π_U
  • Normalization 6π_U + π_U + 0.70π_U = 1 → 7.70π_U = 1 → π_U ≈ 0.1299.
  • Result π_W ≈ 0.779, π_U ≈ 0.130, π_F ≈ 0.091.
  • InterpretThe machine is Working about 77.9% of all days, Under Repair 13.0%, and Failed awaiting parts about 9.1% — in the long run, from any starting state.
π_W ≈ 0.779, π_U ≈ 0.130, π_F ≈ 0.091.
Two Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Reading the stationary distribution directly from a row of P. A row gives transition probabilities from one state, not the long-run balance. Solve πP = π.

Mistake 2: Forgetting normalization. The equations πP = π alone do not uniquely determine π — one equation is redundant and must be replaced by Σⱼ πⱼ = 1.

Section 13.9

Poisson Processes

The Poisson process is the continuous-time analogue of the Bernoulli process. Instead of checking for events at each integer time step, events can occur at any moment t ≥ 0, at a constant average rate λ, and what happens in non-overlapping intervals is independent.

Definition — Poisson Process

A stochastic process {N(t) : t ≥ 0} is a Poisson process with rate λ > 0 if:

1. N(0) = 0.

2. Independent increments: changes over non-overlapping intervals are independent.

3. Stationary increments: the distribution of N(t+s) − N(t) depends only on s.

4. For small h: P(N(h) = 1) = λh + o(h) and P(N(h) ≥ 2) = o(h).

Under these conditions, N(t) − N(s) ~ Poisson(λt) for any interval of length t − s.

Theorem
Count Distribution of a Poisson Process

The number of events in any interval of length t follows a Poisson distribution:

P(N(t) = k) = e^{-λt}(λt)^k / k! for k = 0, 1, 2, … E[N(t)] = λt, Var(N(t)) = λt.

The parameter λ is the rate: the expected number of events per unit time.

Example 13.9.1
Bakery arrivals — Poisson probability computations
  • SetupCustomers arrive at rate λ = 5 per hour.
  • Part aProbability of exactly 3 arrivals in the next 30 minutes (t = 0.5, λt = 2.5): P(N(0.5) = 3) = e^{-2.5}(2.5)³ / 3! ≈ (0.0821)(15.625)/6 ≈ 0.2138.
  • Part bExpected customers in 2 hours: E[N(2)] = λ · 2 = 5 × 2 = 10.
  • Part cProbability of no arrivals in 15 minutes (t = 0.25, λt = 1.25): P(N(0.25) = 0) = e^{-1.25} ≈ 0.2865.
Part a: ≈ 0.214. Part b: 10 customers. Part c: ≈ 28.7% chance of no arrivals.

Superposition and Thinning

If N₁(t) and N₂(t) are independent Poisson processes with rates λ₁ and λ₂, their superposition N(t) = N₁(t) + N₂(t) is Poisson with rate λ₁ + λ₂. Conversely, if events from a Poisson process with rate λ are independently classified as Type 1 (probability p) or Type 2 (probability 1−p), the two types form independent Poisson processes with rates λp and λ(1−p).

Section 13.10

Waiting Times

The Poisson count and the waiting time between events are two views of the same process. The interarrival time T₁ is the time until the first event; T₂ the time between the first and second; and so on. A fundamental theorem connects these waiting times to the exponential distribution.

Theorem
Interarrival Times of a Poisson Process

In a Poisson process with rate λ, the interarrival times T₁, T₂, T₃, … are independent and identically distributed Exponential(λ) random variables.

P(T₁ > t) = P(N(t) = 0) = e^{-λt}. E[Tₖ] = 1/λ, Var(Tₖ) = 1/λ².

The time until the n-th event, Sₙ = T₁ + … + Tₙ, follows a Gamma(n, λ) distribution with E[Sₙ] = n/λ.

The Memoryless Property

For an Exponential(λ) waiting time T:

P(T > s+t | T > s) = P(T > t) for all s, t > 0.

If you have already waited s units of time for the next event, the additional waiting time still has the same Exponential(λ) distribution. The distribution has no memory of the time already elapsed. This is the continuous-time analogue of the geometric distribution's memoryless property.

Example 13.10.1
Memoryless property — waiting for a bus
  • SetupBuses arrive as a Poisson process with rate λ = 2 per hour. You have been waiting 10 minutes already.
  • QuestionWhat is the probability of waiting more than 5 additional minutes?
  • Key ideaBy the memoryless property, the additional waiting time is still Exponential(2). Convert 5 minutes = 1/12 hour.
  • Compute P(T > 1/12) = e^{-2 · (1/12)} = e^{-1/6} ≈ 0.8465.
  • InterpretAbout 84.6% chance of waiting more than 5 more minutes — regardless of how long you have already waited. The 10 minutes of past waiting is irrelevant.
P(wait more than 5 more minutes) ≈ 84.6%. Past waiting time has no effect.
Example 13.10.2
Gamma distribution — time to the n-th event
  • SetupCosmic ray particles hit a detector at rate λ = 3 per second.
  • Part aExpected time until the 10th particle: E[S₁₀] = 10/λ = 10/3 ≈ 3.33 seconds.
  • Part bProbability that the 10th particle arrives within 4 seconds. Use the duality: P(S₁₀ ≤ 4) = P(N(4) ≥ 10) where N(4) ~ Poisson(12). P(N(4) ≥ 10) = 1 − Σₖ₌₀⁹ e^{-12}(12)^k/k! ≈ 0.7576.
Expected time to 10th particle: 3.33 sec. Probability of arrival within 4 sec: ≈ 75.8%.
The Count-Waiting Duality

These two facts are equivalent and can be used interchangeably: P(N(t) < n) = P(Sₙ > t). "Fewer than n events by time t" is the same event as "the n-th event occurs after time t." This duality lets you switch between the Poisson CDF and the Gamma CDF depending on which is easier to compute.

Reliability and Queueing as an Introduction

Reliability Theory

Let T be the lifetime of a component. The reliability function is R(t) = P(T > t) = 1 − F(t). The hazard rate h(t) = f(t)/R(t) gives the instantaneous failure rate at time t given survival to t.

DistributionHazard Rate h(t)Interpretation
Exponential(λ)λ (constant)Memoryless — equally likely to fail at any age. Models electronic components without wear.
Weibull (β > 1)Increasing in tComponents wear out — more likely to fail with age. Standard in reliability engineering.
Weibull (β < 1)Decreasing in tInfant mortality — high early failure rate that decreases over time.
Example 13.11.1
Series and parallel systems — exponential components
  • SetupThree components each with Exponential lifetime, λ = 0.01 failures per hour.
  • SeriesAll three must work. System fails at first component failure: R_series(t) = (e^{-0.01t})³ = e^{-0.03t}. Mean system lifetime = 1/0.03 ≈ 33.3 hours.
  • ParallelAt least one must work. System fails only when all three fail: R_parallel(t) = 1 − (1 − e^{-0.01t})³. At t = 50: R_parallel(50) = 1 − (1 − e^{-0.5})³ ≈ 1 − (0.3935)³ ≈ 0.939.
  • InterpretThe parallel system survives 50 hours with 93.9% probability — far better than the series system.
Series: mean lifetime ≈ 33 hrs. Parallel: 93.9% chance of surviving 50 hrs.

Queueing Theory — The M/M/1 Queue

The M/M/1 queue has Poisson arrivals (rate λ), exponential service (rate μ), and one server. The key parameter is the traffic intensity ρ = λ/μ. The queue is stable — waiting times stay bounded — if and only if ρ < 1.

M/M/1 Queue — Key Results (when ρ < 1)

Stationary distribution: πₙ = (1−ρ)ρⁿ for n = 0, 1, 2, … (geometric)

Expected number in system: E[L] = ρ/(1−ρ)

Expected number in queue: E[L_q] = ρ²/(1−ρ)

Little's Law: L = λW where W is the expected time in the system.

Example 13.11.2
Bank teller — M/M/1 queue analysis
  • SetupOne teller. Arrivals: λ = 10/hr. Service: μ = 15/hr (mean = 4 min).
  • Stabilityρ = 10/15 = 2/3 < 1. The queue is stable.
  • In system E[L] = ρ/(1−ρ) = (2/3)/(1/3) = 2 customers.
  • Wait timeBy Little's Law: W = L/λ = 2/10 = 0.2 hours = 12 minutes total time in system.
  • Queue onlyW_q = W − 1/μ = 12 − 4 = 8 minutes waiting before service begins.
Average 2 customers in system; 12 min total wait; 8 min waiting before service.
Section 13.12

Stochastic Models in Finance, Physics, Biology, and Computing

Finance
Geometric Brownian Motion: S(t) = S(0)exp[(μ − σ²/2)t + σW(t)]. The log-price follows a random walk with normal increments. Forms the basis of Black-Scholes option pricing.
Physics
Brownian motion models diffusion. Einstein's 1905 paper explained the irregular motion of pollen grains as the result of random molecular collisions. The diffusion equation describes the spreading of the particle's probability density over time.
Biology
The Wright-Fisher model treats allele frequencies as a Markov chain across generations. The stochastic SIR model tracks susceptible, infected, and recovered counts as a CTMC — crucial for small populations where randomness can extinguish a disease the deterministic model predicts will persist.
Computing
PageRank computes the stationary distribution of a Markov chain over web pages. MCMC methods sample from complex posteriors by designing chains whose stationary distributions match the target. Hidden Markov Models power speech recognition and bioinformatics.

Section 13.13

Common Stochastic Process Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Markov with Independence
"Since the chain is Markov, X₀ and X₂ are independent." Correct: X₀ and X₂ are conditionally independent given X₁, not marginally independent. The Markov property restricts long-range memory, not correlation.
Mistake 2 — Reading the Stationary Distribution from a Row
Row (0.6, 0.3, 0.1) → concluding π = (0.6, 0.3, 0.1). Correct: A row gives transition probabilities from one state, not the long-run distribution. Solve πP = π with normalization Σⱼ πⱼ = 1.
Mistake 3 — Forgetting the Normalization Condition
Solving πP = π to get π₁ = 2π₂, then stopping without normalizing. Correct: From π₁ = 2π₂ and π₁ + π₂ = 1, conclude π₂ = 1/3 and π₁ = 2/3. The balance equations alone never determine π uniquely.
Mistake 4 — Confusing Rate and Mean Waiting Time
"Average rate is 4 calls/hr, so average waiting time between calls is 4 hours." Correct: Rate λ = 4 calls/hr means average waiting time = 1/λ = 1/4 hour = 15 minutes. Rate and mean waiting time are reciprocals.
Mistake 5 — "I Am Due" Fallacy with Exponential Waiting
"I have waited 30 minutes, so I'm due for a bus — additional wait is less than 30 min." Correct: By the memoryless property, P(T > s+t | T > s) = P(T > t). Additional waiting time has exactly the same Exponential distribution regardless of how long you have already waited.
Mistake 6 — Transposing the Transition Matrix
Setting up the matrix so that column i gives outgoing probabilities from state i (column-stochastic instead of row-stochastic). Correct: Row i of P gives the outgoing transition probabilities from state i. The equation is πP = π (row vector π left-multiplied). Columns need not sum to 1.
Mistake 7 — Applying Convergence Without Checking Conditions
Computing a stationary distribution for a chain with an absorbing state and claiming all starting states converge to it. Correct: First check irreducibility and aperiodicity. Chains with absorbing states are not irreducible. Compute absorption probabilities instead.
Mistake 8 — Stationarity vs. Limiting Distribution
"I found the stationary distribution, so the chain converges to it." Correct: A stationary distribution always exists for a finite irreducible chain. But convergence to it requires aperiodicity as well. A periodic irreducible chain has a unique stationary distribution but does not converge to it.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1A stochastic process is a family of random variables indexed by time. Processes may be discrete- or continuous-time, and discrete- or continuous-state. A sample path is one realization of the full trajectory.
  • Sec 13.2Key structural properties: stationary increments (behavior depends only on interval length, not location) and independent increments (non-overlapping intervals are independent). Both hold for the Poisson process.
  • Sec 13.3A Bernoulli process is a sequence of i.i.d. Bernoulli trials. Counts follow the Binomial distribution; waiting times follow the Geometric distribution; interarrival times are independent and geometric.
  • Sec 13.4A random walk is the sum of i.i.d. steps. The symmetric walk has E[Sₙ] = 0 and Var(Sₙ) = n. The CLT says Sₙ/√n → N(0,1). The Gambler's Ruin gives exact formulas for absorption probabilities in asymmetric walks.
  • Sec 13.5The Markov property: the future is independent of the past given the present. A Markov chain satisfies this property. Transition probabilities pᵢⱼ give one-step movement probabilities. Time-homogeneous chains have constant transition probabilities.
  • Sec 13.6The transition matrix P is row-stochastic. The n-step transition probabilities are entries of Pⁿ (Chapman-Kolmogorov). The state distribution after n steps is π⁽⁰⁾Pⁿ.
  • Sec 13.7Irreducible chains have all states communicating. Aperiodic chains have no state with period > 1. For finite, irreducible, aperiodic chains, n-step probabilities converge to the unique stationary distribution regardless of starting state.
  • Sec 13.8The stationary distribution π satisfies πP = π and Σⱼ πⱼ = 1. Solve using balance equations plus normalization. The stationary distribution gives the long-run fraction of time in each state.
  • Sec 13.9The Poisson process counts events in continuous time with rate λ. Count in any interval of length t is Poisson(λt). Superposition is Poisson with summed rates; thinning produces independent Poisson sub-processes.
  • Sec 13.10Interarrival times in a Poisson process are i.i.d. Exponential(λ) with mean 1/λ. The exponential has the memoryless property. The time to the n-th event is Gamma(n, λ) with mean n/λ. Duality: P(Sₙ ≤ t) = P(N(t) ≥ n).
  • Sec 13.11Reliability: R(t) = P(T > t); hazard rate h(t) = f(t)/R(t). Series systems multiply reliabilities; parallel systems subtract from 1. M/M/1 queue: ρ = λ/μ, stationary distribution geometric, Little's Law L = λW.
  • Sec 13.12Stochastic processes appear in finance (GBM, Black-Scholes), physics (diffusion, Brownian motion), biology (genetic drift, SIR model), and computing (PageRank, MCMC, HMMs, reinforcement learning).
  • Sec 13.13Eight common errors: Markov ≠ independence; rows ≠ stationary distribution; forgetting normalization; rate ≠ wait time; the "due" fallacy; transposed matrix; unchecked convergence conditions; stationarity ≠ convergence.
  • Sec 13.14This chapter is a foundation for continuous-time Markov chains, Brownian motion, stochastic calculus, martingales, hidden Markov models, MCMC, reinforcement learning, and time series analysis.

Key Terms Review

stochastic process
A collection of random variables {X(t) : t ∈ T} indexed by time.
state space
The set of values a stochastic process can take at each time point.
sample path
A single realization (trajectory) of a stochastic process: t ↦ X(t, ω) for one fixed outcome ω.
independent increments
Changes over non-overlapping time intervals are independent random variables.
stationary increments
The distribution of X(t+s) − X(t) depends only on the length s, not on the starting time t.
Bernoulli process
A sequence of independent, identically distributed Bernoulli(p) random variables X₁, X₂, X₃, …
random walk
The process Sₙ = Z₁ + Z₂ + ⋯ + Zₙ formed by summing i.i.d. step variables Zₙ.
Gambler's Ruin
The problem of computing the probability that an asymmetric random walk reaches one boundary before another.
Markov property
The future is conditionally independent of the past given the present: P(Xₙ₊₁ = j | all history) = P(Xₙ₊₁ = j | Xₙ = i).
Markov chain
A stochastic process satisfying the Markov property.
time-homogeneous
A Markov chain whose transition probabilities pᵢⱼ = P(Xₙ₊₁ = j | Xₙ = i) do not depend on the time step n.
transition probability pᵢⱼ
The probability of moving from state i to state j in one step of a Markov chain.
transition matrix P
The k×k matrix of one-step transition probabilities; each row sums to 1 (row-stochastic).
Chapman-Kolmogorov
The theorem that n-step transition probabilities equal the (i,j) entry of Pⁿ.
irreducible
Every state can be reached from every other state in some number of steps.
aperiodic
No state has a period greater than 1; return to the state is possible at consecutive time steps.
absorbing state
A state from which the chain never leaves: pᵢᵢ = 1.
stationary distribution π
A probability vector satisfying πP = π and Σⱼ πⱼ = 1. The long-run fraction of time in each state.
detailed balance
The condition πᵢ pᵢⱼ = πⱼ pⱼᵢ; sufficient for π to be the stationary distribution of a reversible chain.
Poisson process
A continuous-time process with independent, stationary increments where the count in any interval of length t is Poisson(λt).
rate λ
The expected number of Poisson events per unit time; the reciprocal of the mean interarrival time.
interarrival time
The time between consecutive events in a Poisson process; distributed as Exponential(λ).
memoryless property
P(T > s+t | T > s) = P(T > t); the past waiting time has no effect on the remaining waiting time.
Gamma distribution
The distribution of the sum of n independent Exponential(λ) variables; the time to the n-th Poisson event.
reliability function R(t)
R(t) = P(T > t), the probability a component survives past time t.
hazard rate h(t)
h(t) = f(t)/R(t), the instantaneous failure rate given survival to time t.
series system
A system that functions only when all components function; reliability equals the product of component reliabilities.
parallel system
A system that functions when at least one component functions; more reliable than any individual component.
M/M/1 queue
A single-server queue with Poisson arrivals (rate λ), exponential service (rate μ), and geometric stationary distribution.
traffic intensity ρ
ρ = λ/μ; the fraction of time the server is busy. Stability requires ρ < 1.
Little's Law
L = λW: expected number in system equals arrival rate times expected time in system. Holds for any stable queue.
Brownian motion
The continuous-time limit of the random walk with normally distributed increments. Foundation for stochastic calculus.
MCMC
Markov Chain Monte Carlo: computational methods that sample from target distributions using Markov chains with the target as their stationary distribution.
Hidden Markov Model
A model with an unobserved Markov chain and observed outputs that are noisy functions of the hidden state.

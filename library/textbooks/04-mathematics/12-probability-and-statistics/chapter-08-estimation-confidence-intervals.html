Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every statistical investigation begins with a question about the world that cannot be answered by examining every individual in a population. A physician studying blood pressure in adults cannot measure every adult. An engineer testing the lifetime of a manufactured component cannot test every component to failure. A public health researcher estimating vaccine effectiveness cannot enroll an entire country. In each case, the investigator collects a sample, analyzes it carefully, and uses the results to draw conclusions about the larger population. This process is estimation.

Estimation is not guessing. It is disciplined uncertainty: the principled use of probability theory, sampling distributions, and careful assumptions to say not only what we think a parameter is, but how confident we are in that judgment and under what conditions that confidence is warranted. Every concept from Chapters 1 through 7 flows into estimation — probability, random variables, distributions, joint distributions, transformations, limit theorems, and sampling distributions now converge into their main applied purpose.

This chapter introduces two forms of estimation. A point estimate is a single number representing our best guess at a population parameter. A confidence interval surrounds that guess with a carefully calculated range reflecting how much uncertainty the sampling process introduces. Both forms require understanding what estimators are, how to judge their quality, and how to interpret results correctly — including the most commonly misunderstood idea in statistics: what a confidence interval actually means.

Opening Question

A polling organization surveys 1,000 registered voters and finds that 480 plan to vote for a particular candidate. They report "48% support — margin of error ±3%." Where does that margin come from? Why 3% and not 1% or 10%? What exactly does "margin of error" mean? What would it mean to say we are "95% confident"? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to derive every number in that report from first principles — and explain, precisely, why the phrase "there is a 95% probability the true support is between 45% and 51%" is incorrect.

Section 8.1

What Estimation Does

Science, engineering, medicine, economics, and public policy all depend on knowledge of populations: the full set of individuals, items, or outcomes under study. But populations are almost never fully observable. The gap between what we can observe and what we need to know is where statistics lives. Estimation bridges that gap.

Definition — Estimation

Estimation is the process of using information from a sample to make inferences about numerical characteristics of a population. Estimation is not about removing uncertainty — uncertainty cannot be removed. It is about quantifying uncertainty honestly, working within it rigorously, and communicating conclusions that are both useful and defensible.

ModeWhat It ProducesExample
Point estimationA single number — the best guess at the parameter"48% of voters support Candidate A"
Interval estimationA range of plausible values with a stated confidence level"48% ± 3%, with 95% confidence"

A point estimate without an interval gives the impression of false precision. An interval without a point estimate gives less information than is available. Good statistical practice reports both. Estimation rests on probability theory: because sample statistics are random variables that vary from sample to sample according to known distributions, we can use probability to describe how far a statistic is likely to be from the parameter it estimates.

Section 8.2

Parameters and Estimators

A parameter is a numerical characteristic of a population — fixed but unknown. A statistic is any function of observed sample data. When a statistic is used to estimate a parameter, it is called an estimator. When specific data are collected and the rule is applied, the resulting number is called an estimate.

This distinction matters deeply. An estimator is a rule — a function, a random variable with its own distribution. An estimate is the specific number produced by applying that rule to our data. The quality of an estimator is judged not by any single estimate but by the behavior of the estimator over many hypothetical repetitions of the sampling process.

ParameterSymbolEstimatorFormula
Population meanμSample meanX̄ = (1/n) Σ Xᵢ
Population proportionpSample proportionp̂ = X/n
Population varianceσ²Sample varianceS² = (1/(n−1)) Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)²
Difference of meansμ₁ − μ₂Difference of sample meansX̄₁ − X̄₂
Why n − 1 in S²?

The denominator n − 1 rather than n is not arbitrary. It corrects for a systematic underestimation that arises when the sample mean x̄ is used in place of the unknown μ. This correction — Bessel's correction — is developed fully in Section 8.4.

Example 8.2.1
Distinguishing the estimator from the estimate
  • ProblemA sample of n = 25 light bulbs is drawn from a production line. Their lifetimes are recorded. Identify the parameter of interest, an appropriate estimator, and what distinguishes the estimator from the estimate.
  • Parameterμ — the true mean lifetime (in hours) of all light bulbs produced by this line. Fixed but unknown.
  • EstimatorThe sample mean X̄ = (1/25)ΣXᵢ. This is the rule applied to any sample of 25 bulbs. It is a random variable — its value changes across different samples.
  • EstimateAfter collecting data and computing, say, x̄ = 1,247 hours, that number is the estimate. It is the realized value of the estimator in this particular sample.
Estimator X̄ is a random variable. Estimate 1,247 hours is the specific value it took in this sample.

Section 8.3

Point Estimates

A point estimate is the numerical value an estimator produces when applied to a specific sample. It is our single best guess at the population parameter. Point estimates are easy to communicate and easy to compute — their limitation is that they convey no information about uncertainty. Every confidence interval is built around a point estimate; we cannot quantify uncertainty about a quantity without first having an estimate of it.

The Plug-In Principle

Point estimation often follows the plug-in principle: replace the population quantity with its sample analog. The population mean μ is estimated by x̄. The population variance σ² is estimated by s². The population proportion p is estimated by p̂. The population median is estimated by the sample median. This principle is intuitive and often produces good estimators.

Example 8.3.1
Estimating mean and variance from summary statistics
  • ProblemA researcher measures resting pulse rates of a random sample of n = 30 adults and records: Σxᵢ = 2,130 and Σxᵢ² = 152,910. Compute point estimates of μ and σ.
  • Meanx̄ = 2,130 / 30 = 71.0 beats per minute
  • Variances² = (Σxᵢ² − n·x̄²) / (n−1) = (152,910 − 30·(71.0)²) / 29 = (152,910 − 151,230) / 29 = 1,680 / 29 ≈ 57.93
  • Std Devs = √57.93 ≈ 7.61 beats per minute
Point estimates: μ̂ = 71.0 bpm and σ̂ = 7.61 bpm. These are our best guesses from this sample; a different sample would produce different values.
Example 8.3.2
Estimating a population proportion
  • ProblemIn a random sample of 400 registered voters, 184 say they plan to vote in the upcoming election. Estimate the population proportion p.
  • Computep̂ = 184 / 400 = 0.46
  • InterpretWe estimate that 46% of registered voters plan to vote. This is a single number — it contains no information about uncertainty.
Point estimate: p̂ = 0.46. Always accompany a point estimate with a confidence interval to communicate uncertainty.
Section 8.4

Bias

Definition — Bias

An estimator T for parameter θ is unbiased if E[T] = θ. If E[T] ≠ θ, the estimator is biased. The bias of T is:

Bias(T) = E[T] − θ

Positive bias: T tends to overestimate θ on average. Negative bias: T tends to underestimate. Bias is a property of the estimator — the rule — not of any single estimate.

Proof — X̄ is Unbiased for μ

Let X₁, X₂, …, Xₙ be a random sample from a population with mean μ. Then:

E[X̄] = E[(1/n)ΣXᵢ] = (1/n)ΣE[Xᵢ] = (1/n)·nμ = μ

The second equality uses linearity of expectation. The third uses the fact that each Xᵢ has expected value μ, since all observations are drawn from the same population. This result holds for any population distribution — the sample mean is always unbiased for the population mean, as long as the sample is random.

∎
Proof — Why S² Uses n − 1 (Bessel's Correction)

The naive estimator S̃² = (1/n)Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² satisfies E[S̃²] = ((n−1)/n)σ², which underestimates σ² systematically. The bias is −σ²/n.

Defining S² = (1/(n−1))Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² instead gives E[S²] = (n/(n−1))·((n−1)/n)σ² = σ². This is Bessel's correction.

Intuition. When we compute S², we use x̄ instead of the unknown μ. Since x̄ is computed from the same data, it is automatically closer to each xᵢ than μ typically would be — the deviations xᵢ − x̄ are systematically smaller than xᵢ − μ. Dividing by n − 1 compensates for this shrinkage.

∎
Important — S is Not Unbiased for σ

Even though S² is unbiased for σ², the sample standard deviation S = √S² is not an unbiased estimator for σ. By Jensen's inequality, E[√S²] ≤ √E[S²] = σ for a concave function. In practice, the bias in S is small for large n, and S is still used as the standard point estimator for σ.

Example 8.4.1
Comparing two unbiased estimators
  • ProblemBoth the sample mean X̄ and the sample median M are unbiased for μ when sampling from a symmetric distribution. How should we choose between them?
  • AnswerWhen both estimators are unbiased, bias alone cannot distinguish them. We must compare their variances. For symmetric, light-tailed distributions like the normal, the sample mean has lower variance than the median (Section 8.5). For heavy-tailed distributions, the median can be more efficient. Bias is one criterion, not the only one.
When bias is equal, prefer the estimator with lower variance — the subject of the next section.

Section 8.5

Variance of an Estimator

Two unbiased estimators can both center on the true parameter θ and yet behave very differently. One might fluctuate wildly from sample to sample; another might stay relatively close to θ in almost every sample. The variance of an estimator measures this fluctuation. Lower variance means the estimator is more reliable: in most samples, it will be close to the truth.

Variance and Standard Error of the Sample Mean

For a random sample of size n from a population with mean μ and variance σ²:

Var(X̄) = σ²/n

SE(X̄) = σ/√n

This formula encodes two fundamental truths: (1) Larger samples give more reliable estimates — as n grows, SE shrinks. (2) More variable populations require larger samples to achieve the same precision.

Derivation — Var(X̄)

Var(X̄) = Var((1/n)ΣXᵢ) = (1/n²)ΣVar(Xᵢ) [independence: covariance terms vanish] = (1/n²)·nσ² = σ²/n

∎
Variance and Standard Error of the Sample Proportion

For a random sample of n Bernoulli observations with success probability p:

Var(p̂) = p(1−p)/n

SE(p̂) = √(p(1−p)/n)

Since p is unknown, in practice substitute p̂: SE_hat(p̂) = √(p̂(1−p̂)/n)

Example 8.5.1
Standard error and required sample size
  • ProblemA random sample of n = 64 measurements is drawn from a population with σ = 16. Compute the standard error of X̄. Then determine the sample size needed to cut the standard error in half.
  • SESE(X̄) = σ/√n = 16/√64 = 16/8 = 2
  • Halve SETo achieve SE = 1: 16/√n = 1, so √n = 16, giving n = 256. We must quadruple the sample size to halve the standard error.
  • PrincipleTo reduce SE by a factor of k, multiply the sample size by k². Precision is expensive in terms of data.
SE = 2 for n = 64. To achieve SE = 1, we need n = 256 — four times as large.
Efficiency

When comparing two unbiased estimators T₁ and T₂ for the same parameter, the one with smaller variance is called more efficient. The ratio Var(T₁)/Var(T₂) is the relative efficiency. For example, the sample mean is more efficient than the sample median when sampling from a normal population — the median needs roughly 57% more observations to achieve the same precision.

Section 8.6

Mean Squared Error as an Introduction

The MSE and Bias-Variance Decomposition

The mean squared error of an estimator T for parameter θ combines bias and variance:

MSE(T) = E[(T − θ)²]

A fundamental identity decomposes MSE into two components:

MSE(T) = Var(T) + [Bias(T)]²

If T is unbiased, MSE(T) = Var(T). If T is biased, a small amount of bias can sometimes dramatically reduce variance, giving a lower MSE overall — the bias-variance tradeoff.

Proof — Bias-Variance Decomposition

Let b = E[T] − θ (the bias). Write T − θ = (T − E[T]) + (E[T] − θ) = (T − E[T]) + b. Then:

E[(T−θ)²] = E[(T−E[T])²] + 2b·E[T−E[T]] + b² = Var(T) + 0 + b²

since E[T − E[T]] = 0 by definition.

∎
Example 8.6.1
Comparing estimators by MSE
  • ProblemEstimator A has Bias(A) = 0 and Var(A) = 12. Estimator B has Bias(B) = 2 and Var(B) = 3. Compute MSE of each and determine which is preferable by MSE.
  • MSE(A)MSE(A) = 12 + 0² = 12
  • MSE(B)MSE(B) = 3 + 2² = 3 + 4 = 7
  • VerdictEstimator B has lower MSE despite being biased. In terms of average squared error, B is preferable. However, if unbiasedness is required by the application (regulatory, scientific reporting), A may be required regardless. MSE is one criterion, not the only one.
MSE(A) = 12; MSE(B) = 7. B wins on MSE, but context determines the final choice.
The Bias-Variance Tradeoff in Machine Learning

Regularization in machine learning and ridge regression in statistics intentionally introduce a small amount of bias to substantially reduce variance, achieving a lower MSE overall. This is not hypothetical — it is the basis of some of the most widely used modeling techniques. Chapter 12 will revisit this idea in the context of regression.

Section 8.7

Confidence Intervals

A point estimate communicates our best guess but nothing about uncertainty. A confidence interval answers the uncertainty question by constructing a range of values that is designed to capture the true parameter with a specified probability under repeated sampling. Before stating the definition, a critical conceptual issue must be addressed — one that trips up students and professionals alike.

The Most Important Conceptual Point in This Chapter

The true parameter θ is fixed. It does not vary. What is random is the interval itself — each time we draw a new sample, we compute a new confidence interval. A 95% confidence interval means: the procedure that produces this interval, when applied repeatedly to random samples from the same population, will yield intervals that capture the true parameter 95% of the time in the long run. This is a statement about the procedure, not about any specific computed interval.

Formal Definition — Confidence Interval

Let X₁, …, Xₙ be a random sample and θ an unknown parameter. Two statistics L = L(X₁, …, Xₙ) and U = U(X₁, …, Xₙ) form a 100(1−α)% confidence interval for θ if:

P(L ≤ θ ≤ U) = 1 − α

The value 1 − α is the confidence level. Common choices: 90% (α = 0.10), 95% (α = 0.05), 99% (α = 0.01). Intervals are constructed from pivotal quantities whose distributions do not depend on unknown parameters.

Pivotal QuantityDistributionUsed When
Z = (X̄ − μ)/(σ/√n)N(0,1)σ known, or large n
T = (X̄ − μ)/(S/√n)t with n−1 dfσ unknown, population approximately normal
(p̂ − p)/√(p(1−p)/n)N(0,1) approximatelyLarge samples, proportions
(n−1)S²/σ²χ²(n−1)Variance, normal population
Confidence vs. Width

Higher confidence levels produce wider intervals. To be 99% confident, we must accept a wider range of plausible values. There is a fundamental tradeoff: width measures precision (narrower is more informative), while confidence measures reliability (higher means the procedure rarely fails). We cannot simultaneously maximize both. This tradeoff is an expression of unavoidable uncertainty in statistical inference.

Section 8.8

Margin of Error

Definition — Margin of Error

The margin of error (ME) of a confidence interval is half the width of the interval:

ME = (U − L) / 2

Equivalently, the confidence interval is written as: Estimate ± ME

For a Z-interval for a mean: ME = z_{α/2} · σ/√n

Three factors control ME: (1) the critical value z_{α/2} (confidence level); (2) the population standard deviation σ; (3) the sample size n.

Confidence Levelαα/2zα/2
90%0.100.051.645
95%0.050.0251.960
99%0.010.0052.576

Sample Size Determination

To achieve a desired margin of error ME₀ at confidence level 1 − α, solve ME₀ = z_{α/2} · σ/√n for n:

n = (z_{α/2} · σ / ME₀)² ← always round UP to the next integer
Example 8.8.1
Determining sample size for a desired margin of error
  • ProblemA researcher wants to estimate mean systolic blood pressure with ME = 3 mmHg at 95% confidence. Prior studies suggest σ ≈ 15 mmHg. What sample size is needed?
  • Formulan = (z_{α/2} · σ / ME₀)² = (1.960 × 15 / 3)² = (29.4 / 3)² = (9.8)² = 96.04
  • Round Upn = 97. A sample of at least 97 adults is needed. Never round down when determining sample size.
n = 97 for ME = 3 mmHg at 95% confidence.
Example 8.8.2
Effect of increasing confidence on sample size
  • ProblemRepeat Example 8.8.1 with 99% confidence and the same ME = 3, σ = 15.
  • Computen = (2.576 × 15 / 3)² = (12.88)² = 165.9 → n = 166
  • CompareIncreasing confidence from 95% to 99% raises required n from 97 to 166 — an increase of about 71%. Higher confidence demands more data for the same precision.
n = 166 for 99% confidence — 71% more than the 95% requirement of n = 97.

Section 8.9

Confidence Intervals for a Mean

Case 1: Z-Interval (σ Known)

Derivation of the Z-Interval

When σ is known, the pivotal quantity Z = (X̄ − μ)/(σ/√n) ~ N(0,1). We want P(L ≤ μ ≤ U) = 1 − α. Starting from:

P(−z_{α/2} ≤ (X̄ − μ)/(σ/√n) ≤ z_{α/2}) = 1 − α

Rearranging to isolate μ yields the confidence interval.

∎
Z-interval: x̄ ± z_{α/2} · (σ/√n)
Example 8.9.1
Z-interval for mean rod length
  • ProblemA sample of n = 36 steel rods has x̄ = 10.04 cm. Historical records establish σ = 0.12 cm. Construct a 95% CI for the true mean length μ.
  • Valuesz_{0.025} = 1.960 SE = σ/√n = 0.12/√36 = 0.12/6 = 0.020 ME = 1.960 × 0.020 = 0.0392
  • Interval95% CI: 10.04 ± 0.039 = (10.001, 10.079)
  • InterpretWe are 95% confident that the true mean rod length lies between 10.001 cm and 10.079 cm. If we repeated this procedure many times, about 95% of such intervals would contain the true μ.
95% CI: (10.001, 10.079) cm

Case 2: t-Interval (σ Unknown)

In practice, σ is almost never known. When σ must be estimated by s, the pivot changes to T = (X̄ − μ)/(S/√n) ~ t_{n−1}. The t-distribution has heavier tails than the normal, reflecting the additional uncertainty from estimating σ. As n → ∞, t_{n−1} → N(0,1).

t-interval: x̄ ± t_{n−1, α/2} · (s/√n)
Conditions for the t-Interval

The sample is a simple random sample. Observations are independent. Either the population is approximately normal, or n ≥ 30 (common guideline for CLT approximation). The t-interval is reasonably robust to mild non-normality for moderate and large samples.

Example 8.9.2
t-interval for mean protein content
  • ProblemA nutritionist samples n = 16 servings of a food product. The sample mean is x̄ = 22.4 g and the sample standard deviation is s = 3.1 g. Assume protein content is approximately normally distributed. Construct a 95% CI for μ.
  • Valuesdf = n − 1 = 15 t_{15, 0.025} = 2.131 SE = s/√n = 3.1/√16 = 3.1/4 = 0.775 ME = 2.131 × 0.775 = 1.652
  • Interval95% CI: 22.4 ± 1.652 = (20.748, 22.052) ≈ (20.7, 22.1) g
95% CI: (20.7, 22.1) grams per serving.
Example 8.9.3
Effect of changing the confidence level
  • ProblemRepeat Example 8.9.2 at 99% confidence.
  • Computet_{15, 0.005} = 2.947 ME = 2.947 × 0.775 = 2.284 99% CI: 22.4 ± 2.284 = (20.116, 24.684) g
  • CompareThe 99% interval is 4.57 g wide vs. 3.30 g for the 95% interval. Higher confidence costs us precision.
99% CI: (20.1, 24.7) g — wider than the 95% interval, reflecting greater confidence at the cost of precision.

Section 8.10

Confidence Intervals for a Proportion

Many real-world quantities are proportions: the fraction of voters supporting a candidate, the proportion of patients responding to a treatment, the defect rate in manufacturing. We estimate the population proportion p using the sample proportion p̂ = X/n. By the Central Limit Theorem, for large samples the standardized p̂ is approximately standard normal.

Wald interval: p̂ ± z_{α/2} · √(p̂(1−p̂)/n)
Large-Sample Conditions (Wald Interval)

The normal approximation is reasonable when: n·p̂ ≥ 10 and n·(1−p̂) ≥ 10. If these conditions fail, alternative methods — the Wilson interval or exact binomial intervals — are preferred. Statistical software computes these automatically.

Example 8.10.1
Estimating the proportion who exercise regularly
  • ProblemIn a random sample of 500 adults, 320 report exercising at least three times per week. Construct a 95% CI for the population proportion p.
  • Checkp̂ = 320/500 = 0.640 n·p̂ = 320 ≥ 10 ✓ n·(1−p̂) = 180 ≥ 10 ✓
  • SESE = √(0.640 × 0.360 / 500) = √(0.0004608) ≈ 0.02147
  • Interval95% CI: 0.640 ± 1.960 × 0.02147 = 0.640 ± 0.042 = (0.598, 0.682)
95% CI: (0.598, 0.682). We are 95% confident that between 59.8% and 68.2% of adults exercise at least three times per week.
Example 8.10.2
When the large-sample conditions fail
  • ProblemA quality inspector samples 80 items and finds 4 defects. Construct a 99% CI for the defect proportion p.
  • Checkp̂ = 4/80 = 0.05 n·p̂ = 4 ✗ (less than 10)
  • ConclusionThe large-sample conditions are not met. The Wald interval is not reliable here. An exact binomial confidence interval or the Wilson interval would be appropriate. Do not apply a formula outside its valid conditions.
Cannot use the Wald interval; use the Wilson or exact binomial interval instead.

Conservative Sample Size for Proportions

When planning a study and p is unknown, use the conservative bound p(1 − p) ≤ 1/4 (achieved at p = 0.5):

n = z²_{α/2} / (4 · ME₀²) ← conservative bound, p unknown
Example 8.10.3
Sample size for a political poll
  • ProblemA polling firm wants to estimate a candidate's support within ±3% at 95% confidence. What sample size is required?
  • Computen = (1.960)² / (4 × (0.03)²) = 3.8416 / 0.0036 ≈ 1,067.1 → n = 1,068
  • ContextThis is why polls reporting a ±3% margin at 95% confidence almost always report sample sizes around 1,000–1,100.
n = 1,068 for ME = 0.03 at 95% confidence using the conservative bound.

Many research questions compare two groups: does a new drug reduce blood pressure more than a placebo? Do two teaching methods produce different mean scores? These questions are answered by estimating the difference between two population parameters: μ₁ − μ₂ or p₁ − p₂.

Section 8.12

Confidence Intervals for Variance as an Introduction

Estimating variability is as important as estimating center. A manufacturer needs to certify that a production process is consistent; a financial analyst needs to quantify volatility. Confidence intervals for the population variance σ² use the chi-square distribution.

Key Fact — Chi-Square Pivot for Variance

When sampling from a normal population:

(n−1)S²/σ² ~ χ²(n−1)

The chi-square distribution is not symmetric — it is right-skewed and takes only nonneg­ative values. This means the confidence interval for σ² is not symmetric around S².

95% CI for σ²: ( (n−1)s² / χ²_{n−1, α/2} , (n−1)s² / χ²_{n−1, 1−α/2} ) CI for σ: take square roots of each endpoint
Example 8.12.1
Chi-square interval for population variance
  • ProblemA sample of n = 20 observations from a normal population yields s² = 36. Construct a 95% CI for σ².
  • Valuesdf = n − 1 = 19 χ²_{19, 0.025} = 32.852 (upper 2.5% critical value) χ²_{19, 0.975} = 8.907 (lower 2.5% critical value) (n−1)s² = 19 × 36 = 684
  • Interval95% CI for σ²: (684/32.852 , 684/8.907) = (20.82, 76.79)
  • For σ95% CI for σ: (√20.82, √76.79) = (4.56, 8.76)
95% CI for σ²: (20.82, 76.79). For σ: (4.56, 8.76). Note the interval is not centered at s² = 36 — asymmetry is expected and correct.
Asymmetry Is Normal — Do Not Center at s²

The interval (20.82, 76.79) is not symmetric around s² = 36. The center of the interval is not 36. This is a consequence of the right-skewed chi-square distribution and is expected. Never be tempted to center the interval at s² or treat it as a symmetric ± interval. The chi-square interval is also sensitive to the normality assumption; use it only when normality is reasonable.

Section 8.13

Interpreting Confidence Intervals in Context

After computing a 95% confidence interval, students and professionals alike frequently say: "There is a 95% probability that the true parameter lies in this interval." This statement is wrong in the classical framework — and understanding why is one of the most important lessons in this entire course.

Once the data are collected and the interval is computed, the interval is fixed. The true parameter is fixed. Either it is in the interval or it is not — the probability is either 1 or 0; we just do not know which. The 95% refers to the long-run success rate of the procedure, not the probability for any specific interval.

Correct InterpretationIncorrect Interpretation
"We are 95% confident that μ lies in [L, U]."
(Acceptable shorthand for the long-run procedure statement.)		"There is a 95% probability that μ is in [L, U]."
(Treats a fixed parameter as if it were random.)
"The method that produced this interval captures the true parameter in 95% of applications.""95% of all sample means lie in [L, U]."
(Confuses a parameter interval with a data interval.)
"If we repeated this study many times, about 95% of the intervals would contain the true parameter.""The true mean is most likely near the center of the interval."
(No frequentist basis for this claim.)
Example 8.13.1
Correcting a misinterpreted confidence statement
  • ScenarioA 99% CI for the mean delivery time at a logistics company is (2.3 hours, 4.7 hours). A manager says: "There is a 99% chance our true mean delivery time is between 2.3 and 4.7 hours."
  • ErrorThe true mean delivery time is fixed — it either is or is not in (2.3, 4.7). There is no probability to assign to a fixed quantity lying in a fixed interval after the data are collected.
  • Correct"We used a procedure that, over repeated samples, captures the true mean delivery time in 99% of applications. Based on this sample, our 99% confidence interval is (2.3, 4.7) hours."
The manager's statement is incorrect. Confidence describes the procedure's long-run reliability, not the probability for a specific computed interval.
Statistical vs. Practical Significance

A confidence interval that excludes zero is "statistically significant" — zero is not a plausible value for the parameter. But statistical detectability is not the same as practical relevance. A 95% CI for a drug's effect on blood pressure of (0.1 mmHg, 0.8 mmHg) excludes zero but is clinically meaningless. Always interpret intervals in the context of what differences actually matter.

Section 8.14

Common Estimation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Probability Assigned to a Specific Interval
"There is a 95% probability that μ is in (10.1, 12.3)."

Once the data are collected, the interval is fixed. The parameter is fixed. The 95% refers to the long-run procedure, not the specific computed interval. Correct: "We are 95% confident in this interval" — understood as a statement about the procedure.

Mistake 2 — Interpreting the CI as Containing 95% of Individual Observations
"We are 95% confident that a randomly chosen adult's pulse rate falls in (68, 74)."

A confidence interval for a mean is about the parameter, not about individual observations. An interval for individual measurements is a prediction interval or tolerance interval — a different construction entirely.

Mistake 3 — Thinking Wider Is Always Better
"A 99% CI is always better than a 95% CI."

A wider interval is more likely to contain the parameter, but also less informative. The interval (−∞, ∞) has 100% confidence but says nothing. Width and confidence must be balanced given the goals of the analysis.

Mistake 4 — Wrong Critical Value or Distribution
Using z = 1.960 instead of t_{n−1, 0.025} when σ is unknown; or using t_n instead of t_{n−1}.

Always identify whether σ is known or unknown. Use n − 1 degrees of freedom for one-sample t-intervals. Use z_{α/2} (two-tailed), not z_α (one-tailed), for a two-sided interval.

Mistake 5 — Skipping Condition Checks
Applying the Wald proportion interval when np̂ = 4; applying the t-interval to n = 6 from a heavily skewed population.

Before computing any interval, verify: (1) random sample, (2) independence, (3) distributional conditions. The stated confidence level is not guaranteed when conditions are violated.

Mistake 6 — Ignoring Practical Importance
Reporting a 95% CI of (0.1 mmHg, 0.8 mmHg) for a drug effect as proof that the drug is effective.

Statistical significance (the interval excludes zero) is not the same as practical relevance. A detectable effect can be trivially small. Always interpret intervals in the context of what differences actually matter.

Mistake 7 — Using Point Estimates as If They Were Exact
Reporting x̄ = 47.3 without acknowledging sampling variability.

Always accompany point estimates with a confidence interval or at minimum a standard error. A point estimate alone implies false precision.

Mistake 8 — Expecting High Confidence Without Increasing n
"I want 99.9% confidence — I'll just look up the larger critical value."

Higher confidence with the same sample size produces a wider interval. To maintain precision while increasing confidence, the sample size must increase. These are not independent choices.

Mistake 9 — Rounding Down the Required Sample Size
Computing n = 96.04 and reporting n = 96.

Always round UP when computing required sample size. Rounding down gives a margin of error slightly larger than the desired ME₀ — the opposite of the intended guarantee.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1Estimation bridges the gap between an observable sample and an unobservable population. Point estimation produces a single best guess; interval estimation produces a range with a stated confidence level. Both are necessary for honest communication of uncertainty.
  • Sec 8.2A parameter is a fixed, unknown population characteristic. An estimator is a rule — a function of a random sample — and an estimate is the specific value produced from data. Quality is judged by long-run behavior, not by any single sample.
  • Sec 8.3Point estimates follow the plug-in principle: replace population quantities with sample analogs. The sample mean, proportion, and variance are the standard estimators. A point estimate alone conveys no uncertainty; always pair it with an interval.
  • Sec 8.4Bias is E[T] − θ. The sample mean X̄ is always unbiased for μ. The sample variance S² with denominator n − 1 is unbiased for σ² by Bessel's correction. Unbiasedness is desirable but not the only criterion for estimator quality.
  • Sec 8.5Var(X̄) = σ²/n and SE(X̄) = σ/√n. To halve the standard error, quadruple the sample size. The estimator with lower variance among unbiased competitors is more efficient.
  • Sec 8.6MSE(T) = Var(T) + [Bias(T)]². A biased estimator can have lower MSE than an unbiased one if the variance reduction outweighs the bias penalty. This bias-variance tradeoff underlies regularization and ridge regression.
  • Sec 8.7A 100(1−α)% confidence interval satisfies P(L ≤ θ ≤ U) = 1 − α where L and U are random. Intervals are constructed from pivotal quantities. Higher confidence requires wider intervals — precision and reliability trade off.
  • Sec 8.8Margin of error = z_{α/2} · σ/√n. Three factors determine ME: confidence level (critical value), population variability (σ), and sample size (n). Required n = (z_{α/2}·σ/ME₀)², always rounded up.
  • Sec 8.9The Z-interval uses σ known; the t-interval uses s when σ is unknown with n − 1 degrees of freedom. For large n, t and Z intervals are nearly identical. Check conditions: random sample, independence, approximate normality or large n.
  • Sec 8.10The Wald interval for p is p̂ ± z_{α/2}√(p̂(1−p̂)/n), valid when np̂ ≥ 10 and n(1−p̂) ≥ 10. For unknown p, use conservative n = z²_{α/2}/(4·ME₀²). For small samples, use the Wilson or exact binomial interval.
  • Sec 8.11For two independent means, use Welch's t-interval (unequal variances) or pooled t-interval (equal variances). For two proportions, extend the Wald formula. For paired data, analyze differences d̄ with a one-sample t-interval.
  • Sec 8.12The chi-square interval for σ² is based on (n−1)S²/σ² ~ χ²(n−1). The interval is asymmetric because χ² is right-skewed — do not center at s². This interval requires normality and is sensitive to departures from it.
  • Sec 8.13A confidence interval is a statement about the procedure, not about a specific interval. After computing [L, U], we cannot say "P(μ ∈ [L, U]) = 0.95" — the probability is 0 or 1. "95% confident" is shorthand for the long-run coverage rate of the method.
  • Sec 8.14Nine common errors: confusing the CI with a probability for a fixed interval; treating CI as a data interval; thinking wider is always better; wrong critical value or distribution; skipping condition checks; ignoring practical importance; false precision from point estimates; expecting precision without increasing n; rounding required sample size down.

Key Terms Review

bias
The expected difference between an estimator and the parameter it estimates: Bias(T) = E[T] − θ. An estimator with Bias = 0 is called unbiased.
Bessel's correction
The use of n − 1 in the denominator of S² to correct for systematic underestimation and make S² an unbiased estimator of σ².
chi-square distribution
The distribution of (n−1)S²/σ² when sampling from a normal population; right-skewed, non-negative. Used to construct confidence intervals for variance.
confidence interval
A random interval [L, U] constructed from sample data such that P(L ≤ θ ≤ U) = 1 − α over repeated sampling.
confidence level
The probability 1 − α that the confidence interval procedure captures the true parameter across repeated sampling.
critical value
The value z_{α/2}, t_{ν, α/2}, or χ²_{ν, α/2} that determines the cutoff points of the confidence interval for a given confidence level.
degrees of freedom
A parameter of the t and chi-square distributions; for one-sample t-procedures, equal to n − 1. Affects the width of confidence intervals.
efficiency
A comparison of estimators based on variance. Among unbiased estimators, the one with smaller variance is more efficient.
estimate
The specific numerical value produced when an estimator is applied to a dataset. Contrasted with the estimator (the rule).
estimator
A function T(X₁, …, Xₙ) of a random sample used to estimate a population parameter; a random variable with its own distribution.
margin of error
Half the width of a confidence interval; ME = z_{α/2}·σ/√n for a Z-interval. Represents the maximum likely distance between a point estimate and the true parameter.
MSE
Mean squared error: E[(T − θ)²] = Var(T) + [Bias(T)]². Combines both sources of estimation error into a single summary measure.
parameter
A fixed numerical characteristic of a population, such as μ, σ², or p. Generally unknown and estimated from sample data.
pivotal quantity
A function of the sample and the parameter whose distribution is fully known and does not depend on unknown parameters; the basis of confidence interval construction.
plug-in principle
Estimating a population quantity by substituting its sample analog: replace μ with x̄, σ² with s², p with p̂.
point estimate
A single number serving as the best guess for a population parameter, produced by applying an estimator to sample data.
pooled variance
A weighted average of two sample variances: Sₚ² = [(n₁−1)S₁² + (n₂−1)S₂²]/(n₁+n₂−2); used when equal population variances are assumed.
sample mean (X̄)
The arithmetic average of a sample; an unbiased estimator of μ with Var(X̄) = σ²/n.
sample proportion (p̂)
The fraction of sample observations with a characteristic of interest; an unbiased estimator of p with Var(p̂) = p(1−p)/n.
sample variance (S²)
Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)²/(n−1); an unbiased estimator of σ² due to Bessel's correction.
standard error
The standard deviation of a sampling distribution. SE(X̄) = σ/√n measures how much the sample mean varies across samples.
t-distribution
A symmetric, bell-shaped distribution with heavier tails than the normal; used when σ is unknown. Parameterized by degrees of freedom ν; approaches N(0,1) as ν → ∞.
unbiased estimator
An estimator T such that E[T] = θ for all values of θ. The estimator centers on the true parameter in the long run.
Wald interval
The large-sample confidence interval for a proportion: p̂ ± z_{α/2}√(p̂(1−p̂)/n). Requires np̂ ≥ 10 and n(1−p̂) ≥ 10.
Welch's t-interval
A confidence interval for the difference of two means when population variances are unequal; uses the Welch–Satterthwaite approximation for degrees of freedom.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 9: Hypothesis Testing

Chapter 8 established the machinery of estimation: how to use samples to produce informed, probabilistic claims about population parameters. Chapter 9 carries this work forward into hypothesis testing — a complementary form of inference that asks not "what is the parameter?" but "is the parameter consistent with a specific claim?" The connection between the two chapters is deep and precise. If a 95% CI for μ excludes the value μ₀, then a two-sided test of H₀: μ = μ₀ at significance level α = 0.05 will reject H₀ — and vice versa. This duality between confidence intervals and hypothesis tests is one of the most important structural properties of classical statistical inference. Students who understand the estimation framework — sampling distributions, standard errors, pivotal quantities, and the meaning of confidence — will find hypothesis testing to be a natural extension of the same tools, applied to a different question.

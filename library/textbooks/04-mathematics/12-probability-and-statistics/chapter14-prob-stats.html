Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Statistics is the grammar of science.

— Karl Pearson

Every chapter of this book has built one part of a larger structure. In Chapter 1, probability was introduced as the mathematics of uncertainty. Chapters 2 through 4 developed probability rules, random variables, and the distributions that describe how uncertainty behaves. Chapters 5 and 6 extended those ideas to multiple variables and transformations. Chapter 7 established the limit theorems — the law of large numbers and the central limit theorem — that make statistical inference mathematically justified rather than merely plausible.

With that foundation in place, Chapters 8 through 10 introduced the core procedures of statistical inference: confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, likelihood estimation, and Bayesian reasoning. Chapters 11 and 12 developed regression and ANOVA as tools for modeling relationships and comparing groups. Chapter 13 showed how uncertainty unfolds over time through stochastic processes.

Chapter 14 does not introduce a new technique and then apply it. Instead, it brings everything together. It shows how to move from a real question to a probability model, from a model to an inference procedure, from a procedure to a numerical result, and from a numerical result to a responsible, clearly communicated conclusion. It addresses the decisions a statistician or data scientist must make at every step: which model fits? which method is appropriate? which assumptions must be checked? how should uncertainty be reported? how should results be communicated honestly?

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to look at an unfamiliar data problem, identify what kind of model and method it requires, execute the analysis carefully, check the assumptions behind it, quantify the uncertainty in your conclusions, and communicate your findings in a way that is accurate, honest, and useful.

Section 14.1

The Probability and Statistics Modeling Process

Statistical analysis is not a collection of separate procedures. It is a process — a disciplined cycle of question, model, data, inference, checking, and communication. Every procedure in this book is one step inside that cycle.

Before running any calculation, a statistician must ask: What question am I trying to answer? What data are available, and how were they collected? What model describes the process that generated the data? What method answers the question under that model? What assumptions does the method require, and are those assumptions plausible here? How should I report what I find?

The Six-Stage Modeling Cycle

Stage 1 — Formulate the question. Name the population, the variable(s), and the type of claim.

Stage 2 — Collect and describe the data. Summarize centers, spreads, shapes, outliers, and missing values.

Stage 3 — Choose a probability model. Match the data-generating process to an appropriate distribution or regression.

Stage 4 — Estimate, test, or predict. Extract information from data using point estimates, intervals, or tests.

Stage 5 — Check assumptions. Use residual plots, Q-Q plots, and formal tests to verify model conditions.

Stage 6 — Communicate conclusions. Report findings honestly with uncertainty, context, and appropriate hedging.

The Cycle Is Not Linear

Checking assumptions in Stage 5 sometimes reveals that the model chosen in Stage 3 must be revised. Examining data in Stage 2 often reshapes the question from Stage 1. Real statistical work moves back and forth among stages. Recognizing this is part of statistical maturity.

Example 14.1.1
The modeling cycle in practice — disease spread analysis
  • Stage 1A public health analyst asks: what is the rate at which new infections arrive per day, and does it differ across neighborhoods?
  • Stage 2She collects daily case counts from hospital records, discovers a two-day reporting lag in some neighborhoods, and adjusts accordingly.
  • Stage 3She considers Poisson models for count data but notes that variance exceeds the mean in several neighborhoods, suggesting a negative binomial model may fit better.
  • Stage 4She fits both models, estimates rate parameters, and constructs confidence intervals for each neighborhood.
  • Stage 5She checks residuals, compares models using AIC, and discovers that one neighborhood's data was miscoded — returning to Stage 2.
  • Stage 6She reports estimated rates with confidence intervals, notes the assumptions and their limitations, and avoids claiming which neighborhood is riskier.
This is real statistical practice: iterative, careful, honest.
Section 14.2

Choosing a Probability Model

A probability model is a mathematical description of the process that generated the data. Models are not found in nature — they are chosen by the analyst. The goal is not to find the true model (which is usually unknowable) but to find a model that is useful: one that captures the essential features of the data-generating process, is simple enough to work with, and is accurate enough that conclusions based on it are trustworthy.

George Box's observation that "all models are wrong, but some are useful" applies throughout statistics. The question is never whether a model is perfectly correct. The question is whether the model's approximations are close enough to matter.

Model Fit Must Be Checked

Choosing a candidate model based on subject-matter reasoning is the start, not the end. Use a histogram or density estimate compared against the proposed distribution, and a Q-Q plot comparing empirical to theoretical quantiles. A straight-line Q-Q plot supports the model; systematic curvature signals a mismatch.

Example 14.2.1
Checking a Poisson model for call center data
  • SetupA data scientist models calls per hour at a call center over 200 hours. Sample mean = 4.7; sample variance = 4.9.
  • CheckFor a Poisson distribution, mean and variance are equal. The near-equality of 4.7 and 4.9 supports a Poisson model with λ̂ ≈ 4.7.
  • VerifyA histogram of counts is plotted alongside the Poisson(4.7) PMF — close agreement is observed. A chi-squared goodness-of-fit test returns p = 0.38.
No evidence against the Poisson model at any standard significance level. Proceed with Poisson(λ̂ = 4.7).
Example 14.2.2
Comparing Exponential and Gamma fits for component lifetimes
  • SetupAn engineer records lifetimes of 150 electronic components. The histogram is right-skewed.
  • CheckA Q-Q plot against the Exponential distribution shows systematic deviation in the right tail — the data are heavier-tailed than Exponential.
  • ActionShe fits both Gamma and Weibull models, then uses AIC to compare (see Section 14.5).
One graphical check can send the analysis in a meaningfully different direction. Always look before you compute.
Section 14.3

Choosing an Inference Method

Estimation is appropriate when the question asks: what is the value of an unknown parameter? Use estimation when the goal is to measure — a rate, a proportion, a mean, a regression coefficient — and report a point estimate with a confidence interval.

Hypothesis testing is appropriate when the question asks: is a specific claim consistent with the data? Testing produces a test statistic and a p-value. In most serious analyses, both estimation and testing are reported together: the interval tells you where the parameter plausibly lies; the test tells you whether it excludes a specific null value.

Example 14.3.1
Selecting the correct procedure for a clinical comparison
  • QuestionDoes a new treatment reduce mean recovery time compared to standard care?
  • DesignTwo independent groups, continuous outcome, approximate Normality confirmed by Q-Q plots.
  • MethodTwo-sample t-test for the hypothesis test; 95% confidence interval for the difference in means to report effect size in clinically interpretable units.
Report both the test result and the confidence interval. A p-value alone does not communicate the size of the effect.
Section 14.4

Checking Assumptions

Every inferential procedure rests on assumptions. Confidence intervals for a mean assume either Normality or a large sample. Two-sample t-tests assume independence and approximate Normality. Linear regression assumes linearity, independence of errors, constant error variance, and approximate Normality of errors. When assumptions hold, inference is valid. When assumptions fail badly, nominal confidence levels and p-values may be wrong — sometimes dramatically so.

Graphical Methods

Normal Q-Q plots. Plot sample quantiles against theoretical Normal quantiles. A straight-line pattern supports Normality. Curvature suggests skewness; S-shapes suggest heavy or light tails. Residual plots. In regression, plot residuals against fitted values. A fan shape signals heteroscedasticity; a curved pattern signals non-linearity. Time-order plots. Residuals plotted in time order check independence — trends or oscillations suggest correlated observations.

Formal Tests

TestWhat It ChecksCaution
Shapiro-WilkConsistency with NormalityNon-significant ≠ proves Normal; just fails to reject

Bartlett's testEqual variances across groupsSensitive to non-Normality; use Levene's if data skewed

When Assumptions Fail

Possible responses include: (1) Transformations — the log, square root, or reciprocal of a skewed response can restore approximate Normality; (2) Nonparametric methods — rank-based procedures (Wilcoxon, Kruskal-Wallis) avoid distributional assumptions; (3) Robust methods — designed to maintain validity under departures from assumptions; (4) Model revision — a curved residual plot signals the wrong functional form, not just a variance problem.

Example 14.4.1
Addressing heteroscedasticity with a log transformation
  • ProblemRegression of systolic blood pressure on body weight. Residual plot shows a fan shape — variance increases with fitted values.
  • ActionApply log transformation to the response variable. Refit the regression.
  • CheckResidual plot for the transformed model shows an approximately random scatter — no fan pattern remains.
Proceed with the log-transformed model and interpret all coefficients accordingly.
Section 14.5

Comparing Models

When more than one model is plausible, a principled criterion is needed to choose among them. Model comparison balances two competing demands: fit (how well the model describes the data) and parsimony (how simple the model is). Adding parameters always improves fit, but adding irrelevant parameters introduces noise and reduces generalizability.

Information Criteria

AIC = −2 ln L(θ̂) + 2k

where k is the number of parameters. Lower AIC is better. AIC is preferred when the goal is prediction or approximation.

BIC = −2 ln L(θ̂) + k ln n

BIC penalizes additional parameters more heavily for larger n. BIC is preferred when the goal is selecting the true model from a candidate set, favoring simpler models.

Likelihood Ratio Test — For Nested Models

When one model is a special case of another (nested), the likelihood ratio statistic Λ = −2[ln L(θ̂₀) − ln L(θ̂₁)] has an approximate chi-squared distribution with r degrees of freedom under H₀, where r is the number of parameters fixed in the simpler model. A large Λ favors the more complex model.

Example 14.5.1
AIC and likelihood ratio test for regression model selection
  • SetupTwo regression models (n = 100): Model A has 2 parameters, log-likelihood = −152.3. Model B has 3 parameters, log-likelihood = −150.1.
  • AICAIC_A = −2(−152.3) + 2(2) = 308.6 AIC_B = −2(−150.1) + 2(3) = 306.2Model B has the lower AIC.
  • LR TestΛ = 2(150.1 − 152.3) = 4.4, df = 1. The p-value ≈ 0.036. Both criteria support adding the second predictor.
Model B is preferred by both AIC and the likelihood ratio test at α = 0.05.

Section 14.6

Quantifying Uncertainty

A statistical result without a measure of uncertainty is incomplete. Every quantity estimated from data is subject to sampling variability, and that variability must be reported. The standard error of an estimator is the standard deviation of its sampling distribution — it measures how much the estimator would vary across repeated samples of the same size from the same population.

QuantityStandard Error FormulaNotes
Sample mean X̄SE = σ / √nUse s when σ unknown
Sample proportion p̂SE = √(p(1−p)/n)Estimate with p̂ in practice
Regression slope β̂₁From regression outputDepends on residual variance and variability of X

Confidence Interval Interpretation

A 95% confidence interval does not mean there is a 95% probability that the true parameter lies in the computed interval. The parameter is fixed — not random in the frequentist framework. The correct interpretation: if this procedure were applied to many independent samples, 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true value. The Bayesian credible interval provides the direct probability statement — P(a ≤ θ ≤ b | x) = 0.95.

Example 14.6.1
Confidence vs. prediction intervals in regression
  • SetupRegression: Ŷ = 45 + 8x (exam score on study hours). SE(β̂₁) = 1.4. For a student studying 6 hours, predicted score = 93.
  • CI for mean95% confidence interval for the mean score of all students who study 6 hours: (90.1, 95.9). Narrower — only captures uncertainty about the mean.
  • PI for individual95% prediction interval for a specific student studying 6 hours: (82.3, 103.7). Wider — also captures natural variability among individual students.
Prediction intervals are always wider than confidence intervals for the mean. Both must be reported in applications where individual predictions matter.
Section 14.7

Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

Statistical inference answers questions about the world. But many real situations require not just answers but decisions — actions that must be chosen under uncertainty, with consequences that depend on which possible state of the world is true. Statistical decision theory extends inference into a formal framework that accounts for these consequences.

The Three Components of a Decision Problem

States of nature θ — the unknown true state. In statistics, usually a parameter.

Actions a — the possible decisions the analyst can make.

Loss function L(θ, a) — the cost of taking action a when the true state is θ.

The goal is to choose the action that minimizes expected loss. The Bayes risk of an action is the expected loss under the posterior: r(a | x) = E[L(θ, a) | x].

Loss FunctionOptimal Bayesian Action
Squared error: L(θ, a) = (θ − a)²Posterior mean E[θ | x]
Absolute error: L(θ, a) = |θ − a|Posterior median
0-1 loss (discrete θ)Posterior mode
Example 14.7.1
Drug approval as a decision problem
  • SetupA company must decide: seek approval for a new drug (a₁) or abandon it (a₂). The unknown state is the true effect size δ.
  • Loss structureDrug works, approval sought: low loss. Drug works, abandoned: high loss (missed opportunity, patient harm). Drug fails, approval sought: high loss (cost, patient harm from ineffective treatment). Drug fails, abandoned: low loss.
  • DecisionCompute the Bayes risk of each action using the posterior distribution of δ from the clinical trial. Choose the action with lower expected loss.
This is more complete than simply reporting a p-value and applying a significance threshold. The loss function explicitly accounts for asymmetric consequences.
Section 14.8

Risk, Loss, and Expected Utility as an Introduction

Loss functions measure costs. But real decisions involve both costs and benefits, and a decision-maker's preferences over outcomes may be non-linear. Expected utility theory provides a framework for rational decision-making under uncertainty that accounts for these complexities.

A utility function U(c) assigns a numerical value to each consequence c. Expected utility is EU(a) = E[U(c(a, θ))]. The optimal action maximizes expected utility. The shape of the utility function determines the decision-maker's attitude toward risk.

Risk AttitudeUtility ShapeConditionPreference
Risk-averseConcaveU″(c) < 0Certain outcome over equally valuable gamble
Risk-neutralLinearU″(c) = 0Indifferent between certain outcome and gamble
Risk-seekingConvexU″(c) > 0Gamble over equally valuable certain outcome
Jensen's Inequality and Risk Aversion

For a concave utility function U: U(E[c]) ≥ E[U(c)]. The utility of the expected outcome exceeds the expected utility of the random outcome. A risk-averse decision-maker prefers a certain outcome over a gamble with the same expected value — a fact that underlies insurance markets, option pricing, and much of finance.

Section 14.9

Reproducibility and Statistical Practice

A result is reproducible if independent researchers following the same procedure on the same data reach the same conclusion. A result is replicable if independent researchers applying the same procedure to a new independent sample reach consistent conclusions. Across fields including psychology, medicine, economics, and biology, many published findings have failed to replicate.

This reproducibility crisis has multiple statistical causes: small sample sizes producing low statistical power; selective reporting of results that survive a significance threshold (publication bias); multiple comparisons made without correction; flexible data analysis decisions that inflate the false positive rate; and the conflation of statistical significance with practical importance.

p-Hacking — Definition
Running 20 analyses until one yields p < 0.05, then reporting only that result

Because 5% of true null hypothesis tests will yield p < 0.05 by chance, this practice reliably produces false positives. The Bonferroni correction (use α/m as the threshold when testing m hypotheses) is one remedy. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure controls the false discovery rate more efficiently.

Practices That Support Reproducibility

Pre-registration — specify hypotheses, data collection, and analysis plan before the data are collected. Separates confirmatory from exploratory analysis.

Open data and code — make data and analysis scripts publicly available wherever privacy allows. Enables verification and error correction.

Report all tests — when multiple comparisons are made, say so and apply corrections. Label exploratory findings as hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory.

Meta-analysis — synthesize evidence across multiple independent studies. A replicated effect across studies provides much stronger evidence than any single analysis.

Section 14.10

Statistical Ethics

Statistics is not ethically neutral. Statistical analyses influence decisions that affect people's lives: medical treatments are approved or rejected, policies are adopted or abandoned, loans are granted or denied. The statistician who designs the analysis and reports the results bears responsibility for the quality and honesty of that work.

Selective Reporting and Cherry-Picking
Reporting only the significant results, or only results that support a preferred hypothesis

A result's significance depends on the analysis chosen, and the analysis chosen should be determined by the question — not by the desired answer. This applies equally to presenting a chosen statistic without disclosing other relevant findings.

Confusing Association and Causation
"X is correlated with Y, therefore X causes Y"

Regression and correlation measure association. Causal claims require randomized experimentation or explicit causal modeling with identified assumptions. The language used matters: "X is associated with higher Y" is a statistical claim. "X causes Y" is a causal claim requiring additional justification.

Algorithmic Bias
Using a model trained on historically biased data without auditing for differential error rates

Models used in consequential decisions — credit scoring, hiring, medical diagnosis — can encode and amplify biases present in training data. The ethical question is not only whether a model is accurate overall but whether its errors are distributed fairly across different groups.

Section 14.11

Communicating Results with Tables and Graphs

A technically excellent analysis that is communicated poorly is worth little. Statistical results must be translated into language and visuals that make the findings clear, honest, and actionable for the intended audience.

Principles of Effective Statistical Communication

Report estimates with uncertainty — every point estimate should be accompanied by a standard error or confidence interval.

Use appropriate precision — report enough decimal places to convey meaningful information, but not so many as to suggest false precision.

Distinguish statistical and practical significance — a p-value says whether the effect is detectable, not whether it matters. Always report effect sizes.

Describe the data — sample size, collection method, key characteristics, exclusions. A reader who doesn't know how the data were obtained cannot assess the conclusions.

Be clear about what was tested — when multiple tests were performed, say so, and report any multiplicity correction applied.

Section 14.12

Capstone Data Analysis Project

The capstone project asks you to conduct a complete statistical analysis from formulation to communication. You will work with a real or realistic dataset and apply the modeling process from Section 14.1. The project synthesizes every part of this textbook in seven components.

Sample Capstone Scenario
Smoking, lung function, and covariates — a multiple regression analysis
  • QuestionA public health researcher asks: after controlling for age and BMI, is smoking status associated with FEV₁ (a measure of lung function) in 200 adults?
  • EDAFEV₁ ≈ Normal (mean 3.2 L, SD 0.7 L, slight right skew). Age ranges 25–75; BMI ranges 18–41. Current smokers have lower mean FEV₁ than former or never smokers. FEV₁ decreases with age.
  • ModelMultiple linear regression of FEV₁ on age, BMI, and smoking status (indicator variables for current and former smokers; never smokers = reference).
  • InferenceFit model, report coefficients with standard errors and 95% CIs. Test joint significance of smoking coefficients using F-test.
  • AssumptionsResidual plots show approximate linearity and constant variance. Q-Q plot of residuals ≈ Normal. AIC strongly favors the model with smoking status over the model without it.
  • Uncertainty95% CI for current vs. never smoker difference in FEV₁ (adjusted): (−0.48, −0.12) liters — clinically meaningful.
  • ConclusionCurrent smoking is associated with significantly lower FEV₁ after adjusting for age and BMI (p = 0.003). The cross-sectional design precludes causal conclusions. Longitudinal data would be needed to assess causality.
This scenario illustrates all seven capstone components working together as a unified analysis.

Bridge — Data Science and Machine Learning

Machine learning extends regression to non-linear models (decision trees, neural networks, support vector machines). All such models are fit by optimizing an objective function — often a likelihood or a loss function — and all require the probabilistic reasoning developed in Chapters 3 through 10. Bayesian machine learning treats parameters as random variables updated by data, using Markov chain Monte Carlo (Chapter 13) to sample from complex posteriors.

Bridge — Medicine and Public Health

Randomized controlled trials use random assignment to ensure confounders are balanced between treatment and control groups, making causal inference possible — a direct application of the experimental design principles underlying ANOVA (Chapter 12) and two-sample tests (Chapter 9). Diagnostic testing uses Bayes' theorem (Chapter 2) to update disease probability given test results: the posterior depends critically on the prior (disease prevalence), not just the test's sensitivity and specificity.

Section 14.14

Common Modeling and Interpretation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Treating the Sample as the Population
Sample mean = $54,200, therefore population mean = $54,200 Correct: The sample mean estimates the population mean with uncertainty. Always report a confidence interval alongside the point estimate.
Mistake 2 — Misinterpreting the Confidence Level
"There is a 95% probability that the true mean is in (51.8, 56.6)" Correct: The confidence level refers to the procedure, not the specific interval. The population mean is fixed. 95% of intervals produced by this procedure contain the true mean — we do not know whether this one does.
Mistake 3 — Misinterpreting the p-Value
"p = 0.03 means there is a 3% probability the null hypothesis is true" Correct: The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one computed, assuming H₀ is true. It is not the probability that H₀ is true — that belongs to Bayesian inference.
Mistake 4 — Confusing Statistical and Practical Significance
With n = 10,000, a 0.2-point wellness score difference (p = 0.001) is "highly effective" Correct: Large samples detect small differences with high statistical significance. A 0.2-point difference on a 100-point scale may be statistically detectable but practically meaningless. Always report and discuss effect sizes.
Mistake 5 — Ignoring Assumptions
Applying a two-sample t-test with n = 8 per group and strongly skewed data without checking Correct: With small samples and non-Normal data, the t-test may not be reliable. Check assumptions with Q-Q plots and consider the Wilcoxon rank-sum test as a nonparametric alternative.
Mistake 6 — Confusing Correlation and Causation
Cities with more libraries have higher median incomes → build libraries to increase income Correct: Regression measures association, not causation. The library-income association could reflect a common cause (wealthier cities fund both) or reverse causation. Causal claims require randomization or explicit causal modeling.
Mistake 7 — Multiple Comparisons Without Correction
Testing 30 hypotheses at α = 0.05 and reporting the 3 significant results as confirmed findings Correct: With 30 tests and no true effects, P(at least one false positive) ≈ 1 − (0.95)³⁰ ≈ 0.79. Apply Bonferroni correction (α/m) or Benjamini-Hochberg, and label exploratory findings as hypothesis-generating.
Mistake 8 — Data Dredging
Running many models on the same data until finding a significant result, then reporting only that result Correct: Pre-register the analysis plan before examining the data, or report all models considered alongside the final result. Exploratory flexibility inflates the Type I error rate.
Mistake 9 — Overgeneralizing from the Sample
Undergraduate study finds strategy X improves decisions → strategy X works for everyone Correct: Results apply directly only to the population from which the sample was drawn. Generalizing requires arguing representativeness or plausibility of similar effects in other populations.
Mistake 10 — Ignoring Missing Data
Excluding all observations with any missing value and proceeding as if the remaining data are a random sample Correct: If data are missing not at random (e.g., sicker patients more likely to miss follow-up), the complete-case analysis is biased. Multiple imputation and explicit missing-data models should be considered.

Chapter 14 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 14.1Statistical analysis follows a six-stage cycle: question, data description, model choice, inference, assumption checking, and communication. The cycle is iterative — later stages may require returning to earlier ones.
  • Sec 14.2Models are chosen, not discovered. Discrete data suggest Binomial, Poisson, Geometric, or Hypergeometric distributions; continuous data suggest Normal, Exponential, Gamma, Beta, or Uniform depending on shape and support. Fit is checked graphically (histograms, Q-Q plots) and formally (goodness-of-fit tests).
  • Sec 14.3Estimation is appropriate when the question asks for a parameter's value; hypothesis testing when evaluating a specific claim. The choice among procedures (z, t, F, chi-squared) follows from data type, sample size, and assumptions. In most analyses, both estimation and testing are reported.
  • Sec 14.4Every procedure rests on assumptions that must be checked. Graphical tools (Q-Q plots, residual plots, time-order plots) and formal tests (Shapiro-Wilk, Levene's, chi-squared goodness-of-fit) are the primary methods. When assumptions fail, transformations, nonparametric methods, or model revision may be needed.
  • Sec 14.5AIC = −2 ln L(θ̂) + 2k penalizes log-likelihood by the number of parameters; lower is better. BIC uses k ln n and penalizes complexity more heavily for large n. Likelihood ratio tests compare nested models. Cross-validation directly estimates predictive accuracy.
  • Sec 14.6Standard errors measure estimator precision. Confidence intervals quantify uncertainty about parameters. Prediction intervals quantify uncertainty about future observations and are always wider than confidence intervals for the mean. Bayesian credible intervals provide direct probability statements about parameters conditional on data and prior.
  • Sec 14.7Statistical decision theory formalizes decisions as choices among actions under uncertainty, using a loss function to quantify consequences. The optimal Bayesian action minimizes expected posterior loss. Different loss functions (squared error, absolute error, 0-1) lead to different optimal actions.
  • Sec 14.8Utility functions assign numerical values to consequences. Expected utility theory accounts for non-linear preferences. Risk-averse decision-makers (concave utility) prefer certain outcomes over equally valued gambles. Risk-seeking decision-makers prefer gambles. Jensen's inequality formalizes this for concave utilities.
  • Sec 14.9P-hacking, publication bias, multiple comparisons without correction, and lack of pre-specification inflate false positive rates. Bonferroni correction and Benjamini-Hochberg control multiplicity. Pre-registration, open data, and open code support reproducibility. Meta-analysis synthesizes evidence across studies.
  • Sec 14.10Statistical ethics requires: avoiding selective reporting, distinguishing association from causation, disclosing uncertainty, checking assumptions honestly, and attending to fairness in consequential algorithmic applications. The statistician's responsibility extends to honest communication of limitations.
  • Sec 14.11Good communication pairs estimates with uncertainty, distinguishes statistical and practical significance, describes the data and its collection, and uses honest, well-labeled tables and graphs. Axis scales should reveal rather than obscure variation; error bars should always accompany bar charts.
  • Sec 14.12The capstone project integrates all modeling cycle stages: question formulation, EDA, model selection, inference, assumption checking, uncertainty quantification, and written conclusions readable by a non-statistician.
  • Sec 14.13Probability and statistics underlie data science (likelihood, regression, Bayesian methods), finance (stochastic processes, portfolio theory, risk quantiles), medicine (clinical trials, diagnostic testing, survival analysis), and engineering (reliability, quality control, signal detection).
  • Sec 14.14The ten most important errors are conceptual, not arithmetic: misinterpreting p-values and confidence intervals, confusing significance with importance, ignoring assumptions, overgeneralizing, conflating association with causation, multiple comparisons without correction, data dredging, and ignoring missing data.
  • Sec 14.15This course opens pathways to mathematical statistics, Bayesian computation, machine learning, stochastic processes, time series, econometrics, and experimental design. The probabilistic foundation developed here accompanies every subsequent course in quantitative work.

Key Terms Review

AIC
Akaike Information Criterion: −2 ln L(θ̂) + 2k. Balances fit and parsimony; lower is better; preferred for prediction.
BIC
Bayesian Information Criterion: −2 ln L(θ̂) + k ln n. Stronger parsimony penalty than AIC for n > 7; preferred for model identification.
Bayes risk
The expected loss of an action under the posterior distribution of the parameter. The optimal Bayesian action minimizes Bayes risk.
credible interval
A Bayesian interval containing the true parameter with specified posterior probability: P(a ≤ θ ≤ b | x) = 0.95 for a 95% credible interval.
cross-validation
Resampling method for estimating predictive accuracy by fitting on training data and evaluating on held-out test data. K-fold CV averages over K splits.
effect size
A measure of the magnitude of an effect, independent of sample size. Essential for distinguishing statistical from practical significance.
expected utility
The expected value of a utility function over uncertain consequences: E[U(c(a, θ))]. The optimal action maximizes expected utility.
false discovery rate
The expected proportion of rejected null hypotheses that are false rejections. Controlled by the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure in multiple testing.
goodness-of-fit
A hypothesis test for whether observed data are consistent with a proposed probability model. The chi-squared goodness-of-fit test is the most common version.
heteroscedasticity
Non-constant variance of errors in a regression model. Detected by a fan-shaped residual plot; often addressed by a log transformation of the response.
likelihood ratio test
A hypothesis test comparing nested models using Λ = −2[ln L(θ̂₀) − ln L(θ̂₁)], which has an approximate chi-squared distribution under H₀.
loss function
A function L(θ, a) measuring the cost of taking action a when the true parameter is θ. Different loss functions yield different optimal Bayesian actions.
p-hacking
Running many analyses until one yields a significant result, then reporting only that result. Inflates the Type I error rate; countered by pre-registration.
parsimony
The principle that simpler models are preferred when they explain the data equally well. AIC and BIC operationalize parsimony through complexity penalties.
prediction interval
An interval for a single future observation accounting for uncertainty about the mean and natural individual variability. Always wider than a confidence interval for the mean.
pre-registration
Specifying hypotheses, data collection, and analysis plan before data are collected. Separates confirmatory from exploratory analysis and counters p-hacking.
Q-Q plot
Quantile-quantile plot comparing sample quantiles to theoretical quantiles of a proposed distribution. A straight line supports the distributional assumption.
replicability
The ability of independent researchers applying the same procedure to a new sample to reach consistent results. Distinguished from reproducibility (same data).
reproducibility
The ability of independent researchers following the same procedure on the same data to reach the same result. Supported by open data and open code.
risk aversion
The preference for a certain outcome over a gamble with the same expected value. Corresponds to a concave utility function; U(E[c]) ≥ E[U(c)] by Jensen's inequality.
standard error
The standard deviation of an estimator's sampling distribution. Measures precision. Decreases as sample size increases: SE(X̄) = σ/√n.
utility function
A function U(c) assigning a numerical value to each consequence, representing the decision-maker's preferences. Shape determines risk attitude.

Step 2: Exploratory Data Analysis Checklist

  • Report the sample size.
  • For each quantitative variable: mean, median, SD, min, max, histogram or boxplot.
  • For each categorical variable: frequency table, bar chart.

  • Compute point estimates.
  • Compute standard errors.
  • Construct confidence intervals (at least 95%).

  • Describe what was done if any assumption appears to fail.

Step 7: Conclusion Checklist

  • Answer the original question directly.
  • State the effect size and whether it is practically meaningful.
  • Acknowledge the assumptions and their plausibility.
  • State at least one limitation of the analysis.
  • Propose at least one direction for further investigation.
  • Write the conclusion in language readable by a non-statistician.

Error Analysis

Each problem contains a statistical error. Identify it and explain the correction.

Problem 1
From 500 participants, men earn $4,200 more than women → "The gender pay gap is exactly $4,200"

The sample statistic is an estimate with sampling variability. State the estimate, its standard error, and a confidence interval. The population gap might differ substantially.

Problem 2
p = 0.04 → "There is a 4% probability that the null hypothesis is true"

The p-value is the probability of results at least as extreme as observed, assuming H₀ is true — not the probability H₀ is true. That statement requires Bayesian reasoning with a prior on H₀.

Problem 3
Testing 40 predictors at α = 0.05, reporting the 3 significant ones as "confirmed risk factors"

P(at least one false positive among 40 tests) is very high. Apply Bonferroni or Benjamini-Hochberg correction and label findings as exploratory, requiring independent replication.

Problem 4
Residual plot shows a clear U-shape → "Some minor random variation; the linear model is adequate"

A U-shaped residual pattern is not random — it signals that the linear model misses a curvature in the data. A quadratic term or other functional form should be considered.

Problem 5
CI for regression slope is (0.23, 0.87) → "95% probability the true slope is in this interval"

The frequentist CI refers to the long-run procedure, not the specific interval. This specific interval either contains the true slope or it does not. The Bayesian credible interval provides the direct probability statement.

Problem 6
Selecting the model with highest R² among 10 models fit to the same training data, reporting R² = 0.94 as the model's performance

R² on training data always increases with complexity. Performance must be evaluated on held-out data (cross-validation or a test set). Report cross-validated predictive accuracy instead.

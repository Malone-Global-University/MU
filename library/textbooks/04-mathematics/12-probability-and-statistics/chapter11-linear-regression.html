Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Data rarely comes in isolation. A biologist measuring plant growth wants to know whether sunlight hours predict height. An economist studying income wants to know whether education level predicts earnings. An engineer monitoring a manufacturing process wants to know whether temperature predicts output quality. In each case, the question is the same: how does one quantity relate to another, and how well can we use that relationship to understand or predict?

This chapter develops linear regression — the foundational statistical tool for modeling relationships between two quantitative variables. Linear regression connects the algebraic idea of a line to the statistical idea of uncertainty. When we fit a regression line to data, we are not merely drawing a best-fit line through points. We are building a probability model, making assumptions, estimating parameters, and quantifying how much confidence we should place in our conclusions.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to construct and interpret a linear regression model, assess whether a linear model is appropriate, perform statistical inference on the slope of a regression line, and communicate what a regression analysis does and does not tell you about real-world relationships.

Opening Question

A researcher believes that the number of hours a student studies each week is related to their exam score. She collects data on 20 students. How should she describe this relationship? How confident can she be that the relationship she observes in her sample reflects something real in the population? And if she finds a relationship, can she conclude that studying more causes better scores? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer all three questions rigorously.

Section 11.1

Modeling Relationships with Lines

When we observe two quantitative variables measured on the same subjects, we often ask whether they are related. The simplest model is a linear one. A deterministic line takes the form y = β₀ + β₁x exactly — but real data never falls perfectly on a line. A statistical model accounts for variability by adding a random error term.

The Simple Linear Regression Model

y = β₀ + β₁x + ε

β₀ = population intercept    β₁ = population slope    ε = random error

The fitted (estimated) regression line uses hat notation: ŷ = β̂₀ + β̂₁x

TermSymbolRoleKnown?
Predictor variablexExplanatory / independent variableObserved
Response variableyOutcome / dependent variableObserved
Population slopeβ₁True rate of change in mean y per unit xUnknown
Population interceptβ₀Mean y when x = 0Unknown
Error termεUnexplained random variation in yUnknown
Deterministic vs. Statistical

A deterministic model says y is exactly determined by x. A statistical model says y is influenced by x but also contains unexplained randomness. Statistics lives in that randomness — in the gap between the line and the data.

Section 11.2

Scatter Plots and Association

Before fitting any model, we should look at the data. The scatter plot plots each observation (xi, yi) as a point in the plane, with the predictor on the horizontal axis and the response on the vertical axis.

FeatureWhat to Look ForWhy It Matters
DirectionDoes y increase or decrease as x increases?Determines sign of slope; positive or negative association
FormLinear pattern or curved?A curved pattern means a line is not the right model
StrengthHow tightly do points cluster around the trend?Reflects how well x predicts y
OutliersPoints far from the overall pattern?Outliers can distort the fitted line substantially
Heteroscedasticity

If the spread of points around the trend fans out as x increases — larger variability for larger x — the data are heteroscedastic. Standard regression methods assume constant spread (homoscedasticity). A fan-shaped scatter plot is a warning sign to investigate before proceeding.

Example 11.2.1
Reading a scatter plot
  • ScenarioA researcher plots study hours per week (x) against exam score (y) for 20 students. The plot shows a positive association: students who study more tend to score higher. Points cluster roughly near a line, with noticeable but not extreme spread. No obvious outliers.
  • ConclusionDirection: positive. Form: approximately linear. Strength: moderate to strong. Outliers: none apparent. A linear regression model is a reasonable starting point for these data.
A positive, approximately linear association of moderate-to-strong strength supports proceeding with linear regression.
Section 11.3

Correlation

A scatter plot is visual. We also want a numerical summary. The Pearson correlation coefficient r measures the strength and direction of the linear association between x and y.

Pearson Correlation Coefficient
r = (1/(n−1)) · Σ [(xᵢ − x̄)/sₓ] · [(yᵢ − ȳ)/sᵧ]

Equivalent form: r = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) / √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² · Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²]
Value of rInterpretation
r = 1Perfect positive linear relationship; all points on a line with positive slope
r = −1Perfect negative linear relationship; all points on a line with negative slope
r = 0No linear association (a nonlinear relationship may still exist)
|r| > 0.7Strong linear association (context-dependent)
0.3 ≤ |r| ≤ 0.7Moderate linear association
|r| < 0.3Weak linear association
Key Properties of r

−1 ≤ r ≤ 1 always.

r is unit-free: changing measurement units does not change r.

r is symmetric: the correlation of x with y equals the correlation of y with x.

r measures only linear association. A strong curved relationship may have r near zero.

r Measures Linear Association Only

A scatter plot showing a perfect U-shape (quadratic) can have r ≈ 0, because positive and negative products in the formula cancel. Never report r without also examining the scatter plot. A correlation near zero is not evidence that no relationship exists — only that no linear relationship exists.

Example 11.3.1
Computing and interpreting r
  • DataFive (x, y) pairs: (1, 5), (3, 9), (5, 11), (7, 15), (9, 17). We compute x̄ = 5, ȳ = 11.4.
  • NumeratorΣ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) = (−4)(−6.4) + (−2)(−2.4) + (0)(−0.4) + (2)(3.6) + (4)(5.6) = 25.6 + 4.8 + 0 + 7.2 + 22.4 = 60
  • Denominator√[Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²] = √[16+4+0+4+16] = √40 ≈ 6.32 √[Σ(yᵢ−ȳ)²] = √[40.96+5.76+0.16+12.96+31.36] = √91.2 ≈ 9.55 Denominator ≈ 6.32 × 9.55 ≈ 60.36
  • Resultr ≈ 60 / 60.36 ≈ 0.994
r ≈ 0.994 — a very strong positive linear association between x and y.

Section 11.4

Least Squares Criterion

We have a scatter plot and a correlation. Now we want a line. The method of least squares chooses the line that minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances from the data points to the line.

Residual and Residual Sum of Squares eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ     (residual for observation i)

RSS = Σ eᵢ² = Σ (yᵢ − β̂₀ − β̂₁xᵢ)²     (minimize this over β̂₀ and β̂₁)
Least Squares Estimates
β̂₁ = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) / Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²     (slope)

β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁x̄     (intercept)

Equivalently: β̂₁ = r · (sᵧ / sₓ)
Three Facts to Memorize

(1) The least squares line always passes through the point (x̄, ȳ). (2) The slope equals the correlation times the ratio of standard deviations. (3) The residuals always sum to zero: Σeᵢ = 0.

Example 11.4.1
Computing the least squares line
  • DataStudy hours and exam scores for n = 5 students: (2, 58), (4, 68), (6, 74), (8, 82), (10, 88).
  • Meansx̄ = (2+4+6+8+10)/5 = 6    ȳ = (58+68+74+82+88)/5 = 74
  • NumeratorΣ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) = (−4)(−16)+(−2)(−6)+(0)(0)+(2)(8)+(4)(14) = 64 + 12 + 0 + 16 + 56 = 148
  • DenominatorΣ(xᵢ−x̄)² = 16+4+0+4+16 = 40
  • Slopeβ̂₁ = 148/40 = 3.7
  • Interceptβ̂₀ = 74 − 3.7(6) = 74 − 22.2 = 51.8
Fitted regression line: ŷ = 51.8 + 3.7x
Section 11.5

Deriving Least Squares as an Introduction

The least squares formulas are derived by minimizing Q(β₀, β₁) = Σ(yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)² using partial derivatives. Setting both partial derivatives equal to zero yields the normal equations, whose solution gives the estimates in Section 11.4.

Normal Equations (derived by setting ∂Q/∂β₀ = 0 and ∂Q/∂β₁ = 0) (1) Σyᵢ = nβ₀ + β₁ · Σxᵢ

(2) Σxᵢyᵢ = β₀ · Σxᵢ + β₁ · Σxᵢ²

Solving (1): β₀ = ȳ − β₁x̄   ⟹  line passes through (x̄, ȳ).

Substituting into (2) and simplifying: β̂₁ = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²
Connection to Covariance and Variance

The numerator Σ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) is proportional to the sample covariance sxy. The denominator Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² is proportional to the sample variance sx². Therefore β̂₁ = sxy/sx² = r · (sy/sx). This is an elegant link between regression and correlation.

Matrix Notation Preview (for students with Linear Algebra) Model: y = Xβ + ε    where X is the n×2 design matrix with a column of 1s and a column of x values.

Least squares solution: β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy

This formula generalizes directly to multiple regression in Chapter 12.
Section 11.6

Regression Line Interpretation

Example 11.6.1
Interpreting slope and intercept
  • Lineŷ = 51.8 + 3.7x    (study hours → exam score)
  • Slopeβ̂₁ = 3.7. For each additional hour of study per week, the model predicts an exam score increase of 3.7 points, on average.
  • Interceptβ̂₀ = 51.8. A student who studies zero hours is predicted to score 51.8. Whether this is meaningful depends on whether x = 0 is a realistic value in context.
  • Predictionx = 7: ŷ = 51.8 + 3.7(7) = 51.8 + 25.9 = 77.7
  • Cautionx = 7 is within the observed range (2 to 10 hours), so this prediction is appropriate. Predicting for x = 25 hours would be extrapolation.
Slope = 3.7 points per hour (on average). Intercept = 51.8 (predicted score at zero hours; interpret with caution). Prediction at x = 7: ŷ = 77.7.
Predictions Are Averages, Not Guarantees

The fitted value ŷ is the predicted mean response for all individuals with predictor value x. Any particular individual's actual response will differ from ŷ by the residual e = y − ŷ. The line models the average relationship; individual variation around that average is captured by ε.

Section 11.7

Residuals

Residuals eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ serve two purposes: they measure goodness of fit, and they are the primary tool for checking regression assumptions. The total variability in y decomposes into explained and unexplained parts.

Sum of Squares Decomposition
SST = SSR + RSS

SST = Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²   (total variability in y)
SSR = Σ(ŷᵢ − ȳ)²   (variability explained by regression)
RSS = Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²   (unexplained variability — residuals)

R² = SSR/SST = 1 − RSS/SST     (coefficient of determination; 0 ≤ R² ≤ 1)
Example 11.7.1
Computing and interpreting R²
  • GivenFor the study hours data: SST = 500, RSS = 55.2
  • SolveR² = 1 − 55.2/500 = 1 − 0.1104 = 0.8896
  • InterpretThe regression model explains approximately 88.96% of the variability in exam scores. The remaining 11.04% is unexplained variation attributed to other factors and individual differences.
  • ConnectionFor simple linear regression, R² = r². If r = 0.943, then R² = 0.889. ✓
R² ≈ 0.89: the regression explains about 89% of the variability in exam scores.

Section 11.8

Regression Assumptions

For the least squares estimates to support valid inference, four assumptions about the error term εᵢ must hold. Each can be checked diagnostically.

  • Linearity E[y | x] = β₀ + β₁x. The true relationship between x and y is linear. Check with the scatter plot and residual plot; systematic curvature indicates a violation. If linearity fails, the fitted line describes the wrong relationship and predictions will be systematically biased.
  • Independence ε₁, ε₂, …, εₙ are independent. Knowing the error for one observation gives no information about another. Check by examining how data were collected — random sampling supports independence; time series or clustered data may not. Violation produces incorrect standard errors and p-values.
  • Constant Variance Var(εᵢ) = σ² for all i. Homoscedasticity: the spread of y around the regression line is the same at every value of x. Check with the residual plot for a fan shape. Violation means OLS is no longer the most efficient estimator.
  • Normality εᵢ ~ N(0, σ²). The errors follow a normal distribution. Check with a Q-Q plot of residuals; points should fall approximately on a straight line. Normality supports the t-tests and F-tests used in inference. For large samples, the central limit theorem provides some protection against non-normality.
Section 11.9

Standard Error of the Regression

The population error variance σ² is unknown and must be estimated from the residuals. The standard error of the regression s estimates the typical distance a data point falls from the fitted line.

Standard Error of the Regression and Slope
s² = RSS / (n − 2)     (unbiased estimate of σ²; divide by n−2, not n)

s = √(RSS / (n−2))     (residual standard error; same units as y)

SE(β̂₁) = s / √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²]     (estimated standard error of the slope)
Example 11.9.1
Computing s and SE(β̂₁)
  • Givenn = 5, RSS = 55.2, Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² = 40
  • s²s² = 55.2 / (5 − 2) = 55.2 / 3 = 18.4
  • ss = √18.4 ≈ 4.29 points
  • SE(β̂₁)SE(β̂₁) = 4.29 / √40 = 4.29 / 6.32 ≈ 0.679
s ≈ 4.29 exam score points (typical prediction error). SE(β̂₁) ≈ 0.679.
What Increases Precision?

SE(β̂₁) decreases — giving a more precise slope estimate — when: (1) residual variance s² is smaller (better model fit), (2) the x values are spread over a wider range (larger Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²), or (3) the sample size n is larger. Wider x range and larger n are design choices with direct payoff in estimation quality.

Section 11.10

Inference for the Slope

Under the regression assumptions, the slope estimate β̂₁ follows a t-distribution centered at the true slope β₁. This sampling distribution is the foundation for hypothesis tests and confidence intervals.

t-Distribution for the Slope
(β̂₁ − β₁) / SE(β̂₁) ~ t_{n−2}

Test statistic:   t = β̂₁ / SE(β̂₁)   (for H₀: β₁ = 0)

Confidence interval:   β̂₁ ± t*_{n−2} · SE(β̂₁)
Example 11.10.1
Testing H₀: β₁ = 0
  • HypothesesH₀: β₁ = 0 (no linear association).   Hₐ: β₁ ≠ 0 (a linear association exists).
  • Givenβ̂₁ = 3.7, SE(β̂₁) = 0.679, n = 5, so df = n − 2 = 3.
  • Test Statt = 3.7 / 0.679 ≈ 5.45
  • p-valueWith df = 3, |t| = 5.45 yields p ≈ 0.012. At α = 0.05, we reject H₀.
  • ConclusionThere is statistically significant evidence that study hours are positively linearly associated with exam scores in the population.
t ≈ 5.45, p ≈ 0.012. Reject H₀ at α = 0.05. Evidence supports a linear association.
Example 11.10.2
Confidence interval for β₁
  • Givenβ̂₁ = 3.7, SE(β̂₁) = 0.679, t*₃ ≈ 3.182 (for 95%).
  • Solve3.7 ± 3.182 × 0.679 = 3.7 ± 2.16 = (1.54, 5.86)
  • InterpretWe are 95% confident that the population slope β₁ lies between 1.54 and 5.86 points per study hour. The interval does not contain zero, consistent with rejecting H₀.
95% CI for β₁: (1.54, 5.86). The interval excludes zero, confirming a significant positive association.
Section 11.11

Confidence and Prediction Intervals as an Introduction

At a new value x*, two different questions produce two different intervals. The distinction is one of the most important ideas in regression.

QuestionInterval TypeWidthSources of Uncertainty
What is the mean response for all individuals with x = x*?Confidence interval for mean responseNarrowerUncertainty in β̂₀ and β̂₁ only
What will one new individual's response be at x = x*?Prediction intervalWiderUncertainty in regression line + individual variability (σ²)
Formulas at x*
Confidence interval (mean): ŷ* ± t*_{n−2} · s · √(1/n + (x*−x̄)²/Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²)

Prediction interval (new obs.): ŷ* ± t*_{n−2} · s · √(1 + 1/n + (x*−x̄)²/Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²)

Both intervals are widest when x* is far from x̄. The extra "1" under the radical makes prediction intervals always wider.
Example 11.11.1
Computing confidence and prediction intervals at x* = 7
  • Givenŷ* = 77.7, n = 5, x̄ = 6, Σ(xᵢ−x̄)² = 40, s = 4.29, t*₃ = 3.182.
  • CI (mean)77.7 ± 3.182 × 4.29 × √(1/5 + 1/40) = 77.7 ± 3.182 × 4.29 × 0.474 ≈ 77.7 ± 6.5 → (71.2, 84.2)
  • PI (new)77.7 ± 3.182 × 4.29 × √(1 + 1/5 + 1/40) = 77.7 ± 3.182 × 4.29 × 1.107 ≈ 77.7 ± 15.1 → (62.6, 92.8)
  • CompareThe prediction interval (width ≈ 30 points) is far wider than the confidence interval (width ≈ 13 points), reflecting the additional uncertainty of predicting a single individual.
95% CI for mean score at x = 7: (71.2, 84.2). 95% PI for one student: (62.6, 92.8).
Section 11.12

Correlation Is Not Causation

This is one of the most important principles in all of statistics. A significant regression or a large correlation coefficient does not imply that x causes y. Several mechanisms produce association without causation.

MechanismDescriptionExample
Confounding variableA third variable z is associated with both x and y, inducing a spurious association between them.Cities with more firefighters have larger fires — city size confounds both.
Reverse causationy causes x, not x causes y (or they cause each other).Depression and low self-esteem are correlated; the causal direction is unclear.
Spurious correlationTwo unrelated variables happen to be correlated by chance in a particular data set.Mozzarella consumption correlates with civil engineering doctorates awarded.
Selection biasData collection induces a correlation not present in the underlying population.Studying only hospitalized patients can reverse true population associations.
What Causation Requires

The gold standard for causal inference is a randomized controlled experiment. Random assignment breaks the link between x and confounders, so differences in y between groups can be attributed to x. When experiments are infeasible, observational studies use careful design and statistical adjustment — but these approaches rest on assumptions that data alone cannot verify.

Regression as description: A fitted line describes an association in the data and may be useful for prediction. It does not, by itself, explain why the association exists or what would happen if we intervened on x.

Section 11.13

Common Regression Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Ignoring the scatter plot
Fitting a line without examining the data first

A curved relationship, outlier, or cluster structure can make the fitted line meaningless. Always plot before fitting. The scatter plot is not optional.

Mistake 2 — Extrapolation
Predicting ŷ for x far outside the observed range

A linear model fit to crop yield vs. fertilizer cannot predict negative yields. Most relationships change character outside the observed range. Confine predictions to the range of the data or explicitly state the extrapolation assumption.

Mistake 3 — Misreading R²
"R² = 0.92, so the linear model is clearly correct."

A high R² means the model accounts for much variance in the sample — not that the model is correct. A curved relationship can produce high R² over a restricted range while violating linearity. Always examine the residual plot alongside R².

Mistake 4 — Confusing non-significance with no relationship
"p = 0.23 > 0.05, so there is no relationship between x and y."

A non-significant result means the data are consistent with β₁ = 0, not that β₁ is zero. The test may lack power due to small n. Report the estimate and confidence interval, not just the p-value.

Mistake 5 — Assuming causation from regression
"More education causes higher income because β̂₁ = 3.2 with p < 0.01."

A significant regression describes an association. Causation requires experimental evidence or explicit causal assumptions. Use associative language: "is associated with," not "causes."

Mistake 6 — Ignoring influential observations
Fitting a line without checking for high-leverage or high-residual points

A single observation with an extreme x-value (high leverage) can substantially change the slope. Examine residual plots and leverage statistics. Investigate unusual points before reporting results.

Mistake 7 — Confusing the two regression lines
Using the regression of x on y to predict y from x

The regression of y on x minimizes vertical residuals; the regression of x on y minimizes horizontal residuals. They give different lines. Use the regression of y on x when predicting y, and be explicit about which variable is the response.

Mistake 8 — Statistical vs. practical significance
"p < 0.01 means this predictor is practically important."

With large n, even tiny slopes produce small p-values. A statistically significant slope may correspond to a trivially small effect. Always interpret the magnitude of β̂₁ in context alongside the p-value.

Section 11.14

Preparing for Multiple Regression and ANOVA

Chapter 11 developed simple linear regression with one predictor. Chapter 12 extends the framework in two important directions. Everything built in this chapter carries forward directly.

Multiple regression adds more predictors: y = β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + ⋯ + βpxp + ε. The least squares criterion is the same; the solution requires matrix algebra (β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy). Crucially, the interpretation of each slope coefficient changes: β̂j is the predicted change in y per unit increase in xj holding all other predictors fixed. This adjusted interpretation is how regression partially controls for confounders in observational data.

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) compares means across groups. One-way ANOVA with k groups is equivalent to a multiple regression with k − 1 indicator (dummy) variables. The F-test in ANOVA is the same F-test as in regression. Both are unified under the general linear model.

What to Carry Forward

The four regression assumptions carry over unchanged. The decomposition SST = SSR + RSS generalizes; the F-test is built on the same sums of squares. Residual analysis remains the primary diagnostic tool. And the principle that correlation does not imply causation applies with equal force in multiple regression — adding variables does not confer causation.

The move from one predictor to many is a move toward greater realism. Real data involve complex, overlapping influences. Chapter 12 is the tool that begins to address that complexity.

  • Sec 11.3The Pearson correlation coefficient r measures the strength and direction of a linear association; −1 ≤ r ≤ 1. It is unit-free and symmetric. It measures only linear association — a strong curved relationship can have r near zero.
  • Sec 11.4The least squares criterion minimizes RSS = Σeᵢ². The formulas are β̂₁ = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ)/Σ(xᵢ−x̄)² and β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁x̄. The line always passes through (x̄, ȳ) and satisfies β̂₁ = r · sᵧ/sₓ.
  • Sec 11.5Least squares estimates are derived by setting partial derivatives of Q(β₀, β₁) to zero. This gives the normal equations, whose solution yields the slope and intercept. The matrix formula β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy generalizes to multiple regression.
  • Sec 11.6The slope β̂₁ is the predicted change in y per one-unit increase in x, on average. The intercept β̂₀ is the predicted y when x = 0, which may not be meaningful. Predictions should remain within the observed range of x.
  • Sec 11.7Residuals eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ sum to zero for OLS. The decomposition SST = SSR + RSS gives R² = SSR/SST, the proportion of variability in y explained by the regression. Residual plots reveal assumption violations that R² alone cannot detect.
  • Sec 11.8The four regression assumptions are linearity, independence, constant variance (homoscedasticity), and normality of errors. Violations have specific consequences: failed linearity biases predictions; failed independence corrupts inference; failed homoscedasticity reduces efficiency; failed normality affects small-sample tests.
  • Sec 11.9The residual standard error s = √(RSS/(n−2)) estimates σ, the standard deviation of errors. The standard error of the slope SE(β̂₁) = s/√Σ(xᵢ−x̄)² measures slope estimation precision. It decreases with better fit, wider x range, and larger n.
  • Sec 11.10Under regression assumptions, (β̂₁−β₁)/SE(β̂₁) ~ t_{n−2}. The hypothesis test H₀: β₁ = 0 uses t = β̂₁/SE(β̂₁). A confidence interval is β̂₁ ± t*_{n−2}·SE(β̂₁). A non-significant result means the data are consistent with β₁ = 0, not that β₁ is zero.
  • Sec 11.11At x*, a confidence interval for the mean response quantifies uncertainty about E[y|x*]. A prediction interval for a new observation also incorporates individual variability (σ²) and is always wider. Both intervals are widest when x* is far from x̄.
  • Sec 11.12Correlation does not imply causation. Confounders, reverse causation, spurious correlation, and selection bias can produce associations without causal relationships. Causation requires randomized experiments or explicit causal assumptions. Regression describes associations; it does not establish causes.
  • Sec 11.13Eight recurring errors: ignoring the scatter plot, extrapolating, misreading R², treating the line as fixed, confusing non-significance with no relationship, assuming causation, ignoring influential observations, and confusing the two regression lines.
  • Sec 11.14Multiple regression adds predictors; each slope is interpreted holding others fixed. ANOVA compares group means using the same F-test. Both are faces of the general linear model. All Chapter 11 ideas — assumptions, decomposition, residual analysis, causation caveats — carry forward to Chapter 12.

Key Terms Review

simple linear regression
A statistical model y = β₀ + β₁x + ε describing a linear relationship between one predictor and one response variable.
predictor variable (x)
The explanatory or independent variable; placed on the horizontal axis of a scatter plot.
response variable (y)
The outcome or dependent variable; placed on the vertical axis of a scatter plot.
correlation coefficient (r)
The Pearson r: a unit-free measure of linear association strength and direction; −1 ≤ r ≤ 1.
least squares criterion
The principle of choosing the regression line to minimize the sum of squared residuals (RSS).
fitted value (ŷᵢ)
The value β̂₀ + β̂₁xᵢ predicted by the regression line at xᵢ.
residual (eᵢ)
The difference eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ between an observed value and its fitted value.
normal equations
The two linear equations obtained by setting ∂RSS/∂β₀ = 0 and ∂RSS/∂β₁ = 0; their solution gives the OLS estimates.
R² (coefficient of determination)
SSR/SST; the proportion of variability in y explained by the regression. For simple regression, R² = r².
residual plot
A scatter plot of residuals against fitted values (or x); used to check the regression assumptions.
homoscedasticity
The assumption that all errors have the same variance: Var(εᵢ) = σ² for all i.
heteroscedasticity
A violation of constant variance; spread of residuals changes with x or ŷ.
standard error of regression (s)
√(RSS/(n−2)); estimates σ, the standard deviation of errors; measured in units of y.
SE(β̂₁)
s/√Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²; estimated standard error of the slope; measures precision of the slope estimate.
confidence interval (mean response)
An interval at x* capturing where the population mean response E[y|x*] lies, with stated probability.
prediction interval
An interval at x* capturing where a single new observation y* will fall; always wider than the CI for mean response.
extrapolation
Using the fitted line to predict y at x-values outside the observed range; assumes the linear relationship extends.
confounding variable
A third variable associated with both x and y that can produce a spurious association between them.
high-leverage point
An observation with an x-value far from x̄ that has the potential to strongly influence the fitted line.
OLS (ordinary least squares)
The standard estimation method that minimizes the sum of squared residuals.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 12: Multiple Regression and Analysis of Variance

Chapter 11 gave you the foundational tools for modeling a relationship between two quantitative variables. Chapter 12 extends the framework in two directions. Multiple regression adds more predictors simultaneously — each slope coefficient is interpreted holding the others fixed, which is how regression begins to control for confounders. Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) addresses group comparisons — testing whether the mean response differs across several groups using the same F-test structure you saw here. Both are faces of the general linear model, unified by least squares, the normal equations, the SST = SSR + RSS decomposition, residual analysis, and the assumption framework of Chapter 11. Everything you have learned here carries forward directly.

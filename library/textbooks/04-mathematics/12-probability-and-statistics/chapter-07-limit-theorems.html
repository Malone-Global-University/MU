Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"Probability is not a mere computation of odds on the dice or more complicated variants; it is the acceptance of the lack of a sharp boundary between knowledge and ignorance."

— Nassim Nicholas Taleb

Every statistical method in this textbook rests on a foundation of probability results called limit theorems. These theorems describe what happens when chance experiments are repeated many times, when samples are drawn from populations, and when random quantities are averaged across many observations. Without them, statistics would be a collection of numerical recipes with no mathematical justification. With them, statistical procedures become logical consequences of probability theory.

Consider what happens when you flip a fair coin repeatedly. After ten flips, the proportion of heads might be anywhere from 0 to 1. But after a thousand flips, the proportion is almost certainly close to 0.5. After a million flips, it is extremely close to 0.5. This is not an accident — it is the Law of Large Numbers at work. Or consider what happens when you take the average of many independent measurements, each subject to random error. Even if the individual errors have unusual shapes or heavy tails, the distribution of their average gradually begins to look like a bell curve. This is the Central Limit Theorem, arguably the most important single result in all of probability and statistics.

This chapter is the pivot point of the textbook. The probability of Parts I and II becomes the statistical inference of Parts III, IV, and V. The goal is genuine mathematical understanding: why statistical inference works, and under what conditions we can trust it.

Opening Question

A pharmaceutical company tests a new drug on a sample of 200 patients and finds that 68% improve. Should they conclude that roughly 68% of all patients would improve? And how confident should they be? This is a mathematical question about the relationship between a sample and the population it came from. By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to answer it precisely.

Section 7.1

Why Limit Theorems Matter

Suppose a pharmaceutical company tests a new drug on 200 patients and finds that 68% improve. Should they conclude that roughly 68% of all patients would improve? And how confident should they be in that conclusion? This is not merely a question of intuition. It is a mathematical question about the relationship between a sample and the population from which it came.

Here is the central difficulty: every time you draw a sample, you get different data, and therefore different statistics. The variability of a statistic from sample to sample is not a flaw — it is an intrinsic feature of random sampling. The question is how to characterize that variability precisely. Limit theorems provide exactly that characterization.

Two Central ResultsWhat It SaysWhy It Matters
Law of Large NumbersSample means converge to the population mean as n growsJustifies using sample averages to estimate population parameters
Central Limit TheoremSampling distributions of means approach normal shapeJustifies using normal-distribution tools for inference in almost any setting
The Role of Sample Size

Larger samples produce statistics that vary less from sample to sample, are closer to the true population values, and have sampling distributions that are more normal in shape. This is not a vague principle — it is a precise mathematical relationship, and understanding it quantitatively is essential for designing studies and interpreting data responsibly.

Section 7.2

Repeated Sampling

To understand sampling distributions, think carefully about what "repeated sampling" means. Suppose a population has mean μ and standard deviation σ. Draw a random sample of n individuals, compute the sample mean X̄, and record it. Then draw a new sample of n, compute a new X̄, and repeat hundreds of times. Each repetition produces one value of the sample mean. After many repetitions, we have a collection of sample means — their distribution is the sampling distribution of X̄.

In practice, we rarely actually repeat a study many times. But we reason about what would happen if we did, and this reasoning is what justifies statistical inference.

Key Insight

Before data are collected, a sample statistic is a random variable. Its value depends on which individuals happen to be selected, and that selection involves chance. A sample mean X̄ is a random variable. A sample proportion p̂ is a random variable. Each has a probability distribution — its sampling distribution — that describes its variability across repeated samples.

Section 7.3

Sample Mean as a Random Variable

Let X₁, X₂, …, Xₙ be a random sample of size n from a population with mean μ and variance σ². The phrase "random sample" means the observations are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) — each drawn from the same distribution, with no dependence between them.

X̄ = (1/n) Σᵢ Xᵢ = (X₁ + X₂ + ⋯ + Xₙ) / n
Fundamental Results for the Sample Mean

E[X̄] = μ   —  The expected value of the sample mean equals the population mean. On average, across all possible samples of size n, the sample mean correctly targets μ. This makes X̄ an unbiased estimator of μ.

Var(X̄) = σ²/n   —  The variance of the sample mean is the population variance divided by n. The standard deviation of X̄ is σ/√n, called the standard error of the mean.

The variance result captures mathematically the key intuition: larger samples produce less variable sample means. Specifically, doubling the sample size reduces the standard deviation of X̄ by a factor of √2 ≈ 1.41. To halve the standard error, you need four times the data. This square-root relationship is pervasive in statistics — it reflects the fact that random errors tend to cancel when averaged, but only at a 1/√n rate.

The Normal Population Case

If the population is normally distributed, Xᵢ ~ N(μ, σ²), then X̄ is exactly normally distributed: X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n) for every sample size n. For non-normal populations, the CLT (Section 7.8) gives an approximation that holds for large n.

Example 7.1
Mean and standard error of the sample mean
  • ProblemExam scores have μ = 72 and σ = 15. A random sample of n = 25 students is selected. Find E[X̄] and SE(X̄).
  • Solve E[X̄] = μ = 72 SE(X̄) = σ/√n = 15/√25 = 15/5 = 3
  • InterpretThe sample mean is expected to be 72, with a typical variation of about 3 points from sample to sample.
Answer: E[X̄] = 72, SE(X̄) = 3.
Example 7.2
Finding the required sample size
  • ProblemFor the same population (σ = 15), what sample size n reduces SE(X̄) to 1?
  • Solve 15/√n = 1 ⟹ √n = 15 ⟹ n = 225
  • InterpretA sample of 225 reduces the standard deviation of the sample mean to 1 point. Notice: to reduce SE from 3 to 1 (a factor of 3), the sample size must increase by a factor of 9.
Answer: n = 225.
Section 7.4

Sample Proportion as a Random Variable

Many statistical applications involve proportions: the fraction of voters favoring a candidate, the rate of defective items in production, the prevalence of disease in a population. Suppose the true population proportion with some characteristic is p. We draw a sample of n individuals and let X be the count with the characteristic, so X ~ Binomial(n, p). The sample proportion is p̂ = X/n.

Fundamental Results for the Sample Proportion

E[p̂] = p   —  The sample proportion is an unbiased estimator of p.

Var(p̂) = p(1−p)/n   —  Standard deviation: SD(p̂) = √(p(1−p)/n).

Connection to X̄: p̂ is a sample mean of Bernoulli(p) indicator variables. All theory for X̄ applies directly.

Example 7.3
Mean and standard deviation of the sample proportion
  • ProblemA population has true proportion p = 0.40. A random sample of n = 100 is drawn. Find E[p̂] and SE(p̂).
  • Solve E[p̂] = 0.40 SE(p̂) = √(0.40 × 0.60 / 100) = √(0.0024) ≈ 0.049
  • InterpretThe sample proportion will typically be within about 0.049 of the true proportion 0.40.
Answer: E[p̂] = 0.40, SE(p̂) ≈ 0.049.
Section 7.5

Standard Error

The standard error of a statistic is the standard deviation of its sampling distribution. It measures how much the statistic varies from sample to sample.

Standard Error of the Mean
SE(X̄) = σ / √n
Standard Error of the Proportion
SE(p̂) = √(p(1−p) / n)
Estimated SE of the Mean
SÊ(X̄) = S / √n
Estimated SE of the Proportion
SÊ(p̂) = √(p̂(1−p̂) / n)
Standard Deviation vs. Standard Error

The population standard deviation σ measures variability among individual observations. The standard error σ/√n measures variability among sample means — the typical gap between a sample mean and the true population mean. These are very different quantities. A population with σ = 20 has SE(X̄) = 20/√100 = 2 for n = 100. Confusing them leads to wildly incorrect probability calculations.

Section 7.6

Convergence in Probability: An Introduction

The Law of Large Numbers is a statement about what happens as n → ∞. To state it precisely, we need a notion of what it means for a sequence of random variables to "converge." Let Y₁, Y₂, Y₃, … be a sequence of random variables — for example, Yₙ = X̄ₙ, the sample mean based on n observations.

Definition
Convergence in Probability

A sequence of random variables Yₙ converges in probability to a constant c, written Yₙ →ᵖ c, if for every ε > 0:

lim(n → ∞) P(|Yₙ − c| > ε) = 0

In words: the probability that Yₙ differs from c by more than any fixed amount ε goes to zero as n grows. Occasional large deviations are still possible — they just become increasingly rare.

Chebyshev's Inequality

For any random variable Y with mean μ_Y and variance σ²_Y, and any ε > 0:

P(|Y − μ_Y| ≥ ε) ≤ σ²_Y / ε²

This requires only finite mean and variance — no assumption about the shape of the distribution. Applying it to X̄ₙ with σ²_Y = σ²/n:

P(|X̄ₙ − μ| ≥ ε) ≤ σ²/(nε²) → 0 as n → ∞

This is precisely convergence in probability. The sample mean converges in probability to μ. This is the Law of Large Numbers.

Section 7.7

Law of Large Numbers

Theorem
Weak Law of Large Numbers (WLLN)

Let X₁, X₂, … be i.i.d. random variables with mean E[Xᵢ] = μ and finite variance Var(Xᵢ) = σ² < ∞. Then the sample mean converges in probability to μ:

X̄ₙ →ᵖ μ as n → ∞

That is, for every ε > 0:

P(|X̄ₙ − μ| > ε) → 0 as n → ∞

Proof sketch: Follows immediately from Chebyshev's Inequality: P(|X̄ₙ − μ| > ε) ≤ σ²/(nε²) → 0. □

The law also applies to sample proportions: if Xᵢ ~ Bernoulli(p), then p̂ₙ = (1/n)∑Xᵢ →ᵖ p. The sample proportion converges to the true proportion. This is the mathematical foundation for using relative frequency to estimate probability.

What the LLN Does NOT Say

The LLN says that sample means converge to μ in the long run. It does not say that after a run of outcomes in one direction, future outcomes will compensate. A fair coin landing heads 8 times in a row does not make the next flip more likely to be tails — each flip is independent. This error is the Gambler's Fallacy. The early flips become a smaller fraction of a growing total, not targeted deductions from future outcomes.

Example 7.5
LLN for a fair die — applying Chebyshev
  • SetupA fair die is rolled n times. X̄ₙ = sample mean of outcomes. The LLN says X̄ₙ →ᵖ 3.5. For a concrete bound, Var(X) = 35/12 ≈ 2.917.
  • Chebyshev P(|X̄ₙ − 3.5| > 0.1) ≤ 2.917 / (n × 0.01) = 291.7/n
  • InterpretWith n = 3000, this bound is less than 0.1. The sample mean will be within 0.1 of 3.5 with probability at least 0.9.
The sample mean converges to 3.5. With n = 3000, it is within 0.1 of 3.5 with probability ≥ 0.9.

The LLN in Applications

The LLN is the mathematical justification for insurance pricing (expected claims converge to predicted values across large policyholder pools), casino economics (house edges manifest reliably over millions of plays), survey accuracy (opinion polls converge to true population proportions), Monte Carlo simulation (numerical integrals approximated by averaging random evaluations), and machine learning (empirical risk converges to expected population risk).

Section 7.8

Central Limit Theorem

The Law of Large Numbers tells us where the sample mean converges. But it says nothing about the shape of the sampling distribution for finite n. For inference, we need not just the center but the shape — so we can compute probabilities about where the sample mean falls. The Central Limit Theorem answers this with a remarkable universality result.

Theorem
Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Let X₁, X₂, … be i.i.d. random variables with mean μ and finite variance σ² > 0. Define the standardized sample mean:

Zₙ = (X̄ₙ − μ) / (σ/√n)

Then Zₙ converges in distribution to a standard normal random variable:

Zₙ →ᵈ Z ~ N(0, 1)

Equivalently, for large n, X̄ₙ is approximately normally distributed:

X̄ₙ ≈ N(μ, σ²/n)

Why the CLT Is Remarkable

FeatureWhat It Means
UniversalThe population can be anything — skewed, bimodal, discrete, heavy-tailed — as long as it has finite mean and variance. The shape of X̄ₙ still approaches normal.
PreciseThe convergence is in distribution: the CDF of Zₙ converges to the standard normal CDF at every point, justifying normal probability calculations.
PracticalFor moderate n (often n ≥ 30, sometimes less for symmetric populations), the approximation is good enough for real applications.
ExplanatoryMany real measurements are sums of many small independent influences. By the CLT, such quantities tend to be approximately normal — explaining the normal distribution's prevalence in nature.

A Heuristic via Moment Generating Functions

Let Yᵢ = (Xᵢ − μ)/σ so that E[Yᵢ] = 0 and Var(Yᵢ) = 1. Then Zₙ = (1/√n)∑Yᵢ. Expanding the MGF of Y as a Taylor series around 0:

M_Y(s) = 1 + s²/2 + O(s³) (since E[Y] = 0, E[Y²] = 1)

The MGF of Zₙ is [M_Y(t/√n)]ⁿ. Substituting and taking n → ∞:

[1 + t²/(2n) + O(n⁻³/²)]ⁿ → e^(t²/2)

The limit e^(t²/2) is the MGF of the standard normal distribution N(0, 1). Since MGFs characterize distributions, this shows Zₙ converges in distribution to N(0, 1). □

How Large Does n Need to Be?

Population ShapeTypical Guideline
Symmetric, unimodaln ≥ 10 to 15 often sufficient
Moderately skewedn ≥ 30 is a common threshold
Highly skewed or heavy-tailedn ≥ 100 or more may be needed
Discrete (Bernoulli/binomial)np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10
Example 7.6
Applying the CLT to a non-normal population
  • ProblemLight bulb lifetimes follow an exponential distribution with μ = σ = 1000 hours. A shipment of n = 64 bulbs is received. Find: (a) P(X̄ > 1100), (b) P(total lifetime > 68,000 hours).
  • SetupBy the CLT: X̄ ≈ N(1000, 1000²/64) = N(1000, 15625), so SD(X̄) = 125.
  • Part (a) P(X̄ > 1100) ≈ P(Z > (1100 − 1000)/125) = P(Z > 0.8) = 1 − Φ(0.8) ≈ 0.2119
  • Part (b) Total > 68000 ⟺ X̄ > 68000/64 = 1062.5 P(X̄ > 1062.5) ≈ P(Z > 0.5) ≈ 0.3085

Section 7.9

Normal Approximation to the Binomial

Since p̂ is the mean of Bernoulli random variables, the CLT applies directly. For large n, the count X ~ Binomial(n, p) is approximately normal:

X ≈ N(np, np(1−p)) when np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10
Continuity Correction

Because the binomial is discrete and the normal is continuous, adding a continuity correction improves accuracy. The key adjustments:

P(X ≤ k) → use P(X_normal ≤ k + 0.5)

P(X ≥ k) → use P(X_normal ≥ k − 0.5)

P(X = k) → use P(k − 0.5 ≤ X_normal ≤ k + 0.5)

Example 7.7
Normal approximation to the binomial with continuity correction
  • ProblemA 100-question multiple-choice test has 4 choices per question (p = 0.25). A student guesses all answers. Find P(X > 30).
  • Checknp = 25 ≥ 10, n(1−p) = 75 ≥ 10. ✓ Use: μ = 25, σ = √(100 × 0.25 × 0.75) ≈ 4.33.
  • Without CC P(X > 30) ≈ P(Z > (30 − 25)/4.33) = P(Z > 1.155) ≈ 0.124
  • With CC P(X > 30) = P(X ≥ 31) ≈ P(Z > (30.5 − 25)/4.33) = P(Z > 1.270) ≈ 0.102
  • CompareThe exact binomial probability is approximately 0.108. The continuity-corrected approximation (0.102) is more accurate than the uncorrected version (0.124).
Without CC: ≈ 0.124. With CC: ≈ 0.102. Exact: ≈ 0.108.

Section 7.10

Sampling Distribution of the Mean

Complete Description

For any population with mean μ and standard deviation σ:

E[X̄] = μ and SE(X̄) = σ/√n (exact, for all distributions and all n)

If the population is normal: X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n) (exact, for all n)

If the population is not normal: X̄ ≈ N(μ, σ²/n) (CLT approximation, for large n)

Standardized form: Z = (X̄ − μ) / (σ/√n) ≈ N(0, 1)

Example 7.8
Probabilities from the sampling distribution of the mean
  • ProblemCustomer service times have μ = 4.5 min, σ = 1.2 min. A random sample of n = 36 customers is observed. Find: (a) the sampling distribution, (b) P(4.2 < X̄ < 4.8), (c) P(X̄ > 5.0).
  • Part (a)By the CLT: X̄ ≈ N(4.5, 1.44/36) = N(4.5, 0.04). SE(X̄) = 1.2/6 = 0.2.
  • Part (b) P(4.2 < X̄ < 4.8) = P((4.2−4.5)/0.2 < Z < (4.8−4.5)/0.2) = P(−1.5 < Z < 1.5) = Φ(1.5) − Φ(−1.5) = 0.9332 − 0.0668 = 0.8664
  • Part (c) P(X̄ > 5.0) = P(Z > (5.0−4.5)/0.2) = P(Z > 2.5) ≈ 0.0062
Answers: (a) N(4.5, 0.04), (b) 0.8664, (c) ≈ 0.0062.

Section 7.11

Sampling Distribution of the Proportion

Complete Description

For a population with true proportion p:

E[p̂] = p and SE(p̂) = √(p(1−p)/n)

For large n (np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10): p̂ ≈ N(p, p(1−p)/n)

Standardized form: Z = (p̂ − p) / √(p(1−p)/n) ≈ N(0, 1)

Example 7.9
Probabilities from the sampling distribution of the proportion
  • Problem40% of voters favor a ballot measure (p = 0.40). A survey of n = 200 voters is conducted. Find: (a) P(p̂ > 0.45), (b) P(|p̂ − 0.40| > 0.05).
  • Checknp = 80 ≥ 10, n(1−p) = 120 ≥ 10. ✓ SE(p̂) = √(0.40 × 0.60/200) ≈ 0.03464.
  • Part (a) P(p̂ > 0.45) = P(Z > 0.05/0.03464) = P(Z > 1.443) ≈ 0.0745
  • Part (b) P(|p̂ − 0.40| > 0.05) = 2 × P(Z > 1.443) ≈ 0.149
  • InterpretAbout 15% of samples of size 200 would have a proportion more than 5 percentage points away from the true 40%.
Answers: (a) ≈ 0.0745, (b) ≈ 0.149.
Section 7.12

Simulation of Limit Theorems

Mathematical theorems tell us what happens in the limit n → ∞, but not always how fast convergence occurs or how well the approximation works for specific distributions and sample sizes. Simulation fills this gap. By drawing many random samples, computing the statistic for each sample, and plotting the resulting empirical distribution, we can verify theoretical predictions and develop intuition for the convergence process.

Populationn = 1n = 5n = 30n = 100

Simulation in Modern Statistics

Simulation is not just a pedagogical tool. Bootstrap methods simulate the sampling distribution of any statistic without assuming normality. Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulates from complex posterior distributions in Bayesian analysis. Permutation tests simulate the null distribution of test statistics. Power analysis simulates test behavior under specific alternatives. Understanding simulation as a validation tool builds the intuition needed for these advanced methods.

Section 7.13

Common Limit Theorem Mistakes

Mistake 1 — The Gambler's Fallacy
"After 8 heads in a row, the next flip is more likely to be tails to balance out." Correct: Each flip is independent. The LLN says proportions stabilize in the long run because early deviations become a smaller fraction of a growing total — not because future outcomes compensate.
Mistake 2 — Misreading the CLT
"Since I took a large sample, my data are normally distributed." Correct: The CLT describes the distribution of the SAMPLE MEAN, not individual observations. An exponential population remains exponential regardless of sample size. Always distinguish between the population distribution and the sampling distribution of a statistic.
Mistake 3 — Confusing Standard Deviation with Standard Error
For a population with σ = 20 and n = 100: "The standard deviation of X̄ is 20." Correct: SE(X̄) = σ/√n = 20/√100 = 2. The population SD σ describes variability among individuals. The SE σ/√n describes variability among sample means. These are very different — confusing them leads to wildly incorrect probability calculations.
Mistake 4 — Ignoring the Conditions for Normal Approximation
Applying the normal approximation to Binomial(20, 0.03): np = 0.6 < 10. Correct: The conditions np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10 are not met. The normal approximation would be very inaccurate. Use the exact binomial distribution instead.
Mistake 5 — Applying the LLN to Small Samples
"I took a sample of 10, so by the LLN my sample mean should be close to μ." Correct: The LLN is an asymptotic result. With n = 10, the sample mean might differ substantially from μ. Whether a specific n is "large enough" depends on σ — a question the standard error formula quantifies precisely.
Mistake 6 — Treating Approximations as Exact
Reporting a CLT-based probability as 0.3085 (four decimal places) without noting it is an approximation. Correct: For finite n, the CLT provides an approximation. Reporting to four decimal places suggests false precision. Acknowledge approximations and assess whether sample size is adequate for the population shape.
Section 7.14

Preparing for Estimation

This chapter has developed the mathematical tools that make statistical inference possible. The sample mean X̄ and sample proportion p̂ are random variables with computable means and standard deviations. By the Law of Large Numbers, sample statistics converge to population parameters. By the Central Limit Theorem, their sampling distributions are approximately normal for large n.

In practice, we observe one sample and must use it to draw conclusions about unknown population parameters. This is the estimation problem. The connection to this chapter is direct: a 95% confidence interval for a mean uses the fact that, by the CLT:

P(−1.96 ≤ (X̄ − μ)/(σ/√n) ≤ 1.96) ≈ 0.95

Rearranging to solve for μ gives the confidence interval formula. The entire procedure rests on the sampling distribution of X̄ — which we have now fully characterized.

One technical issue to note: the SE formula σ/√n requires knowing σ, which is usually unknown in practice. When σ is estimated by the sample standard deviation S, the standardized statistic (X̄ − μ)/(S/√n) no longer follows the standard normal exactly — it follows a t-distribution with n−1 degrees of freedom. For large n, the t-distribution is nearly indistinguishable from the normal. Chapter 8 will develop both cases.

Chapter 7 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Limit theorems provide the mathematical structure that makes inference possible. The LLN establishes convergence of sample statistics; the CLT establishes their approximate normality. Together they explain why statistical methods work in practice.
  • Sec 7.2Sample statistics are random variables before data are collected. The sampling distribution of a statistic is the distribution of its values across all possible samples of size n from the population.
  • Sec 7.3For a sample of size n from a population with mean μ and variance σ²: E[X̄] = μ and Var(X̄) = σ²/n. The sample mean is unbiased and its variance decreases at rate 1/n.
  • Sec 7.4The sample proportion p̂ = X/n where X ~ Binomial(n, p). E[p̂] = p and Var(p̂) = p(1−p)/n. The sample proportion is a special case of the sample mean, applied to Bernoulli random variables.
  • Sec 7.5The standard error of a statistic is the standard deviation of its sampling distribution. SE(X̄) = σ/√n; SE(p̂) = √(p(1−p)/n). Standard error measures estimation precision. In practice, σ and p are estimated from sample data.
  • Sec 7.6Yₙ converges in probability to c if P(|Yₙ − c| > ε) → 0 for every ε > 0. Chebyshev's Inequality — P(|Y − μ_Y| ≥ ε) ≤ σ²_Y/ε² — provides a concrete tool for establishing this, requiring only finite variance.
  • Sec 7.7Weak LLN: under i.i.d. sampling with finite variance, X̄ₙ →ᵖ μ. The LLN does not say future outcomes compensate for past extremes (Gambler's Fallacy). It also applies to sample proportions: p̂ₙ →ᵖ p.
  • Sec 7.8Central Limit Theorem: under i.i.d. sampling with finite variance, (X̄ₙ − μ)/(σ/√n) →ᵈ N(0, 1). The CLT is universal (applies to any distribution with finite variance), practical (works for moderate n), and explanatory (the reason the normal distribution appears so widely in nature).
  • Sec 7.9For X ~ Binomial(n, p) with np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10: X ≈ N(np, np(1−p)). The continuity correction (±0.5) improves accuracy when approximating discrete probabilities with a continuous distribution.
  • Sec 7.10Complete sampling distribution of X̄: E[X̄] = μ, SE(X̄) = σ/√n for all distributions and all n; X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n) exactly if the population is normal; X̄ ≈ N(μ, σ²/n) by CLT for large n otherwise.
  • Sec 7.11Complete sampling distribution of p̂: E[p̂] = p, SE(p̂) = √(p(1−p)/n) exactly; p̂ ≈ N(p, p(1−p)/n) for large n. SE(p̂) is largest when p = 0.5 and depends on both p and n.
  • Sec 7.12Simulation confirms limit theorems and develops intuition. Sampling distribution histograms approach the predicted normal shape as n increases. Running averages converge to μ as the LLN predicts. Simulation is the foundation of bootstrap, MCMC, permutation tests, and power analysis.
  • Sec 7.13Six common errors: the Gambler's Fallacy; concluding the population is normal from the CLT; confusing σ with SE(X̄) = σ/√n; ignoring conditions for normal approximation; applying LLN to small samples; treating CLT approximations as exact.
  • Sec 7.14The sampling distributions developed here are the scaffolding for all inference. Confidence intervals for means use the CLT directly. When σ is unknown and replaced by S, the t-distribution with n−1 degrees of freedom applies — the subject of Chapter 8.

Key Terms Review

sampling distribution
The probability distribution of a statistic across all possible samples of a fixed size from a given population.
sample mean (X̄)
The arithmetic average of a random sample; a random variable with E[X̄] = μ and Var(X̄) = σ²/n.
sample proportion (p̂)
The fraction of sample items with a specified characteristic; a random variable with E[p̂] = p and Var(p̂) = p(1−p)/n.
standard error
The standard deviation of the sampling distribution of a statistic. For the mean: σ/√n. For the proportion: √(p(1−p)/n).
estimated standard error
Standard error with unknown parameters replaced by sample estimates: S/√n for the mean, √(p̂(1−p̂)/n) for the proportion.
convergence in probability
Yₙ →ᵖ c means P(|Yₙ − c| > ε) → 0 for every ε > 0. Probabilistic closeness that grows with sample size.
Chebyshev's Inequality
For any random variable with mean μ_Y and variance σ²_Y: P(|Y − μ_Y| ≥ ε) ≤ σ²_Y/ε². Requires only finite variance.
Law of Large Numbers
Under i.i.d. sampling with finite variance: X̄ₙ →ᵖ μ. The sample mean converges in probability to the population mean.
Central Limit Theorem
Under i.i.d. sampling with finite variance: (X̄ₙ − μ)/(σ/√n) →ᵈ N(0,1). The standardized sample mean converges to the standard normal in distribution.
normal approximation to binomial
For X ~ Binomial(n, p) with np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10: X ≈ N(np, np(1−p)).
continuity correction
An adjustment of ±0.5 applied when using a continuous normal distribution to approximate discrete binomial probabilities.
Gambler's Fallacy
The incorrect belief that independent random outcomes balance each other in the short run. A coin has no memory; each flip is independent regardless of past outcomes.
i.i.d.
Independent and identically distributed. The standard assumption in sampling theory: each observation drawn from the same distribution, independently.
unbiased estimator
A statistic whose expected value equals the parameter it estimates. Both X̄ and p̂ are unbiased estimators of μ and p respectively.
t-distribution
The distribution of (X̄ − μ)/(S/√n) when σ is unknown. Symmetric and bell-shaped, but with heavier tails than the normal; introduced fully in Chapter 8.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 8: Estimation and Confidence Intervals

Chapter 7 established a complete mathematical description of how sample statistics behave probabilistically. The sample mean and sample proportion have known centers equal to their population counterparts, and standard deviations that shrink as 1/√n. By the Central Limit Theorem, their sampling distributions are approximately normal for large samples.

This is exactly the knowledge needed to move into statistical inference. Chapter 8 will address the practical problem: you observe one sample, compute one value of X̄ or p̂, and must make a statement about the unknown population parameter. You will study point estimation (bias, variance, mean squared error), and confidence intervals for means, proportions, and differences. The t-distribution will appear when σ is estimated from data. Every formula in Chapter 8 is built on the sampling distribution theory you have now mastered.

The limit theorems of this chapter are not background material to be forgotten. They are the active foundation of every estimation procedure, every hypothesis test, and every model in the remainder of this textbook.

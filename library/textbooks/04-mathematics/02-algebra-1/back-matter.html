Appendix A

Pre-Algebra Readiness Review

This appendix reviews the arithmetic and pre-algebra skills that Algebra I requires. If any section feels unfamiliar or produces consistent errors, return to Arithmetic and Pre-Algebra Foundations in the MGU Library before continuing. These skills are the foundation on which every chapter of this book is built.

A.1 — Whole Numbers and Place Value

The whole numbers are 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, … They have no fractions, no decimals, and no negative values. Place value tells you what each digit represents: ones, tens, hundreds, thousands, and so on. In the number 4,703: the 4 is in the thousands place (= 4,000), the 7 is in the hundreds place (= 700), the 0 is in the tens place, and the 3 is in the ones place. The complete value is 4,000 + 700 + 0 + 3 = 4,703.

Operations review: Addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division of whole numbers should be fluent. Pay attention to any zeros in the middle of a multiplication or long-division problem, as these are common sources of error.

A.2 — Integers and Signed Numbers

The integers include all whole numbers and their negatives: …, −4, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, … Positive numbers lie to the right of zero on the number line; negative numbers lie to the left.

OperationRuleExamples
Addition (same sign)Add absolute values; keep the sign−7 + (−4) = −11
Addition (different signs)Subtract smaller absolute value from larger; keep sign of the larger9 + (−3) = 6    −5 + 8 = 3
SubtractionRewrite as addition of the opposite: a − b = a + (−b)6 − 10 = −4    −3 − (−7) = 4
Multiplication / DivisionSame signs → positive. Different signs → negative.(−3)(−5) = 15    (−4)(6) = −24

A.3 — Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

When an expression contains more than one operation, follow the standard order: (1) Parentheses, (2) Exponents, (3) Multiplication and Division left to right, (4) Addition and Subtraction left to right.

Example A.3
Evaluate: 3 + 4² ÷ 2 − (1 + 5) = 3 + 4² ÷ 2 − 6 (parentheses first) = 3 + 16 ÷ 2 − 6 (exponent) = 3 + 8 − 6 (division) = 5 (addition and subtraction, left to right)
Warning

Multiplication and division have equal priority — do them left to right. The same applies to addition and subtraction. Never multiply before dividing simply because M comes before D in PEMDAS.

A.5 — Decimals and Percents

Decimals extend the place-value system to the right of the decimal point: tenths, hundredths, thousandths. A percent is a ratio per 100. To convert a percent to a decimal, divide by 100: 37% = 0.37. To convert a decimal to a percent, multiply by 100: 0.08 = 8%. To find a percent of a number, multiply: 15% of 80 = 0.15 × 80 = 12.

A.6 — Ratios and Proportions

A ratio is a comparison of two quantities by division: a/b or a : b. A proportion is an equation that sets two ratios equal: a/b = c/d. To solve, use cross multiplication: ad = bc, then solve for the unknown.

A.7 — Exponents (Introductory)

An exponent tells how many times the base is multiplied by itself: 4³ = 4 · 4 · 4 = 64. Note that (−3)² = 9 (positive) but −3² = −9, because the exponent applies only to 3 without parentheses. Parentheses make all the difference.

A.8 — Variables and Simple Expressions

A variable is a letter representing a number. An expression combines numbers, variables, and operations with no equals sign. To evaluate, replace each variable with its given value and simplify. Example: evaluate 3x − 2 when x = 5: 3(5) − 2 = 15 − 2 = 13.

A.10 — Coordinate Plane Basics

The coordinate plane has a horizontal x-axis and a vertical y-axis crossing at the origin (0, 0). Every point is described by an ordered pair (x, y). The axes divide the plane into four quadrants: Quadrant I (upper right, x > 0, y > 0), Quadrant II (upper left), Quadrant III (lower left), and Quadrant IV (lower right, x > 0, y < 0). To plot a point, start at the origin, move horizontally by x, then vertically by y.

Appendix E

Equation-Solving Strategy Guide

Use this guide as a step-by-step reference whenever you are solving a linear equation in one variable.

The General Strategy — Four Steps

Step 1. Simplify each side independently: distribute to remove parentheses, then combine like terms on each side.

Step 2. Move all variable terms to one side using addition or subtraction.

Step 3. Isolate the variable using multiplication or division to make its coefficient equal to 1.

Step 4. Check: substitute your answer into the original equation and verify both sides are equal.

E.2 — Common Equation Types and Strategies

Equation TypeKey MoveExample
One-stepOne inverse operationx + 7 = 12 → subtract 7
Two-stepUndo addition/subtraction first, then multiplication/division2x − 3 = 9
With parenthesesDistribute first3(x + 4) = 21
Variables on both sidesMove variable terms to one side5x = 2x + 9
With fractionsMultiply through by LCD to clear fractionsx/3 + 1 = 5
With decimalsMultiply through by a power of 100.2x + 1.4 = 3.0
IdentitySimplifies to a statement that is always true3x + 6 = 3(x + 2) → all reals
No solutionSimplifies to a false statement2x + 1 = 2x + 5 → no solution

E.3 — Fraction-Clearing Technique

When an equation contains fractions, multiply every term on both sides by the LCD to eliminate all fractions before solving.

Example E.3 — Fraction Clearing
Solve: x/4 − 1/2 = 3/8 LCD of 4, 2, and 8 is 8. Multiply every term by 8: 8 · (x/4) − 8 · (1/2) = 8 · (3/8) 2x − 4 = 3 2x = 7 → x = 7/2

F.2 — Compound Inequalities

"And" inequality (conjunction). The solution must satisfy both conditions simultaneously. The solution is the overlap (intersection) of the two sets. Example: −2 < x ≤ 5. On the number line, shade the region between −2 (open circle) and 5 (closed circle).

"Or" inequality (disjunction). The solution satisfies at least one condition. The solution is the union of the two sets. Example: x < −1 or x ≥ 4. Shade to the left of −1 (open circle) and to the right of and including 4 (closed circle).

F.3 — The Reversal Rule

Critical Rule

When you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, the inequality symbol reverses direction.

−3x > 12 → x < −4 (symbol reversed)

This rule does NOT apply when adding or subtracting, nor when multiplying or dividing by a positive number.

Appendix G

Function Notation Reference

G.1 — What Function Notation Means

If f is the name of a function, then f(x) (read "f of x") represents the output when x is the input. This is not multiplication — the parentheses indicate "the function is applied to the input x."

Example G.1
f(x) = 3x − 5 f(2) = 3(2) − 5 = 1 f(0) = 3(0) − 5 = −5 f(−4) = 3(−4) − 5 = −17

G.2 — Domain and Range

The domain is the set of all allowable inputs. The range is the set of all possible outputs. For f(x) = 1/x, the domain excludes 0. For g(x) = √x, the domain is all x ≥ 0.

Appendix H

Linear Equation Forms

All three standard forms below describe the same type of relationship: a line. Each form has its most natural use. Familiarity with all three forms — and the ability to convert between them — is a central Algebra I skill.

H.1 — Slope-Intercept Form

Slope-Intercept Form

y = mx + b

m = slope (rate of change)    b = y-intercept (value of y when x = 0)

Best for: graphing a line directly; identifying slope and y-intercept at a glance; writing equations when slope and y-intercept are known.

H.2 — Point-Slope Form

Point-Slope Form

y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)

m = slope    (x₁, y₁) = any known point on the line

Best for: writing an equation when slope and one point are known, or when two points are given.

H.3 — Standard Form

Standard Form

Ax + By = C

A, B, C are integers; A ≥ 0; A and B are not both zero.

Best for: graphing using intercepts; systems of equations solved by elimination.

x-intercept: set y = 0 and solve.    y-intercept: set x = 0 and solve.

H.4 — Converting Between Forms

FromToMethod

Point-slopeSlope-interceptDistribute; add y₁ to both sides
StandardSlope-interceptSolve for y; slope = −A/B

Appendix J

Factoring Patterns Reference

Factoring rewrites a polynomial as a product of simpler expressions. Always check for a GCF first.

J.1 — Greatest Common Factor

6x² − 9x = 3x(2x − 3)

J.2 — Factoring by Grouping

ax + ay + bx + by = a(x + y) + b(x + y) = (a + b)(x + y)

J.3 — Factoring Trinomials

TypeMethodExample
Leading coefficient 1Find p, q such that pq = c and p + q = b: x² + bx + c = (x + p)(x + q)x² + 7x + 12 = (x + 3)(x + 4)
Leading coefficient > 1Use the AC method: find factors of ac that add to b; rewrite the middle term; factor by grouping2x² + 7x + 3 = (2x + 1)(x + 3)

J.4 — Special Patterns

Special Factoring Patterns

Difference of Squares: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b)

Perfect Square (sum): a² + 2ab + b² = (a + b)²

Perfect Square (diff): a² − 2ab + b² = (a − b)²

Sum of Cubes (Alg II): a³ + b³ = (a + b)(a² − ab + b²)

Diff of Cubes (Alg II): a³ − b³ = (a − b)(a² + ab + b²)

J.5 — Factoring Strategy Checklist

  1. Factor out the GCF from all terms.
  2. Count the terms: two terms → check for difference of squares; three terms → try trinomial factoring; four terms → try grouping.
  3. Check whether any factor can be factored further.
  4. Verify by multiplying the factored form back out.
Appendix K

Quadratic Formula and Discriminant Reference

K.1 — The Quadratic Formula

Quadratic Formula

For any quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 where a ≠ 0:

x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a)

To use the formula: (1) Write the equation in standard form (set equal to zero). (2) Identify a, b, and c. (3) Substitute into the formula. (4) Simplify under the radical sign. (5) Simplify the square root if possible. (6) Evaluate both solutions using + and −.

K.2 — The Discriminant

The discriminant is D = b² − 4ac. Its value predicts the number and type of solutions before you solve.

DiscriminantNumber of SolutionsTypeGraph of y = ax² + bx + c
D > 02Two distinct real solutionsParabola crosses x-axis at two points
D = 01One real solution (repeated root)Parabola touches x-axis at exactly one point
D < 00 realTwo non-real (complex) solutionsParabola does not cross the x-axis

K.3 — Forms of a Quadratic Function

FormEquationBest Used For

Vertexy = a(x − h)² + kGraphing; reading vertex (h, k) directly

Vertex from Standard Form

The x-coordinate of the vertex is x = −b / (2a). Substitute into the equation to find the y-coordinate.

Algebra II Readiness Check

This checklist covers the core Algebra I skills that Algebra II expects students to have mastered. Use it for self-assessment before transitioning to Algebra II. If you find consistent gaps in any section, return to the corresponding chapters before continuing.

O.1 — Expressions and Properties

  • I can identify terms, coefficients, and constants in any algebraic expression.
  • I can combine like terms accurately, including with negative coefficients.
  • I can apply the distributive property, including distributing a negative sign.
  • I can identify and apply the commutative, associative, and distributive properties.
  • I can evaluate expressions with two or more variables and negative values.

  • I can solve one-step, two-step, and multi-step linear equations.
  • I can solve equations with fractions and decimals.
  • I can solve equations with variables on both sides.
  • I can recognize identity equations (all real solutions) and no-solution equations.
  • I can solve linear inequalities, including compound inequalities.
  • I can graph inequalities on a number line.

  • I understand what makes a relationship a function.
  • I can evaluate functions using function notation.
  • I can identify domain and range from a table, graph, or rule.

  • I can graph a line in slope-intercept form.

  • I can write the equation of a line given slope and a point, or given two points.

  • I can factor perfect square trinomials.

  • I can check factoring by multiplying.

O.7 — Quadratics and Radicals

  • I understand what a quadratic function is and what a parabola looks like.
  • I can identify the vertex and axis of symmetry from a graph.
  • I can solve a quadratic equation by factoring.
  • I can solve a quadratic equation using the quadratic formula.
  • I can interpret the discriminant to predict the number of solutions.
  • I can simplify square roots and cube roots.
  • I can add, subtract, and multiply radicals.
  • I can rationalize a denominator.
  • I can solve simple radical equations and check for extraneous solutions.
  • I can connect rational exponents to radical notation.

O.8 — Data and Modeling

  • I can compute mean, median, mode, range, and IQR.
  • I can read and interpret dot plots, histograms, box plots, and scatter plots.
  • I can identify positive, negative, and no correlation in a scatter plot.
  • I can write and interpret the equation of a line of best fit.
  • I understand what a residual represents.
  • I can read and interpret a two-way table.
  • I can make a basic judgment about which model type (linear, quadratic, exponential) fits a data set.

Glossary

This glossary defines every key term introduced in Algebra I, organized alphabetically. Numbers in parentheses refer to the chapter where the term first appears. For fuller treatment of any term, see the relevant chapter section.

A
absolute valueCh. 2
The distance of a number from zero on the number line. Written |a|. Always non-negative. |−7| = 7, |7| = 7.
algebraic expressionCh. 1
A mathematical phrase built from numbers, variables, and operations, with no equals sign.
algebraic modelCh. 1
An expression, equation, or formula that uses defined variables to represent a real-world situation.
associationCh. 14
A pattern in a scatter plot suggesting a relationship between two variables. May be positive, negative, or nonexistent.
axis of symmetryCh. 12
The vertical line through the vertex of a parabola. For y = ax² + bx + c, the axis is x = −b/(2a).
B
baseCh. 9
In the exponential expression aⁿ, the base is a — the quantity that is repeatedly multiplied.
binomialCh. 10
A polynomial with exactly two terms. Example: 3x − 7.
box plotCh. 14
A graph displaying the five-number summary (minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum). Also called a box-and-whisker plot.
C
coefficientCh. 1
The numerical factor of a variable term. In −5x², the coefficient is −5. When no number is written, the coefficient is 1 or −1.
combining like termsCh. 1
Adding or subtracting the coefficients of terms that have identical variable parts.
compound inequalityCh. 4
Two inequalities joined by "and" or "or."
consistent systemCh. 8
A system of equations that has at least one solution.
constantCh. 1
A fixed number in an expression that does not depend on any variable.
constant of variationCh. 7
In a direct variation equation y = kx, the constant k equals the ratio y/x.
coordinate planeCh. 6
A flat surface defined by two perpendicular number lines (x-axis and y-axis) meeting at the origin. Every point is described by an ordered pair (x, y).
correlationCh. 14
The direction and general strength of a linear relationship between two variables in a scatter plot.
cube rootCh. 13
A number that, when cubed, gives the original value. ³√8 = 2 because 2³ = 8.
D
degree of a polynomialCh. 10
The largest exponent among all terms of a polynomial written in standard form.
degree of a termCh. 10
The exponent of the variable in a single-variable term, or the sum of exponents in a multi-variable term.
dependent variableCh. 5
The output variable in a function; its value depends on the independent variable. Usually placed on the vertical axis.
difference of squaresCh. 11
A factoring pattern: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b).
direct variationCh. 7
A relationship in which y = kx for some nonzero constant k. The ratio y/x is constant; the graph passes through the origin.
discriminantCh. 12
The expression b² − 4ac from the quadratic formula. Its sign tells the number and type of solutions of ax² + bx + c = 0.
distributive propertyCh. 1
The algebraic property a(b + c) = ab + ac. Multiplication distributes over addition and subtraction.
domainCh. 5
The set of all allowable input values of a function.
E–F
elimination methodCh. 8
A method for solving a system of equations by adding or subtracting equations to eliminate one variable.
equationCh. 3
A mathematical statement asserting that two expressions are equal, written with an equals sign.
equivalent expressionsCh. 1
Two expressions that produce equal values for every possible value of the variable.
evaluateCh. 1
To find the numerical value of an expression by substituting specific values for each variable and simplifying.
exponentCh. 9
A number indicating how many times a base is used as a factor. In aⁿ, n is the exponent.
exponential decayCh. 9
A situation modeled by y = a · bˣ with 0 < b < 1. The output decreases by a constant factor as the input increases.
exponential growthCh. 9
A situation modeled by y = a · bˣ with b > 1. The output increases by a constant factor as the input increases.
extraneous solutionCh. 13
A value that appears to be a solution after algebraic manipulation but does not satisfy the original equation. Must be identified and rejected.
factorCh. 1
One of the quantities multiplied together in a product.
factored form (quadratic)Ch. 12
y = a(x − p)(x − q). The zeros p and q can be read directly from this form.
factoringCh. 11
Rewriting a polynomial as a product of two or more simpler expressions.
feasible regionCh. 8
In a system of linear inequalities, the region of the coordinate plane where all conditions are satisfied simultaneously.
functionCh. 5
A rule or relationship that assigns exactly one output value to each input value.
function notationCh. 5
The notation f(x) (read "f of x"), used to name a function and represent its output for a given input x.
G–I
graphing methodCh. 8
A method for solving a system of equations by graphing both equations and finding the point of intersection.
greatest common factor (GCF)Ch. 11
The largest expression that divides every term in a polynomial. Always check for and factor out the GCF first.
histogramCh. 14
A graph displaying the frequency of data values organized into intervals (bins), using adjacent bars.
horizontal lineCh. 7
A line with slope 0. Equation form: y = k for some constant k.
identity equationCh. 3
An equation true for all real values of the variable. Simplifies to a statement like 0 = 0.
inconsistent systemCh. 8
A system of equations with no solution. The lines are parallel.
independent variableCh. 5
The input variable in a function; its value is chosen freely. Usually placed on the horizontal axis.
inequalityCh. 4
A mathematical statement comparing two expressions using <, >, ≤, or ≥.
integersCh. 2
The set of whole numbers and their negatives: …, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, …
interquartile range (IQR)Ch. 14
The difference Q₃ − Q₁, measuring the spread of the middle 50% of a data set.
inverse operationCh. 3
An operation that undoes another. Addition and subtraction are inverses; multiplication and division are inverses; squaring and square root are inverses.
irrational numberCh. 2
A real number that cannot be expressed as a ratio of two integers. Its decimal form is non-terminating and non-repeating. Examples: √2, π.
L–M
leading coefficientCh. 10
The coefficient of the term with the highest degree in a polynomial written in standard form.
like termsCh. 1
Terms with exactly the same variable part, including identical exponents. Only like terms may be combined by addition or subtraction.
line of best fitCh. 14
A line drawn through a scatter plot that best represents the linear trend in the data. Used for predictions. Also called a trend line.
linear equationCh. 3
An equation whose graph is a straight line. The variable has degree 1.
linear functionCh. 7
A function whose graph is a non-vertical straight line. Has a constant rate of change.
mapping diagramCh. 5
A diagram showing each input paired with its output, using arrows between two ovals. Useful for visualizing whether a relation is a function.
meanCh. 14
The average of a data set, found by dividing the sum of all values by the number of values.
medianCh. 14
The middle value in an ordered data set. If even number of values, the average of the two middle values.
modeCh. 14
The value appearing most frequently in a data set.
monomialCh. 10
A polynomial with exactly one term. Example: 5x³.
N–P
natural numbersCh. 2
The counting numbers: 1, 2, 3, 4, …
no-solution equationCh. 3
An equation with no real solutions. Simplifies to a false statement like 5 = 0.
ordered pairCh. 6
A pair of numbers (x, y) representing a location on the coordinate plane or an input-output pair of a function.
originCh. 6
The point (0, 0) where the x-axis and y-axis intersect on the coordinate plane.
parabolaCh. 12
The U-shaped curve that is the graph of a quadratic function.
perfect squareCh. 13
An integer that is the square of another integer. Examples: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100.
perfect square trinomialCh. 11
A trinomial of the form a² + 2ab + b² = (a + b)² or a² − 2ab + b² = (a − b)².
point-slope formCh. 7
A form of a linear equation: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁), where m is the slope and (x₁, y₁) is a known point.
polynomialCh. 10
An algebraic expression of one or more terms with non-negative integer exponents, combined by addition and subtraction.
principal square rootCh. 13
The non-negative square root of a non-negative number. √a ≥ 0.
Q–R
quadratic equationCh. 12
An equation of the form ax² + bx + c = 0 with a ≠ 0.
quadratic formulaCh. 12
The formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a), used to solve any quadratic equation.
quadratic functionCh. 12
A function of the form f(x) = ax² + bx + c with a ≠ 0. Its graph is a parabola.
radicalCh. 13
An expression that includes a root symbol √ or ⁿ√.
radicandCh. 13
The expression under the radical symbol. In √(5x + 2), the radicand is 5x + 2.
range (data)Ch. 14
The difference between the maximum and minimum values in a data set. Measures spread.
range (function)Ch. 5
The set of all possible output values of a function.
rate of changeCh. 6
The ratio (change in output) / (change in input), describing how much one quantity changes relative to another. The slope of a linear function is its constant rate of change.
rational exponentCh. 13
An exponent that is a fraction: a^(m/n) = ⁿ√(aᵐ). Connects exponent and radical notation.
rational numberCh. 2
A number expressible as a ratio p/q where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0. Includes all integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals.
real numbersCh. 2
All rational and irrational numbers together. Every point on the number line corresponds to a real number.
relationCh. 5
A set of ordered pairs or any rule that pairs inputs with outputs. A function is a special type of relation.
residualCh. 14
The difference between an actual data value and the predicted value from a model. Residual = Actual − Predicted.
S
scatter plotCh. 14
A graph displaying two-variable data as points on the coordinate plane, used to visualize association or correlation.
scientific notationCh. 9
A way of writing numbers in the form a × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ |a| < 10.
simplifyCh. 1
To rewrite an expression in an equivalent but more compact form by distributing, combining like terms, or applying properties.
slopeCh. 6
The ratio rise/run = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁), measuring the steepness and direction of a line.
slope-intercept formCh. 7
A form of a linear equation: y = mx + b, where m is the slope and b is the y-intercept.
solutionCh. 3
A value (or values) of the variable that make an equation or inequality a true statement.
solution setCh. 4
The complete set of all values that satisfy an equation or inequality.
square rootCh. 13
A number that, when multiplied by itself, gives the original value. The principal square root of a is written √a.
standard form (linear)Ch. 7
Ax + By = C, where A, B, and C are integers and A ≥ 0.
standard form (polynomial)Ch. 10
A polynomial written with terms in order from highest to lowest degree.
standard form (quadratic)Ch. 12
y = ax² + bx + c.
substitutionCh. 1
Replacing a variable with a number or expression. Negative values must always be enclosed in parentheses.
substitution methodCh. 8
A method for solving a system by solving one equation for one variable and substituting into the other equation.
system of equationsCh. 8
A set of two or more equations with the same variables. A solution must satisfy all equations simultaneously.
T–Z
termCh. 1
A single quantity in an expression connected to others by addition or subtraction. May be a constant, a variable, or a product of constants and variables.
trinomialCh. 10
A polynomial with exactly three terms. Example: 2x² − 5x + 3.
two-way tableCh. 14
A table displaying the frequency or relative frequency of data for two categorical variables simultaneously.
undefined slopeCh. 6
The slope of a vertical line. The run is zero, so the slope ratio has zero in the denominator and is undefined.
unlike termsCh. 1
Terms with different variable parts. Unlike terms cannot be combined by addition or subtraction.
variableCh. 1
A letter or symbol representing a number. May represent an unknown to find, an input that can be any value, or a quantity that changes.
vertexCh. 12
The highest or lowest point of a parabola — the turning point of the graph of a quadratic function.
vertex form (quadratic)Ch. 12
y = a(x − h)² + k, where (h, k) is the vertex.
vertical lineCh. 7
A line with undefined slope. Equation form: x = k for some constant k.
vertical line testCh. 5
A visual method for determining whether a graph represents a function. If any vertical line crosses the graph more than once, the graph is not a function.
whole numbersCh. 2
The counting numbers and zero: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, …
x-interceptCh. 6
The point where a graph crosses the x-axis. At this point, y = 0.
y-interceptCh. 6
The point where a graph crosses the y-axis. At this point, x = 0.
zero exponentCh. 9
For any nonzero base a, a⁰ = 1.
zero of a functionCh. 12
An input value for which the output is zero. Graphically, the x-coordinate of an x-intercept.
zero product propertyCh. 11
If ab = 0, then a = 0 or b = 0 (or both). The basis for solving equations by factoring.

Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces one of the most important function families in all of algebra: the quadratic function. Until now, every function you have studied has been linear — its graph is a straight line, its rate of change is constant, and its equation takes the form y = mx + b. Quadratics are fundamentally different. Their graphs curve. Their rates of change are not constant. They can model situations that linear functions cannot: the arc of a thrown ball, the area of a rectangle whose dimensions both change, the relationship between the side of a square and its area, and the revenue of a business whose sales respond to price.

This chapter is not only about solving equations. It is about understanding a function family. You will learn what makes a function quadratic, how to read and build its graph, how to find its key features, and how to solve quadratic equations using multiple methods. You will also learn to recognize when a quadratic model fits a real-world situation and how to interpret what the solution means in context.

Every method you learn in this chapter — factoring, square roots, completing the square as an idea, and the quadratic formula — is a tool. Each tool fits certain situations better than others. Part of becoming skilled at quadratics is learning which tool to reach for and why.

Opening Question

A soccer player kicks a ball from ground level. The height of the ball in feet, h, after t seconds is modeled by the equation h = −16t² + 48t. How high does the ball travel? When does it reach its highest point? When does it land? At what times is it exactly 32 feet high? None of these questions can be answered with the linear tools from earlier chapters. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer every one of them.

Why This Chapter Matters

Quadratic functions appear throughout mathematics, science, engineering, and everyday life. The path of any projectile under gravity follows a quadratic curve. The area of any square or rectangle with variable dimensions involves a quadratic expression. The profit curve of a business, the shape of a satellite dish, the behavior of certain physical forces — all of these involve quadratic relationships.

Beyond applications, quadratic functions are the first major function family beyond linear, and they are the entry point to all of the higher mathematics that follows. Polynomial functions, complex numbers, conic sections, and much of calculus build directly on what you learn here. Understanding the parabola now means you are prepared for every curve that comes after it.

Section 12.1

What Makes a Function Quadratic

In earlier chapters, you studied linear functions. A linear function grows or decreases at a constant rate. Add the same amount to the input, and the output changes by a fixed amount every time. The graph of a linear function is always a straight line.

A quadratic function behaves differently. The rate of change is not constant — it speeds up or slows down depending on where you are on the graph. The graph is not a straight line; it curves. What makes a function quadratic is the presence of a squared variable as the highest-power term. The general form of a quadratic function is:

f(x) = ax² + bx + c    where a ≠ 0

The coefficient a is called the leading coefficient. The term ax² is the quadratic term. The term bx is the linear term. The number c is the constant term.

The crucial requirement is that a ≠ 0. If a = 0, the quadratic term disappears and the function becomes linear. A quadratic function must have a nonzero squared term.

What the Squared Term Does

To see why the squared term changes everything, compare a linear and a quadratic function side by side.

xf(x) = 2x (linear)Changeg(x) = x² (quadratic)Change

The linear function increases by exactly 2 each step. The quadratic function increases by more and more each step. This growing rate of change is what makes the graph curve rather than remain straight.

Example 12.1.1
Identifying quadratic functions and their coefficients
  • ProblemIdentify each as linear, quadratic, or neither. For each quadratic, state a, b, and c.

    (a) f(x) = −4x² + x − 9    (b) g(x) = 7x − 3    (c) h(x) = x²    (d) k(x) = x³ − 2x
  • ThinkA quadratic has a nonzero x² term as its highest power. Look at the degree of each expression.
  • Solve (a) Highest power is 2, coefficient −4 ≠ 0. Quadratic. a = −4, b = 1, c = −9.
    (b) Highest power is 1. Linear.
    (c) Highest power is 2, coefficient 1 ≠ 0. Quadratic. a = 1, b = 0, c = 0.
    (d) Highest power is 3. Neither (this is a cubic function).
  • CheckThe key test: does the function have an x² term with a nonzero coefficient as its leading term?
Answer: (a) quadratic, a = −4, b = 1, c = −9; (b) linear; (c) quadratic, a = 1, b = 0, c = 0; (d) neither.
Tip

Do not confuse a function that contains x² with one where x² is the leading term. In x³ + x², the degree is 3, not 2. The highest power determines the function type.

Section 12.2

Parabolas

Every quadratic function has the same basic graph shape: a smooth, symmetric curve called a parabola. You have likely seen this shape before — it looks like the path of a ball thrown through the air, or the inside of a bowl, or the outline of a satellite dish. A parabola has several important features: it is symmetric about a central vertical line, it has a single turning point called the vertex, and it opens either upward or downward.

Direction and Vertex

If a > 0, the parabola opens upward. The vertex is at the bottom — a minimum.

If a < 0, the parabola opens downward. The vertex is at the top — a maximum.

This matters for applications: if a quadratic models height of a thrown object, the vertex gives the maximum height. If it models profit, the vertex gives maximum profit (when a < 0) or minimum cost (when a > 0).

Width of a Parabola

The value of |a| controls how wide or narrow the parabola is. A large |a| pulls the parabola tighter; a small |a| close to zero spreads it open. For example, f(x) = 3x² is narrower than f(x) = x², which is narrower than f(x) = (1/4)x².

Diagram Instruction

Draw two parabolas on the same coordinate plane. The first is f(x) = x², a standard upward parabola with vertex at the origin. The second is f(x) = −x², a downward parabola with vertex at the origin. Label the vertex (0, 0) for each, mark the axis of symmetry x = 0 with a dashed line, and indicate the direction each opens. Mark the y-intercept (0, 0) for both. Add arrows at the ends of each curve to show the parabola continues.

Example 12.2.1
Describing a parabola from its equation
  • ProblemFor each function, state (i) direction of opening, (ii) minimum or maximum, (iii) y-intercept.

    (a) f(x) = 2x² − 3x + 1      (b) g(x) = −x² + 5
  • ThinkCheck the sign of a for direction. Use x = 0 for the y-intercept.
  • Solve (a) a = 2 > 0: opens upward, vertex is a minimum. f(0) = 1, so y-intercept is (0, 1).
    (b) a = −1 < 0: opens downward, vertex is a maximum. g(0) = 5, so y-intercept is (0, 5).
Answer: (a) upward, minimum, (0, 1); (b) downward, maximum, (0, 5).
Section 12.3

The Vertex and Axis of Symmetry

The vertex is the most important single point on a parabola. It represents the function's maximum or minimum value — the highest point a ball reaches, the minimum cost of production, the maximum area that can be enclosed with a fixed perimeter. For any quadratic function f(x) = ax² + bx + c, the x-coordinate of the vertex is:

x = −b / (2a)

Once you have the x-coordinate, substitute it into the function to find the y-coordinate: y = f(−b/(2a)). The axis of symmetry is the vertical line x = −b/(2a) — it passes directly through the vertex and divides the parabola into two mirror-image halves.

Student Warning — Sign in the Formula

The axis of symmetry is x = −b/(2a), not b/(2a). The negative sign is always part of the formula. If b = −6, then −b/(2a) = −(−6)/(2a) = 6/(2a), which is positive. Write out the sign substitution explicitly to avoid errors.

Example 12.3.1
Finding the vertex and axis of symmetry
  • ProblemFind the vertex and axis of symmetry of f(x) = x² − 6x + 5.
  • ThinkIdentify a, b, c. Use x = −b/(2a) for the x-coordinate. Then substitute to find y.
  • Solve a = 1, b = −6, c = 5.

    x = −(−6) / (2 · 1) = 6 / 2 = 3 f(3) = (3)² − 6(3) + 5 = 9 − 18 + 5 = −4 Vertex: (3, −4).   Axis of symmetry: x = 3.
  • Checka = 1 > 0, so the parabola opens upward and the vertex is a minimum. The y-coordinate −4 is below the y-intercept of 5, which is consistent with a minimum. ✓
Answer: Vertex (3, −4), axis of symmetry x = 3.
Example 12.3.2
Vertex of a downward-opening parabola
  • ProblemFind the vertex of f(x) = −2x² + 8x − 3. Is it a minimum or maximum?
  • Solve a = −2, b = 8, c = −3.

    x = −8 / (2 · (−2)) = −8 / (−4) = 2 f(2) = −2(2)² + 8(2) − 3 = −8 + 16 − 3 = 5 Vertex: (2, 5).
  • Checka = −2 < 0, so the parabola opens downward. The vertex is a maximum. The highest output value is 5, reached when x = 2. ✓
Answer: Vertex (2, 5), which is a maximum.
Section 12.4

Graphing Quadratics from Tables

Before using shortcuts to graph a quadratic, it is valuable to build the graph directly from a table of values. This reveals the shape and symmetry of the parabola and reinforces why graphing by key features works. Choose x-values that include the vertex and extend to both sides of the axis of symmetry. Plot the resulting ordered pairs, then connect them with a smooth curve — never with straight line segments. A parabola is a continuously curving shape, not a polygon.

Example 12.4.1
Graphing from a table of values
  • ProblemGraph f(x) = x² − 4x + 3 using a table of values.
  • ThinkFind the axis of symmetry first to center the table. a = 1, b = −4, so x = −(−4)/(2·1) = 2. Choose x-values from 0 to 4.
  • Solve x = 0: 0 − 0 + 3 = 3 → (0, 3) x = 1: 1 − 4 + 3 = 0 → (1, 0) x = 2: 4 − 8 + 3 = −1 → (2, −1) ← vertex x = 3: 9 − 12 + 3 = 0 → (3, 0) x = 4: 16 − 16 + 3 = 3 → (4, 3) Notice: the values at x = 1 and x = 3 are both 0. The values at x = 0 and x = 4 are both 3. The table is symmetric about x = 2.
  • CheckSymmetry about the axis x = 2 is confirmed. The minimum is at (2, −1). The parabola crosses the x-axis at (1, 0) and (3, 0). ✓
Answer: Vertex (2, −1), x-intercepts (1, 0) and (3, 0), y-intercept (0, 3).
Diagram Instruction

Draw a coordinate plane. Plot the five points (0, 3), (1, 0), (2, −1), (3, 0), (4, 3). Draw a dashed vertical line at x = 2 labeled "axis of symmetry." Connect the points with a smooth upward-opening U-shaped curve. Label the vertex (2, −1), the two x-intercepts (1, 0) and (3, 0), and the y-intercept (0, 3). Add arrows at both ends of the parabola to show it continues.

Tip — Connect with a Smooth Curve

Always connect plotted points with a smooth curve, not jagged line segments. The parabola has no sharp corners. If the curve looks angular in your sketch, add more plotted points between the ones you have.

Section 12.5

Graphing Quadratics in Standard Form

When you need to graph a quadratic quickly and accurately, you can use key features of the standard form rather than building a full table. The strategy below produces a well-labeled, accurate graph efficiently.

Systematic Graphing Strategy

1. Identify a, b, and c. Determine direction (a > 0 opens upward; a < 0 opens downward).

2. Find the axis of symmetry: x = −b/(2a).

3. Find the vertex by substituting the x-coordinate into f(x).

4. Find the y-intercept: let x = 0, giving (0, c).

5. Use symmetry to find the mirror of the y-intercept across the axis.

6. Find x-intercepts if they exist (set f(x) = 0 and solve).

7. Draw a smooth parabola through the key points.

Example 12.5.1
Graphing in standard form
  • ProblemGraph f(x) = −x² + 2x + 3.
  • Thinka = −1 < 0, so the parabola opens downward. Find the vertex, y-intercept, and x-intercepts.
  • Solve Axis of symmetry: x = −2 / (2·(−1)) = 1 Vertex: f(1) = −1 + 2 + 3 = 4 → (1, 4) [maximum] y-intercept: f(0) = 3 → (0, 3) Mirror of y-intercept across x = 1: x = 2 → (2, 3) For x-intercepts, set f(x) = 0:
    −x² + 2x + 3 = 0 → x² − 2x − 3 = 0 (x − 3)(x + 1) = 0 → x = 3 or x = −1 X-intercepts: (3, 0) and (−1, 0).
  • CheckThe parabola opens downward and reaches its highest point at (1, 4). It crosses the x-axis at two symmetric points around x = 1: one at x = 3 (two units right) and one at x = −1 (two units left). Symmetric. ✓
Answer: Vertex (1, 4) [max], axis x = 1, y-intercept (0, 3), x-intercepts (−1, 0) and (3, 0).
Section 12.6

x-Intercepts and Zeros

The x-intercepts of a quadratic function are the points where the parabola crosses the x-axis. At these points, the output of the function equals zero. A value of x that makes f(x) = 0 is called a zero of the function. When zeros are real numbers, they correspond exactly to the x-intercepts. The word root means the same thing in the context of equations.

These three terms — x-intercept, zero, and root — describe the same values from three perspectives: geometric, functional, and algebraic.

DiscriminantNumber of x-InterceptsWhat it Looks Like
D > 0Two distinct x-interceptsParabola crosses the x-axis at two separate points
D = 0One x-intercept (at the vertex)Parabola touches the x-axis at exactly one point
D < 0No x-interceptsParabola does not touch the x-axis at all

The zeros of a quadratic function are symmetric about the axis of symmetry. If the zeros are at x = r and x = s, then the axis of symmetry passes through their midpoint: x = (r + s)/2.

Example 12.6.1
Finding the axis of symmetry from zeros
  • ProblemA parabola crosses the x-axis at (−2, 0) and (6, 0). State the zeros and find the axis of symmetry.
  • Solve Zeros: x = −2 and x = 6.
    Axis of symmetry: x = (−2 + 6) / 2 = 4 / 2 = 2
Answer: Zeros are −2 and 6. Axis of symmetry is x = 2.

Section 12.7

Solving Quadratics by Factoring

To solve a quadratic equation by factoring, you need the equation in standard form with everything on one side and zero on the other. The method relies on the Zero Product Property: if two factors multiply to zero, at least one of them must equal zero.

Zero Product Property

If A · B = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0 (or both).

This property is the entire reason factoring works as a solution method. It converts one equation into two simpler equations, each easily solved.

Critical: The product must equal zero. If the product equals any other number, this property does not apply. Move all terms to one side first.

Example 12.7.1
Solving by factoring — leading coefficient 1
  • ProblemSolve x² − 5x + 6 = 0.
  • ThinkAlready in standard form. Factor: find two numbers that multiply to +6 and add to −5. Those are −2 and −3.
  • Solve x² − 5x + 6 = 0 (x − 2)(x − 3) = 0 x − 2 = 0 or x − 3 = 0 x = 2 or x = 3
  • CheckFor x = 2: (2)² − 5(2) + 6 = 4 − 10 + 6 = 0 ✓    For x = 3: (3)² − 5(3) + 6 = 9 − 15 + 6 = 0 ✓
Answer: x = 2 or x = 3.
Example 12.7.2
Moving terms to standard form before factoring
  • ProblemSolve x² + 3x = 10.
  • ThinkThe right side is not zero. Move all terms left first.
  • Solve x² + 3x − 10 = 0 (x + 5)(x − 2) = 0 x = −5 or x = 2
  • CheckFor x = −5: 25 + 3(−5) = 25 − 15 = 10 ✓    For x = 2: 4 + 6 = 10 ✓
Answer: x = −5 or x = 2.
Example 12.7.3
Solving with a leading coefficient greater than 1
  • ProblemSolve 2x² − 7x + 3 = 0.
  • ThinkLeading coefficient is 2. Use ac-method: find two numbers that multiply to ac = 2·3 = 6 and add to b = −7. Those are −6 and −1.
  • Solve 2x² − 6x − x + 3 = 0 2x(x − 3) − 1(x − 3) = 0 (2x − 1)(x − 3) = 0 x = 1/2 or x = 3
  • CheckFor x = 3: 2(9) − 7(3) + 3 = 18 − 21 + 3 = 0 ✓    For x = 1/2: 2(1/4) − 7(1/2) + 3 = 1/2 − 7/2 + 3 = 0 ✓
Answer: x = 1/2 or x = 3.
Student Warning — Zero Is Required

The Zero Product Property only works when the product equals zero. If you have (x + 3)(x − 2) = 6, you cannot set each factor equal to 6. You must first expand: x² + x − 6 = 6, then rearrange to x² + x − 12 = 0, then factor and solve.

Section 12.8

Solving Quadratics by Square Roots

Some quadratic equations have no x term — only x² and a constant. Equations of the form x² = k or (x + d)² = k can be solved directly by taking square roots, which is faster than factoring. The key principle is the square root property.

Square Root Property

If x² = k and k ≥ 0, then x = √k or x = −√k.

Written compactly: x = ±√k

The ± symbol is essential. Both the positive and negative square roots are valid solutions. Omitting the negative root is one of the most common errors in solving quadratics.

Example 12.8.1
Simple square root solving
  • ProblemSolve x² = 49.
  • Solvex = ±√49 = ±7
  • Check(7)² = 49 ✓    (−7)² = 49 ✓
Answer: x = 7 or x = −7.
Example 12.8.2
Isolating the squared term first
  • ProblemSolve 3x² − 27 = 0.
  • Solve 3x² = 27 x² = 9 x = ±3
  • Check3(9) − 27 = 0 ✓    3(9) − 27 = 0 ✓
Answer: x = 3 or x = −3.
Example 12.8.3
Solving with a binomial squared
  • ProblemSolve (x − 3)² = 16.
  • ThinkThe square root property applies whenever an expression is squared and set equal to a number. Take square roots of both sides, keeping ±.
  • Solve x − 3 = ±√16 = ±4 Case 1: x − 3 = 4 → x = 7 Case 2: x − 3 = −4 → x = −1
  • Check(7 − 3)² = 16 ✓    (−1 − 3)² = (−4)² = 16 ✓
Answer: x = 7 or x = −1.
Example 12.8.4
No real solutions
  • ProblemSolve x² + 9 = 0.
  • Solvex² = −9There is no real number whose square is −9. Squaring any real number gives a result ≥ 0.
Answer: No real solutions. (Algebra II will introduce complex numbers that allow solutions to this type of equation.)
Section 12.9

Completing the Square as an Idea

Completing the square is an algebraic technique for rewriting a quadratic expression so that it contains a perfect square trinomial — a trinomial that factors into (x + d)² for some value d. At the Algebra I level, you need to understand what completing the square means and why it works, because it is the foundation of the quadratic formula and of vertex form. A full mastery of completing the square as a solution procedure is a central topic of Algebra II Chapter 3.

Perfect Square Trinomials

A perfect square trinomial is a trinomial of the form x² + 2dx + d² = (x + d)². The pattern: the constant term is always the square of half the coefficient of x. For example, in x² + 6x + 9: half of 6 is 3, and 3² = 9. Adding 9 completes the square.

ExpressionCompleting Value (half of b)²Perfect Square Form
x² + 6x(6/2)² = 9(x + 3)²
x² − 10x(−10/2)² = 25(x − 5)²
x² + 4x(4/2)² = 4(x + 2)²
Example 12.9.1
Completing the square to find vertex form
  • ProblemRewrite f(x) = x² − 6x + 5 in vertex form by completing the square.
  • ThinkGroup the x² and x terms. Find the completing value: (−6/2)² = 9. Add and subtract 9 to keep the expression equivalent.
  • Solve f(x) = (x² − 6x) + 5 = (x² − 6x + 9) − 9 + 5 = (x − 3)² − 4 Vertex form: f(x) = (x − 3)² − 4. Vertex: (3, −4).
  • CheckThis matches the vertex found in Example 12.3.1 using x = −b/(2a) = −(−6)/2 = 3. ✓
Answer: f(x) = (x − 3)² − 4. Vertex (3, −4).

Section 12.10

The Quadratic Formula

Factoring works beautifully when the quadratic factors nicely over the integers. But many quadratic equations — in fact, most real-world quadratics — do not factor cleanly. The quadratic formula solves any quadratic equation, regardless of whether it factors.

x = [ −b ± √(b² − 4ac) ] / (2a)

This formula applies to any quadratic equation written in standard form ax² + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0. The ± sign means the formula produces two values:

x₁ = (−b + √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a)     x₂ = (−b − √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a)

The formula is derived by applying completing the square to the general standard form — a process you will study fully in Algebra II. The formula is not magic; it is compressed algebra.

Steps for Using the Quadratic Formula

1. Write the equation in standard form: ax² + bx + c = 0.

2. Identify a, b, and c.

3. Compute the discriminant b² − 4ac first.

4. Substitute into the formula.

5. Simplify the square root.

6. Compute both solutions using + and −.

Example 12.10.1
Quadratic formula — two rational solutions
  • ProblemSolve x² − 5x + 4 = 0 using the quadratic formula.
  • Solve a = 1, b = −5, c = 4.
    x = [ −(−5) ± √((−5)² − 4·1·4) ] / (2·1) = [ 5 ± √(25 − 16) ] / 2 = [ 5 ± √9 ] / 2 = [ 5 ± 3 ] / 2 x = (5 + 3)/2 = 4 or x = (5 − 3)/2 = 1
  • Check(4)² − 5(4) + 4 = 0 ✓    (1)² − 5(1) + 4 = 0 ✓
Answer: x = 4 or x = 1.
Example 12.10.2
Quadratic formula — irrational solutions
  • ProblemSolve x² + 4x + 1 = 0.
  • ThinkThis does not factor over the integers. Use the formula with a = 1, b = 4, c = 1.
  • Solve x = [ −4 ± √(16 − 4) ] / 2 = [ −4 ± √12 ] / 2 = [ −4 ± 2√3 ] / 2 = −2 ± √3 Solutions: x = −2 + √3 ≈ −0.27   or   x = −2 − √3 ≈ −3.73
Answer: x = −2 + √3 or x = −2 − √3.
Example 12.10.3
Rearranging before applying the formula
  • ProblemSolve 3x² = 2x + 8.
  • ThinkMove all terms to one side first to get standard form.
  • Solve 3x² − 2x − 8 = 0 a = 3, b = −2, c = −8.
    x = [ 2 ± √(4 + 96) ] / 6 = [ 2 ± √100 ] / 6 = [ 2 ± 10 ] / 6 x = 12/6 = 2 or x = −8/6 = −4/3
  • Check3(4) = 12 and 2(2) + 8 = 12 ✓    3(16/9) = 16/3 and 2(−4/3) + 8 = 16/3 ✓
Answer: x = 2 or x = −4/3.
Tip — Order of Operations in the Formula

Compute b² − 4ac first. Then simplify the square root. Then calculate both values separately. Working through the formula in strict order prevents the most common arithmetic mistakes.

Section 12.11

Discriminant and Number of Solutions

Before solving a quadratic, you can quickly determine how many real solutions to expect by computing the discriminant — the expression under the radical in the quadratic formula:

D = b² − 4ac
If D…Number of Real SolutionsGraph Description
D > 0Two distinct real solutionsParabola crosses x-axis at two points
D = 0One real solution (repeated root)Parabola touches x-axis at exactly one point (vertex)
D < 0No real solutionsParabola does not touch the x-axis

When D < 0, the equation has no real solutions because no real number has a negative square. In Algebra II Chapter 4, you will learn about complex numbers, which allow equations like x² = −9 to have solutions.

Example 12.11.1
Using the discriminant
  • ProblemFind the discriminant of each and state the number of real solutions.

    (a) x² − 4x + 4 = 0    (b) 2x² − 3x − 5 = 0    (c) x² + x + 3 = 0
  • Solve (a) a = 1, b = −4, c = 4: D = (−4)² − 4(1)(4) = 16 − 16 = 0 → one real solution.
    Indeed, x² − 4x + 4 = (x − 2)² = 0 gives x = 2 (repeated).

    (b) a = 2, b = −3, c = −5: D = 9 − 4(2)(−5) = 9 + 40 = 49 → two real solutions.

    (c) a = 1, b = 1, c = 3: D = 1 − 12 = −11 → no real solutions.
Answer: (a) D = 0, one solution; (b) D = 49, two solutions; (c) D = −11, no real solutions.

Section 12.12

Quadratic Word Problems

Many real-world situations lead naturally to quadratic equations or require interpreting a quadratic function. The key is recognizing when a situation involves a squared quantity, a product of two variable expressions, or a known physics formula. Use the Read → Represent → Solve → Check → Explain method throughout.

Projectile Motion

The height of any object launched upward or dropped near Earth's surface follows a quadratic model. In feet and seconds, the general formula is:

h(t) = −16t² + v₀t + h₀

where h₀ is the initial height in feet and v₀ is the initial upward velocity in feet per second. In metric units (meters and seconds), the coefficient −16 is replaced by −4.9.

Example 12.12.1
Maximum height of a projectile
  • ProblemA ball is launched upward from ground level with an initial velocity of 64 feet per second. Its height is h(t) = −16t² + 64t. Find the maximum height and when it occurs.
  • Thinka = −16 < 0, so the parabola opens downward and the vertex is the maximum. Use the vertex formula.
  • Solve t = −64 / (2 · (−16)) = −64 / (−32) = 2 seconds h(2) = −16(4) + 64(2) = −64 + 128 = 64 feet
  • CheckThe ball starts at height 0 and rises to 64 feet — a positive height reached 2 seconds after launch. Physically reasonable. ✓
Answer: The ball reaches a maximum height of 64 feet at 2 seconds after launch.
Example 12.12.2
When does the ball land?
  • ProblemUsing h(t) = −16t² + 64t, find when the ball returns to the ground.
  • ThinkThe ball is on the ground when h(t) = 0. Set the function equal to zero and solve.
  • Solve −16t² + 64t = 0 −16t(t − 4) = 0 t = 0 or t = 4
  • Checkt = 0 is the launch time; t = 4 is the landing. Notice that 4 seconds is exactly symmetric: the ball peaks at t = 2 and lands at t = 4 (two seconds after the peak). ✓
Answer: The ball returns to the ground after 4 seconds.
Example 12.12.3
Area problem with a quadratic equation
  • ProblemA rectangular garden has a length that is 3 meters more than its width. The area is 40 square meters. Find the dimensions.
  • RepresentLet w = width. Then length = w + 3. Area = w(w + 3) = 40.
  • Solve w² + 3w = 40 w² + 3w − 40 = 0 (w + 8)(w − 5) = 0 w = −8 or w = 5 Discard w = −8 (width cannot be negative). So w = 5 meters, length = 8 meters.
  • Check5 × 8 = 40 ✓    Length (8) is 3 more than width (5) ✓
Answer: Width = 5 meters, length = 8 meters.
Example 12.12.4
Revenue model — finding maximum revenue
  • ProblemA company sells handmade notebooks. When the price is p dollars, the number sold per week is 200 − 10p. Find the price that maximizes revenue and the maximum weekly revenue.
  • RepresentRevenue = price × quantity sold.
    R(p) = p(200 − 10p) = 200p − 10p²
  • Solve a = −10, b = 200. This is quadratic with a < 0, so it has a maximum.
    p = −200 / (2 · (−10)) = −200 / (−20) = 10 dollars R(10) = 200(10) − 10(100) = 2000 − 1000 = 1000 dollars
  • CheckAt p = $10, quantity sold = 200 − 100 = 100 notebooks. Revenue = 10 × 100 = $1,000. ✓
Answer: A price of $10 per notebook maximizes revenue. Maximum weekly revenue is $1,000.
Section 12.13

Common Quadratic Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting ± when taking square roots
x² = 36 → x = 6

Correct: x² = 36 → x = 6 or x = −6. Both (6)² and (−6)² equal 36. Always write ±.

Mistake 2 — Applying Zero Product Property when the product is not zero
(x + 3)(x − 2) = 12 → x + 3 = 12 or x − 2 = 12

Correct: Expand, rearrange to standard form (x² + x − 6 = 12 → x² + x − 18 = 0), then factor or use the quadratic formula.

Mistake 3 — Sign error in the vertex formula
For f(x) = x² − 8x + 3:   x = (−8)/(2) = −4

Correct: x = −(−8)/(2·1) = 8/2 = 4. The formula is −b/(2a), not b/(2a). The negative sign is always part of the formula.

Mistake 4 — Misidentifying a, b, c when equation is not in standard form
For 3 = x² + 5x: reading c = 3 without rearranging

Correct: Move all terms left first. x² + 5x − 3 = 0. Now c = −3, not +3.

Mistake 5 — Sign error with b in the quadratic formula
For b = −5: writing −b = −5 in the formula

Correct: If b = −5, then −b = −(−5) = +5. Write it out explicitly: −(−5) = 5.

Mistake 6 — Not simplifying the square root in the quadratic formula
Leaving √12 unsimplified in the answer instead of writing 2√3

Correct: Always simplify. √12 = √(4·3) = 2√3. Simplified radical form is standard unless a decimal approximation is specifically requested.

Mistake 7 — Discarding a valid negative solution
Solving and getting x = 5 or x = −3, then writing only x = 5 because "negatives don't count"

Correct: In pure equation solving, both solutions are valid. Only discard a negative value when the context makes it meaningless (e.g., a negative width, a negative time before launch).

Mistake 8 — Combining unlike terms in the discriminant
b² − 4ac = b² − 4a times c, then adding b² and 4ac as like terms

Correct: b² − 4ac is a single numerical computation. Calculate b², calculate 4ac, then subtract. Do not try to factor or simplify prematurely.

Mistake 9 — Assuming a parabola always crosses the x-axis
Expecting every quadratic to have two x-intercepts

Correct: A parabola can have zero, one, or two x-intercepts. Always check the discriminant. If D < 0, the parabola does not touch the x-axis and the equation has no real solutions.

Mistake 10 — Confusing zeros, roots, and x-intercepts
Treating "zero of f" and "solution to f(x) = 0" as different procedures

Correct: They are the same. A zero of f is a value where f(x) = 0. A root is a solution to the equation ax² + bx + c = 0. An x-intercept is where the graph crosses the x-axis. All three describe the same values.

Chapter 12 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1A quadratic function has the form f(x) = ax² + bx + c with a ≠ 0. Unlike linear functions, the rate of change is not constant — it grows or shrinks, causing the graph to curve. The coefficient a controls direction and width; c is the y-intercept.
  • Sec 12.2The graph of a quadratic is a parabola. If a > 0, it opens upward and has a minimum vertex. If a < 0, it opens downward and has a maximum vertex. Every parabola is symmetric about its axis of symmetry.
  • Sec 12.3The vertex is at x = −b/(2a). Substitute this x-value into f(x) to get the y-coordinate. The axis of symmetry is the vertical line x = −b/(2a). The vertex represents the minimum or maximum output value.
  • Sec 12.4Graph a quadratic from a table by choosing x-values centered on the axis of symmetry, computing outputs, plotting ordered pairs, and connecting with a smooth curve. The table will show symmetric values about the vertex.
  • Sec 12.5Efficient graphing uses key features: direction, axis of symmetry, vertex, y-intercept, its mirror point, and x-intercepts. Plot all key points, then connect with a smooth parabola.
  • Sec 12.6The x-intercepts (zeros, roots) are where f(x) = 0. A parabola can have two, one, or no x-intercepts. Zeros are symmetric about the axis of symmetry; their midpoint equals the axis x-value.
  • Sec 12.7To solve by factoring: write in standard form, factor, apply the Zero Product Property. The ZPP requires the product to equal zero. Check every solution by substitution.
  • Sec 12.8Use the square root property when the equation has the form x² = k or (expression)² = k. Always write ±√k. Isolate the squared expression first. If k < 0, there are no real solutions.
  • Sec 12.9Completing the square adds (b/2)² to create a perfect square trinomial. This converts standard form to vertex form f(x) = a(x − h)² + k, where (h, k) is the vertex. The full procedure is mastered in Algebra II.
  • Sec 12.10The quadratic formula x = [−b ± √(b² − 4ac)] / (2a) solves any quadratic in standard form. Identify a, b, c; compute the discriminant first; simplify the square root; calculate both solutions.
  • Sec 12.11The discriminant D = b² − 4ac tells you the number of real solutions before solving: D > 0 → two solutions; D = 0 → one repeated solution; D < 0 → no real solutions (parabola does not cross x-axis).
  • Sec 12.12Quadratic models appear in projectile motion (h = −16t² + v₀t + h₀), area (length × width = area), and revenue (R = price × quantity). Always define variables, set up the equation, check context, and explain results.
  • Sec 12.13Ten common mistakes: omitting ±, misusing ZPP, sign errors in the vertex formula, wrong a/b/c identification, sign errors in the formula, unsimplified radicals, discarding valid negatives, incorrect discriminant interpretation, assuming two x-intercepts always exist, and confusing zeros/roots/x-intercepts.
  • Sec 12.14Algebra II deepens every topic in this chapter: vertex form, transformations, completing the square as procedure, deriving the formula, complex solutions, and advanced optimization. Quadratics also connect to Geometry, Physics, Finance, and Computer Science.

Key Terms Review

quadratic function
A function of the form f(x) = ax² + bx + c, where a, b, c are real numbers and a ≠ 0. The squared term is the defining feature.
standard form
The form ax² + bx + c for a quadratic function. All terms on one side; the squared term leads.
parabola
The smooth, symmetric, U-shaped (or hill-shaped) curve that is the graph of any quadratic function.
vertex
The highest or lowest point on a parabola; the turning point. Located at (−b/(2a), f(−b/(2a))).
axis of symmetry
The vertical line x = −b/(2a) that passes through the vertex and divides the parabola into two mirror halves.
minimum
The lowest point of a parabola that opens upward (when a > 0). The vertex in this case.
maximum
The highest point of a parabola that opens downward (when a < 0). The vertex in this case.
zero of a function
A value of x where f(x) = 0. For a quadratic, zeros are the x-intercepts when they are real numbers.
root
A solution to a quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0. Same as a zero of the related function.
Zero Product Property
If A · B = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0. This property makes factoring a valid solution method when one side equals zero.
completing the square
Adding (b/2)² to a quadratic expression to create a perfect square trinomial. Used to find vertex form and derive the quadratic formula.
vertex form
f(x) = a(x − h)² + k, where (h, k) is the vertex. An alternative to standard form that makes graphing and transformations transparent.
quadratic formula
x = [−b ± √(b² − 4ac)] / (2a). Solves any quadratic equation in standard form. Derived by completing the square on the general form.
discriminant
D = b² − 4ac. The expression under the radical in the quadratic formula. Determines the number and type of solutions without requiring the full formula.
quadratic equation
An equation of the form ax² + bx + c = 0 with a ≠ 0. Can have zero, one, or two real solutions.

Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 10 developed polynomials as organized algebraic expressions. You learned to add, subtract, and multiply them, and you practiced recognizing special products such as the square of a sum, the square of a difference, and the product of a sum and a difference. Multiplication took separate factors and combined them into a single polynomial.

This chapter reverses that direction. Factoring is the process of rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler expressions. Where multiplication asks, what do I get when I multiply these factors together? factoring asks, what factors multiply together to give me this polynomial?

That reversal is more powerful than it might appear. Factoring allows you to simplify expressions, identify structure that is otherwise hidden, and — most importantly — solve equations that cannot be solved by the methods of Chapter 3. When you reach Chapter 12, you will use factoring to find the solutions of quadratic equations, which describe parabolas, projectile paths, area problems, and dozens of real-world situations.

Opening Question

A rectangle has an area described by the expression 6x² + 9x. You need to find possible dimensions for the rectangle — a length and a width whose product equals this area expression. Can you find them? This is a factoring problem. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer it immediately, and factor far more complex expressions as well.

Why This Chapter Matters

Factoring appears throughout mathematics, science, engineering, finance, and computing. When mathematicians study polynomial equations, they factor to find solutions. When engineers analyze stress or motion, they simplify polynomial expressions by factoring. When programmers optimize algorithms, they factor time-complexity expressions. When financial analysts model revenue, they factor to identify break-even points.

For you right now, the most immediate consequence is this: factoring is the key that unlocks quadratic equations. Without factoring, solving x² + 5x + 6 = 0 requires guessing. With factoring, you will rewrite it as (x + 2)(x + 3) = 0 and read off the solutions directly.

Foundations: What Factoring Is and Why It Works
What Factoring Means · The Distributive Property · Greatest Common Factor
Section 11.1

What Factoring Means

In Chapter 10, you multiplied two binomials and produced a trinomial. For example:

(x + 3)(x + 4) = x² + 7x + 12

The left side shows the factored form — the expression written as a product of factors. The right side shows the expanded form — the polynomial after multiplication. Factoring reverses this process. You start with the expanded form and recover the factored form:

x² + 7x + 12 = (x + 3)(x + 4)

The two forms are equivalent. But the factored form reveals structure that the expanded form hides. In equations, that clarity is essential.

Four Questions: A Factoring Strategy

1. Is there a greatest common factor? Look at all the terms.

2. How many terms does the polynomial have? Two, three, or four each suggest different methods.

3. Does the polynomial match a special pattern? Difference of squares and perfect square trinomials have recognizable forms.

4. Can the remaining polynomial be factored further? After applying one method, check each factor.

Factoring Is Not Guessing

Factoring is structural recognition: reading the polynomial, identifying its features, and selecting the method those features suggest. The four questions above become a strategy, and the strategy becomes reliable.

Section 11.2

Factoring and the Distributive Property

The distributive property states: a(b + c) = ab + ac. When you apply it left to right, you expand. Factoring uses the same property right to left: starting from ab + ac, you rewrite it as a(b + c). This is the foundation of every factoring technique in this chapter.

DirectionProcessExample
Left to rightExpansion (distribute)6(2x + 3) = 12x + 18
Right to leftFactoring (factor out)12x + 18 = 6(2x + 3)
Why Checking Always Works

Because factoring is the reverse of multiplication, you can always verify your factored form by multiplying it out. If the result matches the original polynomial, the factoring is correct. This check should become a habit — most factoring errors are invisible until you check.

Section 11.3

Greatest Common Factor

The greatest common factor (GCF) of a polynomial is the largest expression that divides evenly into every term. Finding the GCF is always the first step in any factoring problem — even when you plan to use a more advanced method later. Removing the GCF first simplifies everything that follows.

To find the GCF: (1) Find the greatest common factor of the numerical coefficients. (2) For each variable, take the lowest power that appears in every term. Multiply these together.

Example 11.3.1
Factoring Out the GCF
  • ProblemFactor 18x⁴ − 12x³ + 6x².
  • ThinkCoefficients: 18, 12, 6. GCF of coefficients is 6. Variable powers: x⁴, x³, x². Lowest power is x². GCF = 6x².
  • Solve18x⁴ − 12x³ + 6x² = 6x²(3x² − 2x + 1)
  • CheckDistribute: 6x² · 3x² − 6x² · 2x + 6x² · 1 = 18x⁴ − 12x³ + 6x² ✓
Answer: 6x²(3x² − 2x + 1)
Example 11.3.2
GCF with Multiple Variables
  • ProblemFactor 15a³b² − 10a²b³ + 5ab.
  • ThinkGCF of coefficients (15, 10, 5) is 5. Lowest power of a across all terms: a¹. Lowest power of b: b¹. GCF = 5ab.
  • Solve15a³b² − 10a²b³ + 5ab = 5ab(3a²b − 2ab² + 1)
  • CheckDistribute 5ab back: 15a³b² − 10a²b³ + 5ab ✓
Answer: 5ab(3a²b − 2ab² + 1)
Student Warning — Don't Write Zero

When every term is divided by the GCF and the result is 1, do not leave a blank or write 0 inside the parentheses. Write 1. For example: 4x² + 4x = 4x(x + 1). The last term inside is 1, not 0.

Negative Leading Coefficient

When the first term has a negative coefficient, it is conventional to factor out a negative GCF so the leading term inside the parentheses is positive. For example: −6x³ + 9x² − 3x = −3x(2x² − 3x + 1). Always verify by distributing.

Sections 11.4 – 11.6

Section 11.4

Factoring by Grouping

Factoring by grouping is used on polynomials with four terms. Split the four terms into two pairs, factor the GCF from each pair separately, then recognize that both pairs share a common binomial factor and factor it out.

Grouping Method — Four Steps

1. Split the polynomial into two groups of two terms.

2. Factor the GCF out of each pair.

3. Identify the common binomial factor.

4. Factor out the common binomial.

Example 11.4.1
Factoring by Grouping
  • ProblemFactor x³ + 2x² + 3x + 6.
  • ThinkNo GCF across all four terms. Group the first two and last two terms, then factor each pair.
  • Solve Step 1: (x³ + 2x²) + (3x + 6) Step 2: x²(x + 2) + 3(x + 2) Step 3: Both pairs share the factor (x + 2) Step 4: (x + 2)(x² + 3)
  • CheckDistribute: (x + 2)(x² + 3) = x³ + 3x + 2x² + 6 = x³ + 2x² + 3x + 6 ✓
Answer: (x + 2)(x² + 3)
Example 11.4.2
Grouping — Two Variables
  • ProblemFactor xy − 3y + 2x − 6.
  • ThinkGroup so that each pair shares a factor.
  • Solve Step 1: (xy − 3y) + (2x − 6) Step 2: y(x − 3) + 2(x − 3) Step 3: Common factor is (x − 3) Step 4: (x − 3)(y + 2)
  • Check(x − 3)(y + 2) = xy + 2x − 3y − 6 = xy − 3y + 2x − 6 ✓
Answer: (x − 3)(y + 2)
When Grouping Doesn't Work

If your two pairs produce different binomial factors, try rearranging the terms into different groups. If no grouping produces matching binomial factors, the polynomial may not be factorable by grouping.

Section 11.5

Factoring Trinomials with Leading Coefficient 1

Trinomials of the form x² + bx + c factor into two binomials (x + p)(x + q) when two numbers p and q satisfy: p + q = b (their sum equals the middle coefficient) and pq = c (their product equals the constant term).

Sign of cSign of bSigns of p and q
PositivePositiveBoth positive
PositiveNegativeBoth negative
NegativeEitherOne positive, one negative
Example 11.5.1
Factoring a Simple Trinomial
  • ProblemFactor x² + 7x + 12.
  • ThinkNeed two numbers with product 12 and sum 7. Factor pairs of 12: 1×12 (sum 13), 2×6 (sum 8), 3×4 (sum 7) ✓
  • Solvex² + 7x + 12 = (x + 3)(x + 4)
  • Check(x + 3)(x + 4) = x² + 4x + 3x + 12 = x² + 7x + 12 ✓
Answer: (x + 3)(x + 4)
Example 11.5.2
Trinomial with a Negative Constant
  • ProblemFactor x² + 2x − 15.
  • ThinkProduct is −15 (so signs differ), sum is 2. Pairs: 5 × (−3) = −15, and 5 + (−3) = 2 ✓
  • Solvex² + 2x − 15 = (x + 5)(x − 3)
  • Check(x + 5)(x − 3) = x² − 3x + 5x − 15 = x² + 2x − 15 ✓
Answer: (x + 5)(x − 3)
Example 11.5.3
Both Numbers Negative
  • ProblemFactor x² − 8x + 15.
  • ThinkProduct is +15, sum is −8. Both numbers must be negative. (−3)(−5) = 15, (−3) + (−5) = −8 ✓
  • Solvex² − 8x + 15 = (x − 3)(x − 5)
  • Check(x − 3)(x − 5) = x² − 5x − 3x + 15 = x² − 8x + 15 ✓
Answer: (x − 3)(x − 5)
Student Warning — Signs Matter in Trinomials

When the constant term is negative, the signs of the two numbers in the binomials will be different. Be careful: (x + 5)(x − 3) ≠ (x − 5)(x + 3). These are different factorings with different middle terms. Always verify by multiplying.

Section 11.6

Factoring Trinomials with Leading Coefficient Greater Than 1

When the leading coefficient is not 1, the trinomial has the form ax² + bx + c where a ≠ 1. The AC method (factoring by grouping on a trinomial) is the most reliable approach.

The AC Method — Five Steps

1. Identify a, b, and c from ax² + bx + c.

2. Compute the product ac.

3. Find two numbers m and n such that m + n = b and m · n = ac.

4. Rewrite the middle term bx as mx + nx.

5. Factor the resulting four-term polynomial by grouping.

Example 11.6.1
AC Method
  • ProblemFactor 2x² + 7x + 3.
  • Thinka = 2, b = 7, c = 3. ac = 6. Find two numbers with product 6 and sum 7: 1 × 6 = 6, 1 + 6 = 7 ✓
  • Solve 2x² + x + 6x + 3 = (2x² + x) + (6x + 3) = x(2x + 1) + 3(2x + 1) = (2x + 1)(x + 3)
  • Check(2x + 1)(x + 3) = 2x² + 6x + x + 3 = 2x² + 7x + 3 ✓
Answer: (2x + 1)(x + 3)
Example 11.6.2
AC Method with Negative Middle Term
  • ProblemFactor 3x² − 10x + 8.
  • Thinka = 3, b = −10, c = 8. ac = 24. Need product 24, sum −10. Both negative: (−4)(−6) = 24, −4 + (−6) = −10 ✓
  • Solve 3x² − 4x − 6x + 8 = (3x² − 4x) + (−6x + 8) = x(3x − 4) + (−2)(3x − 4) = (3x − 4)(x − 2)
  • Check(3x − 4)(x − 2) = 3x² − 6x − 4x + 8 = 3x² − 10x + 8 ✓
Answer: (3x − 4)(x − 2)
Example 11.6.3
AC Method with Negative Constant
  • ProblemFactor 6x² + x − 2.
  • Thinka = 6, b = 1, c = −2. ac = −12. Product −12, sum 1: 4 × (−3) = −12, 4 + (−3) = 1 ✓
  • Solve 6x² + 4x − 3x − 2 = (6x² + 4x) + (−3x − 2) = 2x(3x + 2) + (−1)(3x + 2) = (3x + 2)(2x − 1)
  • Check(3x + 2)(2x − 1) = 6x² − 3x + 4x − 2 = 6x² + x − 2 ✓
Answer: (3x + 2)(2x − 1)
The AC Method vs. Trial and Error

Some students prefer trial and error for trinomials with leading coefficients greater than 1. Trial and error can work on simple examples, but the AC method is more systematic and fails less often. Learn it and use it consistently — it will also serve you in Algebra II.

Section 11.7

Difference of Squares

In Chapter 10, you studied the special product (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b². Factoring reverses it: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b).

To recognize the difference of squares, check all three conditions: (1) the expression has exactly two terms; (2) both terms are perfect squares; (3) the terms are subtracted.

Example 11.7.1
Factoring a Difference of Squares
  • ProblemFactor x² − 49.
  • Thinkx² = (x)² and 49 = 7² are both perfect squares, and they are subtracted. Use a = x, b = 7.
  • Solvex² − 49 = (x + 7)(x − 7)
  • Check(x + 7)(x − 7) = x² − 7x + 7x − 49 = x² − 49 ✓
Answer: (x + 7)(x − 7)
Example 11.7.2
Coefficient in the First Term
  • ProblemFactor 4x² − 25.
  • Think4x² = (2x)² and 25 = 5². This is a difference of squares with a = 2x, b = 5.
  • Solve4x² − 25 = (2x + 5)(2x − 5)
  • Check(2x + 5)(2x − 5) = 4x² − 10x + 10x − 25 = 4x² − 25 ✓
Answer: (2x + 5)(2x − 5)
Example 11.7.3
GCF Then Difference of Squares
  • ProblemFactor 2x² − 50 completely.
  • ThinkGCF of 2x² and 50 is 2. Factor it out first, then check the result.
  • Solve 2x² − 50 = 2(x² − 25) = 2(x + 5)(x − 5)
  • Check2(x + 5)(x − 5) = 2(x² − 25) = 2x² − 50 ✓
Answer: 2(x + 5)(x − 5)
Student Warning — A Sum of Squares Is Prime

The expression x² + 49 does NOT factor over the integers. Only a difference of squares factors with this pattern. Students sometimes write x² + 49 = (x + 7)(x + 7), but multiplying that gives x² + 14x + 49, which is wrong. A sum of squares is prime at this level.

Section 11.8

Perfect Square Trinomials

Chapter 10 introduced the squared binomial products. Factoring reverses them:

Perfect Square Trinomial Patterns

a² + 2ab + b² = (a + b)²

a² − 2ab + b² = (a − b)²

A trinomial is a perfect square when: (1) the first and last terms are perfect squares; (2) both are positive; (3) the middle term equals exactly twice the product of their square roots.

Example 11.8.1
Factoring a Perfect Square Trinomial
  • ProblemFactor x² + 6x + 9.
  • ThinkFirst term: (x)². Last term: (3)². Middle: 2 · x · 3 = 6x ✓. This is a² + 2ab + b² with a = x, b = 3.
  • Solvex² + 6x + 9 = (x + 3)²
  • Check(x + 3)² = x² + 2(x)(3) + 9 = x² + 6x + 9 ✓
Answer: (x + 3)²
Example 11.8.2
Perfect Square with a Minus Sign
  • ProblemFactor 4x² − 12x + 9.
  • ThinkFirst term: (2x)². Last term: (3)². Middle: 2 · 2x · 3 = 12x, with a minus sign. Matches a² − 2ab + b² with a = 2x, b = 3.
  • Solve4x² − 12x + 9 = (2x − 3)²
  • Check(2x − 3)² = 4x² − 12x + 9 ✓
Answer: (2x − 3)²
Section 11.9

Choosing a Factoring Strategy

Decision Process

Step 1: Factor out the GCF first. Always.

Step 2 — 2 terms: Check for difference of squares. If not, it may be prime.

Step 2 — 3 terms: Leading coefficient 1 → sum-product method. Leading coefficient > 1 → AC method. Check for perfect square pattern.

Step 2 — 4 terms: Try factoring by grouping.

Step 3: Check whether any factor can be factored further.

Step 4: Verify by multiplying.

Example 11.9.1
Strategy: GCF then Difference of Squares
  • ProblemFactor 3x³ − 12x completely.
  • ThinkGCF = 3x. Factor it out. Then check the remaining two-term factor for difference of squares.
  • Solve 3x³ − 12x = 3x(x² − 4) = 3x(x + 2)(x − 2)
  • Check3x(x + 2)(x − 2) = 3x(x² − 4) = 3x³ − 12x ✓
Answer: 3x(x + 2)(x − 2)
Example 11.9.2
Strategy: GCF then Trinomial
  • ProblemFactor 2x³ + 14x² + 24x completely.
  • ThinkGCF = 2x. Factor it out. The remaining trinomial has leading coefficient 1 — use sum-product method on x² + 7x + 12.
  • Solve 2x³ + 14x² + 24x = 2x(x² + 7x + 12) = 2x(x + 3)(x + 4)
  • Check2x(x + 3)(x + 4) = 2x(x² + 7x + 12) = 2x³ + 14x² + 24x ✓
Answer: 2x(x + 3)(x + 4)
Section 11.10

Checking by Multiplying

Every factoring problem has a built-in check: multiply the factored form and see whether you recover the original polynomial. There are two approaches:

Distribution — multiply the factors step by step and simplify. This is definitive.

Substitution — choose a simple value like x = 1 or x = 2, evaluate both the original and the factored form, and verify they agree. This is faster but not a complete proof. Use distribution for final verification.

Example 11.10.1
Checking by Both Methods
  • ProblemVerify that x² − x − 6 = (x − 3)(x + 2).
  • SubstitutionLet x = 4. Left: 16 − 4 − 6 = 6. Right: (1)(6) = 6 ✓ (both agree)
  • Distribution(x − 3)(x + 2) = x² + 2x − 3x − 6 = x² − x − 6 ✓
Answer: Factoring verified by both methods.

Section 11.11

Factoring in Geometry Problems

Polynomial expressions frequently describe area in geometry. When a rectangle's area is expressed as a polynomial, factoring can reveal the dimensions. If the area of a rectangle is given by a polynomial A, then its dimensions are factors of A.

Example 11.11.1
Finding Dimensions of a Rectangle
  • ProblemThe area of a rectangle is x² + 8x + 15. Find possible expressions for the length and width.
  • ThinkFactor the area expression. Need two numbers with product 15 and sum 8: 3 and 5.
  • Solvex² + 8x + 15 = (x + 3)(x + 5)
  • Check(x + 3)(x + 5) = x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15 ✓
Answer: Length = (x + 5) and width = (x + 3)
Example 11.11.2
Opening Question Answered — Area with GCF
  • ProblemThe area of a rectangle is 6x² + 9x. Find the dimensions. (This was the chapter's opening question.)
  • ThinkFactor out the GCF. GCF of 6x² and 9x is 3x.
  • Solve6x² + 9x = 3x(2x + 3)
  • Check3x · 2x + 3x · 3 = 6x² + 9x ✓
Answer: Length = (2x + 3) and width = 3x
Example 11.11.3
Volume — Three Dimensions
  • ProblemA box has volume 2x³ + 10x² + 12x. Factor completely to find possible dimensions.
  • ThinkGCF = 2x. Factor out, then factor the remaining trinomial.
  • Solve 2x³ + 10x² + 12x = 2x(x² + 5x + 6) = 2x(x + 2)(x + 3)
  • Check2x(x + 2)(x + 3) = 2x(x² + 5x + 6) = 2x³ + 10x² + 12x ✓
Answer: Dimensions could be 2x, (x + 2), and (x + 3)
Section 11.12

Factoring in Equation Solving

Zero-Product Property

If A · B = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0 (or both).

The only way a product can equal zero is if at least one factor is zero. This property is specific to zero — it does not hold for any other number. When a polynomial equation is set equal to zero and factored, the zero-product property lets you split it into separate, simpler equations.

Example 11.12.1
Solving by Factoring
  • ProblemSolve x² + 5x + 6 = 0.
  • ThinkThe equation is already set equal to zero. Factor the left side, then apply the zero-product property.
  • Solve x² + 5x + 6 = 0 (x + 2)(x + 3) = 0 x + 2 = 0 or x + 3 = 0 x = −2 or x = −3
  • Checkx = −2: (−2)² + 5(−2) + 6 = 4 − 10 + 6 = 0 ✓   x = −3: 9 − 15 + 6 = 0 ✓
Answer: x = −2 or x = −3
Example 11.12.2
Equation That Must Be Rearranged First
  • ProblemSolve x² = 3x + 18.
  • ThinkThe zero-product property requires the equation to equal zero. Move all terms to one side first.
  • Solve x² − 3x − 18 = 0 (x − 6)(x + 3) = 0 x = 6 or x = −3
  • Checkx = 6: 36 = 18 + 18 ✓   x = −3: 9 = −9 + 18 = 9 ✓
Answer: x = 6 or x = −3
Example 11.12.3
Equation with a GCF
  • ProblemSolve 2x² − 8x = 0.
  • ThinkFactor out the GCF first. Do not divide both sides by x.
  • Solve 2x(x − 4) = 0 2x = 0 or x − 4 = 0 x = 0 or x = 4
  • Checkx = 0: 0 − 0 = 0 ✓   x = 4: 2(16) − 8(4) = 32 − 32 = 0 ✓
Answer: x = 0 or x = 4
Critical Warning — Never Divide by a Variable

A common error is to divide both sides of 2x² = 8x by x to get 2x = 8, which gives only x = 4. Dividing by x loses the solution x = 0. Always move all terms to one side, factor, and apply the zero-product property. Do not divide both sides by a variable.

Section 11.13

Common Factoring Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting the GCF
2x² + 6x + 4 = (x + 2)(x + 1)

The GCF of 2 was not factored out first.

Correct: 2x² + 6x + 4 = 2(x² + 3x + 2) = 2(x + 1)(x + 2)
Mistake 2 — Factoring a Sum of Squares
x² + 16 = (x + 4)(x + 4)

A sum of squares is prime over the integers. Only a difference of squares can be factored with this pattern.

Correct: x² + 16 is prime — it cannot be factored further over the integers.
Mistake 3 — Sign Errors in Trinomial Factoring
x² − 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3)

(x + 2)(x + 3) expands to x² + 5x + 6. The signs are wrong.

Correct: x² − 5x + 6 = (x − 2)(x − 3) [both numbers negative: (−2)(−3) = 6, −2 + (−3) = −5]
Mistake 4 — Leaving a Factorable Expression Inside Parentheses
2x³ − 8x = 2x(x² − 4) [incomplete]

The factor x² − 4 is a difference of squares. The factoring is not finished.

Correct: 2x³ − 8x = 2x(x² − 4) = 2x(x + 2)(x − 2)
Mistake 5 — Wrong Use of the Zero-Product Property
x² + 3x = 10 → x(x + 3) = 10 → x = 10 or x + 3 = 10

The zero-product property only applies when the product equals zero, not 10 or any other value.

Correct: x² + 3x − 10 = 0 → (x + 5)(x − 2) = 0 → x = −5 or x = 2
Mistake 6 — Dropping a Factor
x² + 5x + 6 = (x + 3)

Only one factor is written. A trinomial factors into a product of two binomials.

Correct: x² + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3)
Mistake 7 — Wrong Signs in Difference of Squares
x² − 25 = (x − 5)(x − 5)

(x − 5)(x − 5) = x² − 10x + 25. The difference of squares requires opposite signs.

Correct: x² − 25 = (x + 5)(x − 5)
Section 11.14

Preparing for Quadratic Equations

Chapter 12 introduces quadratic functions and equations. A quadratic function has the form f(x) = ax² + bx + c, and its graph is a parabola. To understand where the parabola crosses the x-axis — the zeros of the function — you need to solve equations of the form ax² + bx + c = 0. The most fundamental method is factoring.

Chapter 12 will also introduce additional methods — solving by square roots, completing the square, and the quadratic formula — for situations where factoring is difficult or impossible. But factoring will always be the first method you try, and the structural recognition you have built in this chapter is the foundation for all of it.

A Preview

The following problems foreshadow Chapter 12. You can solve them now.

Solve x² − 9 = 0: Factor as (x + 3)(x − 3) = 0. Solutions: x = 3 or x = −3.

Solve x² − 6x + 9 = 0: Factor as (x − 3)² = 0. Solution: x = 3 (a repeated root).

Solve 2x² + x − 6 = 0: Factor as (2x − 3)(x + 2) = 0. Solutions: x = 3/2 or x = −2.

Chapter 11 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1–11.2Factoring is the distributive property in reverse. It rewrites a polynomial as a product of factors. The factored and expanded forms are equivalent expressions.
  • Sec 11.3Always factor out the GCF first. The GCF is the greatest shared numerical coefficient combined with the lowest power of each variable that appears in every term.
  • Sec 11.4Factoring by grouping works on four-term polynomials: split into two pairs, factor each pair, identify the shared binomial, and factor it out.
  • Sec 11.5For x² + bx + c, find two numbers whose product is c and whose sum is b, then write (x + p)(x + q). Sign analysis guides which pair to try.
  • Sec 11.6For ax² + bx + c with a ≠ 1, use the AC method: compute ac, find two numbers with product ac and sum b, rewrite the middle term, then factor by grouping.
  • Sec 11.7Difference of squares: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b). Applies only when both terms are perfect squares and they are subtracted. A sum of squares is prime over the integers.
  • Sec 11.8Perfect square trinomials: a² + 2ab + b² = (a + b)² and a² − 2ab + b² = (a − b)². Verify by checking that the middle term equals twice the product of the square roots.
  • Sec 11.9Strategy: GCF first; count terms; match pattern; check for further factoring. The four-step decision process applies to every polynomial.
  • Sec 11.10Verify every factoring by multiplying. Distribution is definitive. Substitution is a quick preliminary check but not a complete proof.
  • Sec 11.11In geometry, area and volume expressions are often polynomials whose factors represent dimensions. Factor to find possible length, width, and height expressions.
  • Sec 11.12Zero-product property: if AB = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0. Set the equation equal to zero, factor, apply the zero-product property, and solve each resulting equation.
  • Sec 11.13Seven recurring errors: missing GCF; factoring a sum of squares; sign errors; incomplete factoring; wrong use of zero-product property; dropping a factor; wrong signs in difference of squares.
  • Sec 11.14Chapter 12 connects factoring to parabola x-intercepts and introduces additional solving methods. All Chapter 11 skills carry forward directly into quadratic equations.

Key Terms Review

factor
One of two or more expressions that are multiplied together to produce a product.
factoring
Rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler expressions; the reverse of multiplication.
greatest common factor
The largest factor shared by every term in a polynomial. May include a numerical coefficient and variable parts.
prime polynomial
A polynomial that cannot be factored further over the integers. For example, x² + 16 is prime.
factoring by grouping
A method for four-term polynomials: pair the terms, factor each pair, then factor out the shared binomial.
leading coefficient
The coefficient of the term with the highest degree in a polynomial.
difference of squares
A binomial of the form a² − b², which factors as (a + b)(a − b). Only a difference, not a sum, fits this pattern.
perfect square trinomial
A trinomial of the form a² + 2ab + b² or a² − 2ab + b², which factors as (a ± b)².
AC method
A systematic method for factoring ax² + bx + c: compute ac, find two numbers with that product and sum b, rewrite the middle term, then factor by grouping.
zero-product property
If A · B = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0. Applies only when a product equals zero, not any other value.

Bridge Note — From Chapter 11 to Chapter 12
Chapter 12: Quadratic Functions and Equations

Chapter 12 introduces quadratic functions and equations, which are among the most important topics in Algebra I and in mathematics as a whole. You already know how to solve quadratic equations by factoring: set equal to zero, factor, and apply the zero-product property. Chapter 12 extends this in several directions.

First, it connects solutions to graphs. A quadratic function f(x) = ax² + bx + c has a U-shaped graph called a parabola. The solutions of f(x) = 0 are exactly the x-intercepts of the parabola. Factoring tells you where the graph crosses the x-axis.

Second, Chapter 12 introduces solving methods for when factoring is difficult or impossible: solving by square roots, completing the square, and the quadratic formula — a universal method that works on every quadratic equation. These extend your ability to solve beyond what factoring alone can reach.

Third, Chapter 12 introduces the discriminant — the expression b² − 4ac. The discriminant tells you, before you solve, whether the equation has two real solutions, one real solution, or no real solutions. Understanding it requires the foundation this chapter has built.

Factoring is the bridge. Everything you practiced here — the patterns, the methods, the habit of checking — carries forward directly into Chapter 12.

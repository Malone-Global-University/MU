Chapter 10 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In the chapters before this one, you worked with algebraic expressions involving one or two terms. You learned to evaluate expressions, combine like terms, apply the distributive property, and use exponent rules. All of that work prepared you for this chapter.

A polynomial is an organized algebraic expression built from terms with whole-number exponents. Polynomials appear throughout algebra, geometry, science, engineering, and finance. When you calculate the area of a rectangle with variable side lengths, write a formula for the height of a launched object, or model a growing quantity, you are often working with a polynomial.

This chapter introduces polynomials as a structured family of algebraic expressions. You will learn to classify them, identify their degree and standard form, and perform the four major operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and special products. By the end of the chapter, you will have the algebraic fluency needed for Chapter 11: Factoring, and eventually for Chapter 12: Quadratic Functions and Equations.

Opening Question

A landscaper is designing a rectangular garden. The length is described as (x + 5) feet and the width as (x + 3) feet, where x is an unknown base measurement. The landscaper wants to know: What is the area of the garden? If a second garden is added alongside the first with area 2x² + 7x square feet, what is the total combined area? How does the total area change as x grows? To answer these questions, you need to know how to multiply and add polynomial expressions. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to do exactly that.

Why This Chapter Matters

Polynomials are among the most useful structures in all of algebra. Their importance comes from their versatility. A polynomial can describe a straight line, a curve, a path through the air, a financial model, or a geometric area. The operations you learn in this chapter — adding, subtracting, and multiplying polynomials — form the computational backbone for factoring in Chapter 11, quadratic equations in Chapter 12, and advanced algebra throughout Algebra II.

More immediately, this chapter will improve your control over algebraic expressions. Working carefully with polynomials trains you to track signs, manage exponents, organize terms, and think about algebraic structure. These are skills that carry into every area of mathematics.

Section 10.1

What a Polynomial Is

An algebraic expression is a combination of numbers, variables, and operations. Not every algebraic expression is a polynomial. Polynomials are a specific, organized family of expressions with clear structural rules.

Definition — Polynomial

A polynomial is an algebraic expression consisting of one or more terms in which:

1. Variables have whole-number exponents (0, 1, 2, 3, …).

2. No variable appears in a denominator.

3. No variable appears under a radical (square root) sign.

4. No variable appears as an exponent.

Examples of Polynomials

3x² + 5x − 7 4x³ − 2x + 1 8 −6x x⁴ + 2x³ − x² + 9x − 11

Examples That Are NOT Polynomials

ExpressionReason Not a Polynomial
3/x + 5Variable in denominator
√x + 2Variable under radical
2ˣ + 1Variable as exponent
x⁻² + 3Negative exponent on variable
Why These Rules Exist

Polynomials can be added, subtracted, and multiplied — and the result is always another polynomial. That closure makes them exceptionally useful. When variables appear in denominators, as exponents, or under radicals, the expressions can behave very differently — with restrictions, asymptotes, or complex domains. Polynomials avoid all of that.

Student Note — Signs and Terms

The subtraction signs in a polynomial belong to the terms that follow them. In 5x³ − 2x² + 7x − 4, the term −2x² has a coefficient of negative 2, not positive 2. Always carry the sign with the coefficient when identifying terms.

Section 10.3

Degree of a Term and Degree of a Polynomial

The degree of a polynomial describes how large its exponents are. Degree is one of the most important structural features of a polynomial because it determines the polynomial's shape when graphed and the way it behaves for large values of x.

The degree of a term is the sum of the exponents of all variables in that term. For single-variable polynomials — the focus in Algebra I — the degree of a term is simply the exponent on the variable. The degree of a polynomial is the highest degree among all its terms.

DegreeNameExample
0Constant7
1Linear3x + 1
2Quadraticx² − 4x + 3
3Cubic2x³ + x
4Quarticx⁴ − 5
5Quintic3x⁵ + x³
Student Note — Constants Have Degree Zero

A constant term like 7 has degree 0, not "no degree." You can think of it as 7x⁰, since x⁰ = 1.

Example 10.3.1
Finding the degree of a polynomial
  • ProblemFind the degree of: a. 6x³ − 2x + 5   b. 4x² − 9   c. −3   d. x⁴ − x³ + 2x² − 7x + 1
  • ThinkIdentify the term with the highest exponent in each polynomial.
  • Solve a. Degrees: 3, 1, 0 → Degree 3 (Cubic) b. Degrees: 2, 0 → Degree 2 (Quadratic) c. One constant → Degree 0 (Constant) d. Degrees: 4,3,2,1,0 → Degree 4 (Quartic)

Section 10.4

Standard Form of a Polynomial

A polynomial is in standard form when its terms are written in descending order of degree: the highest-degree term comes first, and lower-degree terms follow in decreasing order. When a polynomial is in standard form, the leading term is the first term and the leading coefficient is its coefficient.

Standard form makes polynomials easier to compare, add, subtract, and factor. When you organize a polynomial consistently, like terms appear in predictable positions, which reduces errors.

Example 10.4.1
Writing a polynomial in standard form
  • ProblemWrite in standard form, then identify the degree and leading coefficient.
    a. 5 + 3x² − x    b. −4x + 7x³ + 2 − x²
  • ThinkArrange terms from highest to lowest degree. Carry each term's sign with it.
  • Solve a. 3x² − x + 5 Degree: 2 Leading coefficient: 3 b. 7x³ − x² − 4x + 2 Degree: 3 Leading coefficient: 7
  • CheckVerify that each term is in its correct position and no sign was dropped.
Student Warning — Carry the Signs

Negative signs belong to the term that follows them. When reordering, carry the sign with each term. 5 + 3x² − x has a term −x, not +x. Write it as 3x² − x + 5, not 3x² + x + 5.

Section 10.5

Adding Polynomials

Adding polynomials is fundamentally the same as combining like terms. The key rule: only like terms can be combined. Like terms have identical variable parts — the same variables raised to the same exponents. When you add polynomials, you add the coefficients of matching terms and leave the variable parts unchanged.

You may add horizontally (grouping like terms in a single line) or vertically (aligning like terms in columns). Both methods produce the same result.

Example 10.5.1
Adding polynomials — horizontal method
  • ProblemAdd (3x² + 5x − 2) + (4x² − 3x + 7).
  • ThinkRemove parentheses, group like terms by degree, then combine.
  • Solve (3x² + 5x − 2) + (4x² − 3x + 7) = 3x² + 5x − 2 + 4x² − 3x + 7 = (3x² + 4x²) + (5x − 3x) + (−2 + 7) = 7x² + 2x + 5
  • CheckSubstitute x = 1: original: (3+5−2)+(4−3+7) = 6+8 = 14. Result: 7+2+5 = 14. ✓
Answer: 7x² + 2x + 5
Example 10.5.2
Adding polynomials — vertical method with placeholder
  • ProblemAdd (2x³ − 4x + 1) + (5x³ + 3x² − 2x − 6).
  • ThinkAlign by degree. The first polynomial has no x² term; insert 0x² as a placeholder.
  • Solve 2x³ + 0x² − 4x + 1 + 5x³ + 3x² − 2x − 6 ───────────────────── 7x³ + 3x² − 6x − 5
  • Checkx = 1: first: 2−4+1=−1. Second: 5+3−2−6=0. Sum: −1. Result: 7+3−6−5=−1. ✓
Answer: 7x³ + 3x² − 6x − 5
Tip — Zero Placeholder

When one polynomial is missing a term that the other has, insert a zero placeholder such as 0x² to keep columns aligned in the vertical method. This prevents accidentally combining terms of different degrees.

Section 10.6

Subtracting Polynomials

Subtracting polynomials adds one layer of complexity: you must distribute the negative sign to every term of the polynomial being subtracted before combining like terms.

The rule: (A) − (B) = A + (−B). To subtract polynomial B from polynomial A, change every sign in B and then add.

Example 10.6.1
Subtracting polynomials — distributing the negative
  • ProblemSubtract (5x² − 3x + 2) − (2x² + 4x − 7).
  • ThinkDistribute the negative sign across every term of the second polynomial. Sign changes: +2x² → −2x², +4x → −4x, −7 → +7.
  • Solve 5x² − 3x + 2 − 2x² − 4x + 7 = (5x² − 2x²) + (−3x − 4x) + (2 + 7) = 3x² − 7x + 9
  • Checkx = 1: first: 5−3+2=4. Second: 2+4−7=−1. Diff: 4−(−1)=5. Result: 3−7+9=5. ✓
Answer: 3x² − 7x + 9
Example 10.6.2
Subtraction with missing terms
  • ProblemSubtract (3x³ − x + 5) − (x³ + 2x² − 4x + 1).
  • ThinkDistribute the negative sign across all four terms of the second polynomial.
  • Solve 3x³ − x + 5 − x³ − 2x² + 4x − 1 = (3x³ − x³) + (−2x²) + (−x + 4x) + (5 − 1) = 2x³ − 2x² + 3x + 4
Answer: 2x³ − 2x² + 3x + 4
Student Warning — Change Every Sign

The most common error in polynomial subtraction is failing to distribute the negative sign to every term. Students often correctly change the sign of the first term inside the parentheses but leave the remaining terms unchanged. Write out every sign change before you begin combining.

Before multiplying polynomials with multiple terms, be fluent with multiplying monomials. The product rule from Chapter 9: when multiplying with the same base, add the exponents: xᵐ · xⁿ = xᵐ⁺ⁿ. Multiply the coefficients and add the exponents on the variable parts.

Multiplying monomials

  • Solve a. (3 · 5)(x²⁺⁴) = 15x⁶ b. (−2 · 4)(x³⁺¹) = −8x⁴ c. (6 · (−3))(x²⁺⁵) = −18x⁷
a. 15x⁶   b. −8x⁴   c. −18x⁷

Multiplying three monomials

Section 10.8

Multiplying a Monomial by a Polynomial

To multiply a monomial by a polynomial with more than one term, apply the distributive property: multiply the monomial by each term of the polynomial separately.

a(b + c + d) = ab + ac + ad

This is the same distributive property from Chapter 1 — only now you are distributing a monomial with a variable and an exponent.

Example 10.8.1
Distributing a monomial
  • ProblemMultiply 3x(x² − 4x + 5).
  • ThinkDistribute 3x to each of the three terms inside the parentheses.
  • Solve 3x · x² − 3x · 4x + 3x · 5 = 3x³ − 12x² + 15x
  • Checkx = 1: inside: 1−4+5=2. Monomial: 3. Product: 6. Result: 3−12+15=6. ✓
3x³ − 12x² + 15x
Example 10.8.2
Distributing a negative monomial
  • ProblemMultiply −2x²(4x³ − x + 7).
  • ThinkDistribute −2x² to each term. The negative sign must reach every term.
  • Solve −2x² · 4x³ −(−2x²) · x + (−2x²) · 7 = −8x⁵ + 2x³ − 14x²
−8x⁵ + 2x³ − 14x²
Student Warning — Distribute to Every Term

When distributing a negative monomial, every term inside the parentheses changes sign. Students sometimes only change the first term. Work through every term in order and count them to make sure none was skipped.

Section 10.9

Multiplying Binomials

Multiplying two binomials requires distributing each term of the first binomial across the entire second binomial. The result is a polynomial with up to four terms, which usually simplifies to a trinomial.

The formula: (a + b)(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd. Every term in the first binomial multiplies every term in the second, producing four products.

The FOIL Method

F — First terms: multiply the first terms of each binomial

O — Outer terms: multiply the outermost terms

I — Inner terms: multiply the innermost terms

L — Last terms: multiply the last terms of each binomial

FOIL is not a magical rule — it is a way to remember to multiply every pair of terms exactly once. Then combine any like terms.

Example 10.9.1
Multiplying binomials with FOIL
  • ProblemMultiply (x + 3)(x + 5).
  • ThinkApply FOIL. Write each of the four products before combining.
  • Solve F: x · x = x² O: x · 5 = 5x I: 3 · x = 3x L: 3 · 5 = 15 x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15
  • Checkx = 2: (5)(7) = 35. Result: 4+16+15 = 35. ✓
x² + 8x + 15
Example 10.9.2
Multiplying binomials with subtraction
  • ProblemMultiply (x − 4)(x + 6).
  • ThinkTreat −4 as the second term of the first binomial. The negative sign travels with it.
  • Solve F: x · x = x² O: x · 6 = 6x I: −4 · x = −4x L: −4 · 6 = −24 x² + 6x − 4x − 24 = x² + 2x − 24
x² + 2x − 24
Example 10.9.3
Multiplying binomials with leading coefficients
  • ProblemMultiply (2x + 3)(3x − 5).
  • Solve F: 2x · 3x = 6x² O: 2x · (−5) = −10x I: 3 · 3x = 9x L: 3 · (−5) = −15 6x² − 10x + 9x − 15 = 6x² − x − 15
6x² − x − 15

The Area Model as an Alternative

Some students find an area model useful for multiplying binomials. Think of the product as the area of a rectangle, where each dimension is divided into two parts. For (x + 3)(x + 5), draw a rectangle with horizontal side split into x and 5, and vertical side split into x and 3. The four interior regions represent the four products.

Total area: x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15. Each region is visible and no product is missed.

Student Note — FOIL is for Two Binomials Only

FOIL applies specifically to the product of two binomials. When multiplying a binomial by a trinomial, or a trinomial by a trinomial, you must extend the distribution more carefully. FOIL is a useful shortcut for the two-term case only.

Section 10.10

Special Products

Certain binomial products occur so frequently in algebra that their results are worth recognizing as patterns. Learning these special product formulas saves time, prevents errors, and — crucially — prepares you to reverse them in Chapter 11 when factoring.

Special Product Formulas

(a + b)(a − b) = a² − b²     Difference of Squares

(a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²    Square of a Sum

(a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b²    Square of a Difference

Special Product 1: Difference of Squares

When two binomials have the same first term and the same second term but with opposite signs, the product is a difference of two perfect squares. The middle terms cancel because −ab + ab = 0.

Example 10.10.1
Difference of squares
  • ProblemMultiply (x + 7)(x − 7).
  • ThinkRecognize the pattern: same terms, opposite signs. Apply the formula with a = x, b = 7.
  • Solve(x + 7)(x − 7) = x² − 7² = x² − 49
  • CheckFOIL: x² − 7x + 7x − 49 = x² − 49. ✓
x² − 49
Example 10.10.2
Difference of squares with coefficients
  • ProblemMultiply (3x + 5)(3x − 5).
  • ThinkHere a = 3x and b = 5.
  • Solve(3x)² − 5² = 9x² − 25
9x² − 25

Special Product 2: Square of a Sum

When you square a binomial sum, the result is a perfect square trinomial: the square of the first term, twice the product of the two terms, and the square of the second term. The middle term 2ab comes from ab + ab in the FOIL expansion.

Example 10.10.3
Square of a sum
  • ProblemExpand (x + 4)².
  • ThinkApply the formula with a = x, b = 4.
  • Solve(x + 4)² = x² + 2(x)(4) + 4² = x² + 8x + 16
  • CheckFOIL: (x+4)(x+4) = x²+4x+4x+16 = x²+8x+16. ✓
x² + 8x + 16

Special Product 3: Square of a Difference

Example 10.10.4
Square of a difference
  • ProblemExpand (x − 6)².
  • ThinkApply the formula with a = x, b = 6. Middle term becomes negative: −2ab.
  • Solve(x − 6)² = x² − 2(x)(6) + 6² = x² − 12x + 36
x² − 12x + 36
Example 10.10.5
Square of a difference with coefficient
  • ProblemExpand (2x − 5)².
  • ThinkHere a = 2x and b = 5.
  • Solve(2x)² − 2(2x)(5) + 5² = 4x² − 20x + 25
4x² − 20x + 25
Student Warning — The Most Common Squaring Mistake

The single most common special-product error is writing (x + 4)² = x² + 16. This is wrong. Squaring a binomial means multiplying it by itself, not squaring each term separately. The middle term 2ab is always present.

(x + 4)² ≠ x² + 16

(x + 4)² = x² + 8x + 16   ← always include the middle term

Section 10.11

Polynomial Expressions in Geometry

Polynomials arise naturally in geometry whenever lengths, areas, and volumes involve variable measurements. When a rectangle has dimensions expressed as binomials, multiplying them produces a polynomial representing area. The same operations appear in perimeter formulas, bordered-region problems, and combined-shape situations.

Example 10.11.1
Area of a rectangle with variable dimensions
  • ProblemA rectangle has length (x + 7) and width (x + 2). Write a polynomial for its area.
  • ThinkArea = length × width. Multiply the two binomials.
  • Solve A = (x + 7)(x + 2) = x² + 2x + 7x + 14 = x² + 9x + 14
Area: x² + 9x + 14 square units
Example 10.11.2
Area of a bordered region
  • ProblemA large square has side length (x + 5). A small square with side 3 is cut from one corner. Write a polynomial for the remaining area.
  • ThinkFind the area of the large square and subtract the area of the small square.
  • Solve Large square: (x + 5)² = x² + 10x + 25 Small square: 3² = 9 Remaining: x² + 10x + 25 − 9 = x² + 10x + 16
x² + 10x + 16 square units
Example 10.11.3
Perimeter and area of the same rectangle
  • ProblemA rectangle has length (3x + 4) and width (x − 1). Write polynomials for its perimeter and area.
  • ThinkUse P = 2l + 2w for perimeter and A = l × w for area.
  • Solve Perimeter: 2(3x + 4) + 2(x − 1) = 6x + 8 + 2x − 2 = 8x + 6 Area: (3x + 4)(x − 1) = 3x² − 3x + 4x − 4 = 3x² + x − 4
Perimeter: 8x + 6 units   |   Area: 3x² + x − 4 square units

Section 10.12

Polynomial Models in Context

Polynomials can describe many real-world quantities — not only geometric shapes, but also revenue, profit, projectile height, population growth, and more. The key skill is translating a verbal description into a polynomial expression and interpreting what the polynomial tells you about the situation.

Example 10.12.1
Revenue model — expanding a product
  • ProblemA school sells tickets to a play. The number of tickets sold is modeled by (200 − 5p), where p is the price per ticket. Write a polynomial expression for total revenue.
  • ThinkRevenue = price × number sold.
  • SolveR = p(200 − 5p) = 200p − 5p²
  • InterpretThe revenue polynomial −5p² + 200p is quadratic in p. As price grows beyond a certain point, the drop in tickets sold causes revenue to fall. Chapter 12 will study this type of model in depth.
R = −5p² + 200p
Example 10.12.2
Combined area of two rooms
  • ProblemRoom 1: (x + 8) by (x + 4) feet. Room 2: (x + 3) by (x + 2) feet. Write a polynomial for total floor area.
  • Solve Room 1: (x + 8)(x + 4) = x² + 12x + 32 Room 2: (x + 3)(x + 2) = x² + 5x + 6 Total: 2x² + 17x + 38
2x² + 17x + 38 square feet
Example 10.12.3
Evaluating a profit model
  • ProblemMonthly profit: P(x) = −x² + 80x − 1200, where x = units produced. Find profit at x = 40.
  • Solve P(40) = −(40)² + 80(40) − 1200 = −1600 + 3200 − 1200 = 400
Profit at 40 units: $400

Section 10.13

Common Polynomial Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting to Distribute the Negative Sign
(3x² + 5x − 2) − (x² − 4x + 1) = 3x² + 5x − 2 − x² − 4x + 1 Correct: 3x² + 5x − 2 − x² + 4x − 1 = 2x² + 9x − 3 Every sign inside the parentheses changes when subtracting.
Mistake 2 — Squaring a Binomial Without the Middle Term
(x + 5)² = x² + 25 Correct: (x + 5)² = x² + 10x + 25 Squaring a binomial means multiplying it by itself. Always write (x + 5)(x + 5) and FOIL.
Mistake 3 — Adding Exponents When Adding Like Terms
3x² + 4x² = 7x⁴ Correct: 3x² + 4x² = 7x² When adding like terms, add coefficients only. The exponent stays the same. Exponents are only added when multiplying (Product Rule), not when adding.
Mistake 4 — Combining Unlike Terms
5x² + 3x = 8x³ Correct: 5x² + 3x cannot be simplified further. x² and x are different degrees — they are unlike terms and cannot be combined.
Mistake 5 — Dropping a Term When Distributing
3x(x² + 4x − 2) = 3x³ + 12x² Correct: 3x(x² + 4x − 2) = 3x³ + 12x² − 6x Count the terms inside before distributing. Verify your result has that many terms.
Mistake 6 — Incomplete FOIL (Skipping Outer or Inner)
(x + 3)(x + 5) = x² + 15 Correct: (x + 3)(x + 5) = x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15 FOIL requires all four products. The Outer and Inner terms (5x and 3x) were skipped.
Mistake 7 — Misidentifying the Degree
3x² + 5x − 7 is linear because it contains an x term. Correct: The degree is 2 (quadratic). Degree is determined by the highest exponent, not by the presence of any particular term.

Section 10.14

Preparing for Factoring

You have now learned how to build polynomials by adding, subtracting, and multiplying. In Chapter 11, you will learn to factor polynomials — to work in the reverse direction.

Every factoring problem is a multiplication problem read backward. You know that (x + 3)(x + 5) = x² + 8x + 15. Factoring asks the reverse: given x² + 8x + 15, can you find the two binomials whose product it equals? You will find (x + 4)(x + 5) — and verify it by multiplying back.

Factoring is one of the most powerful techniques in algebra. It allows you to rewrite complex expressions in simpler forms, solve polynomial equations by finding where a polynomial equals zero, and understand the roots and graphs of functions. In Chapter 12, you will use factoring to solve quadratic equations — to find the exact values of x where a parabola crosses the x-axis.

Skills to Review Before Chapter 11

To succeed in Chapter 11, be confident with: identifying greatest common factors from lists of integers; multiplying binomials using FOIL and the area model; recognizing the special product patterns; and combining like terms carefully with correct signs. If any of these feel uncertain, return to Sections 10.5 through 10.10 for review.

Preview — Chapter 11 Factoring Strategies

Chapter 11 will teach four major strategies: (1) Greatest common factor, (2) Factoring trinomials with leading coefficient 1, (3) Factoring trinomials with leading coefficient greater than 1, and (4) Difference of squares and perfect square trinomial patterns. Each builds directly on what you practiced in this chapter.

Chapter 10 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 10.1A polynomial is an expression with terms having whole-number exponents and no variables in denominators, under radicals, or as exponents. These rules ensure polynomials are closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication.
  • Sec 10.2Polynomials are classified by number of terms: monomial (1), binomial (2), trinomial (3), and polynomial (4+). Always simplify before classifying — like terms must be combined first.
  • Sec 10.3The degree of a term is the sum of its variable exponents. The degree of a polynomial is the highest degree among its terms. Special names: degree 0 (constant), 1 (linear), 2 (quadratic), 3 (cubic), 4 (quartic).
  • Sec 10.4Standard form writes terms in descending order of degree. The leading term has the highest degree; its coefficient is the leading coefficient. Carry signs with terms when reordering.
  • Sec 10.5Adding polynomials means combining like terms. Use the horizontal method (grouping by degree) or the vertical method (aligning columns). Insert zero placeholders when a polynomial is missing a degree.
  • Sec 10.6Subtracting polynomials requires distributing the negative sign to every term of the subtracted polynomial. Every sign inside the parentheses changes. This is the most common source of errors in polynomial subtraction.
  • Sec 10.7Multiplying monomials: multiply coefficients and add exponents using the product rule. With multiple factors, multiply all coefficients together and add all matching exponents.
  • Sec 10.8Multiplying a monomial by a polynomial uses the distributive property: multiply the monomial by every term inside. Negative monomials change every sign. Count terms to verify all were distributed.
  • Sec 10.9Multiplying two binomials produces four products: FOIL (First, Outer, Inner, Last). Combine any like terms. The area model makes all four products visible as regions of a rectangle.
  • Sec 10.10Three special products: (a+b)(a−b) = a²−b² (difference of squares); (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²; (a−b)² = a²−2ab+b². Squaring a binomial always produces a three-term result — never just two terms.
  • Sec 10.11Polynomial operations model geometric quantities: area of rectangles and squares with variable dimensions uses multiplication; perimeter uses distribution and combining; bordered regions use subtraction.
  • Sec 10.12Polynomials model real-world quantities including revenue, profit, height, and combined area. Evaluating a polynomial model means substituting a specific value and simplifying. Quadratic models will be studied fully in Chapter 12.
  • Sec 10.13Seven common mistakes: incomplete sign distribution, squaring without middle term, adding exponents instead of coefficients, combining unlike terms, dropping terms in distribution, incomplete FOIL, and misidentifying degree.
  • Sec 10.14Factoring is multiplication in reverse. Every product you computed in this chapter is a factored form waiting to be recognized. The special products are especially important for Chapter 11: Factoring.

Key Terms Review

polynomial
An algebraic expression with one or more terms having whole-number exponents, no variables in denominators, no variables under radicals, no variables as exponents.
monomial
A polynomial with exactly one term: a number, variable, or product of numbers and variables.
binomial
A polynomial with exactly two unlike terms.
trinomial
A polynomial with exactly three unlike terms.
term
A single number, variable, or product of numbers and variables, separated from other terms by + or −.
coefficient
The numerical factor of a variable term. In −3x², the coefficient is −3. In x, the coefficient is 1.
degree of a term
The sum of the exponents of all variables in that term. In 4x³, degree is 3. Constants have degree 0.
degree of a polynomial
The highest degree among all terms in the polynomial. Determines the polynomial's classification.
leading term
The term with the highest degree when a polynomial is written in standard form.
leading coefficient
The coefficient of the leading term in standard form.
standard form
A polynomial written with terms in descending order of degree, from highest to lowest.
like terms
Terms with exactly the same variable(s) raised to the same exponent(s). Only like terms may be added or subtracted.
FOIL
A method for multiplying two binomials: First, Outer, Inner, Last. Produces four products before combining like terms.
difference of squares
The pattern (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b². The middle terms cancel because they are additive inverses.
perfect square trinomial
The result of squaring a binomial: (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b² or (a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b².
constant term
A term with no variable, having degree 0. In 4x² + 3x − 7, the constant term is −7.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 11: Factoring

Everything you practiced in this chapter — multiplying monomials, distributing, applying FOIL, and recognizing special product patterns — has prepared you to work in reverse.

Chapter 11: Factoring begins with the same polynomials you have been building and asks you to disassemble them. Given x² + 10x + 24, you will find (x + 4)(x + 6). Given 9x² − 25, you will recognize the difference of squares and factor it as (3x + 5)(3x − 5). Given 4x³ − 8x² + 12x, you will factor out the greatest common monomial factor of 4x.

After Chapter 11, Chapter 12 will use factoring as a primary tool for solving quadratic equations. When you solve x² + 8x + 15 = 0 by writing (x + 3)(x + 5) = 0 and finding x = −3 or x = −5, you will be using the skills from both this chapter and the next together.

The progression is: multiply → factor → solve → model. You have completed the first step.

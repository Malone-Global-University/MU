Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most situations in mathematics, science, and everyday life do not involve a single condition. A business must consider both cost and revenue. A traveler plans a route that satisfies both time and distance limits. A scientist studies two variables that must both satisfy a physical law. When two or more conditions hold at the same time, mathematics captures that situation as a system.

Chapter 8 teaches you to think about multiple conditions simultaneously. You will learn to find the values that satisfy two equations at once, recognize when no such values exist, and discover when infinitely many values work. You will extend this thinking to inequalities — situations where solutions are not single points but entire regions of a plane.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to solve systems by graphing, substitution, and elimination; interpret what each type of solution means; write and solve systems from real situations; graph linear inequalities in two variables; and describe feasible regions defined by systems of inequalities.

Opening Question

A small theater sells two types of tickets: student tickets for $6 each and adult tickets for $10 each. On one evening, 200 tickets are sold and the total revenue is $1,520. How many student tickets were sold, and how many adult tickets were sold?

There are two unknowns here. You could write one equation for the total number of tickets and another for the total revenue. You need both equations to find both unknowns. That is a system of equations. By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to solve this problem — and many others like it.

Why This Chapter Matters

Linear equations in one variable give you a single condition with a single unknown. But most real problems involve at least two unknowns and at least two conditions. Systems are not just a tool for harder problems — they are a more accurate model of how the world actually works.

Understanding systems prepares you for Algebra II systems with three or more variables; geometry problems involving intersecting lines; science applications such as mixture problems and equilibrium models; economics and finance applications such as break-even analysis and budget constraints; computer science, where constraint systems are central to optimization; and statistics, where regression lines model relationships between two variables.

Section 8.1

What a System Represents

One Condition Is Not Always Enough

Throughout Chapters 3 and 7, you worked with one equation in one or two variables. A single linear equation in two variables has infinitely many solutions — every point on the line satisfies it.

But what if you know more than one thing about a situation? Suppose you know that two numbers add up to 10. That gives you x + y = 10. There are infinitely many pairs that satisfy this — for example, (3, 7), (0, 10), or (5.5, 4.5). You cannot identify a single unique answer yet.

Now suppose you also know that one number is four more than twice the other: y = 2x + 4. Each equation alone has infinitely many solutions. But together, they describe two separate conditions that must both hold at the same time. The pair (x, y) you are looking for must satisfy both equations simultaneously. When you bring two equations together and ask for a common solution, you have created a system of linear equations.

System Notation

A system is written using a left brace to indicate the equations are considered together:

{ x + y = 10

{ y = 2x + 4

The word simultaneously is critical. Solving a system means finding a pair of values that makes both equations true at the same time — not solving each equation separately.

Graphical Meaning

Every linear equation in two variables corresponds to a line in the coordinate plane. A system of two equations corresponds to two lines. The solution, if it exists, is the point where those two lines intersect — the one point that lies on both lines simultaneously.

Section 8.2

Solutions to Systems

An ordered pair (x, y) is a solution to a system if and only if it satisfies every equation in the system. Satisfying one equation but not the other is not enough.

Example 8.2.1
Checking a proposed solution
  • ProblemDetermine whether (2, 5) is a solution to the system: 3x + y = 11 and x − y = −3.
  • ThinkSubstitute x = 2 and y = 5 into both equations and check each one.
  • Solve Check equation 1: 3(2) + 5 = 6 + 5 = 11 ✓ Check equation 2: 2 − 5 = −3 ✓
  • CheckBoth equations are satisfied when x = 2, y = 5.
Answer: Yes, (2, 5) is a solution to the system.
Example 8.2.2
A pair that fails one equation
  • ProblemDetermine whether (4, 1) is a solution to the system: 2x − y = 7 and x + 3y = 10.
  • ThinkSubstitute and check both equations. Do not stop after the first one.
  • Solve Check equation 1: 2(4) − 1 = 8 − 1 = 7 ✓ Check equation 2: 4 + 3(1) = 4 + 3 = 7 ≠ 10 ✗
  • CheckThe first equation is satisfied, but the second is not.
Answer: No, (4, 1) is not a solution. It satisfies only one equation.
Critical Caution

Do not stop checking after the first equation is satisfied. A solution to a system must satisfy every equation. Students who check only one equation often report wrong answers.

Section 8.3

Solving Systems by Graphing

One natural way to solve a system is to graph both lines and look for their intersection. The point of intersection is the solution — the one point that lies on both lines. The graphing method is especially useful for visualizing the relationship between two equations, or for estimating a solution quickly. It is less precise when the intersection involves fractional coordinates.

Procedure: Solving by Graphing

1. Write both equations in slope-intercept form y = mx + b if not already done.

2. Graph both lines on the same coordinate plane.

3. Identify the point where the lines intersect.

4. Check the intersection point in both original equations.

5. Write the solution as an ordered pair.

Example 8.3.1
Solving by graphing — slope-intercept form
  • ProblemSolve the system: y = 2x − 1 and y = −x + 5.
  • ThinkBoth equations are already in slope-intercept form. Graph each line using the y-intercept and slope, then find where they cross.
  • Solve y = 2x − 1: y-intercept (0, −1), slope 2 → also (1, 1) y = −x + 5: y-intercept (0, 5), slope −1 → also (1, 4)
  • Check At (2, 3): y = 2(2) − 1 = 3 ✓ y = −(2) + 5 = 3 ✓
Answer: (2, 3). Both x = 2 and y = 3 satisfy both equations simultaneously.
Diagram Instruction — Example 8.3.1

Draw a coordinate plane. Plot the line y = 2x − 1 using points (0, −1) and (1, 1), extending in both directions. Label it. Plot y = −x + 5 using points (0, 5) and (1, 4), extending in both directions. Label it. Mark the intersection at (2, 3) with a filled dot and label it "solution: (2, 3)".

Example 8.3.2
Rewriting before graphing
  • ProblemSolve: 2x + y = 6 and x − y = 0.
  • ThinkRewrite both in slope-intercept form before graphing.
  • Solve 2x + y = 6 → y = −2x + 6 x − y = 0 → y = x y = −2x + 6: y-intercept (0, 6), slope −2 y = x: y-intercept (0, 0), slope 1 Lines intersect at (2, 2).
  • Check 2(2) + 2 = 6 ✓ 2 − 2 = 0 ✓
Answer: (2, 2).
Always Check Algebraically

A graph gives a visual estimate, but coordinates can be hard to read precisely. Always verify the answer you read from the graph by substituting into both original equations.

Section 8.4

Intersecting Lines

When the two lines in a system have different slopes, they will always intersect at exactly one point. This is the most common situation in Algebra I systems, and it is called an independent, consistent system. Consistent means a solution exists. Independent means the solution is unique.

Geometrically, two lines with different slopes cannot be parallel. They must cross at one and only one point in the entire coordinate plane.

Example 8.4.1
Confirming an intersection from slopes
  • ProblemWithout graphing, determine whether the system y = 3x + 1 and y = −2x + 6 has a unique solution.
  • ThinkCompare the slopes of both equations.
  • Solve First equation: slope = 3 Second equation: slope = −2 The slopes are different.
  • CheckDifferent slopes → the lines are not parallel → they must intersect at exactly one point.
Answer: The system has exactly one solution. The lines intersect at one point in the coordinate plane.
Section 8.5

No Solution and Infinitely Many Solutions

Parallel Lines: No Solution

When two lines have the same slope but different y-intercepts, they are parallel. Parallel lines never intersect. There is no ordered pair that lies on both lines simultaneously. A system with no solution is called inconsistent.

If you try to solve an inconsistent system algebraically, you will reach a contradiction — a false statement such as 0 = 5. That contradiction is the algebraic signal that no solution exists.

Example 8.5.1
Recognizing no solution
  • ProblemDetermine the number of solutions for: y = 2x + 3 and y = 2x − 4.
  • ThinkCompare slopes and y-intercepts.
  • Solve Both equations have slope 2. y-intercepts are 3 and −4 — different. → The lines are parallel.
  • CheckSubstituting: 2x + 3 = 2x − 4 → 3 = −4. This is a contradiction.
Answer: No solution. The system is inconsistent. The lines are parallel and never intersect.

Coincident Lines: Infinitely Many Solutions

When two equations describe the same line — the same slope and the same y-intercept — every point on that line is a solution to both equations. The system has infinitely many solutions and is called a dependent system.

Algebraically, the process produces a statement that is always true, such as 0 = 0, meaning the two equations are equivalent.

Example 8.5.2
Recognizing infinitely many solutions
  • ProblemDetermine the number of solutions for: y = 3x − 2 and 6x − 2y = 4.
  • ThinkRewrite the second equation in slope-intercept form and compare.
  • Solve 6x − 2y = 4 −2y = −6x + 4 y = 3x − 2
  • CheckBoth equations are y = 3x − 2. They describe the same line. Substituting: 4 = 4. Always true.
Answer: Infinitely many solutions. The equations describe the same line. Every point on y = 3x − 2 is a solution.

Do Not Confuse the Signals

A contradiction like 3 = −4 or 0 = 5 means no solution. A true statement like 0 = 0 or 4 = 4 means infinitely many solutions. These signals go in opposite directions. Check your algebra when you get either result — but if the work is correct, trust the signal.

Section 8.6

Solving Systems by Substitution

Graphing shows you the solution visually, but it has limits. If the intersection involves fractions or decimals, reading it from a graph is imprecise. The substitution method uses one equation to express one variable in terms of the other, then substitutes that expression into the second equation. The result is a single equation in one variable, which you already know how to solve.

Procedure: Solving by Substitution

1. Choose one equation and solve it for one variable.

2. Substitute the expression into the other equation.

3. Solve the resulting one-variable equation.

4. Substitute the value back to find the other variable.

5. Write the solution as an ordered pair.

6. Check in both original equations.

Example 8.6.1
Basic substitution — equation already solved for y
  • ProblemSolve: y = 3x − 1 and 2x + y = 9.
  • ThinkThe first equation is already solved for y. Substitute 3x − 1 in place of y in the second equation.
  • Solve 2x + (3x − 1) = 9 5x − 1 = 9 5x = 10 x = 2 Find y: y = 3(2) − 1 = 5
  • Check Equation 1: 5 = 3(2) − 1 = 5 ✓ Equation 2: 2(2) + 5 = 9 ✓
Answer: (2, 5).
Example 8.6.2
Solving for a variable first
  • ProblemSolve: 3x + y = 7 and x − 2y = −4.
  • ThinkNeither equation is solved for a variable. Solve the first equation for y — it has a coefficient of 1 on y.
  • Solve From equation 1: y = 7 − 3x Substitute into equation 2: x − 2(7 − 3x) = −4 x − 14 + 6x = −4 7x = 10 x = 10/7 y = 7 − 3(10/7) = 49/7 − 30/7 = 19/7
  • Check Equation 1: 3(10/7) + 19/7 = 30/7 + 19/7 = 49/7 = 7 ✓ Equation 2: 10/7 − 2(19/7) = 10/7 − 38/7 = −28/7 = −4 ✓
Answer: (10/7, 19/7).
Example 8.6.3
Substitution revealing no solution
  • ProblemSolve: y = 4x + 2 and 8x − 2y = 3.
  • ThinkSubstitute the first equation into the second.
  • Solve 8x − 2(4x + 2) = 3 8x − 8x − 4 = 3 −4 = 3
  • CheckThis is a contradiction. The algebra is correct.
Answer: No solution. The system is inconsistent; the two lines are parallel.
Choosing Which Variable to Isolate

Choose the variable that is easiest to isolate — the one with no coefficient or with a coefficient of 1 or −1. This minimizes fractions in your work and keeps the algebra cleaner.

Section 8.7

Solving Systems by Elimination

The elimination method — also called the addition method — removes one variable by adding or subtracting the two equations. If the coefficients of one variable are opposites, adding the equations eliminates that variable entirely. This method is especially powerful when both equations are in standard form Ax + By = C.

Procedure: Solving by Elimination

1. Align both equations with like terms in the same columns.

2. Multiply one or both equations by constants so the coefficients of one variable are opposites.

3. Add the equations to eliminate that variable.

4. Solve the resulting one-variable equation.

5. Substitute back to find the other variable.

6. Write the solution. Check in both original equations.

Example 8.7.1
Direct elimination — coefficients already opposite
  • ProblemSolve: 3x + 2y = 12 and 3x − 2y = 0.
  • ThinkThe y-coefficients are +2 and −2. They are already opposites. Add the equations directly.
  • Solve (3x + 2y) + (3x − 2y) = 12 + 0 6x = 12 x = 2 Substitute: 3(2) + 2y = 12 → 2y = 6 → y = 3
  • Check 3(2) + 2(3) = 6 + 6 = 12 ✓ 3(2) − 2(3) = 6 − 6 = 0 ✓
Answer: (2, 3).
Example 8.7.2
Multiplying one equation before eliminating
  • ProblemSolve: 2x + 3y = 13 and 5x + y = 17.
  • ThinkEliminate y. Multiply the second equation by −3 to make the y-coefficients opposites (+3 and −3).
  • Solve Multiply equation 2 by −3: −3(5x + y) = −3(17) → −15x − 3y = −51 Add to equation 1: (2x + 3y) + (−15x − 3y) = 13 + (−51) −13x = −38 x = 38/13 Find y: 5(38/13) + y = 17 190/13 + y = 17 y = 221/13 − 190/13 = 31/13
  • Check 2(38/13) + 3(31/13) = 76/13 + 93/13 = 169/13 = 13 ✓ 5(38/13) + 31/13 = 190/13 + 31/13 = 221/13 = 17 ✓
Answer: (38/13, 31/13).
Example 8.7.3
Multiplying both equations
  • ProblemSolve: 3x + 4y = 10 and 5x − 6y = 12.
  • ThinkTo eliminate y, use LCM(4, 6) = 12. Multiply equation 1 by 3 and equation 2 by 2.
  • Solve Equation 1 × 3: 9x + 12y = 30 Equation 2 × 2: 10x − 12y = 24 Add: 19x = 54 → x = 54/19 3(54/19) + 4y = 10 162/19 + 4y = 190/19 4y = 28/19 → y = 7/19
  • Check 3(54/19) + 4(7/19) = 162/19 + 28/19 = 190/19 = 10 ✓ 5(54/19) − 6(7/19) = 270/19 − 42/19 = 228/19 = 12 ✓
Answer: (54/19, 7/19).
Example 8.7.4
Elimination revealing infinitely many solutions
  • ProblemSolve: 4x − 2y = 8 and −2x + y = −4.
  • ThinkMultiply the second equation by 2 to create opposite coefficients.
  • Solve Equation 2 × 2: −4x + 2y = −8 Add: (4x − 2y) + (−4x + 2y) = 8 + (−8) 0 = 0
  • Check0 = 0 is always true. The equations are equivalent.
Answer: Infinitely many solutions. Every point on 4x − 2y = 8 is a solution.
Multiply Every Term

When multiplying an equation by a constant, multiply every term — including the constant on the right side. A common error is multiplying only the left side. For example, multiplying 3x − y = 7 by −2 must give −6x + 2y = −14, not −6x + 2y = 7.

Section 8.8

Choosing a Solution Method

You now have three methods for solving systems: graphing, substitution, and elimination. Each has advantages. Part of mathematical fluency is choosing the right tool for a given situation.

SituationRecommended Method
One equation is already solved for x or ySubstitution
One variable has coefficient 1 or −1Substitution
Coefficients are already oppositesElimination
Both equations in standard form Ax + By = CElimination
You need a visual estimate or want to check reasonablenessGraphing
Solution involves large or fractional coordinatesElimination or Substitution (not graphing)
Example 8.8.1
Choosing and solving efficiently
  • ProblemSolve: y = −5x + 3 and 2x + 3y = 1.
  • ThinkThe first equation is already solved for y. Use substitution.
  • Solve 2x + 3(−5x + 3) = 1 2x − 15x + 9 = 1 −13x = −8 x = 8/13 y = −5(8/13) + 3 = −40/13 + 39/13 = −1/13
  • Check −1/13 = −5(8/13) + 3 = −40/13 + 39/13 = −1/13 ✓ 2(8/13) + 3(−1/13) = 16/13 − 3/13 = 13/13 = 1 ✓
Answer: (8/13, −1/13).

Section 8.9

Systems from Word Problems

Word problems involving two unknowns often have two distinct conditions built in. Your job is to define your variables clearly, write one equation for each condition, solve the system, and check the solution in context. This follows the Read → Represent → Solve → Check → Explain method used throughout this course.

Example 8.9.1
The ticket problem (opening question)
  • ReadTwo unknowns: student tickets and adult tickets. Two conditions: 200 tickets total; $1,520 revenue total.
  • Represent Let s = number of student tickets ($6 each) Let a = number of adult tickets ($10 each) s + a = 200 (total tickets) 6s + 10a = 1520 (total revenue)
  • Solve From equation 1: s = 200 − a Substitute: 6(200 − a) + 10a = 1520 1200 − 6a + 10a = 1520 4a = 320 a = 80 s = 200 − 80 = 120
  • Check Total tickets: 120 + 80 = 200 ✓ Total revenue: 6(120) + 10(80) = 720 + 800 = 1520 ✓
Answer: 120 student tickets and 80 adult tickets were sold.
Example 8.9.2
A mixture problem
  • ReadMix a 20% acid solution and a 50% acid solution to make 300 mL of 30% acid solution.
  • Represent Let x = mL of 20% solution Let y = mL of 50% solution x + y = 300 (total volume) 0.20x + 0.50y = 90 (acid: 30% of 300 = 90 mL)
  • Solve x = 300 − y 0.20(300 − y) + 0.50y = 90 60 − 0.20y + 0.50y = 90 0.30y = 30 y = 100 x = 300 − 100 = 200
  • Check Total: 200 + 100 = 300 ✓ Acid: 0.20(200) + 0.50(100) = 40 + 50 = 90 ✓
Answer: 200 mL of 20% solution and 100 mL of 50% solution.
Example 8.9.3
A rate problem
  • ReadTwo trains leave the same station traveling in opposite directions at 55 mph and 75 mph. After how many hours will they be 390 miles apart?
  • Represent Let t = hours of travel (same for both trains). Train 1 travels 55t miles; Train 2 travels 75t miles. 55t + 75t = 390
  • Solve 130t = 390 t = 3
  • Check 55(3) + 75(3) = 165 + 225 = 390 ✓
Answer: The trains will be 390 miles apart after 3 hours.
Strategy for Word Problems

Identify what you do not know and name your variables explicitly. Look for exactly two conditions — typically a total count and a total value, a total amount and a total concentration, or two rate conditions. Write one equation per condition. Solve and check in context.

Section 8.10

Linear Inequalities in Two Variables

Everything so far in this chapter involved equations — statements of equality. But many real situations do not require exact equality. A budget says "spend at most $500." A building code says "allow at least 2 square feet per person." These are inequalities.

A linear inequality in two variables compares a linear expression in x and y to a number using <, >, ≤, or ≥. Unlike a linear equation, which corresponds to a line, a linear inequality corresponds to a half-plane — one entire side of a line in the coordinate plane.

Boundary Lines and Half-Planes

Every linear inequality has a corresponding equation called the boundary line. For y ≤ 2x + 3, the boundary line is y = 2x + 3. The boundary line divides the plane into two half-planes. One half-plane satisfies the inequality; the other does not.

≤ or ≥: boundary line IS included → draw a solid line

< or >: boundary line is NOT included → draw a dashed line

Testing a Point

To determine which half-plane satisfies the inequality, test a point not on the boundary line. The point (0, 0) is usually the easiest to test, unless the boundary line passes through the origin — in that case, use (1, 0) or (0, 1) instead.

Section 8.11

Graphing Linear Inequalities

Procedure: Graphing a Linear Inequality

1. Replace the inequality symbol with = and graph the boundary line (solid or dashed).

2. Choose a test point not on the boundary line.

3. Substitute the test point into the original inequality.

4. If true, shade the half-plane containing the test point. If false, shade the other half-plane.

5. Label the boundary line with its equation.

Example 8.11.1
Graphing a non-strict inequality
  • ProblemGraph: y ≤ 2x − 1.
  • ThinkBoundary line: y = 2x − 1. Symbol is ≤, so use a solid line. Test (0, 0).
  • Solve Graph y = 2x − 1 as a solid line. y-intercept: (0, −1), slope: 2 Test (0, 0): 0 ≤ 2(0) − 1 = −1 → False Shade the half-plane NOT containing (0, 0): shade below and to the right of the line.
  • CheckTest a point in the shaded region, e.g. (2, 0): 0 ≤ 2(2) − 1 = 3. True. ✓
Answer: The solution is all points on or below the line y = 2x − 1, shown by a solid boundary with shading below.

Example 8.11.2
Graphing a strict inequality
  • ProblemGraph: 3x + 2y > 6.
  • ThinkBoundary line: 3x + 2y = 6. Symbol is >, so use a dashed line. Test (0, 0).

  • CheckTest (0, 4): 3(0) + 2(4) = 8 > 6. True. ✓
Answer: All points strictly above the line 3x + 2y = 6. The boundary is dashed — points on the line are not included.
Diagram Instruction — Example 8.11.2

Draw a coordinate plane. Draw a dashed line through (0, 3) and (2, 0). Shade the region above the line. Mark (0, 0) as not in the solution. Label the shaded region "solution region."

Solid vs. Dashed — A Common Error

Using a solid line for a strict inequality (< or >) is one of the most frequent graphing mistakes. Points on the boundary do not satisfy a strict inequality. Draw a dashed line to show that the boundary is excluded.

Section 8.12

Systems of Linear Inequalities

A system of linear inequalities consists of two or more inequalities that must be satisfied at the same time. A solution to such a system is any ordered pair that satisfies every inequality simultaneously. Graphically, you graph each inequality as a shaded half-plane. The solution is the region where all shadings overlap — the intersection of the individual solution regions.

Procedure: Graphing a System of Inequalities

1. Graph the first inequality (boundary, shading).

2. On the same coordinate plane, graph the second inequality.

3. Identify the region where both shadings overlap.

4. Test a point in the overlap region in both inequalities to verify.

5. Shade the overlap clearly and label it the solution region.

Example 8.12.1
A system of two inequalities
  • ProblemGraph the system: y ≥ x + 1 and y < −2x + 6.
  • ThinkGraph each inequality separately, then find the overlapping region.
  • Solve y ≥ x + 1: Boundary y = x + 1, solid line, y-int (0,1), slope 1 Test (0,0): 0 ≥ 1 → False → shade above the line y < −2x + 6: Boundary y = −2x + 6, dashed line, y-int (0,6), slope −2 Test (0,0): 0 < 6 → True → shade below the line Solution: region above the solid line AND below the dashed line.
  • Check Test point (1, 3): y ≥ x + 1: 3 ≥ 1 + 1 = 2 ✓ y < −2x + 6: 3 < −2 + 6 = 4 ✓
Answer: The solution region is the area above the solid line y = x + 1 and below the dashed line y = −2x + 6.
Diagram Instruction — Example 8.12.1

Draw a coordinate plane. Draw a solid line through (0, 1) with slope 1 and shade above it. Draw a dashed line through (0, 6) with slope −2 and shade below it. Mark the overlapping region distinctly and label it "solution region."

Example 8.12.2
A system with three inequalities — bounded region
  • ProblemGraph: x ≥ 0, y ≥ 0, and x + y ≤ 5.
  • Thinkx ≥ 0 and y ≥ 0 together restrict the solution to the first quadrant. The third inequality puts an upper bound on their sum.
  • Solve x ≥ 0: shade right of the y-axis (solid line) y ≥ 0: shade above the x-axis (solid line) x + y ≤ 5: Boundary x + y = 5, solid line: (0,5) to (5,0) Test (0,0): 0 ≤ 5 → True → shade below-left of line Solution: triangular region with vertices (0,0), (5,0), (0,5).
  • CheckTest (1, 2): x ≥ 0 ✓, y ≥ 0 ✓, 1 + 2 = 3 ≤ 5 ✓.
Answer: The solution is the triangular region in the first quadrant, bounded by the axes and the line x + y = 5, including all boundary edges.
Diagram Instruction — Example 8.12.2

Draw a coordinate plane. Shade the first quadrant. Draw a solid line from (0, 5) to (5, 0) and shade below it within the first quadrant. The solution region is the triangle with vertices (0, 0), (5, 0), and (0, 5). Label the three vertices and shade the interior.

Section 8.13

Feasible Regions

In many real-world problems — business, engineering, logistics, resource planning — you must satisfy several constraints at once. The set of all points satisfying all constraints is called the feasible region. The word "feasible" means possible or workable. Every point inside the feasible region represents a combination of values that satisfies every constraint.

A feasible region may be bounded (enclosed within a finite polygon), unbounded (extending infinitely in one or more directions), or empty (the shaded regions do not overlap). The corners of a bounded feasible region are called vertices. In optimization problems, maximum or minimum values of a goal often occur at one of these vertices — a topic you will study formally in later courses.

Example 8.13.1
Identifying a feasible region in context
  • ProblemA school store sells notebooks for $2 and pens for $1. A student wants to spend at most $10 and buy at least 3 items. Write and describe the feasible region.
  • Represent Let n = number of notebooks, p = number of pens. Spending constraint: 2n + p ≤ 10 Item count constraint: n + p ≥ 3 Non-negativity: n ≥ 0, p ≥ 0
  • Solve Graph all four inequalities on the same plane. The feasible region is the overlapping area. Test point (2, 3): 2(2) + 3 = 7 ≤ 10 ✓ 2 + 3 = 5 ≥ 3 ✓ n ≥ 0 ✓, p ≥ 0 ✓
  • CheckBuying 2 notebooks and 3 pens: 5 items, cost $7. Both constraints satisfied.
Answer: The feasible region contains all (n, p) combinations the student can purchase within both constraints. Every point in the region represents a valid purchase plan.
Diagram Instruction — Example 8.13.1

Draw a coordinate plane with n on the horizontal axis and p on the vertical axis. Draw the boundary 2n + p = 10 (from (0,10) to (5,0)) and shade below it. Draw n + p = 3 (from (0,3) to (3,0)) and shade above it. Restrict to the first quadrant. The feasible region is the overlapping area. Label the boundary lines and the feasible region.

Non-Negativity Constraints

Whenever variables represent physical quantities — counts of items, hours worked, gallons used — they cannot be negative. Always include x ≥ 0 and y ≥ 0 when the context requires it. These constraints keep the feasible region in the first quadrant.

Section 8.14

Common System Mistakes

Learning to identify, understand, and avoid common errors is part of developing algebraic fluency. Below are the most frequent mistakes students make when working with systems of equations and inequalities.

Mistake 1 — Checking in Only One Equation
Checked equation 1 only, declared solution found.

A solution to a system must satisfy every equation. Always check the proposed solution in all equations in the system.

Mistake 2 — Forgetting to Multiply the Constant in Elimination
Multiply 3x − y = 7 by −2: result written as −6x + 2y = 7

Correct: −2(3x − y) = −2(7) gives −6x + 2y = −14. Every term on both sides must be multiplied.

Mistake 3 — Stopping After One Variable
Solves for x = 3 and writes "The solution is x = 3."

A solution to a system in two variables is an ordered pair (x, y). Always find both values and write the complete ordered pair.

Mistake 4 — Shading the Wrong Half-Plane
Shades the half-plane that does NOT satisfy the inequality.

Always test a specific point in the region you plan to shade. If the point satisfies the inequality, shade that side. If not, shade the other side.

Mistake 5 — Solid Line for a Strict Inequality
Draws a solid line for y < 3x + 1.

Correct: use a dashed line for < and >. Points on the boundary do not satisfy a strict inequality. Use a solid line only for ≤ and ≥.

Mistake 6 — Swapping Variables in Word Problem Setup
In the ticket problem, writes 10s + 6a = 1520 (swapping the prices).

Always define variables explicitly before writing equations. Then re-read the problem to confirm each equation matches a specific condition.

Mistake 7 — Misreading 0 = 0 as No Solution
Elimination gives 0 = 0; student concludes "no solution."

Correct: 0 = 0 is always true — this signals infinitely many solutions (dependent system). A false statement like 0 = 5 signals no solution (inconsistent system).

Mistake 8 — Substituting Without Parentheses
In y = 4x and 2x + y = 18, substitutes to get 2x + 4 = 18.

Correct: substitute the entire expression: 2x + 4x = 18. The variable expression replaces y, not just the coefficient.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1A system of linear equations is two or more equations considered together. A solution must satisfy all equations simultaneously. Graphically, it is the intersection point of two lines.
  • Sec 8.2Verifying a solution requires checking the ordered pair in every equation. Satisfying one equation does not confirm the answer.
  • Sec 8.3To solve by graphing: rewrite in slope-intercept form, graph both lines, find and verify the intersection. Graphing is most useful for visual understanding or when coordinates are integers.
  • Sec 8.4Lines with different slopes always intersect at exactly one point — a consistent, independent system with one solution.
  • Sec 8.5Parallel lines (same slope, different y-intercept) give no solution — an inconsistent system. Coincident lines (same equation) give infinitely many solutions — a dependent system. Algebraic signals: contradiction = no solution; identity = infinitely many.
  • Sec 8.6Substitution: isolate one variable, substitute the expression into the other equation, solve, and find the second variable. Best when one equation is already solved for a variable.
  • Sec 8.7Elimination: multiply to create opposite coefficients, add equations to eliminate one variable, solve, substitute back. Best for standard-form equations. Multiply every term when scaling an equation.
  • Sec 8.8Graphing is best for visual understanding. Substitution is best when one variable is isolated. Elimination is best for standard-form systems with no easy isolation.
  • Sec 8.9Word problems: define two variables, write one equation per condition, solve the system, check in context, and explain the answer with units.
  • Sec 8.10A linear inequality in two variables has a boundary line dividing the plane into two half-planes. One side satisfies the inequality. Use a solid line for ≤ or ≥; dashed for < or >.
  • Sec 8.11Graph the boundary line, test a point, and shade the appropriate half-plane. Always verify the test point satisfies the original inequality.
  • Sec 8.12A system of inequalities is solved by graphing each inequality and identifying the overlapping region. A point in the overlap satisfies all inequalities simultaneously.
  • Sec 8.13The feasible region is the set of all points satisfying every constraint in a system. It may be bounded, unbounded, or empty. Vertices of a bounded region are important for optimization.
  • Sec 8.14Eight recurring errors: checking only one equation; forgetting to scale the constant; reporting only x; shading the wrong region; using a solid line for a strict inequality; swapping variables in setup; misreading 0 = 0; substituting without parentheses.

Key Terms Review

system of linear equations
Two or more linear equations in the same variables, considered together — requiring a solution that satisfies all equations simultaneously.
solution to a system
An ordered pair (x, y) that satisfies every equation in the system. Graphically, the point where all lines intersect.
consistent system
A system that has at least one solution — either exactly one (independent) or infinitely many (dependent).
inconsistent system
A system with no solution. Graphically, the lines are parallel. Algebraically, elimination or substitution produces a false statement.
independent system
A consistent system with exactly one solution. The two lines have different slopes and intersect at one point.
dependent system
A consistent system with infinitely many solutions. The two equations describe the same line. Algebraically produces a true statement like 0 = 0.
substitution method
A strategy for solving a system by isolating one variable and substituting that expression into the other equation to get a one-variable equation.
elimination method
A strategy for solving a system by multiplying equations to create opposite coefficients, then adding to eliminate one variable.
linear inequality in two variables
A statement comparing a linear expression in x and y to a number using <, >, ≤, or ≥. Its solution set is a half-plane.
boundary line
The line corresponding to the equation form of an inequality. Solid for ≤ or ≥; dashed for < or >. Divides the plane into two half-planes.
half-plane
One of the two regions on either side of a boundary line. One half-plane satisfies the inequality; the other does not.
feasible region
The set of all points satisfying every inequality in a system simultaneously — the intersection of all individual solution regions. May be bounded, unbounded, or empty.

Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Algebra is not only a set of procedures for solving equations or simplifying expressions. It is a language for describing patterns, and one of the most important places where patterns appear is in data. Real-world data comes from measurements, surveys, experiments, records, and observations. It comes from science, finance, medicine, sports, engineering, technology, and everyday life. When we collect data, we want to understand it — to describe what is typical, to see how spread out the values are, to find relationships between variables, and to make predictions about the future.

This chapter closes Algebra I by showing how the algebraic tools you have built throughout this course connect directly to data analysis and mathematical modeling. You will use measures of center and spread to describe distributions, scatter plots and lines of best fit to find relationships, slope and intercept to interpret models in context, residuals to measure model error, two-way tables to organize categorical data, and function families to select the model that best fits a situation. By the end of this chapter, algebra should feel not only useful but necessary for reasoning about real situations in the world.

Opening Question

A school is studying whether students who sleep more tend to earn higher grades. A researcher surveys 20 students, recording the average number of hours each student sleeps per night and that student's most recent exam score. When the data is plotted, a pattern appears: students who sleep more tend to score higher, but the points are scattered and do not fall perfectly on a line. How do we describe that pattern? How do we draw the best line through scattered points? How do we use that line to make a prediction for a student who sleeps 7.5 hours? How do we measure how far off our predictions are from the actual values? These are the questions this chapter answers.

Section 14.1

Why Data Matters in Algebra

Throughout this course, you have worked with equations, functions, graphs, and models. In most of those situations, the numbers were exact — the equations had specific solutions, the functions followed clean rules, the graphs were smooth and predictable. But real-world situations are messier than that. Data rarely falls perfectly on a line or follows an exact equation. Measurements have error. People do not all behave the same way. Natural processes are influenced by many factors at once. Despite this messiness, patterns exist — and algebra gives you tools to find them.

Data TypeDescriptionExample
UnivariateRecords one variable for many subjectsTest scores for 30 students
BivariateRecords two variables for each subjectHours studied and exam score, paired
The Problem-Solving Method in Data Work

Read — What is being measured? What question is being asked?

Represent — Display the data in a table, plot, or graph so patterns become visible.

Solve — Compute statistics, fit a model, or make a prediction.

Check — Does the answer make sense given the data, scale, and context?

Explain — State the result in words, with units, in context. A number without context is not a complete answer.

Section 14.2

Measures of Center

When a data set contains many values, one of the first questions we ask is: what is a typical value? Measures of center summarize a data set by identifying a middle or representative value. The three main measures are the mean, the median, and the mode.

Mean

The mean is the arithmetic average of a data set. Add all values and divide by the number of values. The symbol x̄ (read "x-bar") represents the mean of a sample.

x̄ = (sum of all values) ÷ (number of values)
Example 14.2.1
Finding the Mean
  • ProblemHours studied over seven days: 2, 4, 3, 5, 4, 6, 3. Find the mean.
  • ThinkAdd all seven values, then divide by 7.
  • Solvex̄ = (2 + 4 + 3 + 5 + 4 + 6 + 3) ÷ 7 = 27 ÷ 7 ≈ 3.86
  • CheckValues range from 2 to 6. The mean of approximately 3.86 falls within that range and near the middle.
Answer: The mean study time is approximately 3.86 hours per day.

Median

The median is the middle value when a data set is arranged in order from least to greatest. If the data set has an odd number of values, the median is the single middle one. If even, the median is the average of the two middle values. The median is resistant to outliers.

Example 14.2.2 & 14.2.3
Finding the Median — Odd and Even Counts
  • Odd (A)Data: 12, 7, 15, 9, 11. Ordered: 7, 9, 11, 12, 15. The middle value is 11.
  • Even (B)Data: 14, 8, 20, 11, 5, 17. Ordered: 5, 8, 11, 14, 17, 20. Average the two middle values: (11 + 14) ÷ 2 = 12.5.
Answers: (A) Median = 11. (B) Median = 12.5.

Mode

The mode is the value that appears most often. A data set may have one mode, more than one mode, or no mode. The mode is most useful for categorical data or when a particular value is especially common.

MeasureBest Used WhenSensitive to Outliers?
MeanData is roughly symmetric, no strong outliersYes — pulled toward extremes
MedianData is skewed or contains outliersNo — ignores extreme values
ModeMost common value needed; categorical dataNo
Student Note

Many students assume the mean is always best. It is not. A salary survey that includes a few very high earners will have a mean salary pulled far above what most workers earn. In skewed distributions, the median is a more honest summary.

Section 14.3

Measures of Spread

Two data sets can have the same mean but look completely different in how spread out their values are. Measures of spread describe how much variability exists in the data.

Range

Range = Maximum − Minimum

The range is easy to compute but is affected heavily by outliers, since it depends only on the two most extreme values.

Quartiles and the IQR

The data set can be divided into four equal parts by three values: Q₁ (first quartile, the median of the lower half), Q₂ (the median of the entire data set), and Q₃ (third quartile, the median of the upper half). The interquartile range (IQR) measures the spread of the middle 50 percent of the data and is resistant to outliers.

IQR = Q₃ − Q₁
Example 14.3.2
Finding the IQR
  • ProblemFind the IQR for: 4, 7, 9, 12, 14, 18, 22, 25, 27.
  • ThinkFind the median, then the medians of the lower and upper halves.
  • Solve Median (Q₂) = 14 (5th of 9 values) Lower half: 4, 7, 9, 12 → Q₁ = (7 + 9) ÷ 2 = 8 Upper half: 18, 22, 25, 27 → Q₃ = (22 + 25) ÷ 2 = 23.5 IQR = 23.5 − 8 = 15.5
Answer: IQR = 15.5
Standard Deviation — Concept

Standard deviation measures how far values tend to be from the mean on average. A small standard deviation means data clusters closely around the mean; a large one means data is widely scattered. Formal calculation is developed in the MGU Statistics course. At this level, focus on what it means, not the formula.

Section 14.4

Dot Plots, Histograms, and Box Plots

Numbers alone do not show the full picture. Visual displays help you see shape, center, spread, and outliers all at once.

DisplayBest Used ForWhat It Shows

Box PlotComparing two or more distributionsFive-number summary; skewness and outliers visible at a glance
Example 14.4.3
Constructing a Box Plot
  • Data12, 15, 20, 24, 25, 28, 30, 34, 38, 40
  • Solve Minimum = 12 Q₁ = 20 Median = (25 + 28) ÷ 2 = 26.5 Q₃ = 34 Maximum = 40 IQR = 34 − 20 = 14
  • DiagramDraw a number line from 10 to 45. Draw a rectangle (the box) from 20 to 34. Draw a vertical line at 26.5 inside the box. Extend a whisker left from 20 to 12, and a whisker right from 34 to 40. Label all five summary values.
Five-number summary: 12 | 20 | 26.5 | 34 | 40. IQR = 14.

Section 14.5

Scatter Plots

When you have data about two variables measured on the same subjects, you want to see whether those two variables are related. A scatter plot displays bivariate data by placing one variable on the horizontal axis and the other on the vertical axis, plotting each data pair as a single point.

Example 14.5.1
Constructing and Describing a Scatter Plot
  • DataHours studied and exam scores for 8 students:
Hours Studied (x)12345364
Exam Score (y)6065707580688573
  • DiagramDraw a coordinate plane. Label the horizontal axis "Hours Studied" (0 to 8). Label the vertical axis "Exam Score" (50 to 100). Plot each ordered pair as a dot.
  • DescribeDirection: points rise from left to right — positive association. Form: roughly linear. Strength: fairly tight cluster around an imaginary rising line.
Answer: The scatter plot shows a positive, roughly linear association — more hours studied corresponds to higher exam scores.
Describing a Scatter Plot — Three Questions

Direction — Do points rise (positive) or fall (negative) as x increases?

Form — Do points follow a roughly linear pattern, or do they curve?

Strength — Are points tightly clustered near a line, or widely scattered?

Section 14.6

Association and Correlation

Two variables have an association when the value of one tends to change as the other changes. Correlation refers specifically to linear association and is quantified by the correlation coefficient, denoted r, which always falls between −1 and 1.

Value of rMeaning
r = 1Perfect positive linear correlation — all points on a rising line
r close to 1Strong positive linear correlation
r close to 0Weak or no linear correlation
r close to −1Strong negative linear correlation
r = −1Perfect negative linear correlation — all points on a falling line
Correlation Is Not Causation

Just because two variables are correlated does not mean one causes the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates are positively correlated — not because ice cream causes drowning, but because both are influenced by a third variable: hot weather. When you see correlation in data, your job is to describe the pattern, not to declare a cause.

Section 14.7

Lines of Best Fit

When a scatter plot shows a roughly linear pattern, you can draw a line of best fit (also called a trend line) to model the relationship. This line summarizes the overall direction and center of the pattern. To draw one by hand: draw a line that passes through the middle of the cloud of points, with roughly equal numbers of points above and below it. The line does not need to pass through any specific data points.

Example 14.7.3
Finding the Equation of a Line of Best Fit from Two Points
  • ProblemThe line of best fit passes through (2, 67) and (5, 82). Find its equation.
  • ThinkFind slope using the two points, then apply point-slope form.
  • Solve m = (82 − 67) ÷ (5 − 2) = 15 ÷ 3 = 5 y − 67 = 5(x − 2) y = 5x + 57
  • CheckSubstitute (2, 67): 5(2) + 57 = 67. ✓ Substitute (5, 82): 5(5) + 57 = 82. ✓
Answer: y = 5x + 57
Example 14.7.2
Using the Line for Prediction — Interpolation and Extrapolation
  • ProblemUsing y = 5x + 57, predict the score for (A) a student who studies 5 hours, and (B) a student who studies 10 hours.
  • Solve Ay = 5(5) + 57 = 25 + 57 = 82 (x = 5 is within the data range — interpolation)
  • Solve By = 5(10) + 57 = 107 (x = 10 is beyond the data range — extrapolation)
  • CheckA score of 107 exceeds 100 — impossible on most exams. Extrapolation produced a nonsensical result.
Answer: (A) Predicted score ≈ 82 (reliable). (B) Model predicts 107, but this is an unreliable extrapolation outside the data range.
MGU Library Connection
  • In statistics courses, the line of best fit is computed precisely using least squares regression, which minimizes the sum of squared residuals. This is covered formally in the MGU Statistics course and in Algebra II data sections.
Section 14.8

Interpreting Slope and Intercept in Data

When a linear model fits a data set, the slope and intercept have real-world meaning. Interpreting them correctly in context — with units — is one of the most important skills in applied algebra.

How to Interpret Slope in Context

The slope tells you: For each one-unit increase in x, the predicted value of y changes by m units.

Positive slope → both variables increase together. Negative slope → as one increases, the other decreases.

Example 14.8.1 & 14.8.2
Interpreting Slope and y-Intercept
  • Model Ay = 5x + 57, where x = hours studied and y = exam score.
  • SlopeSlope = 5. For each additional hour studied, the predicted exam score increases by approximately 5 points.
  • Intercepty-intercept = 57. A student who studies 0 hours is predicted to score approximately 57. This may represent a baseline from prior knowledge, or it may simply be where the line crosses when extended — interpret with caution.
  • Model By = −1.2x + 14, where x = hours of TV daily and y = hours of physical activity per week. Slope = −1.2: for each additional hour of TV watched daily, weekly physical activity decreases by approximately 1.2 hours.
Interpretations require units and context. "Slope = 5" is not a complete answer. "For each additional hour studied, the score increases by 5 points" is.
Section 14.9

Residuals as Model Error

No line of best fit passes through every point perfectly. The vertical distance between each data point and the line represents the error of the model at that point. These distances are called residuals.

Residual Formula

Residual = Observed − Predicted = y − ŷ

Positive residual → actual point is above the line (model underestimated). Negative residual → actual point is below the line (model overestimated). Residual = 0 → point falls exactly on the line.

Example 14.9.1
Computing a Residual
  • ProblemModel: y = 5x + 57. A student studied 3 hours and scored 70. Find the residual.
  • Solve ŷ = 5(3) + 57 = 72 Residual = 70 − 72 = −2
  • InterpretThe model predicted 72, but the student actually scored 70. The model overestimated by 2 points. The residual is −2.
Answer: Residual = −2. The model over-predicted by 2 points.
Residual Plots

A residual plot graphs residuals on the vertical axis against x-values on the horizontal axis. A well-fitting linear model will show residuals scattered randomly around zero — no clear pattern. If residuals curve or fan out, the linear model may not be the best choice for the data.

Section 14.10

Two-Way Tables

Not all data is numerical. Sometimes two variables are both categorical — belonging to groups or categories rather than taking numerical values. A two-way table organizes data from two categorical variables by showing the counts for each combination.

Example 14.10.1 & 14.10.2
Reading and Computing Frequencies from a Two-Way Table
  • DataSurvey of 100 students: morning vs. evening preference, and alone vs. group preference.
AloneWith a GroupRow Total
Morning302050
Evening153550
Column Total4555100
  • Joint30 students prefer morning AND studying alone.
  • Marginal45 students prefer studying alone, regardless of time of day.
  • ConditionalOf the 50 evening students, 35 ÷ 50 = 70% prefer working with a group. Of the 50 morning students, 30 ÷ 50 = 60% prefer working alone. The conditional proportions differ — evidence of an association.
Interpretation: Evening students are more likely to prefer group work than morning students (70% vs. 40%). There appears to be an association between time preference and study style preference.
Reading Two-Way Tables

Make sure to read row totals and column totals correctly. The value 30 in a cell does not mean 30% unless you divide by the appropriate total. Always identify whether you want a joint frequency, a marginal frequency, or a conditional frequency before computing.

Section 14.11

Choosing Linear, Quadratic, or Exponential Models

You have studied three major function families. Each describes a different pattern of change. When you have a data set and want to model it, one of your tasks is selecting the function family that best describes the data. Given a table of equally spaced x-values, examine the differences and ratios of y-values.

Model TypeTestFormExample Situation
LinearFirst differences constanty = mx + bConstant speed; monthly savings with fixed deposits
QuadraticSecond differences constanty = ax² + bx + cHeight of a thrown ball; area as side length increases
ExponentialRatios of consecutive y-values constanty = a · bˣPopulation growing at constant rate; radioactive decay
Example 14.11.1
Identifying the Model Type from a Table
  • Table Ax: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 — y: 3, 7, 11, 15, 19. First differences: 4, 4, 4, 4. Constant first differences → linear model. Equation: y = 4x + 3.
  • Table Bx: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 — y: 1, 3, 9, 19, 33. First differences: 2, 6, 10, 14 (not constant). Second differences: 4, 4, 4 (constant) → quadratic model.
  • Table Cx: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 — y: 2, 6, 18, 54, 162. Ratios: 3, 3, 3, 3. Constant ratios → exponential model. Equation: y = 2 · 3ˣ.
Use differences for linear or quadratic; use ratios for exponential.
Section 14.12

Algebraic Models in Science and Finance

Algebraic models appear in nearly every quantitative field. Here are examples of each function family in applied contexts.

Science Applications

Example — Free Fall (Quadratic)
Finding When an Object Hits the Ground
  • ModelAn object dropped from 100 feet: h = −16t² + 100, where h = height in feet and t = time in seconds.
  • Solve Set h = 0: −16t² + 100 = 0 16t² = 100 → t² = 6.25 → t = 2.5 seconds
  • CheckAt t = 2.5: h = −16(6.25) + 100 = −100 + 100 = 0. ✓
Answer: The object hits the ground after 2.5 seconds.
Example — Radioactive Decay (Exponential)
Half-Life Model
  • ModelA substance starts with 200 grams and has a half-life of 3 years: A = 200 · (1/2)^(t/3).
  • SolveAfter 9 years: A = 200 · (1/2)³ = 200 · (1/8) = 25 grams
Answer: 25 grams remain after 9 years.

Finance Applications

Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

A = amount after t years. P = principal. r = annual rate (decimal). n = compounding periods per year. t = time in years.

Example 14.12.1
Compound Interest
  • ProblemDeposit $1,000 at 4% annual interest, compounded annually (n = 1). How much after 5 years?
  • Solve A = 1000(1 + 0.04/1)^(1·5) = 1000(1.04)⁵ (1.04)⁵ ≈ 1.2167 A ≈ 1000 × 1.2167 = $1,216.70
  • CheckSimple interest would give $1,000 + 5($40) = $1,200. Compound result is slightly higher. Reasonable. ✓
Answer: After 5 years, the account contains approximately $1,216.70.

Section 14.13

Common Data Interpretation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Correlation with Causation
"As ice cream sales increase, so does drowning. Therefore ice cream causes drowning."

Correct: Both are influenced by a third variable — hot weather. Correlation describes a pattern, not a cause. Always say "there is a positive association between x and y," not "x causes y to increase."

Mistake 2 — Extrapolating Too Far
Using y = 5x + 57 to predict a score when x = 40 hours of study.

Correct: The model is only reliable within or near the range of observed data. Extending far beyond it can produce nonsensical results (a score over 100 or a negative population size).

Mistake 3 — Misinterpreting a Residual
"Residual = +15 means the model predicted 15 more than the actual value."

Correct: Residual = Observed − Predicted. A positive residual means the actual value is 15 MORE than the predicted value — the model underestimated, not overestimated.

Mistake 4 — Assuming All Scatter Plots Are Linear
Drawing a straight line through data that curves sharply upward.

Correct: Use difference patterns and ratios to confirm model type before fitting a line. If points curve exponentially, an exponential model will fit far better than a line.

Mistake 5 — Confusing Mean and Median
"Mean = 42, Median = 40, therefore there are no outliers."

Correct: A mean higher than the median suggests right skew, possibly caused by outliers. You cannot conclude there are no outliers from mean and median alone.

Mistake 6 — Misreading a Two-Way Table Cell as a Percentage
"30 is in the Morning / Alone cell, so 30% of morning students prefer working alone."

Correct: 30 ÷ 50 = 60%. You must divide by the appropriate row or column total to find a conditional frequency. Never read a cell count as a percent without dividing.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 14.1Algebra connects to data through patterns. Univariate data is described with center and spread; bivariate data is explored with scatter plots and models. The Read → Represent → Solve → Check → Explain method applies throughout.
  • Sec 14.2Mean is the arithmetic average; median is the middle value of ordered data; mode is the most frequent value. Use median for skewed data or data with outliers; mean for symmetric, clean distributions.
  • Sec 14.3Range = max − min. IQR = Q₃ − Q₁, measuring the middle 50% of data and resisting outliers. Standard deviation measures how spread values are around the mean — small means clustered, large means dispersed.
  • Sec 14.4Dot plots show individual values on a number line. Histograms display frequency within equal-width intervals. Box plots display the five-number summary and are ideal for comparing two or more distributions.
  • Sec 14.5A scatter plot plots each (x, y) pair as a point. Describe scatter plots by direction (positive or negative), form (linear or curved), and strength (tight cluster or wide scatter).
  • Sec 14.6Association is a general relationship between two variables. Correlation measures linear association by r, which falls between −1 and 1. Strong correlation does not imply causation.
  • Sec 14.7A line of best fit summarizes the trend through a scatter plot. Find its equation from two points on the line. Use it for interpolation (reliable) or extrapolation (use cautiously — outside the data range).
  • Sec 14.8Slope = rate of change in context: "for each one-unit increase in x, y changes by m units." The y-intercept is the predicted y when x = 0 — meaningful only when x = 0 has a real-world interpretation.
  • Sec 14.9Residual = y − ŷ = observed − predicted. Positive residuals mean the model underestimated; negative residuals mean it overestimated. Residual plots reveal whether a linear model is appropriate.
  • Sec 14.10Two-way tables organize bivariate categorical data. Joint frequencies are cell counts; marginal frequencies are row and column totals; conditional frequencies are proportions within a row or column. Differing conditional proportions suggest association.
  • Sec 14.11Linear: constant first differences. Quadratic: constant second differences. Exponential: constant ratios. Use these tests on tables with equally spaced x-values to identify the model type.
  • Sec 14.12Algebraic models describe science and finance. Distance: d = rt (linear). Free fall: h = −16t² + h₀ (quadratic). Decay or growth: exponential. Compound interest: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt).
  • Sec 14.13Six recurring errors: confusing correlation with causation; extrapolating beyond the data; misinterpreting residuals; assuming linear when another model fits better; confusing mean and median; reading cell counts as percents.
  • Sec 14.14Algebra I is complete. Algebra II extends every major topic — functions, quadratics, systems, polynomials, exponential and logarithmic models, sequences, trigonometry, and formal statistics — to greater depth and generality.

association

bivariate data
Data that records two variables for each subject in the study.
box plot
A visual display of the five-number summary: minimum, Q₁, median, Q₃, and maximum.

extrapolation

The set minimum, Q₁, median, Q₃, maximum — the basis for a box plot.
histogram
A display of the frequency of values within equal-width intervals; bars touch each other.
IQR
Interquartile range: Q₃ − Q₁. Measures the spread of the middle 50% of data and resists outliers.
interpolation
Using a model to predict values within the range of observed data; generally reliable.
line of best fit
A line drawn through a scatter plot to model the overall linear trend in bivariate data.
mean
The arithmetic average of a data set: sum of all values divided by the number of values. Sensitive to outliers.
median
The middle value of an ordered data set. Resistant to outliers. Average of two middle values if count is even.
mode
The most frequently occurring value. Most useful for categorical data or when one value clearly dominates.
outlier
A data value that is unusually far from the rest of the data. Can heavily influence the mean and line of best fit.
range
Maximum minus minimum. The simplest measure of spread; sensitive to outliers.
residual
The difference between an observed value and the value predicted by the model: y − ŷ.
scatter plot
A coordinate-plane graph plotting ordered pairs of bivariate numerical data as individual points.
standard deviation
A measure of spread describing how far values tend to be from the mean on average.
two-way table
A table that organizes bivariate categorical data by showing counts for each combination of categories.
univariate data
Data that records one variable for many subjects. Described with measures of center and spread.

Looking Ahead — Bridge Note
From Algebra I to Algebra II

You have completed Algebra I. The tools you have built here — expressions, equations, inequalities, functions, graphs, linear models, systems, exponents, polynomials, factoring, quadratics, radicals, and data analysis — are the foundation of everything that follows in mathematics.

In Algebra II, you will return to almost every topic in this book, but at greater depth and with greater generality. Functions will be studied as a unified family — polynomial, rational, radical, exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric. Quadratics will be revisited in vertex form, and complex numbers will be introduced as solutions. The data and statistics work from this chapter will grow into formal regression, probability distributions, and the normal curve. Exponential functions will be paired with logarithms — their inverses — so you can solve equations like 2ˣ = 100 precisely. Sequences and series will formalize the difference and ratio patterns from Section 14.11. Conic sections will describe circles, parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas algebraically. Trigonometry will begin with right-triangle ratios and extend to the unit circle and periodic functions.

The work you have done in Algebra I is real mathematics. What comes next builds directly on it. The path forward is clear.

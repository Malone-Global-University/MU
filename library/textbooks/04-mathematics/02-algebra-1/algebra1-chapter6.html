Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter deepens your graphing skills and helps you understand graphs as visual representations of relationships between quantities. You will review the coordinate plane, ordered pairs, axes, and quadrants, and you will extend that foundation by studying intercepts, slope, and how graphs communicate meaning. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to read a graph, build a graph from a table or description, calculate slope as a rate of change, and interpret what slope and intercepts mean in real situations.

Graphs are not decoration. A graph shows how two quantities relate to each other, how one changes as the other changes, where a relationship starts, and how fast it grows or falls. Learning to read and build graphs is one of the most important skills in algebra, and it will serve you in every mathematics and science course that follows.

Opening Question

A cell phone plan charges a flat monthly fee of $20 plus $0.05 per text message. If you send 100 texts in a month, your bill is $25. If you send 300 texts, your bill is $35. If you send 0 texts, your bill is exactly $20. How could you show this relationship visually? If you wanted to predict the bill for 500 texts without calculating, where would you look? How steep would the pattern be if the cost per text went up to $0.10? These are graphing questions. The answers live not only in equations, but in pictures of relationships.

Section 6.1

Why Graphs Matter

Arithmetic is the study of numbers and operations on numbers. Algebra extends arithmetic by asking a deeper question: what if we do not know the number, or what if the number can change? Before the coordinate plane existed as a formal tool, mathematicians, scientists, and merchants tracked quantities in tables and words. A table could tell you that a plant was 3 centimeters tall after one week, 7 centimeters tall after two weeks, and 12 centimeters tall after three weeks. But a table requires work to see the pattern. You have to read the numbers carefully, compare them mentally, and build a picture in your mind.

A graph does that work automatically. The moment you plot those three data points and connect them with a line or curve, the pattern jumps off the page. You can see instantly whether growth is steady or accelerating. You can see where the plant started. You can predict future heights by continuing the pattern visually.

Graphs have a second advantage beyond convenience: they reveal structure that equations and tables sometimes hide. A straight line on a graph tells you the relationship is linear, meaning the change is constant. A curve tells you the change is not constant, which signals a different kind of relationship. Two lines crossing on a graph show the moment when two quantities become equal, which is a solution to a system of equations.

The coordinate plane is the tool that makes this possible. The inventor most credited with formalizing this system was René Descartes, a seventeenth-century French mathematician. His name lives on in the term Cartesian plane, another name for the coordinate plane. Descartes recognized that combining algebra with geometry gave mathematicians tools neither subject had alone.

Section 6.2

The Coordinate Plane

The coordinate plane is built from two number lines. The x-axis runs horizontally, like the horizon. The y-axis runs vertically, like a wall. These two lines intersect at a point called the origin, written \((0, 0)\). Numbers to the right of the origin on the x-axis are positive; numbers to the left are negative. Numbers above the origin on the y-axis are positive; numbers below are negative.

The x-axis and y-axis divide the coordinate plane into four regions called quadrants. The quadrants are numbered using Roman numerals, starting in the upper right and moving counterclockwise.

QuadrantLocationx-signy-sign
IUpper rightPositivePositive
IIUpper leftNegativePositive
IIILower leftNegativeNegative
IVLower rightPositiveNegative
Diagram Instruction

Draw a coordinate plane. Draw the x-axis as a horizontal arrow extending left and right, labeled with values from −5 to 5. Draw the y-axis as a vertical arrow extending up and down, labeled with values from −5 to 5. Label the origin at (0, 0). Label each quadrant: Quadrant I in the upper right, Quadrant II in the upper left, Quadrant III in the lower left, Quadrant IV in the lower right. Use light grid lines to show the square grid.

Memory Device

Start in Quadrant I and move counterclockwise. In Quadrant I both signs are positive (+, +). In Quadrant II only x becomes negative (−, +). In Quadrant III both are negative (−, −). In Quadrant IV only y becomes negative (+, −).

Section 6.3

Ordered Pairs and Quadrants

An ordered pair is a pair of numbers written in the form \((x, y)\). The first number is the x-coordinate; the second is the y-coordinate. The order matters: \((3, 5)\) and \((5, 3)\) are different points. A positive x-coordinate means move right; negative means move left. A positive y-coordinate means move up; negative means move down. The quadrant is determined by the signs of both coordinates.

Example 6.3.1
Identifying quadrants from ordered pairs
  • ProblemIdentify the quadrant or axis location of each point: (a) \((4, 7)\)  (b) \((-3, 5)\)  (c) \((-2, -6)\)  (d) \((5, -1)\)  (e) \((0, 3)\)  (f) \((-4, 0)\)
  • ThinkLook at the signs of both coordinates. If a coordinate is zero, the point is on an axis.
  • Solve (a) (4, 7): Both positive → Quadrant I (b) (-3, 5): x negative, y positive → Quadrant II (c) (-2, -6): Both negative → Quadrant III (d) (5, -1): x positive, y negative → Quadrant IV (e) (0, 3): x is zero → On the y-axis (f) (-4, 0): y is zero → On the x-axis
  • CheckEach answer follows directly from the sign pattern. Points on an axis have at least one coordinate equal to zero. ✓
Section 6.4

Plotting Points

Plotting a point means finding and marking its exact location on the coordinate plane. The process has a fixed sequence: start at the origin, move horizontally by the x-coordinate, then move vertically by the y-coordinate.

How to Plot a Point

Step 1 — Start at the origin, (0, 0).

Step 2 — Move right if x is positive; move left if x is negative. Move the number of units given by the x-coordinate.

Step 3 — From that position, move up if y is positive; move down if y is negative.

Step 4 — Mark the point and label it with its ordered pair if needed.

Example 6.4.1
Plotting points on a coordinate plane
  • ProblemPlot and label: \(A = (3, 4)\), \(B = (-2, 5)\), \(C = (-4, -3)\), \(D = (6, -1)\), \(E = (0, -2)\).
  • ThinkFollow the plotting procedure for each point, noting sign and direction.
  • Solve A: right 3, up 4 → Quadrant I B: left 2, up 5 → Quadrant II C: left 4, down 3 → Quadrant III D: right 6, down 1 → Quadrant IV E: no horizontal move, down 2 → on the y-axis
  • CheckVerify each point is in the expected quadrant and matches the sign pattern. ✓
Answer: Points plotted as described. E lies on the y-axis, not in any quadrant.
Diagram Instruction

Draw a coordinate plane with axes from −6 to 6. Plot and label all five points. Show the horizontal and vertical movement from the origin as light dashed lines for each point.

Student Warning

Always move horizontally first, then vertically. Students who vary this process often make sign errors. Building a consistent habit now prevents confusion in later chapters.

Section 6.5

Reading Graphs in Context

Plotting isolated points is a starting skill. More important is reading what a graph communicates about a real relationship. When you read a graph in context, ask: What are the axes? What quantity does each axis represent, and what units? What is the shape — straight line or curve? What do specific points mean?

Example 6.5.1
Interpreting a graph in context
  • ProblemA graph shows candle height (cm) over time (hours). The graph is a straight line from \((0, 20)\) to \((10, 0)\). Interpret (a) the point \((0, 20)\), (b) the point \((10, 0)\), and (c) the overall shape.
  • Thinkx-axis = time in hours. y-axis = height in centimeters. Read each ordered pair as a statement about the candle.
  • Solve (a) (0, 20): At 0 hours, the candle is 20 cm tall. Starting height. (b) (10, 0): At 10 hours, the candle is 0 cm. Fully burned. (c) Straight line falling → constant burn rate, linear decrease.
  • CheckBoth points match the given information. The straight line confirms constant rate of burning. ✓
Answer: The candle starts at 20 cm and burns at a constant rate, reaching zero at 10 hours.
Tip

Every graph problem has a context. Never ignore the axis labels. Numbers alone mean nothing without knowing what quantity they represent.

  2. Plot and label the following points on a single coordinate plane: \(P = (4, 2)\), \(Q = (-3, 5)\), \(R = (-5, -1)\), \(S = (2, -4)\), \(T = (0, 3)\), \(U = (-2, 0)\).

Section 6.6

x-Intercepts and y-Intercepts

Two special points on any graph are the intercepts — the points where the graph crosses the axes. The y-intercept is where the graph crosses the y-axis. On the y-axis, the x-coordinate is always zero. Therefore, the y-intercept occurs where \(x = 0\), and it is written as \((0, b)\). In many applications, the y-intercept represents the starting value of a quantity before any change occurs.

The x-intercept is where the graph crosses the x-axis. On the x-axis, the y-coordinate is always zero. Therefore, the x-intercept occurs where \(y = 0\), and it is written as \((a, 0)\). In applications, the x-intercept often represents the moment a quantity reaches zero — when a balance is paid off, when a candle burns out, or when a resource runs out.

Finding Intercepts from an Equation

y-intercept — Set \(x = 0\) in the equation and solve for y. The y-intercept is \((0, y)\).

x-intercept — Set \(y = 0\) in the equation and solve for x. The x-intercept is \((x, 0)\).

Example 6.6.1
Identifying intercepts from a graph
  • ProblemA straight line passes through \((0, 5)\) and \((4, 0)\). Identify both intercepts and explain their meaning in context.
  • ThinkThe y-intercept has \(x = 0\). The x-intercept has \(y = 0\).
  • Solve y-intercept: (0, 5) — the quantity starts at 5 when x = 0 x-intercept: (4, 0) — the quantity reaches zero when x = 4
  • Check\((0, 5)\) has \(x = 0\) ✓ · \((4, 0)\) has \(y = 0\) ✓
Answer: y-intercept \((0, 5)\) — start value. x-intercept \((4, 0)\) — endpoint.
Example 6.6.2
Finding intercepts from an equation
  • ProblemFind the x-intercept and y-intercept of \(2x + 3y = 12\).
  • ThinkSubstitute zero for each variable in turn to isolate the other.
  • Solve y-intercept (set x = 0): 2(0) + 3y = 12 → 3y = 12 → y = 4 y-intercept: (0, 4) x-intercept (set y = 0): 2x + 3(0) = 12 → 2x = 12 → x = 6 x-intercept: (6, 0)
  • Check\((0, 4)\): \(2(0) + 3(4) = 12\) ✓  ·  \((6, 0)\): \(2(6) + 3(0) = 12\) ✓
Answer: y-intercept at (0, 4); x-intercept at (6, 0).
Student Warning

Students often mix up intercepts. The y-intercept has \(x = 0\) and is a point on the y-axis. The x-intercept has \(y = 0\) and is a point on the x-axis. If you find a y-intercept with a nonzero x-value, you have made an error.

Section 6.7

Change in x and Change in y

Before understanding slope, you need to measure change between two points. Mathematicians use the Greek letter delta, \(\Delta\), to mean "change in." So \(\Delta x\) means "change in x" and \(\Delta y\) means "change in y." Using two points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\):

\[ \Delta x = x_2 - x_1 \qquad \text{(change in x, horizontal)} \] \[ \Delta y = y_2 - y_1 \qquad \text{(change in y, vertical)} \]
Example 6.7.1
Calculating change between two points
  • ProblemFind \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta y\) from \(P_1 = (2, 3)\) to \(P_2 = (7, 9)\).
  • ThinkSubtract the first coordinates from the second. Use the same order (second minus first) for both.
  • Solve Δx = x₂ − x₁ = 7 − 2 = 5 Δy = y₂ − y₁ = 9 − 3 = 6
  • CheckStarting at \((2, 3)\), add 5 to x and 6 to y: \((7, 9)\). ✓
Answer: \(\Delta x = 5\), \(\Delta y = 6\). Movement: 5 right and 6 up.
Example 6.7.2
Change with negative coordinates
  • ProblemFind \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta y\) from \(A = (5, 8)\) to \(B = (1, 2)\).
  • Solve Δx = 1 − 5 = −4 Δy = 2 − 8 = −6
Answer: \(\Delta x = -4\), \(\Delta y = -6\). Movement: 4 left and 6 down.
Tip — Order Consistency

It does not matter which point you call \(P_1\) and which you call \(P_2\), as long as you are consistent. Whichever point is first in \(\Delta y\) must be first in \(\Delta x\). Mixing the order causes a sign error in the slope calculation.

Section 6.8

Slope as Rate of Change

Slope is the ratio of vertical change to horizontal change between any two points on a line. Slope measures rate of change — it answers: for every 1 unit increase in x, how much does y change? The conventional symbol for slope is m.

\[ m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} \]

If \(m = 3\), every time x increases by 1, y increases by 3. If \(m = -2\), every time x increases by 1, y decreases by 2. Slope always has a unit and a meaning: in a distance-time graph it is speed; in a cost-quantity graph it is price per unit; in a temperature-time graph it is rate of heating or cooling.

Example 6.8.1
Finding slope between two points
  • ProblemFind the slope of the line through \((2, 3)\) and \((6, 11)\).
  • ThinkLabel points and apply the slope formula. Expect a positive result since y increases with x.
  • Solve m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁) = (11 − 3) / (6 − 2) = 8 / 4 = 2
  • CheckMove from \((2,3)\) right 4 and up 8: \((6, 11)\). ✓
Answer: m = 2. For every 1 unit increase in x, y increases by 2 units.
Example 6.8.2
Negative slope
  • ProblemFind the slope of the line through \((1, 8)\) and \((5, 2)\).
  • Solve m = (2 − 8) / (5 − 1) = −6 / 4 = −3/2
  • CheckStart at \((1, 8)\), move right 4, down 6: \((5, 2)\). ✓
Answer: m = −3/2. For every 1 unit increase in x, y decreases by 1.5 units.
Example 6.8.3
Slope with negative coordinates
  • ProblemFind the slope of the line through \((-3, 1)\) and \((2, -4)\).
  • Solve m = (−4 − 1) / (2 − (−3)) = −5 / (2 + 3) = −5 / 5 = −1
  • CheckFrom \((-3,1)\), right 5 and down 5: \((2, -4)\). ✓
Answer: m = −1.
Student Warning — Subtracting a Negative

When a coordinate is negative, watch your subtraction carefully. Subtracting a negative means adding: \(2 - (-3) = 2 + 3 = 5\). Many slope errors come from this single step.

Slope is Constant on a Line

On a linear relationship, the slope is the same between any two points you choose. If you calculate different slopes between different pairs of points on the same graph, the relationship is not linear.

Section 6.9

Positive, Negative, Zero, and Undefined Slope

TypeVisualFormulaExample in Context
PositiveRises left to right\(m > 0\)Savings balance growing over time
NegativeFalls left to right\(m < 0\)Car loan balance decreasing with payments
ZeroHorizontal line\(m = 0\)Fixed price regardless of quantity
UndefinedVertical line\(\Delta x = 0\)An instantaneous boundary in geometry

A horizontal line has \(\Delta y = 0\), so \(m = 0/\Delta x = 0\). A vertical line has \(\Delta x = 0\), making the slope formula divide by zero — which is undefined. Zero slope and undefined slope are two completely different mathematical situations, not the same thing.

The absolute value of the slope determines steepness. A slope of \(m = 4\) is steeper than \(m = 1\) because the line rises more sharply. A slope of \(m = \frac{1}{4}\) is shallower.

Example 6.9.1
Classifying slope from two points
  • ProblemClassify slope for: (a) through \((2,3)\) and \((8,7)\)  (b) through \((1,5)\) and \((6,5)\)  (c) through \((4,9)\) and \((4,2)\)  (d) through \((0,10)\) and \((5,3)\)
  • Solve (a) m = (7−3)/(8−2) = 4/6 = 2/3 → Positive slope; rises left to right (b) m = (5−5)/(6−1) = 0/5 = 0 → Zero slope; horizontal line (c) m = (2−9)/(4−4) = −7/0 → Undefined slope; vertical line (d) m = (3−10)/(5−0) = −7/5 → Negative slope; falls left to right

In the second, draw a horizontal line, labeled "Zero slope: m = 0." In the third, draw a vertical line, labeled "Undefined slope." Section 6.10

Graphs from Tables

Procedure: Graphing from a Table

Step 1 — Read the table and identify x-values and y-values.

Step 2 — Write each row as an ordered pair.

Step 3 — Plot each ordered pair on the coordinate plane.

Step 4 — If the relationship is linear, draw a straight line through the points. If not, connect with a smooth curve or leave as individual points depending on context.

Step 5 — Label the axes with variable names and units.

Example 6.10.1
Building a graph from a table — delivery charges
  • ProblemA delivery driver charges a base fee of $5 plus $2 per mile. Build a table for \(x = 0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 10\) and graph the relationship.
  • ThinkTotal charge is \(y = 5 + 2x\). Substitute each x-value to find y.
  • Solve x = 0: y = 5 + 2(0) = 5 → (0, 5) x = 1: y = 5 + 2(1) = 7 → (1, 7) x = 2: y = 5 + 2(2) = 9 → (2, 9) x = 3: y = 5 + 2(3) = 11 → (3, 11) x = 5: y = 5 + 2(5) = 15 → (5, 15) x = 10: y = 5 + 2(10) = 25 → (10, 25)
  • CheckFor \(x = 5\): \(5 + 10 = 15\). ✓ Points fall in a straight line. ✓
Answer: Six points plotted. Straight line. y-intercept (0, 5) = base fee; slope 2 = $2 per mile.
Diagram Instruction

Draw a coordinate plane with x-axis labeled "Miles driven" from 0 to 10 and y-axis labeled "Total charge (dollars)" from 0 to 30. Plot and label each of the six points. Draw a straight line through them. Mark the y-intercept at (0, 5) and label it "Base fee: $5."

Example 6.10.2
Graph from a table — temperature by elevation
  • ProblemTemperature readings at different elevations: \((0, 20),\ (1, 14),\ (2, 8),\ (3, 2),\ (4, -4)\). Plot the data and interpret.
  • ThinkThe y-values decrease by 6 for each 1-unit increase in x. Linear relationship, negative slope.
  • Solve m = (14 − 20) / (1 − 0) = −6/1 = −6
Answer: Temperature drops 6°C for every km of elevation gain. y-intercept (0, 20) = temperature at ground level.
Section 6.11

Graphs from Verbal Descriptions

Procedure: Graphing from a Description

Step 1 — Identify the two quantities. Decide which is x and which is y.

Step 2 — Identify any starting value. This often becomes the y-intercept.

Step 3 — Identify how the relationship changes: constant, increasing, decreasing?

Step 4 — Identify any ending condition or special feature.

Step 5 — Plot key points and connect them in a way that reflects the described behavior.

Example 6.11.1
Steady decrease — draining water tank
  • ProblemA 24-gallon tank drains at a constant rate of 3 gallons per hour. Sketch a graph of volume remaining versus time.
  • ThinkVolume starts at 24 when \(t = 0\). It decreases by 3 per hour. It reaches zero after \(24 \div 3 = 8\) hours.
  • Solve Key points: Start: (0, 24) — tank is full Half: (4, 12) — half full End: (8, 0) — tank is empty Slope: m = −3
Answer: Draw a straight line from (0, 24) to (8, 0). y-intercept = starting volume; x-intercept = when tank empties; slope = −3 gallons/hour.
Diagram Instruction

Draw a coordinate plane with x-axis labeled "Time (hours)" from 0 to 10 and y-axis labeled "Volume (gallons)" from 0 to 30. Plot (0, 24), (4, 12), and (8, 0). Draw a straight line through them. Label the y-intercept at (0, 24) and the x-intercept at (8, 0).

Example 6.11.2
Piecewise description — runner's speed
  • ProblemA runner runs at 6 mph for 3 hours, rests for 1 hour, then runs at 4 mph for 2 more hours. Sketch a graph of speed over time.
  • ThinkSpeed is constant during each segment. Three horizontal sections at different heights.
  • Solve Time 0 to 3 hours: speed = 6 mph (horizontal segment at y = 6) Time 3 to 4 hours: speed = 0 mph (horizontal segment at y = 0) Time 4 to 6 hours: speed = 4 mph (horizontal segment at y = 4)
Answer: Three separate horizontal segments. This piecewise graph shows that speed can hold constant, then change abruptly.
Reading the Language of Graphs

When a description says "constant" or "steady," the graph segment is flat (zero slope). When it says "increasing at a constant rate" or "decreasing at a constant rate," the segment is a straight line with nonzero slope.

Section 6.12

Graphing Real-World Data

Real data is messier than equations: points do not always fall perfectly on a line, and some variation is expected. When real data is graphed with each point plotted individually and no line connecting them, the result is called a scatter plot. Scatter plots are used to look for patterns, trends, and associations.

If the points rise from left to right overall, there is a positive association. If the points fall from left to right, there is a negative association. If the points show no clear direction, there is no association. You will study correlation and lines of best fit formally in Chapter 14.

Example 6.12.1
Graphing a data set and describing its pattern
  • ProblemPlot and describe: hours studied versus test score — \((1, 62),\ (2, 71),\ (3, 78),\ (4, 85),\ (5, 90)\).
  • ThinkEach row is an ordered pair. Plot them and look for direction and shape.
  • SolvePoints plotted as a scatter plot. Points rise left to right. Approximately linear.
  • CheckFrom \((1,62)\) to \((5,90)\): \(\Delta y = 28\), \(\Delta x = 4\), slope ≈ 7. Each hour of study adds about 7 points. ✓
Answer: Positive association. Each additional hour of study is associated with roughly 7–8 more score points.

Section 6.13

Common Coordinate and Slope Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Reversing x and y in an Ordered Pair
"3 right and 5 up" → written as (5, 3)

In an ordered pair \((x, y)\), x comes first. x is horizontal, y is vertical. Always write horizontal movement first as the x-coordinate, vertical second as y.

Mistake 2 — Subtracting Coordinates in the Wrong Order
Points (2,5) and (6,9): Δy = 5−9 = −4 but Δx = 6−2 = 4 → slope = −1 (wrong)

The order of subtraction must be the same for both Δy and Δx. Label points as \((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\) and use \(m = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1)\) consistently.

Mistake 3 — Confusing Rise and Run
slope = run/rise = Δx/Δy

Slope is rise over run: \(m = \Delta y / \Delta x\). The vertical change (rise) goes on top; horizontal change (run) goes on the bottom.

Mistake 4 — Treating Zero Slope and Undefined Slope as the Same
"Horizontal and vertical lines both have no slope."

Zero slope means the slope formula gives exactly 0 (horizontal line). Undefined slope means the formula requires division by zero (vertical line). These are two completely different mathematical situations.

Mistake 5 — Misidentifying Intercepts
Student calls (3, 0) the y-intercept because "y is the second coordinate."

The y-intercept is on the y-axis: \(x = 0\). The x-intercept is on the x-axis: \(y = 0\). Check the zero — whichever coordinate is zero tells you which intercept it is.

Mistake 6 — Writing Slope as a Bare Number Without Meaning
In an application: "Slope = 4." (no units, no interpretation)

In context, slope is always a rate of change. If x is hours and y is dollars, then slope 4 means $4 per hour. Always interpret slope in the language of the problem.

Chapter 6 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1Graphs communicate how two quantities relate. They reveal shape, direction, starting values, and rates of change in ways that tables and equations alone do not. The Cartesian plane was formalized by René Descartes.
  • Sec 6.2The coordinate plane is formed by perpendicular x- and y-axes meeting at the origin (0, 0). The plane is divided into four quadrants numbered counterclockwise from the upper right.
  • Sec 6.3Ordered pairs are written (x, y). The x-coordinate is horizontal, the y-coordinate is vertical. The quadrant is determined by the sign pattern of both coordinates.
  • Sec 6.4Plotting begins at the origin, moves horizontally by x, then vertically by y. Always move horizontally first to build a consistent habit.
  • Sec 6.5Reading a graph in context requires interpreting axis labels, units, shape, specific points, and the overall meaning of the relationship.
  • Sec 6.6The y-intercept is where \(x = 0\); the x-intercept is where \(y = 0\). Both can be found from graphs or from equations by substituting zero for the appropriate variable.
  • Sec 6.7Change in x is \(\Delta x = x_2 - x_1\). Change in y is \(\Delta y = y_2 - y_1\). The order of subtraction must be the same for both.
  • Sec 6.8Slope measures rate of change: \(m = \Delta y / \Delta x\). It is the ratio of vertical change to horizontal change. Slope always has a meaning in context.
  • Sec 6.9Positive slope: line rises left to right. Negative slope: falls. Zero slope: horizontal line. Undefined slope: vertical line. Steepness is determined by the absolute value of the slope.
  • Sec 6.10Graphs from tables require writing each row as an ordered pair and plotting all points. Linear relationships produce points on a straight line.
  • Sec 6.11Graphs from descriptions require identifying quantities, starting values, direction of change, and special features. Constant rates produce straight lines; flat segments indicate no change.
  • Sec 6.12Scatter plots show real data points without a connecting line. Positive association means y tends to increase with x; negative means y tends to decrease.
  • Sec 6.13Six common mistakes: reversing x and y; inconsistent subtraction order; confusing rise and run; treating zero and undefined slope as the same; misidentifying intercepts; writing slope without context.
  • Sec 6.14Every skill in this chapter prepares directly for Chapter 7: slope-intercept form, point-slope form, writing linear equations from graphs, tables, and real-world descriptions.

Key Terms Review

coordinate plane
A flat surface formed by two perpendicular number lines, used for graphing ordered pairs and functions.
x-axis
The horizontal number line in a coordinate plane.
y-axis
The vertical number line in a coordinate plane.
origin
The point where the x-axis and y-axis intersect, written as (0, 0).
ordered pair
A pair of numbers written as (x, y) that identifies a location on the coordinate plane. Order is fixed: x first, y second.
quadrant
One of the four regions of the coordinate plane, labeled I through IV, each with a distinct sign pattern for x and y.
x-intercept
The point where a graph crosses the x-axis. At this point, y = 0. Written as (a, 0).
y-intercept
The point where a graph crosses the y-axis. At this point, x = 0. Written as (0, b).
rise
The vertical change between two points; \(\Delta y = y_2 - y_1\).
run
The horizontal change between two points; \(\Delta x = x_2 - x_1\).
slope
The ratio of vertical change to horizontal change: \(m = \Delta y / \Delta x\). Slope measures rate of change.
rate of change
How much one quantity changes for each unit change in another quantity. Slope gives the rate of change of y per unit of x.
positive slope
A line that rises from left to right; m > 0.
negative slope
A line that falls from left to right; m < 0.
zero slope
A horizontal line; \(\Delta y = 0\), so m = 0. The y-value does not change.
undefined slope
A vertical line; \(\Delta x = 0\), making the slope formula undefined. Not the same as zero slope.
scatter plot
A graph of real data points plotted individually, without a connecting line, used to identify patterns and associations.
positive association
A pattern in a scatter plot where y tends to increase as x increases.

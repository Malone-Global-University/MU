Chapter 2 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Algebra is built on numbers. Before a student can solve equations, evaluate functions, graph relationships, or work with polynomials, they need a clear and confident understanding of the number system that underlies all of those activities. Chapter 2 provides that foundation.

In Arithmetic and Pre-Algebra Foundations, you worked with whole numbers, fractions, decimals, percents, negative numbers, and the basic rules of arithmetic. You learned how to add, subtract, multiply, and divide. You learned order of operations. You learned what a signed number means. This chapter does not simply repeat that work. It organizes it. It asks you to look at the number system as a whole, understand how different kinds of numbers relate to one another, and connect the arithmetic properties you already know to the algebraic reasoning you are about to begin.

Properties such as the commutative property and the distributive property are not vocabulary words to memorize and forget. They are rules that allow you to rewrite expressions without changing their meaning. They are the reason you can rearrange terms, simplify expressions, and solve equations step by step without making errors. Understanding why a step is legal is just as important as knowing how to perform it.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand the structure of the real number system, feel confident working with signed numbers in any context, recognize and apply the major properties of operations, and use those properties to simplify both numerical and algebraic expressions. This chapter is the bridge between arithmetic and formal algebra. Every chapter that follows depends on the ideas developed here.

Opening Question

A student is simplifying the expression −3(x − 4) + 2x. She writes: −3(x − 4) + 2x = −3x − 12 + 2x = −x − 12. Another student writes: −3(x − 4) + 2x = −3x + 12 + 2x = −x + 12. Both students followed a procedure. Only one is correct. The difference between them is not a matter of being fast or slow, smart or careless — it is a precise understanding of signed numbers and the distributive property. By the time you complete Chapter 2, you will know exactly which answer is correct, why it is correct, and how to explain the error in the other solution.

Section 2.1

The Real Number System

Mathematics would not get very far if it only used one kind of number. Over time, mathematicians extended the number system to handle problems that simpler numbers could not solve. The result is the real number system: a collection of numbers organized into layers, each layer containing and extending the previous one.

Think of the real number system as a set of nested categories. The largest category is the real numbers, and smaller categories live inside it. Understanding this structure helps you know what kind of number you are working with and what rules apply.

CategoryDescriptionExamples
Natural NumbersCounting numbers; positive integers only1, 2, 3, 4, 5, ...
Whole NumbersNatural numbers plus zero0, 1, 2, 3, 4, ...
IntegersWhole numbers and their negatives..., -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, ...
Rational NumbersExpressible as a/b where a, b are integers, b ≠ 0-3/4, 0.25, 0.333..., 7
Irrational NumbersNon-terminating, non-repeating decimals; cannot be written as a/b√2, π, √5
Real NumbersAll rational and irrational numbers; the entire number lineEvery number listed above
Structure — Containment

Every natural number is also whole, integer, rational, and real. Every integer is rational and real. But not every rational number is an integer, and not every real number is rational. The categories nest inward: Natural ⊂ Whole ⊂ Integer ⊂ Rational ⊂ Real.

Closure

A set is closed under an operation if performing that operation on members of the set always produces another member of the same set. The integers are closed under addition (integer + integer = integer) but not under division (3 ÷ 4 is not an integer). The real numbers are closed under addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division — except division by zero.

Section 2.2

Natural Numbers, Whole Numbers, Integers, and Rational Numbers

Natural numbers are the numbers used for counting: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and so on. They do not include zero, fractions, or negatives.

Whole numbers include all natural numbers and also zero. The number zero represents the absence of quantity and acts as the additive identity (Section 2.11).

Integers extend whole numbers into the negatives: . . . , −4, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, . . . Negative integers describe temperatures below zero, debts, depths below sea level, and positions behind a starting point.

Rational numbers are all numbers expressible as a fraction a/b where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0. This includes all integers (since n = n/1), all proper fractions, all mixed numbers, all terminating decimals, and all repeating decimals.

Section 2.3

Irrational Numbers and Real Numbers

Not every number can be expressed as a fraction. Some numbers, when written as decimals, go on forever without repeating any pattern. These are irrational numbers. The most famous example is π ≈ 3.14159265…, whose digits never end and never repeat. Another class of irrationals comes from square roots of non-perfect squares: √2 ≈ 1.41421356… continues forever without settling into a repeating block.

Not every square root is irrational. The square roots of perfect squares are rational: √4 = 2, √9 = 3, √25 = 5. But √3, √5, √7, and √10 are all irrational.

The real numbers are the union of the rational and irrational numbers. Together they fill the entire number line without gaps. Every point on the number line corresponds to exactly one real number, and every real number corresponds to exactly one point.

Example 2.3.1
Identifying rational and irrational numbers
  • ProblemClassify each as rational or irrational: √9    √11    0.666…    π    −√16    √5
  • ThinkCan this number be expressed exactly as a ratio of two integers? If yes, rational. If it is a non-terminating, non-repeating decimal or the square root of a non-perfect square, it is irrational.
  • Solve √9 = 3 → rational (equals a whole number) √11 → irrational (11 is not a perfect square) 0.666... = 2/3 → rational (repeating decimal) π → irrational −√16 = −4 → rational (equals a negative integer) √5 → irrational
  • CheckThe key test is whether the exact value can be written as a/b. Calculator approximations do not determine rationality.
Answer: √9, 0.666…, and −√16 are rational. √11, π, and √5 are irrational.

Section 2.4

Number Lines and Ordering Real Numbers

A number line is a straight line with a designated point for zero, positive numbers extending to the right, and negative numbers extending to the left. Every real number corresponds to exactly one location on this line.

The number line gives a visual representation of order. For any two real numbers a and b:

Order on the Number Line

a < b   a is to the left of b

a > b   a is to the right of b

a = b   a and b occupy the same location

The further left a number is, the smaller it is. Negative numbers are always less than zero and less than any positive number.

Common Mistake — Negative Numbers

Do not confuse the size of the digits with the size of the number. The number −100 has large digits, but −100 < −1 because −100 is further left on the number line. Think in context: owing $100 is a worse financial position than owing $1.

Example 2.4.1
Ordering real numbers
  • ProblemPlace in order from least to greatest: −3    1.5    −1/2    0    √5    −2.8
  • ThinkConvert to decimal approximations, then order on a mental number line.
  • Solve −3 = −3.000 −2.8 = −2.800 −1/2 = −0.500 0 = 0.000 1.5 = 1.500 √5 ≈ 2.236 Order: −3, −2.8, −1/2, 0, 1.5, √5
  • CheckEach number is larger than the one before. Moving left to right on a number line, we encounter them in this order.
Answer: −3 < −2.8 < −1/2 < 0 < 1.5 < √5

Section 2.5

Absolute Value and Distance

The absolute value of a number is its distance from zero on the number line. Distance is always non-negative, so absolute value is always non-negative. The absolute value of a is written |a|.

Definition of Absolute Value

|a| = a   if a ≥ 0      |a| = −a   if a < 0

If a number is positive or zero, its absolute value is itself. If a number is negative, its absolute value is its opposite (which is positive).

|7| = 7     |−7| = 7     |0| = 0

The distance between a and b on the number line is |a − b|, which always equals |b − a|.

Example 2.5.1
Computing absolute value expressions
  • ProblemEvaluate: (a) |−15|    (b) |3 − 8|    (c) −|6|    (d) |−4 + 9|
  • ThinkComplete any arithmetic inside the bars first, then take the non-negative version. Note: a negative sign outside the bars is applied after the absolute value is evaluated.
  • Solve (a) |−15| = 15 (b) |3 − 8| = |−5| = 5 (c) −|6| = −(6) = −6 (The negative sign is outside the bars; it applies after.) (d) |−4 + 9| = |5| = 5
  • CheckIn (c), the absolute value of 6 is 6, and then the negative outside makes it −6. The bars are not a magic "make positive" button applied to the whole expression.
Answer: (a) 15    (b) 5    (c) −6    (d) 5
Common Mistake — Negative Signs Outside the Bars

−|−7| is NOT 7. Evaluate the absolute value first: |−7| = 7. Then apply the negative outside: −7. Always follow the order of operations around absolute value bars.

Section 2.6

Adding and Subtracting Real Numbers

In arithmetic, you learned to add and subtract positive numbers. In algebra, you need to be equally confident with negative numbers. Signed number arithmetic is the foundation of expression simplification, equation solving, and graphing.

Adding Real Numbers

Sign Rules for Addition

Same signs: Add the absolute values. Keep the common sign.

5 + 8 = 13     (−5) + (−8) = −13

Different signs: Subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger. Keep the sign of the number with the larger absolute value.

(−9) + 4 = −5     9 + (−4) = 5

Subtracting Real Numbers

Subtraction as Addition of the Opposite

a − b = a + (−b)

Every subtraction problem can be rewritten as an addition problem. This is the most important rule for signed subtraction.

7 − 12 = 7 + (−12) = −5

−4 − (−9) = −4 + 9 = 5

−3 − 8 = −3 + (−8) = −11

Example 2.6.1
Adding and subtracting signed numbers
  • ProblemEvaluate: (a) (−6) + (−10)    (b) 14 + (−20)    (c) −5 − (−13)    (d) −8 − 7
  • ThinkFor subtractions, rewrite as addition of the opposite first. Then apply the same-sign or different-sign rule for addition.
  • Solve (a) (−6) + (−10): same signs → −(6 + 10) = −16 (b) 14 + (−20): different signs, |−20| > |14| → −(20 − 14) = −6 (c) −5 − (−13) = −5 + 13: different signs, |13| > |5| → +(13 − 5) = 8 (d) −8 − 7 = −8 + (−7): same signs → −(8 + 7) = −15
  • CheckUse a mental number line. Starting at −8 and moving 7 units left (adding −7) lands at −15. ✓
Answer: (a) −16    (b) −6    (c) 8    (d) −15

Section 2.7

Multiplying and Dividing Real Numbers

Sign Rules for Multiplication and Division

positive × positive = positive     negative × negative = positive

positive × negative = negative     negative × positive = negative

Same signs → positive result     Different signs → negative result

These rules apply equally to division.

Multiple Factors

When multiplying three or more signed numbers, count the negative factors. An even number of negative factors gives a positive product. An odd number of negative factors gives a negative product.

Example 2.7.1
Multiplying and dividing signed numbers
  • ProblemEvaluate: (a) (−8)(5)    (b) (−6)(−9)    (c) −36 / −4    (d) (−1)(−2)(−3)(−4)
  • ThinkApply the sign rule first, then multiply or divide the absolute values. For (d), count the negatives.
  • Solve (a) (−8)(5): different signs → negative → −40 (b) (−6)(−9): same signs → positive → 54 (c) −36/−4: same signs → positive → 9 (d) (−1)(−2)(−3)(−4): 4 negative factors (even) → positive (−1)(−2) = 2, (2)(−3) = −6, (−6)(−4) = 24
Answer: (a) −40    (b) 54    (c) 9    (d) 24
Division by Zero

Division by zero is undefined. No real number equals a/0 because no number times zero equals a nonzero number. You will encounter this as an excluded value when studying rational functions in Algebra II. For now, never divide by zero.

Section 2.8

Opposites and Reciprocals

Opposites (Additive Inverses)

The opposite of a real number a is −a. Opposites are the same distance from zero but on opposite sides of the number line. Their sum is always zero:

Opposite (Additive Inverse)

a + (−a) = 0

Opposite of 7 is −7     Opposite of −5 is 5     Opposite of 0 is 0

This property is used in equation solving: adding the opposite of a quantity to both sides isolates the variable.

Reciprocals (Multiplicative Inverses)

The reciprocal of a nonzero real number a is 1/a. For a fraction a/b, the reciprocal is b/a. Their product is always one:

Reciprocal (Multiplicative Inverse)

a · (1/a) = 1    (a ≠ 0)

Reciprocal of 4 is 1/4     Reciprocal of −2/3 is −3/2     Reciprocal of 1 is 1

This property is used in equation solving: multiplying both sides by the reciprocal of a coefficient isolates the variable.

Zero Has No Reciprocal

There is no number you can multiply by zero to get one, because anything times zero is zero. The expression 1/0 is undefined. This is not a detail to ignore — it becomes critical when you study domain restrictions in rational equations.

Section 2.9

Properties of Addition and Multiplication

Arithmetic has structure. The way numbers combine under addition and multiplication follows rules that are always true, regardless of which real numbers are involved. These rules — called properties of operations — are what allow algebra to work. When you rearrange terms, regroup a calculation, or simplify an expression, you are applying one of these properties.

Learn each property by understanding what it allows you to do, not just by memorizing its name.

The Commutative Property

Commutative Property

a + b = b + a     a · b = b · a

The order of addition or multiplication does not change the result. You can move the numbers around. This does NOT apply to subtraction or division.

3 + 7 = 7 + 3 = 10     4 · 6 = 6 · 4 = 24

3x + 5 = 5 + 3x     (same expression, different appearance)

Commutativity Does Not Apply to Subtraction or Division

8 − 3 ≠ 3 − 8    and    12 ÷ 4 ≠ 4 ÷ 12. Do not apply the commutative property to subtraction or division expressions.

The Associative Property

Associative Property

(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)

(a · b) · c = a · (b · c)

The grouping of addition or multiplication does not change the result. You can re-associate (regroup) freely. This does NOT apply to subtraction or division.

(2 + 5) + 3 = 2 + (5 + 3) = 10

(3 · 4) · 5 = 3 · (4 · 5) = 60

Section 2.10

The Distributive Property Revisited

The distributive property was introduced in Chapter 1. In the context of signed numbers, it deserves a full revisit. Incorrect distribution — especially with negative signs — is one of the most common sources of error in algebra.

The Distributive Property

a(b + c) = ab + ac

a(b − c) = ab − ac

Multiply the factor by every term inside the parentheses. With more than two terms: a(b + c + d) = ab + ac + ad

The most error-prone case involves a negative number outside the parentheses. Consider −3(x − 4). The negative distributes to every term:

−3(x − 4) = (−3)(x) + (−3)(−4) = −3x + 12

Notice: (−3)(−4) = +12. This answers the opening question of the chapter. The correct simplification is:

−3(x − 4) + 2x = −3x + 12 + 2x = −x + 12
Example 2.10.1
Distributing with negative signs
  • ProblemSimplify each expression.

    (a) 4(3x + 2)    (b) −5(2y − 7)    (c) −(a + 6)    (d) 3(x + 4) − 2(x − 1)
  • ThinkMultiply every term inside by the factor outside. For negatives: a negative times a negative is positive.
  • Solve (a) 4(3x + 2) = 12x + 8 (b) −5(2y − 7) = (−5)(2y) + (−5)(−7) = −10y + 35 (c) −(a + 6) = (−1)(a) + (−1)(6) = −a − 6 (d) 3(x + 4) − 2(x − 1) = 3x + 12 − 2x + 2 [note: −2(−1) = +2] = x + 14
  • CheckFor (d), let x = 0: original gives 3(4) − 2(−1) = 12 + 2 = 14; simplified gives 0 + 14 = 14. ✓
Answer: (a) 12x + 8    (b) −10y + 35    (c) −a − 6    (d) x + 14
Distributing a Negative — Every Term Changes Sign

A negative sign in front of parentheses distributes to every term inside, not just the first. −(3x − 5) = −3x + 5, NOT −3x − 5. Write out −1 × each term if you are uncertain.

Section 2.11

Identity and Inverse Properties

Identity Properties

Identity Properties

Additive Identity: a + 0 = a    The number 0 is the identity for addition.

Multiplicative Identity: a · 1 = a    The number 1 is the identity for multiplication.

Adding 0 leaves a number unchanged. Multiplying by 1 leaves a number unchanged. Both identities will be used to verify equation-solving steps.

Inverse Properties

Inverse Properties

Additive Inverse: a + (−a) = 0    Every real number has an additive inverse (its opposite).

Multiplicative Inverse: a · (1/a) = 1   (a ≠ 0)    Every nonzero number has a multiplicative inverse (its reciprocal).

Additive inverses combine with addition to give 0 (the identity). Multiplicative inverses combine with multiplication to give 1 (the identity). Zero has no multiplicative inverse.

Why These Properties Matter for Equations

When you solve x + 5 = 12, you add the additive inverse of 5 to both sides (−5) to isolate x. When you solve 3x = 15, you multiply by the multiplicative inverse of 3 (which is 1/3) to isolate x. These properties are the justification for every isolation step in Chapter 3.

Example 2.11.1
Naming the property used
  • ProblemState the property that justifies each step.

    (a) x + 0 = x    (b) 1 · (x + 3) = x + 3    (c) 7 + (−7) = 0    (d) 1/4 · 4 = 1
  • Solve (a) Additive Identity Property (adding 0 leaves x unchanged) (b) Multiplicative Identity Property (multiplying by 1 leaves (x + 3) unchanged) (c) Additive Inverse Property (7 and −7 are opposites; their sum is 0) (d) Multiplicative Inverse Property (1/4 and 4 are reciprocals; their product is 1)
Answer: (a) Additive Identity    (b) Multiplicative Identity    (c) Additive Inverse    (d) Multiplicative Inverse

Section 2.12

Simplifying Numerical and Algebraic Expressions

Simplifying an expression means rewriting it in a more compact form without changing its value. The number properties you have studied give the justification for every step.

Example 2.12.1
Simplifying a numerical expression with signed numbers
  • ProblemSimplify: −4 + 3 · (−2)² − 5
  • ThinkFollow the order of operations: exponents first, then multiplication, then addition and subtraction left to right.
  • Solve −4 + 3 · (−2)² − 5 = −4 + 3 · 4 − 5 (evaluate exponent: (−2)² = 4) = −4 + 12 − 5 (multiply: 3 · 4 = 12) = 8 − 5 (add left to right: −4 + 12 = 8) = 3
  • CheckWork backward: 3 + 5 = 8, 8 − 12 = −4. Starting value matches. ✓
Answer: 3
(−2)² ≠ −4

(−2)² = (−2)(−2) = 4. Compare with −2² = −(2²) = −4. The placement of the negative sign relative to the exponent changes the result entirely. Always use parentheses when substituting negative values.

Example 2.12.2
Simplifying an algebraic expression with distribution
  • ProblemSimplify: 5x − 3(2x − 4) + x
  • ThinkApply the distributive property to −3(2x − 4) first. Then group and combine like terms.
  • Solve 5x − 3(2x − 4) + x = 5x − 6x + 12 + x (distribute −3) = (5x − 6x + x) + 12 (group x-terms) = 0x + 12 (combine: 5 − 6 + 1 = 0) = 12
  • CheckLet x = 2: original gives 10 − 3(0) + 2 = 12. Simplified gives 12. ✓
Answer: 12 (the x-terms cancel; the result is a constant)
Example 2.12.3
Simplifying with multiple variable types
  • ProblemSimplify: 4a + 3b − 2a + 7 − b + 1
  • ThinkGroup like terms: a-terms together, b-terms together, constants together.
  • Solve 4a + 3b − 2a + 7 − b + 1 = (4a − 2a) + (3b − b) + (7 + 1) = 2a + 2b + 8
Answer: 2a + 2b + 8

Section 2.13

Real Numbers in Measurement and Models

Real numbers describe the measurable world. Length, area, volume, time, temperature, speed, weight, cost, and population are all quantities represented by real numbers. Many real-world quantities require negative numbers: temperatures below zero, depths below sea level, debts, and decreases. Algebra connects arithmetic with real-world measurement through formulas, equations, and models.

Example 2.13.1
Using a formula with real numbers — perimeter
  • ProblemA rectangular room has a length of 14.5 feet and a width of 10.25 feet. Find the perimeter.
  • ThinkUse P = 2(l + w). The distributive property guarantees this is equivalent to P = 2l + 2w.
  • Solve P = 2(l + w) P = 2(14.5 + 10.25) P = 2(24.75) P = 49.5 feet
Answer: The perimeter is 49.5 feet.
Example 2.13.2
Net change with signed numbers — temperature
  • ProblemA city's temperature at 6 AM was −8°F. By noon it rose 19 degrees. By evening it fell 12 degrees from the noon reading. What was the evening temperature?
  • RepresentNoon temperature = −8 + 19. Evening temperature = (noon) − 12.
  • Solve Noon: −8 + 19 = 11°F Evening: 11 − 12 = −1°F
  • CheckA rise of 19 from −8 gives 11. A fall of 12 from 11 gives −1. The temperature ended just below zero. Reasonable. ✓
Answer: The evening temperature was −1°F.

Section 2.14

Preparing for Equation Solving

Every technique in Chapter 3 and beyond depends on the arithmetic and properties studied in this chapter. The table below connects each property directly to a step you will use in equation solving.

PropertyHow It Is Used in EquationsExample
Additive InverseAdd the opposite to both sides to remove a constant termx + 7 = 15: add −7 to both sides
Multiplicative InverseMultiply by the reciprocal to remove a coefficient3x = 15: multiply both sides by 1/3
Distributive PropertyExpand parentheses before isolating the variable2(x + 3) = 10: distribute first
Commutative PropertyRearrange terms freely to group like terms5 + 3x = 3x + 5
Associative PropertyRegroup terms to combine them efficiently(3x + 7) + (−7) = 3x + (7 + (−7)) = 3x

Students sometimes approach Chapter 3 as if it is completely new material. It is not. Chapter 3 is Chapter 2 applied to balanced statements. The variable is just an unknown real number. Your job in solving an equation is to use real number operations and properties systematically to figure out which real number the variable represents.

A note on precision: students who understand that adding −9 to both sides preserves equality because of the additive inverse property will make fewer errors than students who only remember "move the number to the other side." Meaning drives reliability. Keep these properties in mind as you work through Chapter 3.

Chapter 2 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 2.1The real number system is organized in nested layers: Natural ⊂ Whole ⊂ Integer ⊂ Rational ⊂ Real. Rational and irrational numbers together make up all real numbers. Every real number lives on the number line.
  • Sec 2.2Natural numbers count. Whole numbers include zero. Integers include negatives. Rational numbers include all numbers expressible as a/b (integers, fractions, terminating decimals, and repeating decimals).
  • Sec 2.3Irrational numbers cannot be expressed as a/b; their decimals are non-terminating and non-repeating. Examples: π, √2, √5. Square roots of perfect squares are rational; all others are irrational.
  • Sec 2.4On the number line, smaller numbers are to the left. Negative numbers are always less than zero. The number with larger digits is not necessarily larger — context and sign determine order.
  • Sec 2.5Absolute value is distance from zero; always non-negative. |a| = a when a ≥ 0; |a| = −a when a < 0. A negative sign outside the bars applies after the absolute value is evaluated.
  • Sec 2.6Same-sign addition: add absolute values, keep the sign. Different-sign addition: subtract smaller from larger, keep the sign of the larger. Subtraction is addition of the opposite: a − b = a + (−b).
  • Sec 2.7Same signs multiply/divide to a positive result; different signs to a negative result. An even number of negative factors gives a positive product; an odd number gives a negative product. Division by zero is undefined.
  • Sec 2.8The opposite of a is −a; opposites sum to zero. The reciprocal of a is 1/a; reciprocals multiply to one. Zero has no reciprocal. Both operations are tools for equation solving.
  • Sec 2.9Commutative property: order does not matter for addition or multiplication. Associative property: grouping does not matter for addition or multiplication. Neither applies to subtraction or division.
  • Sec 2.10The distributive property: a(b + c) = ab + ac. Every term inside must be multiplied. Distributing a negative changes every sign inside the parentheses. This was the key to the opening question.
  • Sec 2.11Additive identity: 0. Multiplicative identity: 1. Additive inverse: −a (sum with a equals 0). Multiplicative inverse: 1/a (product with a equals 1). These justify every isolation step in equation solving.
  • Sec 2.12To simplify: use order of operations for numerical expressions; distribute and combine like terms for algebraic expressions. (−2)² = 4 but −2² = −4. Always use parentheses when substituting negative values.
  • Sec 2.13Real numbers model temperature, elevation, finance, and measurement. Formulas such as P = 2(l + w) use the distributive property. Signed arithmetic describes real changes in context.
  • Sec 2.14Every equation-solving step is justified by a real number property. Additive and multiplicative inverses isolate variables. The distributive property expands parentheses. Commutativity and associativity allow free rearrangement.

Key Terms Review

absolute value
The distance of a number from zero on the number line; always non-negative. Written |a|.
additive identity
The number 0; adding 0 to any number leaves it unchanged: a + 0 = a.
additive inverse
The opposite of a number; the number that sums with it to give 0. The additive inverse of a is −a.
associative property
Grouping does not affect the result of addition or multiplication: (a + b) + c = a + (b + c).
closure
A set is closed under an operation if performing that operation on members always produces a member of the same set.
commutative property
Order does not affect the result of addition or multiplication: a + b = b + a and ab = ba.
distributive property
a(b + c) = ab + ac. Multiplication distributes over addition and subtraction. Every term inside must receive the factor.
integer
A whole number or its negative: …, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, …
irrational number
A real number that cannot be written as a/b; its decimal expansion is non-terminating and non-repeating. Examples: √2, π.
multiplicative identity
The number 1; multiplying any number by 1 leaves it unchanged: a · 1 = a.
multiplicative inverse
The reciprocal of a nonzero number a; equals 1/a. The product of a number and its reciprocal is 1.
natural number
A positive counting number: 1, 2, 3, 4, …
number line
A line representing all real numbers, with zero at the origin, positives to the right, negatives to the left.
opposite
The additive inverse of a number; the same distance from zero on the opposite side. The opposite of a is −a.
rational number
A number expressible as a/b where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0. Includes all integers, fractions, and repeating or terminating decimals.
real number
Any number on the number line; the union of all rational and irrational numbers.
reciprocal
The multiplicative inverse; for a nonzero number a, the reciprocal is 1/a. For a fraction a/b, the reciprocal is b/a.
signed number
A number with an explicit positive or negative sign, indicating both magnitude and direction.
whole number
A non-negative integer: 0, 1, 2, 3, …

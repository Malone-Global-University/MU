Chapter 3 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every major idea in Algebra I eventually depends on your ability to solve an equation. Factoring produces equations. Functions produce equations. Graphing produces equations. Word problems produce equations. The skills built in this chapter are not optional tools for one unit — they are the central engine of the entire course.

This chapter teaches equations as balanced mathematical statements. An equation does not simply ask you to calculate. It makes a claim: two expressions are equal. Your job as a student is to determine whether that claim is true, and if it involves an unknown, to find the value that makes it true.

You will begin with the simplest equations — those requiring only one operation to solve — and move progressively toward equations involving multiple steps, parentheses, variables on both sides, fractions, decimals, and real-world situations. By the end of this chapter, you will not only know how to solve equations — you will understand why each step is valid, what it means for an answer to be correct, and how to check your work in every case.

Opening Question

A shipping company charges a flat fee of $8.00 plus $3.50 per pound to deliver a package. You have a budget of exactly $29.00 for shipping. How heavy can your package be? The structure of this problem — a fixed starting cost, a rate per unit, and a known total — is an algebraic pattern that appears in finance, science, engineering, and everyday life. This chapter will show you how to write and solve that equation precisely.

Section 3.1

What an Equation Says

An equation is not a question. It is a statement. The equation 3x + 5 = 17 does not ask, "What is 3x + 5?" It claims: "There exists a value of x such that 3x + 5 equals 17."

The equals sign is the heart of every equation. It is not a prompt to calculate. It is a declaration that the expression on the left and the expression on the right have the same value. In an equation, both sides are simultaneously equal. Your job is to find which value of the variable, if any, makes that simultaneous equality true.

The Structure of a Linear Equation

A linear equation in one variable is an equation that can be written in the form ax + b = c, where a, b, and c are numbers and a ≠ 0. The word "linear" means the variable appears only to the first power — no x², no √x, no 1/x. This chapter focuses exclusively on linear equations in one variable.

EquationPlain-Language Reading
x + 4 = 11Some number plus four equals eleven.
3x = 18Three times some number equals eighteen.
2x − 5 = 9Twice some number, minus five, equals nine.
x/3 = 7Some number divided by three equals seven.
Linear EquationsNot Linear in One VariableReason

Section 3.2

Solutions and Checking

A solution to an equation is a value of the variable that makes the equation a true statement. For x + 4 = 11, the solution is x = 7 because substituting 7 gives 7 + 4 = 11, which is true. Any other value produces a false statement.

The habit of checking solutions is one of the most important skills in algebra. To check a solution: substitute the value into the original equation. Simplify both sides independently. If both sides equal the same number, the solution is correct.

Example 3.2.1
Checking a proposed solution
  • ProblemIs x = 4 a solution to 3x − 5 = 7?
  • ThinkSubstitute 4 in place of x and simplify both sides independently.
  • Solve3(4) − 5 = 7 12 − 5 = 7 7 = 7 ✓
  • CheckBoth sides equal 7.
Answer: Yes. x = 4 is a solution.
Example 3.2.2
Checking a value that is not a solution
  • ProblemIs x = 6 a solution to 2x + 3 = 14?
  • Solve2(6) + 3 = 14 12 + 3 = 14 15 ≠ 14
Answer: No. x = 6 is not a solution.
Student Warning

Always check with the original equation, not a simplified version you produced during solving. If you made an algebra error earlier, checking with a simplified form will hide the mistake.

Section 3.3

Balance and Inverse Operations

The most useful way to picture an equation is as a balance scale. The equals sign is the balance point. Whatever is on the left side has the same total weight as whatever is on the right. The key principle follows directly: if you do the same thing to both sides of a balanced scale, it stays balanced.

3x + 5
=
17
Properties of Equality

Addition: If a = b, then a + c = b + c for any number c.

Subtraction: If a = b, then a − c = b − c for any number c.

Multiplication: If a = b, then ac = bc for any number c.

Division: If a = b and c ≠ 0, then a/c = b/c.

OperationInverse OperationExample
AdditionSubtractionx + 5 = 12 → subtract 5
SubtractionAdditionx − 3 = 9 → add 3
MultiplicationDivision3x = 18 → divide by 3
DivisionMultiplicationx/4 = 6 → multiply by 4
Key Idea

You are not "moving things to the other side." You are performing operations on both sides simultaneously. The phrase "moving to the other side" is informal shorthand that causes errors when equations grow more complex. Think: balance and inverse operations.

Section 3.4

One-Step Equations

A one-step equation requires a single inverse operation. There are four types, one for each basic arithmetic operation.

Example 3.4.1
Solving by subtracting
  • ProblemSolve x + 9 = 15.
  • ThinkThe variable has 9 added to it. Undo by subtracting 9 from both sides.
  • Solvex + 9 = 15 x + 9 − 9 = 15 − 9 x = 6
  • Check6 + 9 = 15 ✓
Answer: x = 6
Example 3.4.2
Solving by adding
  • ProblemSolve x − 7 = 4.
  • ThinkThe variable has 7 subtracted from it. Undo by adding 7 to both sides.
  • Solvex − 7 = 4 x − 7 + 7 = 4 + 7 x = 11
  • Check11 − 7 = 4 ✓
Answer: x = 11
Example 3.4.3
Solving by dividing
  • ProblemSolve 5x = 35.
  • ThinkThe variable is multiplied by 5. Undo by dividing both sides by 5.
  • Solve5x = 35 5x/5 = 35/5 x = 7
  • Check5(7) = 35 ✓
Answer: x = 7
Example 3.4.4
Solving by multiplying
  • ProblemSolve x/4 = 9.
  • ThinkThe variable is divided by 4. Undo by multiplying both sides by 4.
  • Solvex/4 = 9 4 · (x/4) = 4 · 9 x = 36
  • Check36/4 = 9 ✓
Answer: x = 36
Example 3.4.5
Negative coefficient
  • ProblemSolve −6x = 42.
  • ThinkThe variable is multiplied by −6. Divide both sides by −6.
  • Solve−6x = 42 −6x/(−6) = 42/(−6) x = −7
  • Check−6(−7) = 42 ✓
Answer: x = −7

Section 3.5

Two-Step Equations

A two-step equation requires two inverse operations. The standard approach: undo addition or subtraction first, then undo multiplication or division. This is the reverse of the order in which the expression was built.

Example 3.5.1
Standard two-step
  • ProblemSolve 2x + 5 = 13.
  • ThinkThe variable term is 2x. It has 5 added. Undo the addition first, then the multiplication.
  • Solve2x + 5 = 13 2x + 5 − 5 = 13 − 5 2x = 8 2x/2 = 8/2 x = 4
  • Check2(4) + 5 = 8 + 5 = 13 ✓
Answer: x = 4
Example 3.5.2
Subtraction in the original
  • ProblemSolve 3x − 7 = 11.
  • Solve3x − 7 = 11 3x − 7 + 7 = 11 + 7 3x = 18 x = 6
  • Check3(6) − 7 = 18 − 7 = 11 ✓
Answer: x = 6
Example 3.5.3
Negative coefficient
  • ProblemSolve −4x + 3 = −9.
  • Solve−4x + 3 = −9 −4x + 3 − 3 = −9 − 3 −4x = −12 −4x/(−4) = −12/(−4) x = 3
  • Check−4(3) + 3 = −12 + 3 = −9 ✓
Answer: x = 3
Example 3.5.4
Division in the original equation
  • ProblemSolve x/5 − 2 = 6.
  • ThinkThe variable is divided by 5, and then 2 is subtracted. Undo the subtraction first, then multiply.
  • Solvex/5 − 2 = 6 x/5 − 2 + 2 = 6 + 2 x/5 = 8 5 · (x/5) = 5 · 8 x = 40
  • Check40/5 − 2 = 8 − 2 = 6 ✓
Answer: x = 40

Section 3.6

Multi-Step Equations

General Strategy

1. Simplify each side independently (combine like terms).

2. Use inverse operations to isolate the variable term.

3. Solve for the variable.

4. Check by substituting into the original equation.

Example 3.6.1
Combining like terms on one side
  • ProblemSolve 3x + 2x + 7 = 32.
  • ThinkCombine 3x and 2x before solving.
  • Solve3x + 2x + 7 = 32 5x + 7 = 32 5x = 25 x = 5
  • Check3(5) + 2(5) + 7 = 15 + 10 + 7 = 32 ✓
Answer: x = 5
Example 3.6.2
Multiple constants
  • ProblemSolve 6x − 4 − 2x + 10 = 22.
  • ThinkCombine variable terms and constant terms separately on the left side.
  • Solve6x − 2x − 4 + 10 = 22 4x + 6 = 22 4x = 16 x = 4
  • Check6(4) − 4 − 2(4) + 10 = 24 − 4 − 8 + 10 = 22 ✓
Answer: x = 4

Section 3.7

Equations with Parentheses

Parentheses appear in equations when a quantity inside is being multiplied by something outside. The Distributive Property removes the parentheses: a(b + c) = ab + ac. Multiply the factor outside by every term inside, then proceed to solve.

Example 3.7.1
Distributing a positive factor
  • ProblemSolve 2(x + 3) = 14.
  • Solve2(x + 3) = 14 2x + 6 = 14 2x = 8 x = 4
  • Check2(4 + 3) = 2(7) = 14 ✓
Answer: x = 4
Example 3.7.2
Distributing over subtraction
  • ProblemSolve 3(2x − 5) = 9.
  • Solve3(2x − 5) = 9 6x − 15 = 9 6x = 24 x = 4
  • Check3(2(4) − 5) = 3(3) = 9 ✓
Answer: x = 4
Example 3.7.3
Parentheses on both sides
  • ProblemSolve 4(x + 2) = 2(x + 8).
  • ThinkDistribute on both sides, then collect like terms.
  • Solve4(x + 2) = 2(x + 8) 4x + 8 = 2x + 16 4x − 2x = 16 − 8 2x = 8 x = 4
  • Check4(6) = 24; 2(12) = 24 ✓
Answer: x = 4
Example 3.7.4
Subtracting a parenthetical expression
  • ProblemSolve 10 − 3(x − 2) = 7.
  • ThinkDistribute −3 to every term inside. Both terms change sign.
  • Solve10 − 3(x − 2) = 7 10 − 3x + 6 = 7 16 − 3x = 7 −3x = −9 x = 3
  • Check10 − 3(3 − 2) = 10 − 3(1) = 7 ✓
Answer: x = 3
Student Warning — Distributing a Negative

When a negative sign precedes a parenthetical expression, every term inside changes sign. A very common mistake is distributing the negative to only the first term. −(x − 4) = −x + 4, not −x − 4. Distribute to every term.

Section 3.8

Equations with Variables on Both Sides

When variable terms appear on both sides of an equation, collect all variable terms on one side and all constant terms on the other. Choose whichever side produces a positive coefficient for the variable to reduce sign errors.

Example 3.8.1
Collecting variable terms on the left
  • ProblemSolve 7x − 3 = 4x + 9.
  • ThinkSubtract 4x from both sides to collect variable terms on the left.
  • Solve7x − 3 = 4x + 9 7x − 4x − 3 = 9 3x − 3 = 9 3x = 12 x = 4
  • Check7(4) − 3 = 25; 4(4) + 9 = 25 ✓
Answer: x = 4
Example 3.8.2
Variable term larger on the right
  • ProblemSolve 2x + 11 = 5x − 4.
  • ThinkThe coefficient of x is larger on the right. Move constants right and variable terms left, or vice versa — both work.
  • Solve2x + 11 = 5x − 4 11 + 4 = 5x − 2x 15 = 3x x = 5
  • Check2(5) + 11 = 21; 5(5) − 4 = 21 ✓
Answer: x = 5
Example 3.8.3
Distribution combined with variables on both sides
  • ProblemSolve 3(x + 4) = x + 20.
  • Solve3(x + 4) = x + 20 3x + 12 = x + 20 3x − x = 20 − 12 2x = 8 x = 4
  • Check3(8) = 24; 4 + 20 = 24 ✓
Answer: x = 4

Section 3.9

Equations with Fractions

Strategy — Clearing Fractions

Multiply both sides of the equation by the least common denominator (LCD) of all fractions in the equation. This eliminates every denominator in one step. Then solve as usual.

Example 3.9.1
Single fraction — solve directly
  • ProblemSolve x/3 + 4 = 9.
  • Solvex/3 + 4 = 9 x/3 = 5 x = 15
  • Check15/3 + 4 = 5 + 4 = 9 ✓
Answer: x = 15
Example 3.9.2
Clearing fractions with the LCD
  • ProblemSolve x/4 + x/6 = 5.
  • ThinkDenominators are 4 and 6. LCD = 12. Multiply every term by 12.
  • Solvex/4 + x/6 = 5 12 · (x/4) + 12 · (x/6) = 12 · 5 3x + 2x = 60 5x = 60 x = 12
  • Check12/4 + 12/6 = 3 + 2 = 5 ✓
Answer: x = 12
Example 3.9.3
Fraction with a constant in the numerator
  • ProblemSolve (2x − 1)/3 = 5.
  • ThinkMultiply both sides by 3 to clear the fraction.
  • Solve(2x − 1)/3 = 5 3 · (2x − 1)/3 = 3 · 5 2x − 1 = 15 2x = 16 x = 8
  • Check(2(8) − 1)/3 = 15/3 = 5 ✓
Answer: x = 8
Student Warning

When you multiply both sides by the LCD, you must multiply every term — including terms that are not fractions. A common mistake is to multiply only the fractional terms and leave whole-number terms unchanged.

Section 3.10

Equations with Decimals

Equations with decimals can be solved directly or by clearing the decimals. To clear decimals, multiply both sides by a power of 10 that converts all decimal coefficients to whole numbers. Use 10 for tenths, 100 for hundredths.

Example 3.10.1
Solving directly
  • ProblemSolve 0.5x = 7.5.
  • Solve0.5x = 7.5 x = 7.5/0.5 = 15
  • Check0.5(15) = 7.5 ✓
Answer: x = 15
Example 3.10.2
Clearing decimal tenths (multiply by 10)
  • ProblemSolve 0.3x + 0.6 = 1.5.
  • ThinkAll values are in tenths. Multiply every term by 10.
  • Solve0.3x + 0.6 = 1.5 3x + 6 = 15 3x = 9 x = 3
  • Check0.3(3) + 0.6 = 0.9 + 0.6 = 1.5 ✓
Answer: x = 3
Example 3.10.3
Clearing decimal hundredths (multiply by 100)
  • ProblemSolve 0.25x + 1.50 = 4.00.
  • ThinkValues are in hundredths. Multiply every term by 100.
  • Solve0.25x + 1.50 = 4.00 25x + 150 = 400 25x = 250 x = 10
  • Check0.25(10) + 1.50 = 2.50 + 1.50 = 4.00 ✓
Answer: x = 10
Connection — Finance

Decimal equations with hundredths are the standard form of most money problems. Prices, interest rates, and costs are nearly always expressed in tenths or hundredths. The habit of clearing decimals keeps calculations clean and reduces arithmetic errors.

Section 3.11

Identity Equations and No-Solution Equations

Most linear equations have exactly one solution. But when variables on both sides eliminate each other entirely, you are left with a statement involving only constants. That constant statement tells you everything.

If the resulting constant statement is true (like 5 = 5 or 0 = 0), the equation is an identity — it is true for every real number. If the resulting statement is false (like 5 = 8 or 0 = −2), the equation is a contradiction — it has no solution.

Example 3.11.1
Identity — all real numbers are solutions
  • ProblemSolve 3(x + 4) = 3x + 12.
  • Solve3(x + 4) = 3x + 12 3x + 12 = 3x + 12 0 = 0
  • Check0 = 0 is always true. Every value of x makes this equation true.
Answer: All real numbers. This is an identity. Solution set = all real numbers.
Example 3.11.2
No solution — contradiction
  • ProblemSolve 2x + 5 = 2x − 3.
  • Solve2x + 5 = 2x − 3 5 = −3
  • Check5 = −3 is never true, for any value of x.
Answer: No solution. Solution set = ∅ (the empty set).
Interpretation

Variable disappears → true statement (0 = 0): all real numbers are solutions (identity).

Variable disappears → false statement (5 = −3): no solution (contradiction).

These are not errors — they are meaningful results about the structure of the equation.

Section 3.12

Writing Equations from Words

Before you can solve a word problem, you must translate the verbal statement into an algebraic equation. This requires careful reading and attention to specific signal words.

Verbal PhraseAlgebraic Meaning
"a number"x (or any variable)
"the sum of x and 5"x + 5
"3 less than x"x − 3
"twice a number"2x
"one-third of a number"x/3
"5 more than three times a number"3x + 5
"a number increased by 4 is 11"x + 4 = 11
"the product of x and 6 is 30"6x = 30
"is", "equals", "is equal to"=
Example 3.12.1
Simple phrase
  • Problem"Five more than twice a number is 19."
  • Think"Twice a number" → 2x. "Five more than" → add 5. "Is 19" → equals sign and right side.
  • Equation2x + 5 = 19
Equation: 2x + 5 = 19
Example 3.12.2
Multi-operation phrase
  • Problem"Three less than four times a number equals twenty-one."
  • Think"Four times a number" → 4x. "Three less than" → subtract 3 from 4x, not 3 − 4x.
  • Equation4x − 3 = 21
Equation: 4x − 3 = 21
Example 3.12.3
Multi-step phrase with division
  • Problem"A number is divided by 5 and then increased by 3. The result is 11."
  • Equationx/5 + 3 = 11
Equation: x/5 + 3 = 11
Student Warning — "Less Than"

"Three less than x" means x − 3, not 3 − x. The phrase "less than" describes a reduction from the unknown, not from 3. Always re-read after writing to make sure the subtraction is in the correct order.

Section 3.13

Equation Word Problems

Problem-Solving Method

Read — Understand the situation. Identify what is known and unknown.

Represent — Define a variable clearly. Write the equation.

Solve — Use inverse operations to find the variable.

Check — Verify numerically or in context.

Explain — Write a complete sentence with units or context.

Example 3.13.1
The shipping problem (chapter opener)
  • ReadFlat fee: $8.00. Rate: $3.50 per pound. Total: $29.00. Find: weight.
  • RepresentLet w = the weight of the package in pounds. 8 + 3.50w = 29
  • Solve3.50w = 29 − 8 3.50w = 21 w = 21/3.50 = 6
  • Check8 + 3.50(6) = 8 + 21 = 29 ✓
Explain: The package weighs 6 pounds.
Example 3.13.2
Consecutive integers
  • ReadTwo consecutive integers differ by 1. Their sum is 47.
  • RepresentLet the first integer = n. The next = n + 1. n + (n + 1) = 47
  • Solve2n + 1 = 47 2n = 46 n = 23
  • Check23 + 24 = 47 ✓
Explain: The two consecutive integers are 23 and 24.
Example 3.13.3
Perimeter problem
  • ReadPerimeter = 54 cm. Length is 3 cm more than twice the width.
  • RepresentLet w = width. Then l = 2w + 3. 2(2w + 3) + 2w = 54
  • Solve4w + 6 + 2w = 54 6w + 6 = 54 6w = 48 w = 8 l = 2(8) + 3 = 19
  • Check2(19) + 2(8) = 38 + 16 = 54 ✓
Explain: The rectangle is 8 cm wide and 19 cm long.
Example 3.13.4
Rate and time
  • ReadRate: $12/hr tutoring. Already earned: $42. Total this week: $90. Find: hours in today's session.
  • RepresentLet h = hours in this session. 42 + 12h = 90
  • Solve12h = 48 h = 4
  • Check42 + 12(4) = 42 + 48 = 90 ✓
Explain: The tutoring session was 4 hours long.
Practice 3.13Word Problems

For each problem: define your variable, write an equation, solve, check, and explain in a complete sentence.

  1. A gym membership costs $40 per month plus a one-time enrollment fee of $30. If a member has paid $190 in total, how many months have they been a member?
  2. A student scored 82 on the first test and 79 on the second. What must they score on the third test to have an average of 85?
  3. Two numbers differ by 13. Their sum is 47. Find both numbers.
  4. A plumber charges a flat call-out fee of $55 plus $45 per hour. If a customer paid $190, how many hours did the plumber work?
  5. A runner's total distance for the week is 31 miles. She ran 5 miles each day for the first three days and the same distance on each of the remaining two days. How far did she run each of those last two days?
  6. The perimeter of a rectangle is 72 feet. The length is four feet more than three times the width. Find the length and width.
  7. Two angles are supplementary (they sum to 180°). One angle is 24° more than three times the other. Find both angles.

Common Equation Mistakes

These mistakes appear frequently in student work. Recognizing them is as important as solving correctly — and recognizing them in your own work before submission is what checking is for.

Mistake 1 — Not operating on both sides
2x + 5 − 5 = 13 → 2x = 13 Correct: 2x + 5 − 5 = 13 − 5, giving 2x = 8, so x = 4. The subtraction must appear on both sides simultaneously.
Mistake 2 — Distributing to only the first term
3(x + 5) = 3x + 5 Correct: 3(x + 5) = 3x + 15. The factor 3 multiplies every term inside the parentheses, not just the first.
Mistake 3 — Sign error distributing a negative
−(x − 4) = −x − 4 Correct: −(x − 4) = −x + 4. The negative distributes to both terms. The minus inside becomes a plus.
Mistake 4 — Forgetting to multiply all terms when clearing fractions
x/3 + 4 = 9 → (×3) → x + 4 = 27 Correct: 3·(x/3) + 3·4 = 3·9 → x + 12 = 27. Every term must be multiplied by the LCD, including non-fractional terms.
Mistake 5 — Stopping at the variable term without isolating fully
After reaching 3x = 12, writing the answer as "3x = 12" Correct: Continue dividing both sides by 3 to reach x = 4. The goal is always x alone with a coefficient of 1.
Mistake 6 — Not checking the answer
Completing a solution without substituting back to verify Correct: Always substitute your answer into the original equation. If both sides match, the answer is correct. Checking catches arithmetic errors, sign errors, and distribution mistakes.
Mistake 7 — Misreading "less than"
"Three less than a number" → 3 − x Correct: "Three less than x" means x − 3. You start with x and remove 3. The English phrase is counterintuitive — the "less" describes the amount removed from the unknown, not a new starting quantity.
Chapter 3 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 3.1An equation is a statement that two expressions are equal. A linear equation in one variable contains the variable only to the first power. The equals sign is not a prompt to calculate — it asserts simultaneous equality.
  • Sec 3.2A solution is a value that makes the equation true. Always verify by substituting into the original equation. Never check with a simplified intermediate form.
  • Sec 3.3The balance model: both sides of an equation have equal value. The properties of equality allow the same operation on both sides without destroying the balance. Inverse operations — subtraction undoes addition; division undoes multiplication — isolate the variable.
  • Sec 3.4One-step equations require a single inverse operation. The four types correspond to addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Negative coefficients are handled by dividing by the negative number.
  • Sec 3.5Two-step equations require undoing addition or subtraction first, then multiplication or division. This is the reverse of the order of operations used to build the expression.
  • Sec 3.6Multi-step equations require simplification — combining like terms — before or alongside inverse operations. Always simplify each side independently first.
  • Sec 3.7Equations with parentheses require the Distributive Property first. A negative factor outside parentheses changes the sign of every term inside. Distribute before combining or isolating.
  • Sec 3.8Equations with variables on both sides require collecting all variable terms on one side and all constants on the other. Prefer the side that produces a positive coefficient.
  • Sec 3.9Equations with fractions are cleared by multiplying every term by the LCD. Every term must be multiplied — not only fractional terms. After clearing, solve as usual.
  • Sec 3.10Equations with decimals can be cleared by multiplying every term by the appropriate power of 10. Tenths use 10; hundredths use 100. This converts the equation to whole-number coefficients.
  • Sec 3.11When the variable eliminates and leaves a true constant statement (0 = 0), the equation is an identity with all real numbers as solutions. When it leaves a false statement (5 = −3), there is no solution.
  • Sec 3.12Verbal descriptions translate to equations using signal words: "sum" → +, "difference" → −, "less than" → reverse order subtraction, "twice" → ×2, "is / equals" → =. Always re-read after translating.
  • Sec 3.13The Read → Represent → Solve → Check → Explain method organizes word problems. Define the variable clearly, write the equation from the defined variable, solve, verify numerically, and state the answer with context.
  • Sec 3.14Seven common mistakes: not applying operations to both sides; incomplete distribution; sign errors with negatives; not multiplying all terms when clearing; stopping short of full isolation; skipping the check; misreading "less than."

Key Terms Review

equation
A mathematical statement that two expressions are equal, written with an equals sign.
solution
A value of the variable that makes an equation a true statement.
solution set
The complete collection of all values that make an equation true.
inverse operations
Operations that undo each other. Addition and subtraction are inverse. Multiplication and division are inverse.
properties of equality
Rules that allow the same operation on both sides of an equation without changing its truth.
linear equation
An equation in which the variable appears to the first power only. No x², no radicals, no products of variables.
identity
An equation that is true for every value of the variable. Results in a true constant statement like 0 = 0.
no-solution equation
An equation that is true for no value of the variable. Results in a false constant statement like 5 = −3.
coefficient
The number multiplied by the variable in a term. In −3x, the coefficient is −3. In x alone, it is 1.
LCD
Least Common Denominator — the smallest multiple shared by all denominators in an expression; used to clear fractions.
like terms
Terms with the same variable raised to the same power. Only like terms can be combined by addition or subtraction.
distributive property
The rule a(b + c) = ab + ac. Used to remove parentheses before solving; must be applied to every term inside.

Real-World Applications

Define your variable, write an equation, solve, check, and explain in context.

  1. Finance: A savings account has a balance of $420. Money is withdrawn at a steady rate of $35 per week. After how many weeks will the balance reach $175?
  2. Finance: A store marks up a wholesale item by 40% and sells it for $84. What was the wholesale price? (Hint: Selling price = wholesale price + 40% of wholesale price.)
  3. Science: The formula for converting Celsius to Fahrenheit is F = (9/5)C + 32. If the temperature is 68°F, what is the Celsius temperature?
  4. Science: An experiment starts with 200 mL of water. Water evaporates at 8 mL per hour. After how many hours will only 104 mL remain?
  5. Geometry: A rectangular field has a perimeter of 300 meters. The length is 15 meters more than twice the width. Find the length and width.
  6. Geometry: Two angles are complementary (they sum to 90°). One angle is 12° less than three times the other. Find both angles.
  7. Everyday life: A cell phone plan charges $25 per month plus $0.10 per text message. If a customer's bill was $37.50, how many texts did they send?
  8. Everyday life: A runner's total distance this week is 31 miles. She ran 5 miles each day for the first three days and the same distance on each of the last two days. How far did she run each of those two days?

Bridge Note — Looking Ahead
Chapter 4 and Beyond

The skills built in Chapter 3 are the foundation for almost everything that follows in Algebra I.

Chapter 4 — Inequalities: Inequalities use the same inverse-operation strategy as equations, but produce ranges of solutions rather than single values. One important difference: the inequality symbol reverses when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number.

Chapter 5 — Functions: Many function problems ask you to evaluate (substitute a value and simplify) or to solve (find which input produces a given output). Both are equation-solving skills.

Chapter 7 — Linear Equations in Two Variables: Every line in the coordinate plane is described by a linear equation. Finding slopes, intercepts, and points on a line all require solving equations.

Chapter 8 — Systems: Solving a system means applying substitution — a direct extension of what you learned here.

Chapters 11–12 — Factoring and Quadratics: The multi-step and distribution skills from this chapter carry directly into solving quadratic equations.

Science, Finance, and Computing: Every physical formula, financial model, and computational algorithm you encounter depends on the same balance-and-inverse-operation reasoning developed in this chapter.

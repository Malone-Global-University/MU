Chapter 1 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter opens the study of algebra by explaining what algebra is, why it is useful, and how it grows naturally out of arithmetic. You have already learned to work with numbers directly — adding, subtracting, multiplying, dividing, and working through order of operations. Algebra extends that work by introducing symbols that stand in for numbers, patterns, and quantities that may be unknown or changing.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to read and write algebraic expressions, evaluate them for given values, simplify them by combining like terms and applying the distributive property, and begin to see how algebra translates real situations into symbolic form. These skills will carry you through every topic in this book.

Opening Question

A cell phone plan charges a flat monthly fee of $25, plus $0.10 for every text message sent. If you send 80 messages in one month, how much do you owe? What if you send 200? What if you do not know how many messages you will send? By the end of this chapter, you will know how to write an expression that models this situation for any number of messages — and why a symbol is more useful than any one number alone.

Section 1.1

What Algebra Studies

Arithmetic is the study of numbers and operations on numbers. Algebra extends arithmetic by asking a deeper question: what if we do not know the number, or what if the number can change? Algebra gives you a method for working in generality — writing 5x = 35 instead of computing one specific case at a time. The letter x holds the place of any number you are looking for.

What Algebra StudiesDescriptionExample
StructureHow expressions are built; why rules hold; how to rewrite in equivalent forms3(x + 2) = 3x + 6
RelationshipsHow one quantity depends on another; formulas that work for any inputP = 2l + 2w
SolvingFinding values that satisfy an equation or inequality5x = 35 → x = 7
Why Symbols?

In natural language: "Multiply a number by three, then add seven." In algebra: 3n + 7. The symbolic form is compact, precise, and ready to be manipulated. It also applies to any value of n, not only one case.

Practice 1.1Concept Check

  2. Why is it useful to write x instead of a specific number when describing a general situation?
  3. A recipe calls for 3 cups of flour per batch. Explain why a symbol is more useful than listing specific cases.
Section 1.2

From Arithmetic to Algebra

Arithmetic StatementAlgebraic Statement
6 + 6 + 6 = 183n = 18 when n = 6
2 × 5 − 3 = 72x − 3 = 7
10 ÷ 2 = 5x / 2 = 5
4² = 16n² = 16

Operations in Algebra

Multiplication is written without the × sign to avoid confusion with the variable x. Use a coefficient before a variable (3x), two variables side by side (ab), a number with parentheses (4(n + 2)), or a dot (3 · x). Division is written as a fraction: x/4 means x ÷ 4.

Tip — Test with Numbers

Whenever you are unsure whether an algebraic step is correct, substitute a specific number and check both sides. This habit of verifying with numbers is one of the most powerful tools in mathematics.

Section 1.4

Constants, Coefficients, Terms, and Factors

Four Key Parts of Any Expression

Constant — a fixed number with no variable. In 3x + 7, the constant is 7.

Term — a single quantity connected to others by + or −. In 5x² − 3x + 9, the terms are 5x², −3x, and 9.

Coefficient — the numerical factor of a variable term. In −3x, the coefficient is −3. In x, the coefficient is 1.

Factor — one of the quantities multiplied in a product. In 5x², the factors are 5, x, and x.

Example 1.4.1
Identifying parts of an expression
  • ProblemIdentify the terms, coefficients, and constant in 6a² − 4a + 1.
  • ThinkSeparate the expression at every + and − sign. Each piece, including its sign, is one term.
  • Solve Terms: 6a², −4a, 1 Coefficient of 6a²: 6 Coefficient of −4a: −4 Constant term: 1
  • CheckReassemble: 6a² + (−4a) + 1 = 6a² − 4a + 1. ✓
Answer: Terms: 6a², −4a, 1. Coefficients: 6 and −4. Constant: 1.
Student Warning

When you see −x, the coefficient is −1, not 0 or just "negative." Writing −1 · x and writing −x mean exactly the same thing. Never drop the sign from the coefficient.

Writing Expressions from Words

Student Warning — "Less Than"

"Five less than x" means x − 5, not 5 − x. The phrase "less than" reverses the expected order. You are taking 5 away from x, not the other way around. Always re-read the phrase after writing the expression.

Example 1.5.1
Translating a simple phrase

Answer: n + 8

Translating a multi-operation phrase

Section 1.6

Evaluating Expressions

Evaluating an expression means finding its numerical value when the variable is replaced by a specific number. The process: write the expression → replace each variable with the given value using parentheses → simplify using the order of operations (parentheses, exponents, multiplication and division left to right, addition and subtraction left to right).

Example 1.6.1
Evaluating a linear expression
  • ProblemEvaluate 4x − 3 when x = 5.
  • Solve 4x − 3 = 4(5) − 3 = 20 − 3 = 17
  • Check4 × 5 = 20; 20 − 3 = 17. ✓
Answer: 17
Example 1.6.2
Evaluating with a negative value
  • ProblemEvaluate 2x² + 3x − 1 when x = −2.
  • ThinkReplace every x with (−2). Use parentheses. Evaluate the exponent first.
  • Solve 2x² + 3x − 1 = 2(−2)² + 3(−2) − 1 = 2(4) + (−6) − 1 = 8 − 6 − 1 = 1
  • Check(−2)² = 4; 2(4) = 8; 3(−2) = −6; 8 − 6 − 1 = 1. ✓
Answer: 1
Example 1.6.3
Evaluating a real-world formula
  • ProblemA cell phone plan: total cost = 0.10t + 25 where t = messages. Find the cost for 80 and for 200 messages.
  • Solve t = 80: 0.10(80) + 25 = 8 + 25 = 33 t = 200: 0.10(200) + 25 = 20 + 25 = 45
  • CheckMore messages cost more. Base is $25; 80 msgs add $8; 200 msgs add $20. Reasonable. ✓
Answer: 80 messages → $33. 200 messages → $45.
Student Warning

Always write substituted values in parentheses. Writing 2(−2)² is clear and correct. Forgetting the parentheses leads to errors like treating −2² as −4 instead of +4, because −2² means −(2²) = −4, while (−2)² = +4.

Section 1.7

Substitution

The most common substitution error is omitting parentheses when the substituted value is negative. Compare −3² with (−3)². The first means −(3²) = −9. The second means (−3)(−3) = +9. These are not the same. Whenever you replace a variable with a negative number, always enclose the entire value in parentheses before applying any exponents.

Example 1.7.1
Substitution with a negative value — squaring
  • ProblemEvaluate 3 − x² when x = −4.
  • ThinkReplace x with (−4). Evaluate the exponent before subtracting.
  • Solve 3 − x² = 3 − (−4)² = 3 − 16 = −13
  • Check(−4)² = 16; 3 − 16 = −13. ✓
Answer: −13
Common Substitution Error
3 − −4² = 3 − (−16) = 19

Error: −4² is computed as −(4²) = −16 rather than (−4)² = +16. Always use parentheses: replace x with (−4), not −4.

Section 1.8

Like Terms

Like terms are terms that have exactly the same variable part — the same variable or variables raised to the same powers. The coefficient does not affect whether terms are like. Only the variable part does.

Like TermsUnlike TermsReason Unlike
3x and 7x3x and 3x²Different exponents
5x² and −2x²5x and 5yDifferent variables
4xy and 9xy4xy and 4xDifferent variable parts
6 and −117 and 7xOne constant, one not
Key Idea

Like terms have the same variable part. Unlike terms do not. Only like terms can be combined by addition or subtraction.

Section 1.9

Combining Like Terms

To combine like terms, add or subtract the coefficients and keep the variable part unchanged. This is valid because the distributive property works in reverse: 3x + 7x = (3 + 7)x = 10x. The variable is factored out and the coefficients are added.

Procedure

3x + 7x = 10x    Add coefficients; keep variable part.

9a² − 4a² = 5a²    Subtract coefficients; keep variable part.

5y − y = 4y    Note: y = 1y, so 5y − 1y = 4y.

Example 1.9.1
Combining like terms — single variable
  • ProblemSimplify 5x + 3 − 2x + 8.
  • Think5x and −2x are like terms. 3 and 8 are like terms (both constants). Group and combine.
  • Solve 5x + 3 − 2x + 8 = (5x − 2x) + (3 + 8) = 3x + 11
  • CheckLet x = 2. Original: 10 + 3 − 4 + 8 = 17. Simplified: 6 + 11 = 17. ✓
Answer: 3x + 11
Example 1.9.2
Combining like terms — multiple variable types
  • ProblemSimplify 4a² − 3a + 7 + 2a² + 5a − 1.
  • ThinkGroup by variable part: a²-terms, a-terms, constants.
  • Solve = (4a² + 2a²) + (−3a + 5a) + (7 − 1) = 6a² + 2a + 6
  • CheckLet a = 1. Original: 4 − 3 + 7 + 2 + 5 − 1 = 14. Simplified: 6 + 2 + 6 = 14. ✓
Answer: 6a² + 2a + 6
Student Warning

Do not combine unlike terms. 3x + 4x² cannot be simplified further — degree 1 and degree 2 are unlike. And exponents are not added by addition; only by multiplication. 3x + 4x² ≠ 7x³.

Section 1.10

The Distributive Property

The Distributive Property

a(b + c) = ab + ac

a(b − c) = ab − ac

To multiply a factor by a sum or difference, multiply the factor by each term inside the parentheses, then add or subtract the results. This rule comes from the meaning of multiplication as repeated addition.

Example 1.10.1
Distributing a positive factor
  • ProblemSimplify 4(3x − 2).
  • Solve4(3x − 2) = 4 · 3x − 4 · 2 = 12x − 8
  • CheckLet x = 1: 4(3 − 2) = 4(1) = 4. Right side: 12(1) − 8 = 4. ✓
Answer: 12x − 8
Example 1.10.2
Distributing a negative factor
  • ProblemSimplify −3(2x − 7).
  • Solve −3(2x − 7) = −3 · 2x − (−3) · 7 = −6x + 21
  • CheckLet x = 2: −3(4 − 7) = −3(−3) = 9. Right: −12 + 21 = 9. ✓
Answer: −6x + 21
Example 1.10.3
Distributing then combining like terms
  • ProblemSimplify 2(3x + 4) − 5x.
  • ThinkDistribute first; then combine like terms.
  • Solve 2(3x + 4) − 5x = 6x + 8 − 5x = x + 8
  • CheckLet x = 3: original = 2(13) − 15 = 11. Simplified: 3 + 8 = 11. ✓
Answer: x + 8
Student Warning — Distributing a Negative

When you distribute a negative sign, every term inside the parentheses changes sign — not just the first. −(3x − 5) = −3x + 5, not −3x − 5. This is one of the most common errors in algebra.

Section 1.11

Equivalent Expressions

Two expressions are equivalent if they produce the same value for every possible value of the variable. Equivalent expressions are different symbolic forms of the same quantity. Every time you simplify, distribute, factor, or rearrange an expression, you are producing an equivalent form.

SourceOriginal FormEquivalent Form
Distributing5(x + 2)5x + 10
Combining like terms3x + 4x − 17x − 1
Factoring (reverse distribution)6x + 93(2x + 3)
Example 1.11.1
Checking equivalence
  • ProblemAre 3(x − 4) and 3x − 12 equivalent?
  • ThinkApply the distributive property to the first expression and compare to the second.
  • Solve3(x − 4) = 3 · x − 3 · 4 = 3x − 12 ✓
  • CheckLet x = 5: first gives 3(1) = 3; second gives 15 − 12 = 3. ✓ Let x = 0: first gives −12; second gives −12. ✓
Answer: Yes. 3(x − 4) and 3x − 12 are equivalent expressions.

Section 1.12

Algebraic Models in Real Situations

An algebraic model is an expression, equation, or formula that represents a real-world situation using symbols. Building a model: (1) read and identify all quantities; (2) define variables with descriptions and units; (3) identify the operations; (4) write the expression; (5) check by substituting a reasonable value.

Example 1.12.1
Building an expression model
  • ProblemA parking garage charges a flat entry fee of $4 plus $2 per hour. Write an expression for the total parking cost.
  • DefineLet h = the number of hours parked.
  • SolveTotal cost = 2h + 4
  • CheckFor 3 hours: 2(3) + 4 = $10. Three hours at $2/hr plus $4 base = $10. ✓
Answer: 2h + 4 dollars, where h = number of hours parked.
Example 1.12.2
A model with two variables
  • ProblemA farmer plants c corn rows (200 ears each) and s soybean rows (80 lbs each). Write expressions for total production.
  • ThinkTotal ears of corn: 200c. Total pounds of soybeans: 80s. Different units — cannot be combined.
  • Solve Corn: 200c ears Soybeans: 80s pounds
Answer: 200c ears of corn and 80s pounds of soybeans.

Section 1.13

Common Mistakes with Variables and Expressions

Mistake 1 — Confusing 2x with x²
"Two times x and x squared are the same thing." Correct: 2x = x + x (repeated addition). x² = x · x (repeated multiplication). When x = 5: 2x = 10, but x² = 25.
Mistake 2 — Squaring without parentheses
When x = −3, x² = −9 Correct: (−3)² = (−3)(−3) = 9. Always substitute with parentheses: x² = (−3)² = 9.
Mistake 3 — Distributing to only the first term
3(x + 4) = 3x + 4 Correct: 3(x + 4) = 3x + 12. The factor 3 multiplies every term inside.
Mistake 4 — Distributing a negative sign incompletely
−(2x − 5) = −2x − 5 Correct: −(2x − 5) = −2x + 5. Distributing −1 changes every sign inside.
Mistake 5 — Combining unlike terms
3x + 4x² = 7x³ Correct: 3x + 4x² is already fully simplified. Different degrees cannot be combined; exponents are not added by addition.
Mistake 6 — Misreading "less than"
"Five less than x" written as 5 − x Correct: "Five less than x" means x − 5. You start with x and remove 5.

Section 1.14

Preparing for Equations and Functions

This chapter introduced the vocabulary and mechanics of algebraic expressions. The next step is equations — statements that two expressions are equal. An equation uses an equals sign to assert that two expressions have the same value. For example, 3x + 5 = 17 says there is a value of x that makes the left side equal to the right. Chapter 3 will teach you to find it. Every skill from this chapter — substitution, combining like terms, and the distributive property — will be used directly there.

Chapters 5 through 8 will introduce functions, which are rules that assign exactly one output to every input. The expressions you practiced here become function rules. The habits you built now — reading carefully, using parentheses, checking by substitution — are habits that serve every mathematician, scientist, engineer, and programmer.

Practice 1.14Readiness Check
  1. Evaluate 2x + 5 for x = 6. Then find the value of x that makes the expression equal to 17.
  2. Evaluate 3x − 4 for x = 3, x = 4, and x = 5. Which value makes the expression equal to 8?
  3. Write an expression for "a number multiplied by 4, minus 3." What value makes this expression equal to 9?
  4. A rental car costs $40/day plus a $15 fee. Write an expression for d days. For how many days can you rent the car for $175?
  5. Two students simplify 3(x + 2) − x. Student A gets 2x + 6. Student B gets 2x + 2. Who is correct? Show all steps.
Chapter 1 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 1.1Algebra extends arithmetic by using symbols for unknown, changing, or general quantities. It studies structure, relationships, and solving.
  • Sec 1.2Algebraic statements parallel arithmetic ones but use letters. Multiplication is written without ×; division is written as a fraction. Always test symbolic steps with numbers.
  • Sec 1.3Variables serve three roles: unknown (one value to find), input (any chosen value), and changing quantity (varies over time). Always define variables with descriptions and units.
  • Sec 1.4An expression is built from terms (connected by + and −). Each term has a coefficient and a variable part. Constants have no variable. Factors are quantities multiplied within a term.
  • Sec 1.5Verbal phrases translate to algebraic expressions using signal words for each operation. "Less than" and "subtracted from" reverse the order of subtraction. Always re-read after translating.
  • Sec 1.6Evaluating means substituting a specific value and simplifying with the order of operations. Negative values must be enclosed in parentheses to avoid sign errors with exponents.
  • Sec 1.7Parentheses are essential in substitution. −3² = −9 but (−3)² = 9. Always enclose negative substituted values in parentheses before applying any exponent.
  • Sec 1.8Like terms have exactly the same variable part, including the same exponents. Only like terms can be combined. The coefficient does not determine likeness; the variable part does.
  • Sec 1.9To combine like terms, add or subtract coefficients and keep the variable part unchanged. This is valid because the distributive property works in reverse. Unlike terms cannot be combined.
  • Sec 1.10The distributive property: a(b + c) = ab + ac. Multiply each term inside. When distributing a negative, every sign inside changes. Distribute before combining like terms.
  • Sec 1.11Two expressions are equivalent if they produce the same value for every variable value. Distribution, combining like terms, and factoring all produce equivalent expressions.
  • Sec 1.12An algebraic model uses a defined variable and an expression to represent a real-world rule. Always define variables, write the expression, and check by substituting a reasonable value.
  • Sec 1.13Six recurring errors: confusing 2x with x²; omitting parentheses on negative substitution; incomplete distribution; sign errors with negative factors; combining unlike terms; misreading "less than."
  • Sec 1.14Equations assert that two expressions are equal; solving finds the value that makes the statement true. Functions assign one output to each input. All Chapter 1 skills carry forward directly.

Key Terms Review

algebra
The branch of mathematics that uses symbols to represent unknown, changing, or general quantities and their relationships.
variable
A symbol (usually a letter) representing an unknown, an input, or a changing quantity.
constant
A fixed number in an expression that does not depend on any variable.
coefficient
The numerical factor multiplied by the variable part of a term. The coefficient of −3x is −3; of x it is 1.
term
A single quantity in an expression connected to others by addition or subtraction.
factor
One of the quantities multiplied together in a product.
expression
A mathematical phrase made of numbers, variables, and operations — no equals sign.
like terms
Terms with exactly the same variable part, including identical exponents. Only like terms may be combined.
unlike terms
Terms with different variable parts. They cannot be combined by addition or subtraction.
distributive property
The rule a(b + c) = ab + ac. Multiplication distributes over addition and subtraction.
equivalent expressions
Two expressions that produce the same value for every value of the variable.
evaluate
To find the numerical value of an expression by substituting a specific value for each variable.
substitution
Replacing a variable with a number or expression. Negative values must always be enclosed in parentheses.
simplify
To rewrite an expression in an equivalent but more compact form by distributing and combining like terms.
algebraic model
An expression, equation, or formula that represents a real-world situation using defined variables.
equation
A mathematical statement that two expressions are equal, written with an equals sign. Introduced fully in Chapter 3.

Word Problems

For each problem: define your variable, write an algebraic expression or model, and answer the specific question.

  1. A gym membership costs $50 per month. Write an expression for total cost after any number of months. How much after one full year?
  2. A store sells pens for $1.25 and notebooks for $3.00. Write an expression for the total cost of p pens and n notebooks. Find the total for 4 pens and 3 notebooks.
  3. A car travels at 55 mph. Write an expression for the distance in t hours. How far in 3.5 hours?
  4. A student scored 72 on the first test and p additional points on the second. Write an expression for the combined score. If the combined score is 155, how many points on the second test?
  5. A field has length 3x meters and width (x + 5) meters. Write a simplified expression for the perimeter.
  6. A business sells candles for $8 each. Materials cost $3 per candle; monthly overhead is $200. Write an expression for monthly profit on c candles. Find profit for 60 candles sold.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 2: Real Numbers and Algebraic Properties

Chapter 1 gave you the vocabulary and mechanics of algebraic expressions. Chapter 2 will strengthen the number-system foundation on which all algebra is built — integers, rational numbers, irrational numbers, real numbers, absolute value, and operations with signed numbers. You will also see why the properties of arithmetic (commutative, associative, distributive, identity, inverse) are not arbitrary rules but the deep structural reasons that every algebraic manipulation is valid. Chapter 3 will then apply all of this to the central task of Algebra I: solving equations.

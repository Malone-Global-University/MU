Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Mathematics is a language. Like any language, it needs rules so that the people using it understand each other. When you write a sentence in English, word order changes meaning. "The dog bit the man" says something very different from "The man bit the dog," even though both sentences use the same words.

Mathematical expressions work the same way. When an expression contains more than one operation, the order in which those operations are performed changes the result. Without shared rules, two students could evaluate the same expression and reach two completely different answers, and both could believe they were correct.

Order of operations is not an arbitrary list of rules handed down for no reason. It is an agreement, a shared convention that makes mathematical communication reliable. This chapter teaches that agreement carefully. It begins by showing why the rules are necessary, then builds through grouping symbols, exponents, multiplication and division, addition and subtraction, and increasingly complex expressions. You will also learn to evaluate expressions involving whole numbers, negative numbers, fractions, decimals, nested parentheses, and formulas.

Opening Question

Two students evaluate the same expression: 3 + 4 × 2. Student A adds first, then multiplies: 3 + 4 = 7, then 7 × 2 = 14. Student B multiplies first, then adds: 4 × 2 = 8, then 3 + 8 = 11. Only one student is correct. Which one, and why? By the end of this chapter, you will not only know the answer — you will understand the principle behind it.

Why This Chapter Matters

Order of operations appears in every topic that follows this chapter. When you evaluate algebraic expressions, simplify formulas, use a calculator, write a spreadsheet formula, solve physics problems, or read any mathematical text, you are relying on the order of operations every time. Students who treat this chapter as minor often make consistent errors in Algebra I, geometry, and chemistry, not because they do not know arithmetic, but because they evaluate expressions in the wrong order.

Key Terms

order of operations
The agreed-upon sequence of steps for evaluating a mathematical expression containing more than one operation.
expression
A mathematical phrase combining numbers, operations, and sometimes variables, but with no equals sign.
grouping symbols
Symbols such as parentheses, brackets, braces, and fraction bars that indicate which part of an expression must be evaluated first.
exponent
A number written above and to the right of a base, indicating how many times the base is multiplied by itself.
PEMDAS
Memory acronym: Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication, Division, Addition, Subtraction.
nested parentheses
Grouping symbols placed inside other grouping symbols, evaluated from the innermost group outward.

A diagram showing the structure of a mathematical expression by organizing its operations hierarchically.
fraction bar
The horizontal line separating numerator and denominator; acts as a grouping symbol requiring both to be simplified before dividing.

The rule that multiplication and division (or addition and subtraction), when at the same priority level, are performed from left to right.

Section 11.1

Why Order Matters

Consider the expression 2 + 3 × 4. This expression contains two operations: addition and multiplication. If you work left to right, you get 2 + 3 = 5, then 5 × 4 = 20. If you multiply before you add, you get 3 × 4 = 12, then 2 + 12 = 14. Both computations involve correct arithmetic. The question is which one represents what the expression actually means. Without a rule, the expression is ambiguous — it could mean two different things to two different people.

Mathematics depends on precise communication. If an engineer, scientist, accountant, or teacher writes an expression, every reader must interpret it identically. The agreed-upon rule, developed and standardized over centuries of mathematical practice, is that multiplication is performed before addition unless grouping symbols indicate otherwise. Under this agreement, 2 + 3 × 4 = 14, not 20.

Grouping symbols override this priority. If you want addition to happen first, you can force it with parentheses: (2 + 3) × 4 = 20. Parentheses give you the power to say exactly what you mean.

The Core Hierarchy — PEMDAS

1st — Evaluate anything inside grouping symbols, innermost group first.

2nd — Evaluate exponents.

3rd — Perform multiplication and division from left to right.

4th — Perform addition and subtraction from left to right.

About PEMDAS

PEMDAS is a memory tool, not a strict six-step checklist. Multiplication and division are at the same priority level. Addition and subtraction are at the same priority level. The left-to-right rule governs what happens when operations at the same level appear together.

Example 11.1.1
Demonstrating why order changes results
  • ProblemEvaluate 10 − 2 × 3 using correct order. Then show what happens working left to right.
  • ThinkOne subtraction and one multiplication. Multiplication comes before subtraction.
  • Correct10 − 2 × 3 = 10 − 6 = 4
  • Wrong10 − 2 = 8, then 8 × 3 = 24 ← incorrect
  • CheckThe correct answer follows the agreement that multiplication is evaluated before subtraction.
Answer: 10 − 2 × 3 = 4

Section 11.2

Grouping Symbols

Grouping symbols are tools that tell you to evaluate a portion of an expression before applying the surrounding operations. They are the highest priority step in the order of operations. No matter what other operations appear in an expression, anything inside grouping symbols must be simplified first. The most common grouping symbols are parentheses ( ), brackets [ ], and braces { }. The fraction bar, the radical sign, and absolute value bars also function as grouping symbols.

Parentheses are the most familiar. When you see an expression inside parentheses, evaluate that expression completely before moving on. Compare (3 + 5) × 2 = 8 × 2 = 16 to the same numbers without parentheses: 3 + 5 × 2 = 3 + 10 = 13. The parentheses change the answer entirely. They let the writer override the default order and say, evaluate this part first.

The fraction bar groups both the numerator and denominator separately. Both must be fully simplified before the division is performed. Absolute value bars group the expression inside them; the expression is evaluated first, then the absolute value is applied.

Common Mistake — Absolute Value

Some students find the absolute value of each number separately before subtracting. |10| − |14| = 10 − 14 = −4 is not the same as |10 − 14| = 4. The subtraction happens inside the bars first.

Example 11.2.1
Parentheses changing the result
  • ProblemEvaluate (6 + 2) × (5 − 3).
  • ThinkTwo sets of parentheses. Evaluate each first, then multiply.
  • Solve(6 + 2) × (5 − 3) = 8 × 2 = 16
Answer: 16
Example 11.2.2
Fraction bar as a grouping symbol
  • ProblemEvaluate (5 + 7) ÷ (3 × 2), written as a fraction.
  • ThinkSimplify numerator and denominator separately, then divide.
  • SolveNumerator: 5 + 7 = 12 Denominator: 3 × 2 = 6 Result: 12 ÷ 6 = 2
Answer: 2
Example 11.2.3
Absolute value as a grouping symbol
  • ProblemEvaluate 2 × |10 − 14|.
  • ThinkEvaluate inside the absolute value first, then find the absolute value, then multiply.
  • Solve2 × |10 − 14| = 2 × |−4| = 2 × 4 = 8
Answer: 8

Section 11.3

Exponents in Expressions

After grouping symbols have been evaluated, exponents are next. When you encounter an expression with both an exponent and another operation, evaluate the exponent before performing multiplication, division, addition, or subtraction, unless a grouping symbol tells you otherwise.

A critical point: an exponent applies only to its base, which is the number or group immediately to its left. Pay careful attention to what is and what is not inside the base.

Negative Sign and Exponent — Critical Distinction

−3² means the negative of 3². The exponent applies only to 3, not the negative sign.

−3² = −(3²) = −9

(−3)² means the quantity (−3) is being squared. The parentheses make −3 the base.

(−3)² = (−3) × (−3) = +9

Student Warning — Squaring a Sum

Squaring a sum is not the same as summing the squares. (3 + 4)² = 7² = 49, which is not equal to 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25. Always evaluate the group inside parentheses first, then apply the exponent. This error is extremely common and becomes very important in algebra.

Example 11.3.1
Exponents in a multi-operation expression
  • ProblemEvaluate 4 + 3² × 2.
  • ThinkExponents first, then multiplication, then addition.
  • Solve4 + 3² × 2 = 4 + 9 × 2 = 4 + 18 = 22
Answer: 22
Example 11.3.2
Negative sign and exponent — the critical distinction
  • ProblemEvaluate −5² and (−5)². Explain the difference.
  • Solve−5² = −(5 × 5) = −25 [exponent applies only to 5] (−5)² = (−5)(−5) = +25 [parentheses make −5 the base]
  • CheckThey are not equal. The parentheses make all the difference.
Answer: −5² = −25 and (−5)² = 25
Example 11.3.3
Exponent after parentheses
  • ProblemEvaluate (1 + 4)² − 3.
  • ThinkEvaluate the parentheses first, then apply the exponent, then subtract.
  • Solve(1 + 4)² − 3 = 5² − 3 = 25 − 3 = 22
Answer: 22

Section 11.4

Multiplication and Division from Left to Right

Multiplication and division have the same priority in the order of operations. They are not in a strict sequence where all multiplications happen before all divisions. Instead, when multiplication and division appear together in an expression, you perform them from left to right in the order you encounter them.

Student Warning — "M Always Before D"

Because multiplication comes before division in the acronym PEMDAS, many students think all multiplication must be completed before any division. This is incorrect. They are equal in priority. Work left to right. For 12 ÷ 4 × 3: divide first (it's on the left): 12 ÷ 4 = 3, then multiply: 3 × 3 = 9. Not 4 × 3 = 12, then 12 ÷ 12 = 1.

Example 11.4.1
Left-to-right rule with division and multiplication
  • ProblemEvaluate 20 ÷ 5 × 2.
  • ThinkDivision comes first because it is on the left. Multiply second.
  • Solve20 ÷ 5 × 2 = 4 × 2 = 8
  • CheckIf you multiply first: 5 × 2 = 10, then 20 ÷ 10 = 2. That would be wrong under the left-to-right rule.
Answer: 8
Example 11.4.2
Mixing multiplication, division, and addition
  • ProblemEvaluate 5 + 6 × 3 ÷ 9 − 1.
  • ThinkMultiplication and division first, left to right. Then addition and subtraction.
  • SolveStep 1 — M/D left to right: 6 × 3 = 18, then 18 ÷ 9 = 2 Step 2 — A/S: 5 + 2 − 1 = 6
Answer: 6

Section 11.5

Addition and Subtraction from Left to Right

Addition and subtraction share the lowest priority in the standard order of operations. They are evaluated after all grouping symbols, exponents, and multiplication and division have been handled. Like multiplication and division, they share a level: when they appear together, work from left to right.

Consider 10 − 3 + 2. Working left to right: 10 − 3 = 7, then 7 + 2 = 9. If you added first: 3 + 2 = 5, then 10 − 5 = 5. That would be wrong. The left-to-right rule governs the result.

Student Warning — "A Always Before S"

Some students think all addition must be done before all subtraction because A appears before S in PEMDAS. This is a misreading of the acronym. Addition and subtraction are at the same level. Work left to right.

PriorityOperation(s)Rule
1stGrouping symbolsEvaluate from innermost outward
2ndExponentsApply to the base only
3rdMultiplication and DivisionLeft to right — equal priority
4thAddition and SubtractionLeft to right — equal priority

Addition and subtraction left to right
  • ProblemEvaluate 15 − 8 + 3 − 2.
  • ThinkAll addition and subtraction — work left to right.
  • Solve15 − 8 = 7, 7 + 3 = 10, 10 − 2 = 8
Answer: 8
Example 11.5.2
Full multi-operation expression
  • ProblemEvaluate 3² + 4 × 2 − (5 + 1).
  • ThinkGrouping symbol, exponent, multiplication, then addition/subtraction.
  • SolveStep 1 — Grouping: (5 + 1) = 6 Step 2 — Exponent: 3² = 9 Step 3 — Multiply: 4 × 2 = 8 Step 4 — A/S: 9 + 8 − 6 = 11

Section 11.6

PEMDAS with Whole Numbers

Now that each component of PEMDAS has been introduced separately, this section applies all the steps together to whole number expressions. A reliable process for evaluating any expression is: read the entire expression before writing anything; identify grouping symbols and evaluate the innermost first; evaluate exponents; evaluate multiplication and division from left to right; evaluate addition and subtraction from left to right; write the final result. Show your work step by step, rewriting the expression after each step.

Work-Showing Strategy

Write the expression again after each step, replacing only what you just computed. This keeps the rest of the expression visible and reduces copying errors. Each rewrite is a checkpoint.

Example 11.6.1
Four operations with whole numbers
  • ProblemEvaluate 2 + 3² × (8 − 5) − 4.
  • SolveStep 1 — Parentheses: (8 − 5) = 3 → 2 + 3² × 3 − 4 Step 2 — Exponent: 3² = 9 → 2 + 9 × 3 − 4 Step 3 — Multiply: 9 × 3 = 27 → 2 + 27 − 4 Step 4 — A/S: 2 + 27 = 29; 29 − 4 = 25
Answer: 25
Example 11.6.2
Expression with division and multiple operations
  • ProblemEvaluate 40 ÷ (2³) + 5 × 3 − 6.
  • SolveStep 1 — Exponent inside ( ): 2³ = 8 → 40 ÷ 8 + 5 × 3 − 6 Step 2 — M/D left to right: 40 ÷ 8 = 5; 5 × 3 = 15 → 5 + 15 − 6 Step 3 — A/S: 5 + 15 = 20; 20 − 6 = 14
Answer: 14

Section 11.7

PEMDAS with Negative Numbers

Negative numbers interact with the order of operations in ways that students often find confusing, particularly around the placement of negative signs, signed multiplication and division, and the distinction between subtraction and negation. A negative sign in front of a group, such as −(4 + 3), means you evaluate the group first and then negate the result: −(4 + 3) = −7. This is different from −4 + 3 = −1, where the negative sign applies only to 4. When a negative number appears in an expression with other operations, apply the multiplication or division first, keeping track of the sign.

Example 11.7.1
Order of operations with negative numbers
  • ProblemEvaluate (−2)³ + 4 × (−3) − 5.
  • SolveStep 1 — Exponent: (−2)³ = (−2)(−2)(−2) = −8 → −8 + 4 × (−3) − 5 Step 2 — Multiply: 4 × (−3) = −12 → −8 + (−12) − 5 Step 3 — A/S: −8 + (−12) = −20; −20 − 5 = −25
Answer: −25
Example 11.7.2
Mixing negative numbers and exponents
  • ProblemEvaluate −2⁴ + (−2)⁴.
  • Solve−2⁴ = −(2⁴) = −16 [exponent on 2 only] (−2)⁴ = (−2)(−2)(−2)(−2) = +16 −16 + 16 = 0
Answer: 0

Section 11.8

PEMDAS with Fractions

Fractions require two additional considerations. First, the fraction bar is a grouping symbol, so simplify the numerator and denominator separately before dividing. Second, fractions within larger expressions participate in the multiplication-division level and must be evaluated in the correct left-to-right order.

Example 11.8.1
Expression with fraction bar as grouping symbol
  • ProblemEvaluate (3² + 7) ÷ (4 − 2) + 5, written with a fraction bar.
  • SolveNumerator: 3² + 7 = 9 + 7 = 16 Denominator: 4 − 2 = 2 Division: 16 ÷ 2 = 8 Add: 8 + 5 = 13
Answer: 13
Example 11.8.2
Multiplying by a fraction inside an expression
  • ProblemEvaluate 4 + (3/4) × 8 − 2.
  • ThinkMultiplication first, then addition and subtraction.
  • Solve(3/4) × 8 = 24/4 = 6 4 + 6 − 2 = 8
Answer: 8
Example 11.8.3
Division of fractions inside an expression
  • ProblemEvaluate (1/2) ÷ (1/4) + 3.
  • ThinkDivision before addition. Dividing by 1/4 means multiplying by 4.
  • Solve(1/2) ÷ (1/4) = (1/2) × 4 = 2 2 + 3 = 5
Answer: 5

Section 11.9

PEMDAS with Decimals

The order of operations does not change when decimals appear in an expression. Every rule from the previous sections applies exactly as before. The only additional care required is accurate decimal arithmetic: aligning decimal points when adding or subtracting, and tracking decimal places when multiplying. When a decimal expression is complex, it is helpful to estimate first. After evaluating, check whether your answer is close to the estimate. This catches errors in decimal placement.

Example 11.9.1
Decimals with multiplication and addition
  • ProblemEvaluate 3.5 + 2.4 × 5.
  • Solve2.4 × 5 = 12.0 3.5 + 12.0 = 15.5
Answer: 15.5
Example 11.9.2
Decimals with parentheses and exponent
  • ProblemEvaluate (1.2 + 0.8)² − 1.5 × 2.
  • SolveStep 1 — Parentheses: 1.2 + 0.8 = 2.0 Step 2 — Exponent: 2.0² = 4.0 Step 3 — Multiply: 1.5 × 2 = 3.0 Step 4 — Subtract: 4.0 − 3.0 = 1.0
Answer: 1.0
Example 11.9.3
Decimal expression with division
  • ProblemEvaluate 0.5 × 4 + 6.0 ÷ 1.5 − 0.2.
  • SolveM/D left to right: 0.5 × 4 = 2.0; 6.0 ÷ 1.5 = 4.0 A/S: 2.0 + 4.0 − 0.2 = 5.8
Answer: 5.8

Section 11.10

Nested Parentheses

Nested parentheses occur when grouping symbols appear inside other grouping symbols. The rule is straightforward: begin with the innermost group and work outward, one layer at a time. Mathematicians often use different grouping symbols at different nesting levels to make an expression easier to read. Parentheses are used at the innermost level, brackets at the next, and braces at the outermost level. All three work identically in arithmetic, but alternating them helps readers identify the layers. Always find the innermost group, the one with no further grouping symbols inside it, and evaluate that first. When you finish evaluating an inner group, rewrite the whole expression before continuing.

Example 11.10.1
Three layers of grouping
  • ProblemEvaluate 4 + {3 × [2 + (6 − 4)] − 5}.
  • SolveInnermost: (6 − 4) = 2 → 4 + {3 × [2 + 2] − 5} Next layer: [2 + 2] = 4 → 4 + {3 × 4 − 5} Inside { }: 3 × 4 = 12; 12 − 5 = 7 → 4 + 7 Final: 4 + 7 = 11
Answer: 11
Example 11.10.2
Exponent outside nested parentheses
  • ProblemEvaluate [(3 + 1)² − 4] × 2.
  • SolveInnermost: (3 + 1) = 4 Exponent: 4² = 16 Inside [ ]: 16 − 4 = 12 Multiply: 12 × 2 = 24
Answer: 24

Section 11.11

Expression Trees and Step-by-Step Evaluation

An expression tree is a diagram that represents the structure of a mathematical expression. The tree shows which operations depend on which values, and in what order they should be evaluated. Expression trees make the hierarchy of PEMDAS visible as a structure rather than a sequence of rules. They are common in computer science, where programming languages and calculators use them to parse and evaluate mathematical input.

In an expression tree, the root represents the last operation to be performed, the one with the lowest priority. Moving downward, each node represents an earlier operation. The leaves at the very bottom represent individual numbers. To evaluate, you start at the leaves and work upward.

Expression tree for 3 + 4 × 2: + ← root (last operation: addition) / \ 3 × ← evaluated before addition / \ 4 2 ← leaves (individual numbers) Evaluate bottom up: 4 × 2 = 8, then 3 + 8 = 11
Expression tree for (2 + 3)² − 4 × 5: − ← root (last operation: subtraction) / \ ^ × / \ / \ ( ) 2 4 5 / \ 2 3 Evaluate: 2 + 3 = 5, 5² = 25, 4 × 5 = 20, 25 − 20 = 5
When to Draw a Tree

You do not need to draw a full tree every time. But when you make a mistake you cannot find, sketching the tree can help you see which operation should be evaluated last, and trace backward to find where the evaluation went wrong.

Example 11.11.1
Step-by-step evaluation with written record
  • ProblemShow a complete step-by-step evaluation of 2 + [(6 − 2)² ÷ 4] × 3.
  • SolveStep 1: (6 − 2) = 4 → 2 + [4² ÷ 4] × 3 Step 2: 4² = 16 → 2 + [16 ÷ 4] × 3 Step 3: 16 ÷ 4 = 4 → 2 + 4 × 3 Step 4: 4 × 3 = 12 → 2 + 12 Step 5: 2 + 12 = 14
Answer: 14

Section 11.12

Common PEMDAS Misunderstandings

Most errors in the order of operations come not from carelessness, but from a handful of persistent misunderstandings. This section names each one directly, explains why it is wrong, and shows a correction.

Misunderstanding 1 — "M Always Before D"
12 ÷ 3 × 2 = 12 ÷ 6 = 2 Correct: 12 ÷ 3 × 2 = 4 × 2 = 8 [division is on the left; go left to right]
Misunderstanding 2 — "A Always Before S"
10 − 3 + 2 = 10 − 5 = 5 Correct: 10 − 3 + 2 = 7 + 2 = 9 [work left to right at the same level]
Misunderstanding 3 — "−3² = 9"
−3² = (−3)² = 9 Correct: −3² = −(3²) = −9 [exponent applies only to 3, not the negative sign]
Misunderstanding 4 — Distributing an Exponent Over Addition
(3 + 4)² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 Correct: (3 + 4)² = 7² = 49 [evaluate the group first, then apply the exponent]
Misunderstanding 5 — Ignoring the Fraction Bar as Grouping
(6 + 4) ÷ 2 treated as 6 + (4 ÷ 2) = 6 + 2 = 8 Correct: (6 + 4) ÷ 2 = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 [the numerator is fully grouped]
Misunderstanding 6 — Working Nested Parentheses Outside In
Evaluating the outer group before the inner group Correct: Always find the innermost group first — the one with no further grouping inside — and work outward.
Misunderstanding 7 — Skipping the Check
Moving to the next problem without verifying the answer Fix: After evaluating, look back at the original expression. A mistake in an early step affects every step after it. The check habit is especially valuable in PEMDAS work.
Example 11.12.1
Correcting a PEMDAS error
  • ProblemA student evaluates 5 + 10 ÷ 2 × 5 − 3 by multiplying 2 × 5 = 10 first, getting 5 + 10 ÷ 10 − 3 = 5 + 1 − 3 = 3. Find the error and give the correct answer.
  • ErrorThe student multiplied before dividing. But division is to the left of multiplication here, so it must go first.
  • Correct10 ÷ 2 = 5, then 5 × 5 = 25 5 + 25 − 3 = 27
Answer: 27

Section 11.13

Order of Operations in Formulas

Formulas are expressions that calculate specific quantities. Every formula you encounter in mathematics, science, finance, or engineering depends on the order of operations to give the correct result. If you evaluate a formula in the wrong order, you will calculate the wrong quantity, even if your arithmetic is otherwise perfect. This section applies order of operations to formulas from geometry and science that students have encountered or will encounter throughout this textbook and in later MGU courses.

Chapter 11 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 11.1Order of operations is a shared convention that makes expressions unambiguous. Without it, the same expression produces different results for different readers. PEMDAS is the agreed-upon hierarchy.
  • Sec 11.2Grouping symbols — parentheses, brackets, braces, fraction bars, absolute value bars, and radical signs — override the default order. Evaluate the innermost group first. The fraction bar groups both the numerator and denominator separately.
  • Sec 11.3Exponents are evaluated after grouping symbols. An exponent applies only to its base. Without parentheses, −3² = −9. With parentheses, (−3)² = 9. Squaring a sum is not the same as summing the squares.
  • Sec 11.4Multiplication and division share equal priority and are performed from left to right. Writing M before D in PEMDAS does not mean all multiplication happens before all division.
  • Sec 11.5Addition and subtraction share the lowest priority and are performed from left to right. They are equal in priority. Writing A before S in PEMDAS does not mean all addition happens before all subtraction.
  • Sec 11.6Applying full PEMDAS to whole numbers: read the expression, evaluate grouping, evaluate exponents, evaluate M/D left to right, evaluate A/S left to right. Rewrite the expression after each step.
  • Sec 11.7Negative numbers require attention to negative signs before groups, signed multiplication, and the distinction between subtraction and negation. The order of operations rules are unchanged.
  • Sec 11.8Fractions: the fraction bar groups numerator and denominator separately. Simplify each before dividing. Fractions within expressions follow the same PEMDAS hierarchy as other numbers.
  • Sec 11.9Decimals follow the same order of operations rules. Additional care is needed for accurate decimal arithmetic. Estimate before evaluating to check decimal placement.
  • Sec 11.10Nested parentheses are evaluated from the innermost group outward. Alternating parentheses, brackets, and braces identifies layers. Rewrite the expression after each inner group is evaluated.
  • Sec 11.11An expression tree diagrams the hierarchy of an expression. The root is the last operation; leaves are individual numbers. Trees help locate errors in complex multi-step evaluations.
  • Sec 11.12Seven recurring misunderstandings: M before D; A before S; −3² = 9; distributing exponents over sums; ignoring the fraction bar; working nested groups outside in; skipping the check.
  • Sec 11.13Every formula depends on correct order of operations. Trapezoid area requires adding bases before multiplying. Temperature conversion requires subtracting 32 before multiplying by 5/9. Cylinder volume requires squaring r before multiplying.

Key Terms Review

order of operations
The agreed-upon sequence for evaluating a multi-operation expression. Ensures every reader gets the same result.
expression
A mathematical phrase with numbers, operations, and possibly variables — no equals sign.
grouping symbols
Parentheses, brackets, braces, fraction bars, and radical symbols that indicate evaluation priority. Always evaluated first.
exponent
A symbol showing repeated multiplication of a base. Applies to the base only unless parentheses make a negative sign part of the base.
PEMDAS
Memory acronym for Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication, Division, Addition, Subtraction. M/D and A/S are equal pairs evaluated left to right.
nested parentheses
Grouping symbols inside other grouping symbols. Always evaluate the innermost group first, then work outward.
expression tree
A diagram showing the hierarchical structure of an expression. Root = last operation. Leaves = individual numbers.
fraction bar
The grouping symbol separating numerator from denominator. Both must be fully simplified before the division is performed.
left-to-right rule
When operations at the same priority level appear together, evaluate them from left to right. Applies to M/D and to A/S.

Bridge Note — Preparing for Algebra I
Chapter 12: Coordinate Plane and Patterns

Order of operations does not disappear when you move into Algebra I. It becomes more important, because algebraic expressions use letters alongside numbers, and every step of simplifying, solving, or evaluating an expression depends on correct order. In Algebra I, you will encounter expressions such as 2x² − 3(x + 4). Evaluating this for a specific value of x requires substitution, applying the exponent, distributing the multiplication across the parentheses, and combining like terms, all in the correct order. You will also work with formulas in science and with function notation, where parentheses and operations appear together constantly. Every skill in this chapter carries forward. If any section felt unclear, return to it before moving on.

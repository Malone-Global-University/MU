Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 3 built a full foundation for fractions, including what they mean, how they relate to division, how to place them on a number line, and how to add, subtract, multiply, and divide them. Chapter 4 extends that foundation into a form of number that appears constantly in everyday life: the mixed number.

A mixed number combines a whole number and a fraction into a single quantity. When a recipe calls for 2½ cups of flour, when a board is 3¾ feet long, or when someone runs 1⅛ miles, the values are stated as mixed numbers because that form is easy to read, easy to estimate, and connected to the way people naturally measure things.

This chapter will teach you to move fluently between mixed numbers and improper fractions, to perform all four operations with mixed numbers, and to recognize which form is the better tool for a given problem. The chapter will also prepare you for measurement applications, word problems, and the kind of fraction fluency that algebra requires.

Opening Question

A carpenter is building a bookshelf. The plans say each shelf is 2⅛ feet long. She needs to cut four shelves from a single piece of wood that is 10 feet long. Does she have enough wood? How much will be left over?

You could answer this question with careful decimal estimation, or you could try to work directly with the fractions, but the most natural and exact path uses mixed number arithmetic. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to solve this problem and many like it clearly and confidently.

Why This Chapter Matters

Fractions appear in almost every practical measurement context, and most of the time they appear as mixed numbers rather than as pure fractions. A fraction like 11/4 is mathematically correct, but a carpenter, a cook, or a nurse is more likely to say 2¾. Mixed numbers are the everyday language of fractions.

At the same time, improper fractions are often easier to compute with. When you multiply or divide mixed numbers, converting to improper fractions first makes the arithmetic cleaner and less error-prone. Understanding both forms, and knowing when to use each, is one of the most practical skills in arithmetic. It also prepares you for algebra, where fractional expressions appear constantly in equations, formulas, and word problems.

Section 4.1

Why Mixed Numbers Matter

When people measure, build, cook, or calculate in everyday life, they rarely encounter quantities that land exactly on whole numbers. A person might weigh 142½ pounds. A piece of pipe might be 4⅜ inches long. A trip might take 2¼ hours. A bag of rice might hold 5½ pounds.

In each of these situations, the quantity is more than a whole number but less than the next whole number. A mixed number captures this naturally. It says: here are some complete wholes, and here is a fraction of the next one.

Mixed numbers also make estimation easier. If someone tells you a recipe needs 19/8 cups of milk, you might have to stop and think. But if they say 2⅜ cups, you immediately know it is a little more than 2 cups. The mixed number form communicates size instantly.

Why this matters beyond convenience. Mixed numbers appear in practical measurements, technical specifications, blueprints, maps, dosing charts, and financial calculations. A student who cannot work fluently with mixed numbers will find many real-world math problems much harder than necessary.

Section 4.2

Whole Numbers and Fraction Parts

A mixed number has exactly two parts: a whole number and a proper fraction. These two parts are written side by side, and together they represent a single value.

Structure of a Mixed Number

3 whole number part + 2/5 fraction part = 3 and two-fifths

The whole number part tells you how many complete units are included. The fraction part tells you how much of the next unit is included. The fraction part must always be a proper fraction — its numerator must be smaller than its denominator.

Reading mixed numbers. The mixed number 3⅔ is read as "three and two-fifths." The word "and" is the dividing line between the whole number and the fraction. In careful mathematical writing, the word "and" is reserved specifically for this purpose.

Visualizing mixed numbers. Imagine a set of circles, each divided into fifths. To show 3⅔, you would shade three entire circles and two-fifths of a fourth circle. The three full circles represent the whole number part. The partially shaded circle represents the fraction part.

The fraction part must be proper. A mixed number is only correctly written if its fraction part has a numerator smaller than its denominator. The value 3⅗⁄₅ is not in proper form because 5/5 = 1, which means the true value is 4. Similarly, 3⅙⁄₅ is not in proper form because 7/5 is itself an improper fraction.

Student Tip

When you finish a calculation involving mixed numbers, always check your fraction part. If the numerator is larger than or equal to the denominator, simplify by carrying the excess into the whole number.

Negative mixed numbers. Mixed numbers can be negative. The value −2⅓ means negative two and one-third. On a number line, it sits between −3 and −2, closer to −2. The negative sign applies to the entire mixed number.

Section 4.3

Improper Fractions

An improper fraction is a fraction whose numerator is greater than or equal to its denominator. Examples: 7/3, 11/4, 9/9, 15/2. Each of these represents a value of one or more.

Improper fractions are not wrong. The word "improper" might suggest that these fractions are somehow incorrect or forbidden. They are not. Improper fractions are perfectly valid numbers that represent values of one or greater. The name "improper" is historical, not judgmental.

Why improper fractions are useful. When performing multiplication and division with mixed numbers, improper fractions are much easier to work with. Trying to multiply 2¾ × 1½ directly in mixed number form leads to errors. Converting both to improper fractions first gives 11/4 × 3/2, which is a straightforward fraction multiplication.

Placing improper fractions on a number line. To place 11/4 on a number line, divide: 11 ÷ 4 = 2 remainder 3, so 11/4 = 2¾. This tells you the fraction falls between 2 and 3, three-quarters of the way from 2 to 3.

Section 4.4

Converting Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions

Converting a mixed number to an improper fraction is one of the most important mechanical skills in this chapter. You will use this conversion every time you multiply or divide mixed numbers.

Conversion Method

1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator of the fraction part.

2. Add the numerator of the fraction part to that product.

3. Write the result over the original denominator.

a (b/c) = (a × c) + b over c

The denominator does not change.

Example 4.4.1
Converting a mixed number to an improper fraction
  • ProblemConvert 3⅔ to an improper fraction.
  • ThinkThe whole number is 3. The fraction part is 2/5. The denominator is 5. I need to find how many fifths are in this mixed number in total.
  • Solve Step 1: Multiply the whole number by the denominator. 3 × 5 = 15 Step 2: Add the numerator. 15 + 2 = 17 Step 3: Write over the original denominator. 17/5
  • CheckDoes 17/5 equal 3⅔? Divide 17 by 5: 17 ÷ 5 = 3 remainder 2. So 17/5 = 3⅔. ✓
Answer: 3 and 2/5 = 17/5
Example 4.4.2
Converting with a larger whole number
  • ProblemConvert 7⅜ to an improper fraction.
  • ThinkThe whole number is 7. The denominator is 8. Multiply, then add the numerator.
  • Solve 7 × 8 = 56 56 + 3 = 59 59/8
  • Check59 ÷ 8 = 7 remainder 3, so 59/8 = 7⅜. ✓
Answer: 7 and 3/8 = 59/8
Example 4.4.3
Converting a negative mixed number
  • ProblemConvert −4⅓ to an improper fraction.
  • ThinkThe negative sign applies to the whole mixed number. I will convert the positive version first, then apply the negative sign.
  • Solve 4 × 3 = 12 12 + 1 = 13 13/3 Apply the negative sign: −13/3
  • Check13/3 = 4⅓, so −13/3 = −4⅓. ✓
Answer: −4 and 1/3 = −13/3
Common Mistake

Some students forget to add the numerator and instead just write the product. For example, they convert 3⅔ as 15/5, forgetting the +2. Always complete both steps: multiply, then add.

Why the Method Works

The whole number 3 contains 3 × 5 = 15 fifths. The fraction part adds 2 more fifths. The total is 15 + 2 = 17 fifths. The denominator stays 5 because the size of each piece has not changed.

Section 4.5

Converting Improper Fractions to Mixed Numbers

The reverse conversion, from improper fraction to mixed number, uses division.

Conversion Method

1. Divide the numerator by the denominator.

2. The quotient becomes the whole number part.

3. The remainder becomes the new numerator.

4. The denominator stays the same.

Example 4.5.1
Converting an improper fraction to a mixed number
  • ProblemConvert 17/5 to a mixed number.
  • ThinkDivide 17 by 5 and find the quotient and remainder.
  • Solve 17 ÷ 5 = 3 remainder 2 Whole number part: 3 New numerator: 2 Denominator: 5 17/5 = 3 and 2/5
  • CheckConvert back: 3 × 5 + 2 = 17. ✓
Answer: 17/5 = 3 and 2/5
Example 4.5.2
Remainder of zero — result is a whole number
  • ProblemConvert 24/6 to a mixed number.
  • Solve 24 ÷ 6 = 4 remainder 0 24/6 = 4
  • NoteWhen the remainder is zero, the result is a whole number, not a mixed number.
Answer: 24/6 = 4
Example 4.5.3
Larger improper fraction
  • ProblemConvert 47/6 to a mixed number.
  • Solve 47 ÷ 6 = 7 remainder 5 47/6 = 7 and 5/6
  • Check7 × 6 + 5 = 42 + 5 = 47. ✓
Answer: 47/6 = 7 and 5/6
Student Tip

Keep the denominator unchanged throughout this process. The denominator does not get divided or adjusted. Only the numerator goes through the division step.

Section 4.6

Adding Mixed Numbers

Adding mixed numbers follows the same principle as adding whole numbers: you add the whole number parts together and the fraction parts together, as long as you handle the fraction parts with a common denominator.

General Method for Adding Mixed Numbers

1. Find a common denominator for the fraction parts, if needed.

2. Add the fraction parts.

3. Add the whole number parts.

4. Simplify the fraction part if needed.

5. If the fraction part becomes improper, carry into the whole number.

Example 4.6.1
Adding with the same denominator
  • Problem2⅕ + 3⅔
  • ThinkThe denominators match. Add the fractions, then add the whole numbers.
  • Solve Fraction parts: 1/5 + 2/5 = 3/5 Whole number parts: 2 + 3 = 5 Result: 5 and 3/5
  • CheckConvert to improper fractions: 11/5 + 17/5 = 28/5 = 5⅕. ✓
Answer: 5 and 3/5
Example 4.6.2
Adding with different denominators
  • Problem1⅔ + 2¼
  • ThinkThe denominators 3 and 4 are different. The least common denominator (LCD) is 12.
  • Solve Convert fractions: 2/3 = 8/12 1/4 = 3/12 Add fraction parts: 8/12 + 3/12 = 11/12 Add whole numbers: 1 + 2 = 3 Result: 3 and 11/12
  • Check5/3 + 9/4 = 20/12 + 27/12 = 47/12 = 3⅝⁄₁₂. ✓
Answer: 3 and 11/12
Example 4.6.3
Sum with an improper fraction part — carrying required
  • Problem4¾ + 2¾
  • ThinkThe denominators match, but the fractions add to more than one.
  • Solve Fraction parts: 3/4 + 3/4 = 6/4 6/4 is improper: 6/4 = 1 and 2/4 = 1 and 1/2 Whole number parts: 4 + 2 = 6 Add the carried 1: 6 + 1 = 7 Result: 7 and 1/2
  • Check19/4 + 11/4 = 30/4 = 15/2 = 7½. ✓
Answer: 7 and 1/2
Student Warning

Do not forget to carry when the fraction parts add to more than one. If the fraction part of your answer has a numerator larger than its denominator, you must simplify it and carry the whole number part into the whole number sum.

Section 4.7

Subtracting Mixed Numbers

Subtracting mixed numbers is similar to adding them, except you must also be alert to a situation where the fraction in the first number is smaller than the fraction you need to subtract. When this happens, regrouping is required.

General Method for Subtracting Mixed Numbers

1. Find a common denominator for the fraction parts, if needed.

2. Check whether the first fraction part is large enough to subtract from. If not, regroup.

3. Subtract the fraction parts.

4. Subtract the whole number parts.

5. Simplify if needed.

Example 4.7.1
Subtracting with the same denominator — no regrouping needed
  • Problem5⅜⁄₇ − 2⅙⁄₇
  • ThinkThe denominators match. The first fraction (3/7) is larger than the second (1/7). No regrouping needed.
  • Solve Fraction parts: 3/7 − 1/7 = 2/7 Whole number parts: 5 − 2 = 3 Result: 3 and 2/7
  • Check38/7 − 15/7 = 23/7 = 3⅚⁄₇. ✓
Answer: 3 and 2/7
Example 4.7.2
Subtracting with different denominators
  • Problem6⅙⁄₆ − 1⅓
  • ThinkFind the LCD. LCD(6, 3) = 6.
  • Solve Convert: 1/3 = 2/6 Fraction parts: 5/6 − 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2 Whole number parts: 6 − 1 = 5 Result: 5 and 1/2
  • Check41/6 − 8/6 = 33/6 = 11/2 = 5½. ✓
Answer: 5 and 1/2
Section 4.8

Regrouping with Mixed Numbers

Regrouping is the most conceptually demanding step in mixed number subtraction. It occurs when the fraction in the first mixed number is smaller than the fraction you need to subtract.

The idea. When you cannot subtract a larger fraction from a smaller one, you "borrow" one whole from the whole number part and convert it into a fraction using the common denominator. This increases the fraction part while decreasing the whole number part by 1.

The process. Borrowing one whole means borrowing d/d, where d is the denominator. The borrowed whole becomes d/d, which is then added to the existing fraction part.

Example 4.8.1
Regrouping in subtraction
  • Problem5¼ − 2¾
  • ThinkThe denominators match. But 1/4 < 3/4, so I cannot subtract yet. I must regroup.
  • Solve Regroup the first mixed number: Borrow 1 from the whole number 5. 5 and 1/4 = 4 + 1 + 1/4 = 4 + 4/4 + 1/4 = 4 and 5/4 Now subtract: Fraction parts: 5/4 − 3/4 = 2/4 = 1/2 Whole number parts: 4 − 2 = 2 Result: 2 and 1/2
  • Check21/4 − 11/4 = 10/4 = 5/2 = 2½. ✓
Answer: 2 and 1/2
Example 4.8.2
Regrouping with the same denominator
  • Problem7⅙ − 3⅙⁄₆
  • ThinkSame denominator, but 1/6 < 5/6. Regroup.
  • Solve 7 and 1/6 = 6 + 6/6 + 1/6 = 6 and 7/6 Fraction parts: 7/6 − 5/6 = 2/6 = 1/3 Whole number parts: 6 − 3 = 3 Result: 3 and 1/3
  • Check43/6 − 23/6 = 20/6 = 10/3 = 3⅓. ✓
Answer: 3 and 1/3
Example 4.8.3
Subtracting a mixed number from a whole number
  • Problem8 − 3⅔
  • ThinkThe first number has no fraction part. I must regroup the whole number 8 to create a fraction.
  • Solve Rewrite 8 as 7 and 5/5 (borrow 1 from 8; write it as 5/5) Fraction parts: 5/5 − 2/5 = 3/5 Whole number parts: 7 − 3 = 4 Result: 4 and 3/5
  • Check40/5 − 17/5 = 23/5 = 4⅕. ✓
Answer: 4 and 3/5
Student Warning

After regrouping, you must reduce the whole number by 1. If you borrowed 1 from 5 to create 4/4, the whole number must become 4, not remain at 5. Forgetting to reduce the whole number is one of the most common errors in this section.

Alternative Method

Some students prefer to convert both mixed numbers to improper fractions before subtracting, especially when regrouping feels uncertain. Both methods are equally correct. Use whichever you find more reliable, but practice regrouping as well, because it is faster when numbers are large and the fractions are simple.

Section 4.9

Multiplying Mixed Numbers

Multiplication is where improper fractions show their advantage most clearly. To multiply mixed numbers, the standard and most reliable method is to convert both to improper fractions first, then multiply as with regular fractions.

Why not multiply in mixed number form? Some students try to multiply the whole number parts together and the fraction parts together separately. This does not give the correct result because the distributive property requires cross-products that are easy to miss. Converting to improper fractions avoids this problem entirely.

Method for Multiplying Mixed Numbers

1. Convert each mixed number to an improper fraction.

2. Multiply numerators together.

3. Multiply denominators together.

4. Simplify and convert the result back to a mixed number if appropriate.

Example 4.9.1
Multiplying two mixed numbers
  • Problem2½ × 1⅔
  • ThinkConvert both to improper fractions first.
  • Solve 2 and 1/2 = 5/2 1 and 2/3 = 5/3 5/2 × 5/3 = 25/6 Convert: 25/6 = 4 and 1/6
  • CheckEstimate: 2.5 × 1.67 ≈ 4.17. And 4⅙ ≈ 4.17. ✓
Answer: 4 and 1/6
Example 4.9.2
Using cancellation before multiplying
  • Problem3⅓ × 2¼
  • ThinkConvert to improper fractions. Look for common factors before multiplying to keep numbers smaller.
  • Solve 3 and 1/3 = 10/3 2 and 1/4 = 9/4 Cross-cancel: 9 and 3 share a factor of 3: 9÷3=3, 3÷3=1 10 and 4 share a factor of 2: 10÷2=5, 4÷2=2 (10/3) × (9/4) = (5/1) × (3/2) = 15/2 Convert: 15/2 = 7 and 1/2
  • Check3.33 × 2.25 ≈ 7.49 ≈ 7.5 = 7½. ✓
Answer: 7 and 1/2
Example 4.9.3
Mixed number times a whole number
  • Problem4 × 2⅜
  • ThinkWrite 4 as 4/1, then proceed normally.
  • Solve 4/1 × 19/8 Cancel: 4 and 8 share a factor of 4. 4÷4=1, 8÷4=2 (1/1) × (19/2) = 19/2 = 9 and 1/2
  • Check4 × 2.375 = 9.5 = 9½. ✓
Answer: 9 and 1/2
Student Tip — Cancellation

Cancellation before multiplying (also called cross-cancellation) keeps the numbers smaller and reduces the need to simplify at the end. It is not required, but it is a good habit that saves time on harder problems.

Section 4.10

Dividing Mixed Numbers

Dividing mixed numbers follows the same rule as dividing fractions: multiply by the reciprocal. The additional step is converting mixed numbers to improper fractions before applying the rule.

Method for Dividing Mixed Numbers

1. Convert each mixed number to an improper fraction.

2. Change the division to multiplication by the reciprocal of the divisor.

3. Multiply and simplify.

4. Convert the result to a mixed number if appropriate.

Example 4.10.1
Dividing two mixed numbers
  • Problem3½ ÷ 1¾
  • ThinkConvert to improper fractions. Flip the second fraction (the divisor) and multiply.
  • Solve 3 and 1/2 = 7/2 1 and 3/4 = 7/4 7/2 ÷ 7/4 = 7/2 × 4/7 Cancel: the 7s cancel; 4 and 2 cancel. = (1/1) × (2/1) = 2
  • Check3.5 ÷ 1.75 = 2. ✓
Answer: 2
Example 4.10.2
Dividing a mixed number by a whole number
  • Problem5¼ ÷ 3
  • ThinkWrite 3 as 3/1. Convert and divide.
  • Solve 5 and 1/4 = 21/4 21/4 ÷ 3/1 = 21/4 × 1/3 = 21/12 = 7/4 = 1 and 3/4
  • Check5.25 ÷ 3 = 1.75 = 1¾. ✓
Answer: 1 and 3/4
Example 4.10.3
Applied division — splitting a recipe
  • ProblemA recipe calls for 3¾ cups of sugar to make 5 dozen cookies. How much sugar is needed per dozen?
  • Solve 3 and 3/4 ÷ 5 = 15/4 ÷ 5/1 = 15/4 × 1/5 = 15/20 = 3/4
  • ExplainEach dozen cookies requires 3/4 cup of sugar.
Answer: 3/4 cup per dozen.
Student Warning

Always flip the divisor — the second number in the expression. A common error is flipping the first number instead. In a ÷ b = a × (1/b), it is b, the divisor, that gets flipped.

Section 4.11

Relationships of Numbers

The ability to work with mixed numbers deepens your understanding of how different categories of numbers relate to one another. This section places mixed numbers into the broader picture of the number system.

Fractions, mixed numbers, and improper fractions. These are three representations that can all describe the same value. The number three and three-quarters can be written as 3¾ = 15/4 = 30/8 = 45/12. All of these are the same point on the number line. The different forms are useful for different purposes.

Decimals and mixed numbers. Every mixed number can be converted to a decimal. The whole number part becomes the integer part of the decimal. The fraction part is converted to its decimal equivalent. For example: 3¾ = 3 + 0.75 = 3.75. This connection is important for estimation, calculator work, and practical measurement.

Negative mixed numbers. Negative mixed numbers extend the number line to the left of zero. The value −2½ sits between −3 and −2 on the number line. The negative sign applies to the entire number: −2½ = −5/2.

Order and comparison. To compare two mixed numbers, compare the whole number parts first. If they are equal, compare the fraction parts using a common denominator. For example, 4⅓ < 4½ because ⅓ < ½, and the whole number parts are equal.

Section 4.12

Adding Mixed Measures

In many practical situations, measurements are expressed using two units at once. A height might be 5 feet 9 inches. A time might be 2 hours 45 minutes. A length might be 3 yards 2 feet. These are called mixed measures, and adding them requires the same thinking as adding mixed numbers, with one added layer: unit conversion.

General Method for Adding Mixed Measures

1. Add the smaller units together.

2. If the smaller units exceed one full larger unit, convert and carry.

3. Add the larger units together, including any carried amount.

4. Write the result in proper form.

Example 4.12.1
Adding feet and inches
  • ProblemAdd 3 feet 9 inches and 2 feet 7 inches.
  • ThinkThere are 12 inches in a foot. Add the inches first, then check whether they exceed 12.
  • Solve Inches: 9 + 7 = 16 inches Since 16 ≥ 12, convert: 16 = 1 foot 4 inches Feet: 3 + 2 + 1 = 6 feet
  • Answer6 feet 4 inches.
Answer: 6 feet 4 inches
Example 4.12.2
Adding hours and minutes
  • ProblemA student spent 1 hour 47 minutes on homework and 2 hours 35 minutes reading. What was the total time?
  • ThinkThere are 60 minutes in an hour.
  • Solve Minutes: 47 + 35 = 82 minutes Since 82 ≥ 60, convert: 82 = 1 hour 22 minutes Hours: 1 + 2 + 1 = 4 hours
  • Answer4 hours 22 minutes.
Answer: 4 hours 22 minutes
Example 4.12.3
Adding fractional measurements
  • ProblemAdd 2⅜ inches and 1⅙⁄₈ inches.
  • ThinkThe denominators are the same. Add the fraction parts, then add the whole numbers.
  • Solve 3/8 + 7/8 = 10/8 = 1 and 2/8 = 1 and 1/4 2 + 1 + 1 = 4 Total: 4 and 1/4 inches
Answer: 4 and 1/4 inches
Student Tip

Mixed measures look different from mixed numbers on the surface, but the same regrouping logic applies. The only additional step is knowing the conversion relationship between units — 12 inches in a foot, 60 minutes in an hour, 16 ounces in a pound, and so on.

Section 4.13

Mixed Numbers in Measurement Problems

Measurement problems are one of the most important real-world contexts for mixed numbers. Carpenters measure board lengths. Nurses calculate medication doses. Cooks scale recipes. Athletes track performance. All of these situations use mixed numbers frequently. This section connects mixed number operations to realistic multi-step problems using the Read-Represent-Solve-Check-Explain method throughout.

Example 4.13.1
Cutting boards — the opening question answered
  • ReadA carpenter has a board that is 12 feet long. She needs to cut four pieces, each 2¾ feet long. How much wood will remain?
  • RepresentTotal cut = 4 × 2¾. Remainder = 12 − total cut.
  • Solve 4 × 2 and 3/4 = 4 × 11/4 = 44/4 = 11 feet 12 − 11 = 1 foot
  • Check4 × 2.75 = 11. 12 − 11 = 1. ✓
  • ExplainAfter cutting four pieces of 2¾ feet each, the carpenter will have 1 foot of wood remaining.
Answer: 1 foot remains.
Example 4.13.2
Scaling a recipe
  • ReadA recipe for one batch of cookies uses 2¼ cups of flour. A baker wants to make 3 batches. How much flour does she need?
  • Solve 3 × 2 and 1/4 = 3/1 × 9/4 = 27/4 = 6 and 3/4 cups
  • ExplainThe baker needs 6¾ cups of flour.
Answer: 6 and 3/4 cups of flour.
Example 4.13.3
Splitting a quantity equally
  • ReadA nurse has 4½ ounces of medication to divide equally among 6 patients. How much does each patient receive?
  • Solve 4 and 1/2 ÷ 6 = 9/2 ÷ 6/1 = 9/2 × 1/6 = 9/12 = 3/4 ounce
  • ExplainEach patient receives 3/4 of an ounce.
Answer: 3/4 ounce per patient.
Section 4.14

Choosing the Best Form

One of the most important judgment skills in this chapter is deciding when to use a mixed number and when to use an improper fraction. The best form depends on what you are trying to do.

Use a mixed number when you are communicating a size or measurement to someone, making an estimation, adding or subtracting with simple fractions, or writing a final answer to a word problem.

Use an improper fraction when you are multiplying or dividing, working within an algebraic expression, or comparing sizes using common denominators.

Situation Better Form
Reading or reporting a measurementMixed number
Estimating a quantityMixed number
Adding with the same denominatorMixed number
Multiplying or dividingImproper fraction
Expressing a final answerMixed number
Algebraic expressions and equationsOften improper fraction
A Note on Algebra Notation

In algebra, you will encounter expressions like (7/2)x more often than 3½x. The improper fraction form fits more naturally into algebraic notation. A mixed number like 3½ placed next to a variable can be misread as 3 × ½, which is a different value. For this reason, algebra generally avoids mixed number notation inside expressions and equations. The mixed number form is best reserved for final answers and real-world communication.

Chapter 4 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 4.1Mixed numbers appear in measurement, cooking, construction, medicine, and time. The mixed number form communicates size naturally and makes estimation immediate.
  • Sec 4.2A mixed number combines a whole number and a proper fraction. The fraction part must always be proper. If it equals or exceeds one, the mixed number must be simplified by carrying.
  • Sec 4.3An improper fraction has a numerator greater than or equal to its denominator. The term "improper" is traditional, not judgmental. Improper fractions are valid numbers preferred for computation.
  • Sec 4.4To convert a mixed number to an improper fraction: multiply the whole number by the denominator, add the numerator, and write the result over the original denominator.
  • Sec 4.5To convert an improper fraction to a mixed number: divide numerator by denominator. The quotient is the whole number part, the remainder is the new numerator, and the denominator stays the same.
  • Sec 4.6To add mixed numbers: find a common denominator, add the fraction parts, add the whole number parts, and carry if the fraction sum is improper.
  • Sec 4.7To subtract mixed numbers: find a common denominator, then subtract the parts. If the first fraction is too small to subtract from, regroup first.
  • Sec 4.8Regrouping in subtraction means borrowing one whole from the whole number part and rewriting it as d/d, where d is the denominator. The whole number part decreases by 1.
  • Sec 4.9To multiply mixed numbers: convert to improper fractions, multiply numerators, multiply denominators, and simplify. Use cancellation before multiplying when possible.
  • Sec 4.10To divide mixed numbers: convert to improper fractions, then multiply by the reciprocal of the divisor. Always flip the divisor, not the dividend.
  • Sec 4.11Mixed numbers, improper fractions, and decimals are different representations of the same values. Comparison uses whole number parts first, then fraction parts with a common denominator.
  • Sec 4.12Mixed measures use two units such as feet and inches or hours and minutes. Adding them requires carrying when the smaller unit exceeds one full larger unit.
  • Sec 4.13Measurement problems use mixed numbers for cutting, scaling, splitting, and combining. The Read-Represent-Solve-Check-Explain method keeps problem solving organized and verifiable.
  • Sec 4.14Mixed numbers are best for communication and estimation. Improper fractions are best for multiplication, division, and algebra. Knowing when to use each form is as important as knowing how to compute with each.

Key Terms Review

mixed number
A number written as a whole number part and a proper fraction part together. Example: 5⅔.
improper fraction
A fraction with a numerator greater than or equal to its denominator. Example: 17/3. Represents a value of one or greater.
proper fraction
A fraction with a numerator less than its denominator. Example: ⅔. Represents a value less than one.
equivalent forms
Different representations of the same value. Example: 2¾ = 11/4 = 2.75 are all the same number.
regrouping
Borrowing one whole unit from the whole number part and rewriting it as a fraction in order to perform subtraction.
mixed measure
A measurement expressed using two units, such as 4 feet 6 inches or 2 hours 15 minutes.
common denominator
A shared denominator used when adding or subtracting fractions with different denominators.
reciprocal
The fraction obtained by flipping numerator and denominator. The reciprocal of ¾ is 4/3. Used when dividing fractions.
cancellation
Dividing a numerator and a denominator by a common factor before multiplying, to simplify the computation.
converting
Rewriting a number in a different but equivalent form, such as changing a mixed number to an improper fraction.
whole number part
The integer portion of a mixed number. In 3¾, the whole number part is 3.
fraction part
The proper fraction portion of a mixed number. In 3¾, the fraction part is ¾.

Bridge Note — From Mixed Numbers to Algebra
Chapter 5: Decimals — and the Path to Pre-Algebra

Mixed numbers are the last major fraction topic before the decimal and percent chapters begin. The skills built in Chapters 3 and 4 will appear throughout the rest of this textbook and throughout Algebra I.

In Chapter 9, you will write algebraic expressions that contain fractions and mixed numbers. An expression like (3/4)x + 1½ combines a coefficient, a variable, a constant, and a mixed number. In Chapter 10, you will solve equations like 2x = 4½ or x/3 = 1⅔. Both require dividing or multiplying with mixed numbers. In Chapter 14, geometry formulas involve fractions frequently — the area of a triangle uses division by 2, and volume formulas often carry fractional dimensions.

As you move into algebra, you will notice that mixed numbers appear less often inside equations and expressions. Algebra generally prefers the improper fraction form because it stays as a single fraction and follows fraction rules cleanly. The flexibility you have developed here — knowing both forms, converting between them comfortably, and choosing the right form for the task — is exactly the kind of fluency algebra expects.

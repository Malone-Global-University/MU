Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces two connected mathematical ideas: exponents and radicals. Exponents are a compact way to represent repeated multiplication. Radicals are a way to reverse that process, finding what number was multiplied by itself a certain number of times to produce a given result. Together, exponents and radicals form one of the most important structural pairs in all of mathematics.

Students who understand exponents can read formulas in science, work with area and volume, interpret large and small numbers in scientific notation, and build a strong foundation for Algebra I. Students who understand radicals can simplify expressions, solve geometric problems, and work with square roots the way professional mathematicians and scientists do, as natural tools rather than confusing symbols.

This chapter does not try to become a full Algebra II treatment of exponents and radicals. Its purpose is clear: to introduce these ideas at the right depth for pre-algebra students, to explain the rules through visible patterns, and to prepare students for the next level of mathematics without leaving them behind.

Opening Question

You are filling a storage room with boxes. Each box is a perfect cube, and each side of the cube measures 4 feet. How much space, in cubic feet, does each box take up? You could write the calculation as 4 × 4 × 4. Or you could write it as 4³. Both expressions say the same thing, but the second one is more compact, easier to read, and easier to work with in formulas. This is why exponents exist: to shorten repeated multiplication into a cleaner form. By the end of this chapter, you will write, interpret, simplify, and apply expressions like 4³, (−2)⁵, x³ · x⁴, and √81 with confidence and understanding.

Section 7.1

Why Exponents Exist

Suppose you need to calculate the area of a square room with 9-foot sides. You multiply length times width: 9 × 9 = 81 square feet. Now suppose you need the volume of a 5-centimeter cube: 5 × 5 × 5 = 125 cubic centimeters. And now suppose someone asks you to evaluate a compound interest formula, or a formula in chemistry describing how fast a reaction progresses. You might find yourself writing 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2, which is 2 multiplied by itself ten times. Writing this out in full is tedious and easy to miscount. Exponents exist to solve this problem.

Instead of writing the full repeated multiplication, we write the base once and record how many times it is used as a factor with a small raised number called the exponent.

2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 2¹⁰

This is read as "two to the tenth power." The base is 2. The exponent is 10. The expression 2¹⁰ is compact, readable, and easy to use inside larger formulas.

Note on the Word "Power"

The word "power" is used in two ways. Sometimes it refers to the exponent itself ("six to the fourth power"). Sometimes it refers to the entire expression or its result ("evaluate the power 2¹⁰"). Context usually makes this clear.

Section 7.2

Repeated Multiplication

The most important thing to understand about exponents at this stage is exactly what they mean. An exponent does not mean multiplication by the exponent. It means the base is multiplied by itself that many times. This is a common source of confusion, so it deserves a direct statement:

3⁴ ≠ 3 × 4 = 12       3⁴ = 3 × 3 × 3 × 3 = 81
Example 7.2.1
Evaluating a basic exponential expression
  • ProblemEvaluate 5³.
  • ThinkThe base is 5 and the exponent is 3. This means 5 is used as a factor three times.
  • Solve5³ = 5 × 5 × 5 5 × 5 = 25 25 × 5 = 125
  • Check5 × 5 = 25, and 25 × 5 = 125. Confirmed.
Answer: 5³ = 125
Example 7.2.2
Evaluating a power of ten
  • ProblemEvaluate 10⁴.
  • Solve10⁴ = 10 × 10 × 10 × 10 = 10,000
  • Check10¹ = 10, 10² = 100, 10³ = 1,000, 10⁴ = 10,000. Each multiplication by 10 adds one zero. Confirmed.
Answer: 10⁴ = 10,000
Powers of Ten Pattern

The exponent on a power of ten tells you exactly how many zeros follow the 1 in the result. 10⁶ = 1,000,000 has six zeros. This pattern is the foundation for scientific notation, which you will study in Chapter 8.

Two Special Cases

Exponent of one: Any base raised to the first power equals the base itself. 7¹ = 7    15¹ = 15    100¹ = 100

Exponent of zero: Any nonzero base raised to the zero power equals 1. 5⁰ = 1    12⁰ = 1    100⁰ = 1

The reason for the zero-power rule will become clear in Section 7.8 when we study the quotient rule. You will see why it must be true, not just memorized.

Section 7.3

Squares and Cubes

The most common exponential expressions involve exponents of 2 and 3. When a number is raised to the second power, it is called a square. The word comes from geometry: if you build a square with sides of length n, the area of that square is n². A square with side length 4 has an area of 4² = 16 square units. Squaring answers the question: what is the area of a square with this side length?

When a number is raised to the third power, it is called a cube. The geometric connection is equally real: if you build a cube with sides of length n, the volume of that cube is n³.

Section 7.4

Exponents with Whole Number Bases

While squares and cubes are most common in everyday applications, students should be comfortable evaluating expressions with larger exponents. The process is always the same: expand, then multiply step by step. Fractions and decimals can also serve as bases, following the same rule.

Example 7.4.1
Evaluating a larger exponent
  • ProblemEvaluate 2⁷.
  • Solve2⁷ = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 4 → 8 → 16 → 32 → 64 → 128
  • Check2¹=2, 2²=4, 2³=8, 2⁴=16, 2⁵=32, 2⁶=64, 2⁷=128. Seven total factors. Confirmed.
Answer: 2⁷ = 128
Example 7.4.2
A fraction as a base
  • ProblemEvaluate (2/3)³.
  • ThinkMultiply the fraction by itself three times. Both numerator and denominator are raised to the power.
  • Solve(2/3)³ = (2 × 2 × 2) / (3 × 3 × 3) = 8/27
Answer: (2/3)³ = 8/27
Fractions Raised to a Power

When a fraction is raised to a power, both the numerator and the denominator are raised to that power. This follows directly from the definition: multiplying fractions means multiplying numerators together and denominators together.

Section 7.5

Exponents with Negative Bases

When a negative number is used as a base, careful attention to parentheses becomes essential. There is an important difference between two expressions that look similar but mean completely different things. Compare (−3)² and −3². In the first, the base is −3. The negative sign is inside the parentheses and is part of the base. In the second, the base is 3, and the negative sign is applied after the exponent is evaluated.

(−3)² = (−3) × (−3) = +9      −3² = −(3 × 3) = −9

The sign of the result when a negative base is raised to a power follows a consistent pattern tied to the rules for multiplication you studied in Chapter 1: a negative base raised to an even exponent always produces a positive result, and a negative base raised to an odd exponent always produces a negative result.

ExpressionExpandedResultPattern

Example 7.5.1
Evaluating a negative base with an even exponent
  • ProblemEvaluate (−5)⁴.
  • ThinkThe base is −5 and the exponent is 4. Even exponent → expect a positive result.
  • Solve(−5)⁴ = (−5)(−5)(−5)(−5) (−5)(−5) = 25 25 × (−5) = −125 (−125)(−5) = 625
  • CheckEven exponent on a negative base → positive result. 625 > 0. Confirmed.
Answer: (−5)⁴ = 625
Example 7.5.2
Distinguishing the base from the sign
  • ProblemEvaluate −4².
  • ThinkThe exponent applies only to 4, not to −4. The negative sign is applied after the exponent is evaluated.
  • Solve−4² = −(4²) = −(4 × 4) = −(16) = −16
  • CheckThis is NOT (−4)² = 16. The absence of parentheses changes the base entirely.
Answer: −4² = −16
Warning — Parentheses Determine the Base

This is one of the most common errors in all of pre-algebra. Always check whether the negative sign is inside parentheses (part of the base) or outside parentheses (applied after). When in doubt, add parentheses to clarify your intended base before evaluating.

Section 7.6

Product Rule for Exponents

To discover the product rule, start with a specific example. Consider 2³ × 2⁴. Expand each factor: 2³ gives three factors of 2, and 2⁴ gives four factors of 2. Together they make seven factors of 2, which is 2⁷. The rule: when multiplying two exponential expressions with the same base, keep the base and add the exponents.

Product Rule

b^m · b^n = b^(m+n)

This rule does not require memorization if you understand why it works. You are simply counting factors. If one group has m factors of b and another has n factors, together they have m + n factors of b.

Example 7.6.1
Applying the product rule
  • ProblemSimplify 5³ × 5⁶.
  • ThinkBoth bases are 5. Apply the product rule: add the exponents.
  • Solve5³ × 5⁶ = 5^(3+6) = 5⁹
  • Check3 factors + 6 factors = 9 total factors of 5. Matches 5⁹. Confirmed.
Answer: 5³ × 5⁶ = 5⁹
Example 7.6.2
Product rule with a variable base
  • ProblemSimplify x² · x⁵.
  • Solvex² · x⁵ = x^(2+5) = x⁷
Answer: x² · x⁵ = x⁷
Warning — Same Base Required

The product rule only applies when the bases are identical. You cannot apply it to 3⁴ × 5⁴, because the bases (3 and 5) are different. With different bases, evaluate each power separately, then multiply the results.

Section 7.7

Power Rule

The power rule describes what happens when an exponential expression is itself raised to a power. Consider (2³)⁴. The outer exponent tells you to use 2³ as a factor four times: 2³ × 2³ × 2³ × 2³. By the product rule, this gives 2^(3+3+3+3) = 2¹². You took the exponent 3 and multiplied it by 4 to get 12. This is the power rule.

Power Rule

(b^m)^n = b^(m·n)

When a power is raised to another power, keep the base and multiply the exponents. Also: (ab)^n = a^n · b^n — when a product is raised to a power, each factor receives the exponent.

Example 7.7.1
Applying the power rule
  • ProblemSimplify (3⁴)².
  • Solve(3⁴)² = 3^(4×2) = 3⁸
  • Check(3⁴)² = 3⁴ × 3⁴ = 3^(4+4) = 3⁸. Product rule confirms the same result. Confirmed.
Answer: (3⁴)² = 3⁸
Example 7.7.2
Power rule with a variable
  • ProblemSimplify (x⁵)³.
  • Solve(x⁵)³ = x^(5×3) = x¹⁵
Answer: (x⁵)³ = x¹⁵
Example 7.7.3
Power of a product
  • ProblemSimplify (2x)³.
  • ThinkThe entire product 2x is raised to the third power. Apply the exponent to each factor separately.
  • Solve(2x)³ = 2³ · x³ = 8x³
Answer: (2x)³ = 8x³
Warning — Add vs. Multiply Exponents

The product rule (multiplying same-base expressions) uses addition: x² · x⁵ = x⁷. The power rule (raising a power to a power) uses multiplication: (x²)⁵ = x¹⁰. These two rules are commonly confused. Check which situation you are in before applying a rule.

Section 7.8

Quotient Rule

The quotient rule handles division of same-base expressions. Consider 2⁷ / 2³. Expand: three factors of 2 in the denominator cancel three factors in the numerator, leaving four factors of 2. So 2⁷ / 2³ = 2^(7−3) = 2⁴. The pattern: subtract the denominator's exponent from the numerator's exponent.

Quotient Rule

b^m / b^n = b^(m−n) when b ≠ 0 and m > n

This rule also proves why b⁰ = 1. When numerator and denominator have the same exponent: 5⁴ / 5⁴ = 5^(4−4) = 5⁰, but any nonzero number divided by itself equals 1. Therefore 5⁰ = 1. This is not an arbitrary rule — it follows directly from the quotient rule and the definition of division.

Example 7.8.1
Applying the quotient rule
  • ProblemSimplify 6⁸ / 6⁵.
  • ThinkSame base of 6. Subtract the exponents.
  • Solve6⁸ / 6⁵ = 6^(8−5) = 6³
  • CheckExpanding and canceling five pairs of 6 leaves three factors in the numerator. Matches 6³. Confirmed.
Answer: 6⁸ / 6⁵ = 6³

Section 7.9

Common Exponent Mistakes

This section collects the most frequent errors students make when working with exponents. Reading this section carefully before independent practice will help you avoid unnecessary mistakes.

Mistake 1 — Treating the Exponent as a Multiplier
3⁴ = 3 × 4 = 12

Correct: 3⁴ = 3 × 3 × 3 × 3 = 81. The exponent tells how many times the base is used as a factor, not what to multiply the base by.

Mistake 2 — Ignoring Parentheses with Negative Bases
(−4)² = −16

Correct: (−4)² = (−4)(−4) = +16. With parentheses, −4 is the full base. Without parentheses, −4² = −(4²) = −16. Placement of parentheses determines everything.

Mistake 3 — Adding Instead of Multiplying Exponents in the Power Rule
(x³)⁴ = x^(3+4) = x⁷

Correct: (x³)⁴ = x^(3×4) = x¹². The power rule requires multiplication of exponents. Save addition for the product rule, which applies to multiplying two separate expressions.

Mistake 4 — Applying the Product Rule to Different Bases
2³ × 3⁴ = 6⁷

Correct: 2³ × 3⁴ = 8 × 81 = 648. The product rule only works when the bases are identical. With different bases, evaluate each expression separately and then multiply.

Mistake 5 — Assuming Larger Exponents Always Mean Larger Numbers
(1/2)⁵ > (1/2)²

Correct: (1/2)² = 1/4 and (1/2)⁵ = 1/32. When the base is a fraction between 0 and 1, larger exponents produce smaller values. The number gets smaller with each multiplication.

Mistake 6 — Forgetting That b¹ = b and b⁰ = 1
"7¹ is undefined" or "8⁰ = 0"

Correct: 7¹ = 7 (any base to the first power equals itself). 8⁰ = 1 (any nonzero base to the zero power equals 1). The zero exponent is not zero — it is one.

Section 7.10

Square Roots and Radicals

An exponent asks: what do you get when you multiply this base by itself a certain number of times? A square root asks the inverse question: what number, multiplied by itself, gives this result?

The square root of 25 asks: what number multiplied by itself equals 25? The answer is 5, because 5 × 5 = 25. The symbol √ is called the radical symbol. The number under the symbol is called the radicand. The entire expression is called a radical expression.

√25 = 5     because     5 × 5 = 25
Key Rule — Product Property of Square Roots

√(a · b) = √a · √b

The square root of a product equals the product of the square roots. This rule is used both to simplify square roots and to multiply radical expressions.

Example 7.10.1
Evaluating a square root of a perfect square
  • ProblemEvaluate √64.
  • ThinkWhat number multiplied by itself equals 64?
  • Solve8 × 8 = 64 so √64 = 8
  • Check8² = 64. Confirmed.
Answer: √64 = 8
Example 7.10.2
Simplifying a square root that is not a perfect square
  • ProblemSimplify √50.
  • Think50 is not a perfect square. But 50 = 25 × 2, and 25 is a perfect square.
  • Solve√50 = √(25 × 2) = √25 · √2 = 5√2
  • Check(5√2)² = 25 × 2 = 50. Confirmed.
Answer: √50 = 5√2
Example 7.10.3
Simplifying with the largest perfect square factor
  • ProblemSimplify √72.
  • ThinkFind the largest perfect square factor of 72. 72 = 36 × 2, and 36 is a perfect square.
  • Solve√72 = √(36 × 2) = √36 · √2 = 6√2
  • Check(6√2)² = 36 × 2 = 72. Confirmed.
Answer: √72 = 6√2
Tip — Find the Largest Perfect Square Factor First

Always look for the largest perfect square factor, not just any perfect square factor. If you factor out a smaller perfect square, you may need to simplify again. Finding the largest factor first saves steps. For √72, using 4 instead of 36 gives √(4 × 18) = 2√18, but then √18 = √(9 × 2) = 3√2, giving 2 · 3√2 = 6√2 anyway — the same answer, but more work.

Section 7.11

Adding and Subtracting Radicals

Radicals can be added or subtracted only when they are like radicals, meaning they share the same radicand. This parallels the rule for like terms in algebra: you can add 3x + 5x = 8x because the variable parts are the same. In the same way, you can add 3√2 + 5√2 = 8√2 because the radical parts are the same. To add or subtract like radicals, add or subtract the coefficients and keep the radical part unchanged.

a√n + b√n = (a + b)√n
Example 7.11.1
Adding like radicals
  • ProblemSimplify 4√3 + 7√3.
  • ThinkBoth radicals have the same radicand (3). Add the coefficients.
  • Solve4√3 + 7√3 = (4 + 7)√3 = 11√3
Answer: 4√3 + 7√3 = 11√3
Example 7.11.2
Simplifying before adding
  • ProblemSimplify √12 + √27.
  • ThinkThese are unlike radicals as written, but both can be simplified to reveal a common radicand.
  • Solve√12 = √(4 × 3) = 2√3 √27 = √(9 × 3) = 3√3 2√3 + 3√3 = 5√3
  • Check(2√3)² = 4 × 3 = 12. (3√3)² = 9 × 3 = 27. Simplifications confirmed.
Answer: √12 + √27 = 5√3

Section 7.12

Multiplying Radicals

To multiply two square roots, multiply the radicands and then simplify if possible. The rule follows from the product property introduced in Section 7.10.

√a · √b = √(a · b)
Example 7.12.1
Multiplying a radical by itself
  • ProblemSimplify √6 · √6.
  • Solve√6 · √6 = √(6 × 6) = √36 = 6
  • CheckA square root times itself should equal the radicand. √6 · √6 = 6. This previews the inverse relationship: squaring and square-rooting undo each other. Confirmed.
Answer: √6 · √6 = 6
Example 7.12.2
Multiplying different radicals
  • ProblemSimplify √3 · √12.
  • Solve√3 · √12 = √(3 × 12) = √36 = 6
Answer: √3 · √12 = 6
Example 7.12.3
Multiplying with coefficients
  • ProblemSimplify 3√2 · 4√5.
  • ThinkMultiply the coefficients together and the radicands together separately.
  • Solve3√2 · 4√5 = (3 × 4)(√2 × √5) = 12√10
  • Check10 has no perfect square factors, so √10 is already in simplest form. Answer: 12√10.
Answer: 3√2 · 4√5 = 12√10

Section 7.13

Dividing Radicals

Division of radicals follows a rule parallel to multiplication. To divide two square roots, divide the radicands and then simplify if possible.

√a / √b = √(a/b)     when b ≠ 0
Example 7.13.1
Dividing radicals
  • ProblemSimplify √50 / √2.
  • Solve√50 / √2 = √(50/2) = √25 = 5
  • Check5² = 25, and 25 × 2 = 50. Confirmed.
Answer: √50 / √2 = 5
Example 7.13.2
Dividing with coefficients
  • ProblemSimplify (8√18) / (4√2).
  • ThinkDivide the coefficients and the radicands separately.
  • Solve(8√18) / (4√2) = (8/4) · √(18/2) = 2 · √9 = 2 × 3 = 6
Answer: (8√18) / (4√2) = 6

Section 7.14

Radical Expressions

A radical expression is in simplest radical form when the radicand has no perfect square factor other than 1. For this chapter, the primary requirement is that condition. Now that each individual operation has been established, it is valuable to practice expressions that combine simplification, addition, and multiplication in a single problem.

Example 7.14.1
Simplifying a mixed radical expression
  • ProblemSimplify 2√8 + √18 − √2.
  • ThinkSimplify each radical before combining.
  • Solve√8 = √(4×2) = 2√2 → 2√8 = 4√2 √18 = √(9×2) = 3√2 4√2 + 3√2 − √2 = (4 + 3 − 1)√2 = 6√2
Answer: 2√8 + √18 − √2 = 6√2
Example 7.14.2
A radical expression in a geometry context
  • ProblemTwo fence sections measure √45 feet and √20 feet. Find the total length.
  • Solve√45 = √(9×5) = 3√5 √20 = √(4×5) = 2√5 3√5 + 2√5 = 5√5 feet

Section 7.15

Exponents and Radicals as Inverse Ideas

Throughout this chapter, exponents and radicals have appeared as related but distinct operations. Now it is time to state their relationship clearly. Squaring and taking a square root are inverse operations. They undo each other. This is the same kind of inverse relationship that exists between addition and subtraction, or between multiplication and division.

√(5²) = √25 = 5       (√36)² = 6² = 36

More generally, for positive values: √(b²) = b and (√b)² = b. This inverse relationship becomes essential in Algebra I when solving equations. If an equation contains x², you isolate x by taking the square root of both sides. If an equation contains √x, you eliminate the radical by squaring both sides.

OperationInverse Operation
AdditionSubtraction
MultiplicationDivision
Squaring (x²)Square root (√x)
Cubing (x³)Cube root (∛x)
Raising to the nth powerTaking the nth root
Example 7.15.1
Using the inverse relationship
  • ProblemWhat value of x satisfies x² = 121?
  • ThinkSquaring and square rooting are inverses. Take the square root of both sides.
  • Solvex = √121 = 11
  • Check11² = 121. Also, (−11)² = 121. Both values satisfy the equation.
Answer: x = 11 or x = −11. (In Algebra I, this is written x = ±11.)
The Habit That Carries Forward

The most important habit to carry forward from this chapter is this: do not memorize the rules without understanding them. The product rule, power rule, and quotient rule each come directly from the definition of an exponent as repeated multiplication. When you forget a rule, you can always rebuild it from scratch by expanding and counting factors. The ability to reason from definition rather than relying on memory alone is one of the most valuable mathematical skills you will develop.

Chapter 7 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Exponents give a compact notation for repeated multiplication. Every exponential expression bⁿ has a base b and an exponent n. The exponent counts how many times the base is used as a factor.
  • Sec 7.2To evaluate an exponential expression, expand into repeated multiplication and compute step by step. Any base to the first power equals itself. Any nonzero base to the zero power equals 1. Powers of ten follow the pattern: the exponent equals the number of zeros in the result.
  • Sec 7.3Squaring and cubing have direct geometric meanings: side² = area of a square; side³ = volume of a cube. Memorizing perfect squares through 15² and perfect cubes through 10³ accelerates all later work with radicals and geometry.
  • Sec 7.4Any number can be a base, including fractions and decimals. When a fraction is raised to a power, both numerator and denominator are raised to that power.
  • Sec 7.5Parentheses determine whether a negative sign is part of the base. (−4)² = 16 but −4² = −16. A negative base raised to an even exponent produces a positive result; an odd exponent produces a negative result.
  • Sec 7.6Product Rule: bᵐ · bⁿ = b^(m+n). When multiplying same-base expressions, add the exponents. The rule only applies when the bases are identical.
  • Sec 7.7Power Rule: (bᵐ)ⁿ = b^(m·n). When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents. Also: (ab)ⁿ = aⁿ · bⁿ. Do not confuse this multiplication with the addition used in the product rule.
  • Sec 7.8Quotient Rule: bᵐ / bⁿ = b^(m−n). When dividing same-base expressions, subtract the exponents. This rule also proves why b⁰ = 1 for any nonzero base.
  • Sec 7.9Six common mistakes: treating the exponent as a multiplier; ignoring parentheses with negative bases; adding instead of multiplying exponents in the power rule; applying the product rule to different bases; assuming larger exponents always give larger numbers; forgetting b¹ = b and b⁰ = 1.
  • Sec 7.10A square root reverses squaring: √b asks what number times itself equals b. Square roots of perfect squares are whole numbers. Non-perfect-square roots are simplified by factoring out the largest perfect square using the rule √(a·b) = √a · √b.
  • Sec 7.11Like radicals (same radicand) can be added or subtracted by combining coefficients. Unlike radicals must be simplified first — they may then reveal a common radicand that allows combining.
  • Sec 7.12To multiply radicals, multiply the radicands and simplify: √a · √b = √(ab). Multiply coefficients separately. A radical times itself equals the radicand.
  • Sec 7.13To divide radicals, divide the radicands and simplify: √a / √b = √(a/b). Divide coefficients separately.
  • Sec 7.14A radical is in simplest form when its radicand has no perfect square factor other than 1. Complex expressions combining multiple radical operations are simplified by working step by step: simplify each radical, then combine like radicals.
  • Sec 7.15Squaring and square rooting are inverse operations: √(b²) = b and (√b)² = b. This inverse relationship is the foundation for solving equations with squared variables in Algebra I. The same pattern extends to cubing and cube roots.

Key Terms Review

base
The number being multiplied repeatedly in an exponential expression. In 5³, the base is 5.
exponent
The small raised number that tells how many times the base is used as a factor. In 5³, the exponent is 3.
exponential expression
A mathematical expression written in the form bⁿ, where b is the base and n is the exponent.
power
Another word for an exponential expression or its result. "Four to the third power" means 4³.
squared
Raised to the second power. 5² means 5 squared. Geometrically, the area of a square with that side length.
cubed
Raised to the third power. 3³ means 3 cubed. Geometrically, the volume of a cube with that side length.
product rule
bᵐ · bⁿ = b^(m+n). When multiplying same-base exponential expressions, add the exponents.
power rule
(bᵐ)ⁿ = b^(m·n). When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents.
quotient rule
bᵐ / bⁿ = b^(m−n). When dividing same-base expressions, subtract the exponents.
radical
A mathematical expression involving a root, written with the radical symbol √.
radicand
The number or expression under the radical symbol. In √50, the radicand is 50.
square root
A number that, when multiplied by itself, produces the radicand. √25 = 5 because 5 × 5 = 25.
perfect square
A whole number whose square root is also a whole number: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, …
like radicals
Radical expressions with the same radicand, which can be combined by adding or subtracting their coefficients.
simplest radical form
A radical expression in which the radicand has no perfect square factor other than 1.
inverse operations
Pairs of operations that undo each other. Squaring and taking a square root are inverse operations.

Chapter 8: Scientific Notation

In Chapter 8, you will use positive and negative powers of 10 to write very large and very small numbers in scientific notation. Scientific notation is the standard language of scale used across astronomy, chemistry, physics, engineering, medicine, and computing.

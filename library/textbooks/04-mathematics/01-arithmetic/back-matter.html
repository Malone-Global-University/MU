Appendix C

Divisibility Rules

A divisibility rule is a shortcut for deciding whether one number divides evenly into another, without performing full division. These rules are useful for factoring, reducing fractions, finding GCF and LCM, and simplifying calculations.

C.1 The Rules

DivisorRuleExample
2The number ends in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8 (it is even).348 is divisible by 2 because it ends in 8.
3The sum of the digits is divisible by 3.4 + 2 + 3 = 9, and 9 ÷ 3 = 3, so 423 is divisible by 3.
4The last two digits form a number divisible by 4.732 → 32 ÷ 4 = 8, so 732 is divisible by 4.
5The number ends in 0 or 5.285 ends in 5, so it is divisible by 5.
6The number is divisible by both 2 and 3.312: even, and 3 + 1 + 2 = 6 (divisible by 3), so divisible by 6.
8The last three digits form a number divisible by 8.1,024 → 024 = 24, and 24 ÷ 8 = 3, so 1,024 is divisible by 8.
9The sum of the digits is divisible by 9.8 + 1 + 9 = 18, and 18 ÷ 9 = 2, so 819 is divisible by 9.
10The number ends in 0.4,730 ends in 0, so it is divisible by 10.

C.2 Divisibility by 7

There is no simple one-step rule for 7. The most reliable approach at this level is to divide and check. For those interested, one method is to double the last digit, subtract it from the rest of the number, and check whether the result is divisible by 7. This process can be repeated on smaller numbers. For most pre-algebra work, however, direct division or prime factorization is the more practical choice.

C.3 Using Divisibility Rules Together

Divisibility rules work together. To test whether a number is divisible by 6, you test for 2 and for 3 separately. Both must be true.

Example — Is 5,742 divisible by 6?

Test for 2: 5,742 ends in 2, so yes, it is even.

Test for 3: 5 + 7 + 4 + 2 = 18, and 18 ÷ 3 = 6, so yes.

Since 5,742 is divisible by both 2 and 3, it is divisible by 6.

C.4Divisibility Practice

  2. Is 7,308 divisible by 2? By 4? By 6?
  3. Is 960 divisible by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, and 10?
  4. Find the smallest three-digit number divisible by both 4 and 9.
  5. A number is divisible by 9 but not by 6. Give an example.

Appendix G

Geometry Formula Reference

These formulas were introduced in Chapter 14. This appendix collects them for quick reference. Students are encouraged to understand the meaning of each formula, not only how to compute with it.

G.1 Perimeter Formulas

Perimeter is the total distance around the outside of a figure. It is measured in linear units such as centimeters, inches, feet, or meters.

FigureFormula
SquareP = 4s
RectangleP = 2l + 2w
TriangleP = a + b + c
Regular polygon (n sides)P = n × s
Circle (circumference)C = 2πr or C = πd

G.2 Area Formulas

Area measures the space inside a two-dimensional figure. It is expressed in square units such as cm², in², or ft².

FigureFormula
SquareA = s²
RectangleA = l × w
ParallelogramA = b × h
TriangleA = (1/2) × b × h
TrapezoidA = (1/2)(b₁ + b₂) × h
CircleA = πr²

G.3 Volume Formulas

Volume measures the space inside a three-dimensional figure. It is expressed in cubic units such as cm³ or ft³.

FigureFormula
Rectangular prism (box)V = l × w × h
CubeV = s³
CylinderV = πr²h
Triangular prismV = (1/2) × b × h × l
Notes on Geometry Formulas

The variable r always refers to the radius, the distance from the center of a circle to its edge. The variable d refers to the diameter: d = 2r. The variable h always refers to the height, measured perpendicular to the base. Area is always in square units and volume is always in cubic units.

Appendix H

Calculator Skills

A calculator is a tool, not a substitute for understanding. This appendix introduces the most important calculator skills for students using a standard four-function or scientific calculator. Before relying on a calculator, always estimate the answer mentally so that you can catch input errors.

H.1 Basic Entry Skills

Entering negative numbers: On most calculators, a button labeled (+/−) or (−) changes the sign of a number. This is different from the subtraction key. To enter −7, type 7, then press (+/−).

Entering fractions as decimals: Most basic calculators do not handle fractions directly. Convert the fraction to a decimal first by dividing the numerator by the denominator.

Order of operations: Standard four-function calculators do not follow order of operations automatically. They compute from left to right. A scientific calculator with algebraic logic will follow order of operations correctly. Always know which type of calculator you are using.

H.2 Using Parentheses

Example — (3 + 4) × 5

Press: ( 3 + 4 ) × 5 =  →  Result: 35

Without parentheses on a basic left-to-right calculator, compute the grouped part first, write down the result, and then continue.

H.3 Exponents on a Calculator

On most scientific calculators, the exponent key is labeled x^y, ^, or y^x. To compute 3⁴: press 3, then the exponent key, then 4, then =. Result: 81.

H.4 Square Roots on a Calculator

The square root key is labeled √. To compute √144: press √, then 144, then = (on some calculators, press the number first). Result: 12.

H.5 Scientific Notation on a Calculator

The key for entering scientific notation is usually labeled EE, EXP, or E. It means "times ten to the power of." To enter 3.2 × 10⁶: press 3, period, 2, then EE or EXP, then 6. The display may show 3.2E6 or 3.2 × 10⁶, depending on the calculator.

Do Not

Do not press the multiplication key or the 1 and 0 separately when entering scientific notation. Using the EE key is the correct method.

H.6 Checking Reasonableness

Before accepting a calculator result, always ask: Is the answer in the right ballpark? Did I enter the numbers in the correct order? Did I handle negative signs correctly? Did I use parentheses where the problem required grouping? A calculator will give you an exact answer to whatever expression you enter. It will not tell you whether you entered the right expression.

Appendix I

Word Problem Strategy Guide

Word problems are the place where arithmetic connects to real life. Many students find them difficult, not because they lack mathematical knowledge, but because the translation from words to mathematics is a separate skill that must be developed.

I.1
The Problem-Solving Method
Read · Represent · Solve · Check · Explain

Throughout this textbook, the following five-step method is used. This is not a rigid formula. It is a mental habit. Over time, students who practice this method internalize it and move through these steps naturally.

The Five Steps

Read — Understand the problem fully before writing anything.

Represent — Draw a diagram, define a variable, or set up a table.

Solve — Carry out the mathematics, showing each step.

Check — Verify the answer using estimation or substitution.

Explain — Write a complete sentence that answers the question in context.

I.2 Signal Words by Operation

Signal Word or PhraseLikely Operation
sum, total, in all, combined, more thanAddition
difference, less than, decreased by, how many moreSubtraction
product, times, of (with fractions), multiplied byMultiplication
quotient, divided by, per, ratio, split equallyDivision
is, equals, the result isEquals
at least, no more than, minimum, maximumInequality
Use as Guides, Not Rules

The problem context always takes priority over signal words alone. The word "more" sometimes signals addition and sometimes signals a comparison requiring subtraction. Read carefully.

I.3 Common Word Problem Mistakes

Answering the wrong question. Re-read the question after solving. Make sure your answer addresses exactly what was asked.

Ignoring units. Track units throughout the problem. Miles per hour times hours gives miles, not miles per hour.

Forgetting to define the variable. Writing an equation with x before saying what x represents leads to confusion in multi-step problems.

Solving only one step of a multi-step problem. Re-read the problem and ask whether the computation you did is sufficient to answer the question, or whether more steps are needed.

Using a signal word blindly. Context matters. Do not substitute an operation for careful reading.

Back Matter

Glossary

This glossary defines key mathematical terms used throughout the textbook. Terms are arranged alphabetically. Each definition is followed by the chapter where the term is first introduced.

Absolute valueChapter 1
The distance of a number from zero on the number line. Absolute value is always non-negative. Written as |a|.
Algebraic expressionChapter 9
A mathematical phrase that contains one or more variables, combined with numbers and operations. An expression does not contain an equals sign.
BaseChapters 7, 14
In an exponential expression such as x^n, the base is x, the number being multiplied repeatedly. Also the bottom side of a geometric figure used to compute area or volume.
CoefficientChapter 9
The numerical factor multiplied by a variable in a term. In 5x, the coefficient is 5.
Composite numberChapter 2
A whole number greater than 1 that has more than two factors. The number 12 is composite because its factors include 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12.
ConstantChapter 9
A value that does not change. In an expression, a constant is a fixed number such as 7 or −3.
Coordinate planeChapter 12
A flat surface defined by two perpendicular number lines called the x-axis and the y-axis, used to locate and graph points and relationships.
Cross-multiplicationChapter 6
A method for solving or verifying proportions by multiplying each numerator by the opposite denominator. If a/b = c/d, then a × d = b × c.
DecimalChapter 5
A number expressed in the base-ten system using a decimal point to separate the whole number part from the fractional part.
DenominatorChapter 3
The bottom number of a fraction, indicating the number of equal parts into which a whole is divided.
DiameterChapter 14
The distance across a circle through its center. The diameter equals twice the radius: d = 2r.
DivisibilityChapter 2
A number is divisible by another if the division results in a whole number with no remainder.
EquationChapter 10
A mathematical statement that two expressions are equal, shown with an equals sign.
Equivalent fractionsChapter 3
Fractions that represent the same value but are written with different numerators and denominators. 1/2 = 3/6.
EvaluateChapter 9
To substitute a given value for a variable and compute the resulting number.
ExponentChapter 7
A number written as a superscript that tells how many times the base is used as a factor. In 2⁵, the exponent is 5.
FactorChapter 2
Any number that divides evenly into another number. Also, to express a number as a product of its factors.
Greatest Common Factor (GCF)Chapter 2
The largest factor that two or more numbers share. The GCF of 12 and 18 is 6.
Identity numberChapter 1
A number that does not change another number when combined by a specific operation. The additive identity is 0. The multiplicative identity is 1.
Improper fractionChapter 4
A fraction in which the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator. 7/3 is an improper fraction.
InequalityChapter 10
A mathematical statement that compares two expressions using symbols such as <, >, ≤, or ≥.
IntegerChapter 1
The set of whole numbers and their opposites: {…, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, …}.
Irrational numberChapter 7
A number that cannot be written as a simple fraction. Its decimal expansion continues without repeating. √2 and π are irrational numbers.
Least Common Multiple (LCM)Chapter 2
The smallest multiple that two or more numbers share. The LCM of 4 and 6 is 12.
Like termsChapter 9
Terms in an algebraic expression that have the same variable raised to the same power. 3x and 7x are like terms; 3x and 3x² are not.
Linear patternChapter 12
A pattern in which values change by a constant amount each step, producing a straight-line graph when plotted on a coordinate plane.
Mixed numberChapter 4
A number written as a whole number combined with a proper fraction, such as 3 1/4.
MultipleChapter 2
A number obtained by multiplying a given number by a whole number. The multiples of 4 are 4, 8, 12, 16, and so on.
Negative numberChapter 1
A number less than zero, located to the left of zero on the number line.
Number lineChapter 1
A line on which numbers are placed at equal intervals to represent their values and positions relative to one another.
NumeratorChapter 3
The top number of a fraction, indicating how many parts of the whole are being considered.
OppositeChapter 1
The number that is the same distance from zero on the number line but in the opposite direction. The opposite of 5 is −5.
Order of operationsChapter 11
A set of rules for the order in which operations must be performed: grouping symbols first, then exponents, then multiplication and division left to right, then addition and subtraction left to right.
Ordered pairChapter 12
A pair of numbers written in the form (x, y) that identifies a point on the coordinate plane.
PercentChapter 6
A ratio that compares a number to 100. The symbol for percent is %. Fifty percent means 50 out of 100, or 50/100 = 0.5.
PerimeterChapter 14
The total distance around the outside boundary of a two-dimensional figure.
Prime factorizationChapter 2
Writing a composite number as a product of prime numbers only. The prime factorization of 12 is 2² × 3.
Prime numberChapter 2
A whole number greater than 1 that has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. The numbers 2, 3, 5, 7, and 11 are prime.
ProportionChapter 6
A statement that two ratios are equal. 3/4 = 9/12 is a proportion.
QuadrantChapter 12
One of the four regions of the coordinate plane separated by the x-axis and y-axis. They are numbered I, II, III, and IV counterclockwise from the upper right.
RadicalChapter 7
A symbol (√) used to denote a root, most commonly the square root.
RadicandChapter 7
The number or expression under a radical sign.
RatioChapter 6
A comparison of two quantities using division. A ratio can be written as a to b, a:b, or a/b.
RateChapter 6
A ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units. Miles per hour and cost per pound are rates.
ReciprocalChapter 3
The multiplicative inverse of a number. The reciprocal of 3/4 is 4/3. A number multiplied by its reciprocal equals 1.
Reducing a fractionChapter 3
Dividing both the numerator and denominator by their GCF to write the fraction in lowest terms.
Repeating decimalChapter 5
A decimal in which one or more digits repeat endlessly. 0.3̄ = 0.333… is a repeating decimal.
Scientific notationChapter 8
A way of writing very large or very small numbers as a product of a number between 1 and 10, and a power of 10.
Simple interestChapter 6
Interest computed only on the original principal, calculated using the formula I = Prt.
SolutionChapter 10
A value for the variable that makes an equation or inequality true.
Square rootChapter 7
A number that, when multiplied by itself, produces the given number. √25 = 5 because 5 × 5 = 25.
TermChapter 9
A single number, variable, or product of numbers and variables in an expression. In 3x + 7, the terms are 3x and 7.
Unit rateChapter 6
A rate with a denominator of 1. Miles per hour and price per ounce are unit rates.
VariableChapter 9
A letter or symbol that represents an unknown or changing quantity.
VolumeChapter 14
The measure of the space inside a three-dimensional figure, expressed in cubic units.
Whole numberChapter 1
The numbers 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, and so on. Whole numbers do not include negative numbers or fractions.

Chapter 10 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces two of the most important ideas in mathematics: equations and inequalities. An equation is a statement that two expressions are equal. An inequality is a statement that one expression is greater than, less than, or related to another by size. Both ideas grow directly from arithmetic, and both become essential tools in algebra, science, finance, engineering, and everyday problem solving.

Students who understand equations and inequalities at this stage will find Algebra I much more manageable. The symbols, language, and methods introduced here will appear in nearly every mathematics course that follows. The goal of this chapter is not speed. It is understanding: understanding what an equation claims, why inverse operations work, what a solution means, and why the rules for inequalities differ in one critical way from the rules for equations.

Opening Question

A store sells notebooks for $3 each. You spend exactly $18. How many notebooks did you buy? Most students can answer this quickly by mental arithmetic. But what if the price were $3.75, the total were $26.25, and you also had a $2 coupon? Mental arithmetic becomes harder. Equations give you a reliable method for answering questions like this, whether they are simple or complicated. This chapter teaches you that method.

Why This Chapter Matters

Arithmetic is largely about computing with known numbers. Algebra is largely about finding unknown numbers. The equation is the tool that connects those two ideas. Every time you write 3x = 18 and solve for x, you are using arithmetic operations, the concept of balance, and the logic of inverse operations together.

Inequalities extend that same logic to situations where an answer is not a single value but a range of values, which comes up constantly in budgeting, science, and data analysis. This chapter is the last major conceptual bridge between arithmetic and algebra. Use it carefully.

Section 10.1

What an Equation Says

An equation is a complete mathematical sentence. It has two sides separated by an equals sign. The equals sign is not a signal to compute. It is a claim: it says that the expression on the left and the expression on the right name the same value.

Consider 4 + 3 = 7. Both sides equal 7. The equation is true. Now consider 4 + 3 = 10. Both sides do not equal the same value. This equation is false. Equations are statements. They can be true or false. When an equation contains a variable, the question becomes: is there a value for the variable that makes the equation true?

In x + 3 = 7, if x = 4, then 4 + 3 = 7, which is true. If x = 10, then 10 + 3 = 7, which is false. The value x = 4 is the solution to this equation.

Three Parts of Every Equation

Left Side  —  an expression

Equals Sign  —  the claim that both sides name the same value

Right Side  —  an expression

Student Tip

The equals sign means "is the same value as." It does not mean "the answer comes next." An equation makes a claim. Your job is to find the value of the variable that makes the claim true.

Practice 10.1Concept Check

Section 10.2

Solutions and Checking

A solution to an equation is a number that, when substituted for the variable, makes the equation true. Checking a solution by substitution is not optional. It is a mathematical habit that catches errors, confirms your work, and teaches you to trust your reasoning. Even experienced mathematicians check their answers.

The process: substitute the proposed value for the variable, simplify both sides completely, and confirm that both sides produce the same number.

Example 10.2.1
Checking a proposed solution
  • ProblemIs x = 5 a solution to 3x − 4 = 11?
  • ThinkReplace x with 5 and see whether the left side equals 11.
  • Solve3(5) − 4 = 15 − 4 = 11
  • CheckLeft side = 11. Right side = 11. They match. ✓
Answer: Yes. x = 5 is a solution.
Example 10.2.2
Checking a value that is not a solution
  • ProblemIs x = 3 a solution to 4x + 1 = 17?
  • Solve4(3) + 1 = 12 + 1 = 13
  • CheckLeft side = 13. Right side = 17. They do not match.
Answer: No. x = 3 is not a solution. The equation is false when x = 3.
Student Warning

Many students skip checking because they feel confident. This is one of the most common sources of avoidable errors. Always substitute your answer back into the original equation before moving on.

Section 10.3

Balance and Inverse Operations

Think of an equation as a balance scale. The equals sign is the center. Whatever is on the left side must weigh the same as whatever is on the right side. If you add weight to one side without adding the same weight to the other, the scale tips and the equation is no longer balanced.

The rule is: whatever you do to one side of an equation, you must do exactly the same thing to the other side. This rule comes from the properties of equality.

PropertyStatementExample
AdditionIf a = b, then a + c = b + cIf x − 5 = 3, add 5 to both sides
SubtractionIf a = b, then a − c = b − cIf x + 7 = 12, subtract 7 from both sides
MultiplicationIf a = b, then a · c = b · cIf x/4 = 3, multiply both sides by 4
DivisionIf a = b and c ≠ 0, then a/c = b/cIf 6x = 42, divide both sides by 6
Inverse Operation Pairs

Addition  ↔  Subtraction

Multiplication  ↔  Division

To isolate a variable, undo whatever operation is being applied to it by using the inverse operation on both sides of the equation.

Student Tip

Think of solving an equation as peeling layers off the variable. Each layer is an operation applied to the variable. Remove the layers one at a time using inverse operations, working from the outside in.

Practice 10.3Concept Check

Section 10.4

One-Step Equations

A one-step equation requires exactly one inverse operation to isolate the variable. These equations look simple, but they carry the full logic of all future equation solving. Mastering them here makes two-step and multi-step equations easier later.

Type 1 — Addition Equations

Example 10.4.1
One-step addition equation
  • ProblemSolve x + 12 = 20.
  • ThinkThe variable has 12 added to it. Subtract 12 from both sides.
  • Solvex + 12 − 12 = 20 − 12 x = 8
  • Check8 + 12 = 20 ✓
Answer: x = 8

Type 2 — Subtraction Equations

Example 10.4.2
One-step subtraction equation
  • ProblemSolve x − 7 = 3.
  • ThinkThe variable has 7 subtracted. Add 7 to both sides.
  • Solvex − 7 + 7 = 3 + 7 x = 10
  • Check10 − 7 = 3 ✓
Answer: x = 10

Type 3 — Multiplication Equations

Example 10.4.3
One-step multiplication equation
  • ProblemSolve 5x = 35.
  • ThinkThe variable is multiplied by 5. Divide both sides by 5.
  • Solve5x / 5 = 35 / 5 x = 7
  • Check5(7) = 35 ✓
Answer: x = 7

Type 4 — Division Equations

Example 10.4.4
One-step division equation
  • ProblemSolve x / 4 = 9.
  • ThinkThe variable is divided by 4. Multiply both sides by 4.
  • Solve4 · (x / 4) = 9 · 4 x = 36
  • Check36 / 4 = 9 ✓
Answer: x = 36

One-Step Equations with Negative Numbers

Example 10.4.5
One-step equation with a negative result
  • ProblemSolve x + 15 = 6.
  • ThinkSubtract 15 from both sides. The result will be negative because 6 is less than 15.
  • Solvex + 15 − 15 = 6 − 15 x = −9
  • Check−9 + 15 = 6 ✓
Answer: x = −9
Example 10.4.6
One-step equation with a negative coefficient
  • ProblemSolve −3x = 21.
  • ThinkDivide both sides by −3.
  • Solve−3x / −3 = 21 / −3 x = −7
  • Check−3(−7) = 21 ✓
Answer: x = −7
Student Warning

Equations can have negative solutions. A negative result is not a sign of an error. Always check your answer by substituting it back into the original equation to confirm.

Section 10.5

Two-Step Equations

A two-step equation requires two inverse operations to isolate the variable. The most common structure is ax + b = c, where a, b, and c are numbers. To solve, work in two steps: first undo the addition or subtraction, then undo the multiplication or division. Think of it as peeling the outer layer off before the inner one.

Two-Step Order

Step 1: Undo the addition or subtraction (outer layer).

Step 2: Undo the multiplication or division (inner layer).

Example 10.5.1
Two-step equation — addition then multiplication
  • ProblemSolve 2x + 5 = 13.
  • Step 1Subtract 5 from both sides.2x + 5 − 5 = 13 − 5 2x = 8
  • Step 2Divide both sides by 2.2x / 2 = 8 / 2 x = 4
  • Check2(4) + 5 = 8 + 5 = 13 ✓
Answer: x = 4
Example 10.5.2
Two-step equation — subtraction then multiplication
  • ProblemSolve 3x − 7 = 14.
  • Step 1Add 7 to both sides.3x − 7 + 7 = 14 + 7 3x = 21
  • Step 2Divide both sides by 3.3x / 3 = 21 / 3 x = 7
  • Check3(7) − 7 = 21 − 7 = 14 ✓
Answer: x = 7
Example 10.5.3
Two-step equation — negative coefficient
  • ProblemSolve −4x + 3 = 19.
  • Step 1Subtract 3 from both sides.−4x + 3 − 3 = 19 − 3 −4x = 16
  • Step 2Divide both sides by −4.−4x / −4 = 16 / −4 x = −4
  • Check−4(−4) + 3 = 16 + 3 = 19 ✓
Answer: x = −4
Example 10.5.4
Two-step equation — division structure
  • ProblemSolve x/3 + 2 = 8.
  • Step 1Subtract 2 from both sides.x/3 + 2 − 2 = 8 − 2 x/3 = 6
  • Step 2Multiply both sides by 3.3 · (x/3) = 6 · 3 x = 18
  • Check18/3 + 2 = 6 + 2 = 8 ✓
Answer: x = 18

Section 10.6

Equations with Fractions

Equations sometimes contain fractional coefficients or constants. You can solve these the same way you solve any equation, using inverse operations. There is also a useful shortcut: multiply both sides by the least common denominator (LCD) to clear all fractions at once before solving. Both methods are correct. Choose whichever makes the arithmetic cleaner for you.

Example 10.6.1
Equation with a fractional coefficient — multiply by the reciprocal
  • ProblemSolve (3/4)x = 12.
  • ThinkThe coefficient is 3/4. Multiply both sides by its reciprocal, 4/3.
  • Solve(4/3) · (3/4)x = 12 · (4/3) x = 48/3 = 16
  • Check(3/4)(16) = 48/4 = 12 ✓
Answer: x = 16
Example 10.6.2
Clearing fractions by multiplying by the LCD
  • ProblemSolve x/3 + 1/2 = 5/6.
  • ThinkDenominators are 3, 2, and 6. LCD = 6. Multiply every term by 6.
  • Solve6 · (x/3) + 6 · (1/2) = 6 · (5/6) 2x + 3 = 5 2x = 2 x = 1
  • Check1/3 + 1/2 = 2/6 + 3/6 = 5/6 ✓
Answer: x = 1
Example 10.6.3
Two-step equation with a fraction
  • ProblemSolve x/5 − 3 = 1.
  • Step 1Add 3 to both sides.x/5 − 3 + 3 = 1 + 3 x/5 = 4
  • Step 2Multiply both sides by 5.x = 20
  • Check20/5 − 3 = 4 − 3 = 1 ✓
Answer: x = 20
Student Tip

Clearing fractions by multiplying by the LCD often makes the arithmetic much simpler. This is a legal move because you are multiplying both sides by the same nonzero number, which preserves the balance of the equation.

Section 10.7

Equations from Word Problems

Many real problems are word problems that can be solved by translating the situation into an equation. Use this five-step method for every word problem in this chapter:

Five-Step Problem-Solving Method

Read — Understand what the problem asks. Find known values and identify the unknown.

Represent — Define the variable. Write an equation that models the situation.

Solve — Use inverse operations to find the value of the variable.

Check — Substitute the answer back into the original equation to confirm it works.

Explain — Write a complete sentence that answers the question in context.

Example 10.7.1
Basic word problem — addition
  • ReadA number increased by 14 is equal to 31. What is the number?
  • RepresentLet n = the unknown number.   n + 14 = 31
  • Solven = 31 − 14 = 17
  • Check17 + 14 = 31 ✓
  • ExplainThe number is 17.
Answer: The number is 17.
Example 10.7.2
Multiplication word problem
  • ReadA teacher divides students equally into 6 groups. Each group has 5 students. How many students are there in total?
  • RepresentLet s = total students.   s / 6 = 5
  • Solves = 5 × 6 = 30
  • Check30 / 6 = 5 ✓
  • ExplainThere are 30 students in total.
Answer: 30 students.
Example 10.7.3
Two-step word problem — cell phone bill
  • ReadA cell phone plan charges a flat monthly fee of $10 plus $0.05 per text message. This month your bill was $17.50. How many text messages did you send?
  • RepresentLet t = number of text messages.   0.05t + 10 = 17.50
  • Step 10.05t = 17.50 − 10 = 7.50
  • Step 2t = 7.50 / 0.05 = 150
  • Check0.05(150) + 10 = 7.50 + 10 = 17.50 ✓
  • ExplainYou sent 150 text messages this month.
Answer: 150 text messages.
Student Tip

The hardest part of a word problem is writing the equation. Read the problem at least twice. Look for the unknown first, give it a variable name, and then describe the relationship in symbols. If you write the equation correctly, the solving step is usually straightforward.

Section 10.8

What an Inequality Says

An inequality is a mathematical statement that compares two expressions, not by claiming they are equal, but by describing which is larger, which is smaller, or whether they might be equal. Where an equation says x = 5, meaning one exact value, an inequality like x > 3 says that any number larger than 3 is a solution. There is no single answer. There is a range.

Inequalities describe realistic constraints. A speed limit says the legal speed is at most 65 miles per hour. A nutritional guideline says sodium intake should be less than 2,300 milligrams per day. An elevator capacity says the load must not exceed 2,000 pounds. In arithmetic and algebra, inequalities answer a different kind of question than equations. An equation answers "what is the exact value?" An inequality answers "what values are acceptable?"

RelationshipSymbolExampleMeaning
Greater than>x > 5x is strictly more than 5; 5 is not included
Less than<x < 10x is strictly less than 10; 10 is not included
Greater than or equal to≥x ≥ 3x is 3 or more; 3 is included
Less than or equal to≤x ≤ 7x is 7 or less; 7 is included
Student Tip

Think of the inequality symbol as a mouth. The open side always faces the larger value. In 3 < 7, the open side faces 7. In 10 > 4, the open side faces 10.

Section 10.9

Inequality Symbols

Learning to read inequality symbols fluently is essential before solving inequalities. Practice translating both directions, from symbols into words and from words into symbols. Pay special attention to phrases like "at most," "at least," "no more than," and "no less than." These signal ≤ or ≥, not strict inequality.

WordsSymbolInequality
A number is more than 6>x > 6
A number is at most 12≤x ≤ 12
A number is no less than 8≥x ≥ 8
A number is fewer than 20<x < 20
A number is at least 5≥x ≥ 5
A number is no more than 100≤x ≤ 100
Example 10.9.1
Writing an inequality from a real situation
  • ProblemYou must be at least 16 years old to get a driver's permit. Write an inequality for the age requirement.
  • Think"At least 16" means 16 is included. Use ≥.
  • SolveLet a = a person's age. a ≥ 16
Answer: a ≥ 16
Example 10.9.2
Interpreting an inequality in context
  • ProblemThe inequality t < 30 describes the number of minutes a student spent on homework. What does this mean?
  • AnswerThe student spent fewer than 30 minutes on homework. The value 30 is not included in the solution set.
Answer: Fewer than 30 minutes; 30 itself is not included.
Student Warning

"At least" means ≥, not >. "At most" means ≤, not <. Mistranslating these phrases is one of the most common errors students make when writing inequalities from word problems. The boundary value is included whenever the phrase allows equality.

Section 10.10

Solving Inequalities

Solving an inequality is very similar to solving an equation. You use inverse operations to isolate the variable, and the solution is a range of numbers described by the inequality. There is one critical difference: when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, you must reverse the direction of the inequality symbol.

Why? Consider: we know that 2 < 6. If we multiply both sides by −1, we get −2 and −6. On the number line, −2 is to the right of −6, so −2 > −6. The direction flipped. This reversal is not optional; it must happen, or the resulting inequality is incorrect.

The Reversal Rule

When you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, reverse the direction of the inequality symbol.

Adding, subtracting, multiplying by a positive number, or dividing by a positive number: no reversal needed.

Multiplying or dividing by a negative number: reverse the symbol.

Example 10.10.1
One-step inequality — addition
  • ProblemSolve x + 4 > 9.
  • ThinkSubtract 4 from both sides. Positive operation, no reversal.
  • Solvex + 4 − 4 > 9 − 4 x > 5
Answer: x > 5. All numbers greater than 5 are solutions.
Example 10.10.2
One-step inequality — multiplication by a positive number
  • ProblemSolve 3x ≤ 18.
  • ThinkDivide both sides by 3. Positive divisor, no reversal.
  • Solve3x / 3 ≤ 18 / 3 x ≤ 6
Answer: x ≤ 6.
Example 10.10.3
One-step inequality — division by a negative (reversal required)
  • ProblemSolve −5x < 20.
  • ThinkDivide both sides by −5. Negative divisor: reverse the symbol.
  • Solve−5x / −5 > 20 / −5 x > −4
  • CheckTry x = 0 (greater than −4): −5(0) = 0 < 20 ✓ Try x = −10 (not greater than −4): −5(−10) = 50 < 20? No. ✓ (Confirms −10 is not a solution.)
Answer: x > −4.
Example 10.10.4
Two-step inequality
  • ProblemSolve 2x − 3 ≥ 7.
  • Step 1Add 3 to both sides.2x − 3 + 3 ≥ 7 + 3 2x ≥ 10
  • Step 2Divide both sides by 2. Positive divisor, no reversal.2x / 2 ≥ 10 / 2 x ≥ 5
Answer: x ≥ 5.
Student Warning

Forgetting to reverse the inequality symbol when dividing or multiplying by a negative number is one of the most common errors in this entire chapter. The reversal rule applies only when multiplying or dividing by a negative number. Adding or subtracting never causes a reversal.

Section 10.11

Graphing Inequalities

Because inequalities have infinitely many solutions, we represent them visually using a number line. The graph shows every number that makes the inequality true. The process has three steps.

How to Graph an Inequality

Step 1: Draw a number line with the boundary value labeled.

Step 2: Place an open circle at the boundary if the value is not included (> or <), or a closed circle if it is included (≥ or ≤).

Step 3: Shade in the direction of the solutions. Shade right for greater than; shade left for less than.

Example 10.11.1
Graphing x > 3
  • Symbol> means open circle (3 is not included). Shade to the right.
  • Graph
Answer: Open circle at 3. Shade right. Solutions: all numbers greater than 3.
Example 10.11.2
Graphing x ≤ −1
  • Symbol≤ means closed circle (−1 is included). Shade to the left.
  • Graph
Answer: Closed circle at −1. Shade left. Solutions: all numbers less than or equal to −1.
Example 10.11.3
Solving then graphing — 4x − 8 < 12
  • Solve4x − 8 + 8 < 12 + 8 4x < 20 x < 5
  • GraphOpen circle at 5 (not included). Shade left.
Answer: x < 5. Open circle at 5, shaded left.
Example 10.11.4
Solving and graphing with a negative coefficient — −2x + 1 ≥ 7
  • Step 1−2x + 1 − 1 ≥ 7 − 1 −2x ≥ 6
  • Step 2Divide by −2. Reverse the symbol.x ≤ −3
  • GraphClosed circle at −3 (included). Shade left.
Answer: x ≤ −3. Closed circle at −3, shaded left.
Student Tip

After graphing, always test one value from the shaded region in the original inequality to confirm the shading is in the correct direction. This takes only a few seconds and prevents errors.

Section 10.12

Solution Sets

The solution set of an inequality is the collection of all values that make the inequality true. When graphed on a number line, the shaded region represents the solution set. When described in words, it tells you which values are acceptable.

In more advanced mathematics, solution sets are written using set-builder notation or interval notation. At this level you are not required to use these forms, but you should be able to read them when you encounter them in later courses.

SolutionIn WordsSet-Builder (preview)Interval (preview)
x > 3All real numbers greater than 3{ x | x > 3 }(3, ∞)
x ≤ −2All real numbers less than or equal to −2{ x | x ≤ −2 }(−∞, −2]
x ≥ 0All real numbers greater than or equal to 0{ x | x ≥ 0 }[0, ∞)
Example 10.12.1
Describing a solution set
  • ProblemSolve 5x − 10 > 15. Describe the solution set in words.
  • Solve5x > 25 x > 5
  • DescribeAll real numbers greater than 5. Open circle at 5, shaded right.
Answer: x > 5. All real numbers greater than 5.
When Is Every Number a Solution?

Some inequalities are true for all values of the variable. Consider x + 3 > x + 1. Subtract x from both sides: 3 > 1. This is always true. The solution set is all real numbers. Other inequalities are never true: x + 5 < x + 2 simplifies to 5 < 2, which is always false. The solution set is empty.

Section 10.13

Common Equation and Inequality Mistakes

The following mistakes appear frequently in student work. Study them carefully. Being able to recognize and explain an error is a sign of real understanding, not just mechanical ability.

Mistake 1 — Performing the operation on only one side
x + 7 = 15  →  x = 15

The student forgot to subtract 7 from the right side. Both sides must change. Correct answer: x = 8.

Mistake 2 — Using the wrong inverse operation
x + 9 = 20  →  x = 29

The student added 9 to the right side instead of subtracting. The inverse of addition is subtraction. Correct answer: x = 11.

Mistake 3 — Forgetting to reverse the inequality symbol
−3x < 12  →  x < −4

The student divided by −3 but did not reverse the symbol. Dividing by a negative requires reversal. Correct answer: x > −4.

Mistake 4 — Using an open circle for ≥ or ≤
Graphing x ≥ 4 with an open circle at 4

The symbol ≥ includes 4 as a valid solution. A closed circle is required. An open circle incorrectly excludes the boundary value.

Mistake 5 — Shading in the wrong direction
x < 3: student shades to the right

Shading should go left for "less than." Always test one value from the shaded region in the original inequality before accepting the graph.

Mistake 6 — Losing the variable during solving
2x + 4 = 10  →  2 = 6

The student dropped the variable. Keep the variable in every step until isolation is complete. Correct work: 2x = 6, then x = 3.

Mistake 7 — Skipping the check step
4x − 5 = 7  →  x = 2  (unchecked)

Check: 4(2) − 5 = 3 ≠ 7. The answer is wrong. Correct: x = 3. Skipping the check allowed the arithmetic error to go unnoticed.

Section 10.14

Preparing for Algebra I Equations

This chapter has introduced the foundational ideas that make Algebra I possible. Before leaving, connect what you have learned to what comes next.

What this chapter gave you: a clear understanding that an equation is a balanced statement; the habit of checking solutions by substitution; fluency with inverse operations on one-step and two-step equations; experience solving equations with fractions, integers, and decimals; practice translating word problems into equations; the ability to read, solve, and graph inequalities; and an understanding of solution sets.

What Algebra I will add: multi-step equations with variables on both sides, such as 3x + 4 = x + 12; literal equations where you solve for one variable in terms of others; systems of two equations to satisfy simultaneously; compound inequalities such as 2 < x < 8, which define a range bounded on both sides; absolute value equations and inequalities; and eventually quadratic equations.

Each of these builds directly on what you have practiced here. The balance concept, the inverse operation method, the habit of checking, and the idea of a solution set will all carry forward unchanged. An equation is a question in disguise. It asks: for what value is this statement true? When you solve an equation, you are reasoning backward from a result to find the input that produced it. That backward reasoning, guided by the logic of inverse operations, is the heart of algebraic thinking.

Chapter 10 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 10.1An equation is a statement claiming two expressions are equal. It can be true or false. When it contains a variable, the question is: what value makes it true?
  • Sec 10.2A solution is a value that makes the equation true. Checking by substitution is required, not optional. It confirms correct work and catches arithmetic errors.
  • Sec 10.3The balance model shows that every operation on one side must be applied to the other side. Inverse operations are used to isolate the variable: addition undone by subtraction, multiplication undone by division.
  • Sec 10.4One-step equations require a single inverse operation. All four types are solved by applying the inverse of whatever operation is applied to the variable. Equations can have negative solutions.
  • Sec 10.5Two-step equations require two operations. Remove the addition or subtraction first (outer layer), then the multiplication or division (inner layer). Always check the final answer.
  • Sec 10.6Fractional coefficients are undone by multiplying by the reciprocal. Fractions in equations can also be cleared by multiplying every term by the LCD before solving.
  • Sec 10.7Word problems are translated into equations using the five-step method: Read, Represent, Solve, Check, Explain. Define the variable clearly before writing the equation.
  • Sec 10.8An inequality compares two expressions using >, <, ≥, or ≤. The solution is not a single value but a range. Inequalities describe real-world constraints such as limits, minimums, and maximums.
  • Sec 10.9Signal phrases translate to specific symbols: "at least" means ≥, "at most" means ≤, "more than" means >, "fewer than" means <. The boundary is included only with ≥ or ≤.
  • Sec 10.10Inequalities are solved with inverse operations like equations, with one exception: multiplying or dividing by a negative number reverses the inequality symbol. No reversal for addition or subtraction.
  • Sec 10.11Number line graphs show the solution set. Open circle: boundary not included (> or <). Closed circle: boundary included (≥ or ≤). Shading right means greater than; left means less than.
  • Sec 10.12The solution set is all values making an inequality true. It is described in words, shown on a number line, or written in set-builder or interval notation in more advanced work.
  • Sec 10.13Seven common errors: operating on one side only; wrong inverse; missing reversal; open circle for ≥/≤; shading the wrong direction; losing the variable; skipping the check step.
  • Sec 10.14Algebra I extends these skills to multi-step equations, variables on both sides, literal equations, systems, and compound inequalities. Every skill from this chapter carries forward directly.

Key Terms Review

equation
A mathematical statement that two expressions are equal, written with an equals sign.
solution
A value that makes an equation or inequality true when substituted for the variable.
balance model
The idea that an equation is like a scale; whatever is done to one side must be done to the other.
inverse operation
An operation that reverses another. Addition and subtraction are inverses; multiplication and division are inverses.
isolate
To get a variable alone on one side of an equation or inequality with a coefficient of 1.
properties of equality
Rules stating that performing equal operations on both sides of an equation preserves equality.
equivalent equations
Equations that have the same solution, produced by applying the same operation to both sides.
LCD
Least common denominator; used to clear all fractions from an equation by multiplying every term.
inequality
A mathematical statement comparing two expressions using >, <, ≥, or ≤.
solution set
All values that satisfy an inequality; represented as a shaded region on a number line.
open circle
An unfilled dot on a number line showing the boundary value is not included (> or <).
closed circle
A filled dot on a number line showing the boundary value is included (≥ or ≤).
reversal rule
The rule that the inequality symbol must be reversed when multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number.
two-step equation
An equation requiring two inverse operations to isolate the variable, typically of the form ax + b = c.
checking
Substituting the solution back into the original equation or inequality to verify it is correct.
variable
A letter representing an unknown or changing quantity in an equation or inequality.
coefficient
The number multiplied by a variable in a term; determines the scaling of the variable.

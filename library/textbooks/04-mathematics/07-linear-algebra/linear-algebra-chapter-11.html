Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces one of the most geometrically beautiful ideas in linear algebra: the structure of perpendicularity in vector spaces. You already understand perpendicular lines from coordinate geometry. In this chapter, you will extend that idea into higher dimensions, where two vectors can be perpendicular regardless of how many components they have. From perpendicularity, you will develop the idea of projection — the mathematical process of finding the closest vector in a given direction or subspace. Projection is the bridge between exact answers and approximate ones, and it appears throughout data science, physics, statistics, computer graphics, and machine learning.

By the end of this chapter, you should understand why orthogonality is not just a geometric nicety but a powerful structural tool, and why projections allow linear algebra to solve problems that have no exact solution.

Opening Question

Suppose you are a scientist collecting measurements of a physical process. You record many data points, and you want to fit a straight line through them. But the data does not fall perfectly on any line — no line passes through all the points at once. How do you find the best line?

This problem cannot be solved by substitution or elimination. The system of equations you would write to force every point onto a line is inconsistent. But linear algebra gives you a way to find the closest possible solution. The answer uses projection: you project the data onto the space of all possible linear relationships and find the vector in that space nearest to your data. This is least squares, and it rests entirely on orthogonality and projection.

Section 11.1

Dot Product Review

You first encountered the dot product in Chapter 2 when studying vectors in two and three dimensions. Before extending these ideas, it is worth reviewing the dot product carefully, because every major idea in this chapter depends on it.

Definition — The Dot Product

Let \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) be vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). The dot product of \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) is the scalar:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n$$

Notice that the dot product produces a number, not a vector. In matrix notation: \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v}\).

The dot product satisfies the following algebraic properties for all vectors \(\mathbf{u}\), \(\mathbf{v}\), \(\mathbf{w}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and all scalars \(c\):

PropertyStatement
Commutativity\(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u}\)
Distributivity\(\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{w}\)
Scalar factoring\((c\mathbf{u}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = c(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})\)
Dot with itself\(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} \geq 0\), and \(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0\) only when \(\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\)
Inner Products

The dot product is the most familiar example of an inner product. In later mathematics, you will meet inner products on function spaces and polynomial spaces — objects where "vectors" are functions, and the inner product is an integral. The dot product on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the finite-dimensional model for all of them.

Computing a dot product in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

Section 11.2

Length and Distance

Definition — Length (Norm) of a Vector

The length or norm of a vector \(\mathbf{v}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is:

$$\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}} = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}$$

This extends the Pythagorean distance formula into any number of dimensions. A unit vector has length exactly 1. To normalize: \(\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \dfrac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}\). The distance between \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) is \(d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}\|\).

Example 11.2.1
Finding length and a unit vector
  • ProblemFind the length of \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\-3\\6\end{pmatrix}\) and find the unit vector in the direction of \(\mathbf{v}\).
  • Calculate$$\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{(2)^2 + (-3)^2 + (6)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 9 + 36} = \sqrt{49} = 7$$ The unit vector: \(\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \dfrac{1}{7}\begin{pmatrix}2\\-3\\6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2/7\\-3/7\\6/7\end{pmatrix}\)
  • CheckVerify the unit vector has length 1: \(\|\hat{\mathbf{v}}\| = \sqrt{\frac{4+9+36}{49}} = \sqrt{\frac{49}{49}} = 1\) ✓
Answer: \(\|\mathbf{v}\| = 7\); unit vector: \(\bigl(2/7,\,-3/7,\,6/7\bigr)\)
Example 11.2.2
Distance between two vectors
  • ProblemFind the distance between \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\4\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}5\\1\end{pmatrix}\).
  • Calculate$$\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}-4\\3\end{pmatrix}, \quad d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \left\|\begin{pmatrix}-4\\3\end{pmatrix}\right\| = \sqrt{16+9} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$
  • InterpretThe two points \((1,4)\) and \((5,1)\) are 5 units apart. This matches the standard coordinate-geometry distance formula.
Answer: \(d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = 5\)
Section 11.3

Angles Between Vectors

The Angle Formula

For any nonzero vectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), the angle \(\theta\) between them satisfies:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|}, \quad 0 \leq \theta \leq \pi$$

The sign of the dot product tells you the type of angle: positive → acute, negative → obtuse, zero → exactly \(90°\) (orthogonal).

Example 11.3.1
Finding the angle between two vectors
  • ProblemFind the angle between \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\1\end{pmatrix}\).
  • Calculate$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 5, \quad \|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{5}, \quad \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{10}$$ $$\cos\theta = \frac{5}{\sqrt{5}\cdot\sqrt{10}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{50}} = \frac{5}{5\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$ $$\theta = \arccos\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = 45°$$
  • InterpretThe two vectors make a \(45°\) angle. Draw them from the origin on a coordinate grid and confirm they look roughly like this.
Answer: \(\theta = 45°\)
Diagram
Draw \(\mathbf{u} = (1,2)\) as an arrow from the origin pointing up and to the right. Draw \(\mathbf{v} = (3,1)\) as an arrow from the origin pointing to the right and slightly up. The angle between them, measured at the origin where both arrows begin, is 45°.

Section 11.4

Orthogonal Vectors

Definition — Orthogonality

Two vectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) are orthogonal if and only if:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$$

Orthogonal means perpendicular in the sense of the dot product. In \(\mathbb{R}^2\) and \(\mathbb{R}^3\), orthogonal vectors are geometrically perpendicular. The zero vector is orthogonal to every vector, but this is a trivial case. In applications, "orthogonal" typically refers to nonzero vectors.

If \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) are orthogonal, the Pythagorean Theorem generalizes:

Pythagorean Theorem for Vectors

If \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0\), then:

$$\|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$$

Proof: \(\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 = (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2\). When \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=0\), the middle term vanishes.

Example 11.4.1
Verifying orthogonality
  • ProblemAre \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\4\\0\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}-4\\3\\0\end{pmatrix}\) orthogonal?
  • Calculate$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = (3)(-4) + (4)(3) + (0)(0) = -12 + 12 + 0 = 0$$
  • InterpretYes, they are orthogonal. In the \(xy\)-plane they are perpendicular. Notice that \(\mathbf{u}\) has slope \(4/3\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) has slope \(-3/4\) — these are negative reciprocals, confirming perpendicularity.
Answer: Yes — \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0\) ✓

Section 11.5

Orthogonal Sets

Definition — Orthogonal and Orthonormal Sets

A set \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}\) is an orthogonal set if \(\mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{v}_j = 0\) whenever \(i \neq j\).

It is an orthonormal set if, additionally, every vector has length 1.

Key fact: Every orthogonal set of nonzero vectors is automatically linearly independent.

The independence proof uses orthogonality cleanly. Suppose \(c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}\). Take the dot product of both sides with \(\mathbf{v}_1\). All cross terms vanish because the set is orthogonal, leaving \(c_1\|\mathbf{v}_1\|^2 = 0\). Since \(\mathbf{v}_1\) is nonzero, \(c_1 = 0\). The same argument forces every coefficient to zero. This result means that building an orthogonal set automatically builds an independent set — orthogonality is a stronger condition than independence alone.

Example 11.5.1
Verifying an orthogonal set
  • ProblemShow that \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{v}_3\}\) is an orthogonal set, where \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\\0\end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}\).
  • Check all pairs$$\mathbf{v}_1\cdot\mathbf{v}_2 = (1)(1)+(1)(-1)+(0)(0) = 0 \checkmark$$ $$\mathbf{v}_1\cdot\mathbf{v}_3 = 0 \checkmark \quad \mathbf{v}_2\cdot\mathbf{v}_3 = 0 \checkmark$$
  • InterpretAll three pairs are orthogonal, so the set is orthogonal. It is not orthonormal because \(\|\mathbf{v}_1\| = \|\mathbf{v}_2\| = \sqrt{2} \neq 1\). To normalize, divide \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2\) each by \(\sqrt{2}\).
Answer: Orthogonal ✓ but not orthonormal (\(\mathbf{v}_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2\) have length \(\sqrt{2}\))
Section 11.6

Orthogonal Bases

An orthogonal basis for a subspace \(W\) is a basis for \(W\) that is also an orthogonal set. An orthonormal basis is an orthogonal basis where every vector has length 1. The standard basis vectors \(\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n\) for \(\mathbb{R}^n\) form the most familiar orthonormal basis.

When you have an orthogonal basis \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}\) for a subspace \(W\), you can find the coordinates of any vector \(\mathbf{w}\) in \(W\) using simple dot product formulas — no row reduction required:

Coordinates via an Orthogonal Basis

If \(\mathbf{w}\) is in \(W\) and \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}\) is an orthogonal basis for \(W\):

$$\mathbf{w} = \frac{\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{v}_1}{\|\mathbf{v}_1\|^2}\mathbf{v}_1 + \frac{\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{v}_2}{\|\mathbf{v}_2\|^2}\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + \frac{\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{v}_k}{\|\mathbf{v}_k\|^2}\mathbf{v}_k$$

For an orthonormal basis \(\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_k\}\), the denominators are all 1: each coordinate is simply \(\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{q}_j\).

Section 11.7

Orthogonal Complements

Definition — Orthogonal Complement

Let \(W\) be a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^n\). The orthogonal complement of \(W\), written \(W^\perp\) ("W perp"), is:

$$W^\perp = \{\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n : \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} = 0 \text{ for all } \mathbf{w} \in W\}$$

Key properties: \(\dim(W) + \dim(W^\perp) = n\); \(\;W \cap W^\perp = \{\mathbf{0}\}\); \(\;(W^\perp)^\perp = W\).

Every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) can be written uniquely as \(\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{w} + \mathbf{w}^\perp\) where \(\mathbf{w}\in W\) and \(\mathbf{w}^\perp \in W^\perp\). This is the orthogonal decomposition and is the foundation of projection.

Connection to Fundamental Subspaces

The null space of \(A\) is the orthogonal complement of the row space of \(A\). The left null space of \(A\) is the orthogonal complement of the column space of \(A\). These relationships reveal deep structural symmetry in linear systems — they appeared in Chapter 10 and reappear in least squares.

Section 11.8

Projections onto Lines

Projection is the process of finding the "shadow" of one vector onto another. Imagine shining a light perpendicular to the direction of a vector \(\mathbf{u}\). The shadow that \(\mathbf{v}\) casts along \(\mathbf{u}\) is the projection of \(\mathbf{v}\) onto \(\mathbf{u}\). More precisely: among all scalar multiples of \(\mathbf{u}\), which one is closest to \(\mathbf{v}\)? The answer is the projection.

The Projection Formula

The projection of \(\mathbf{v}\) onto the line spanned by \(\mathbf{u}\):

$$\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}} \mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|^2}\,\mathbf{u}$$

The error vector (residual) \(\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{v} - \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v}\) is perpendicular to \(\mathbf{u}\): \(\mathbf{z}\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0\).

The decomposition \(\mathbf{v} = \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{z}\) splits \(\mathbf{v}\) into a component along \(\mathbf{u}\) and a component perpendicular to \(\mathbf{u}\).

Example 11.8.1
Projecting a vector onto a line in \(\mathbb{R}^2\)
  • ProblemFind the projection of \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix}\) onto \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix}\).
  • Calculate$$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u} = 3+8=11, \quad \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 = 1+4=5$$ $$\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v} = \tfrac{11}{5}\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}11/5\\22/5\end{pmatrix}$$
  • Error vector$$\mathbf{z} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}11/5\\22/5\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4/5\\-2/5\end{pmatrix}$$
  • Check\(\mathbf{z}\cdot\mathbf{u} = \tfrac{4}{5}(1)+(-\tfrac{2}{5})(2) = \tfrac{4}{5}-\tfrac{4}{5} = 0\) ✓
  • InterpretThe projection \(\bigl(11/5,\,22/5\bigr)\) is the closest point on the line through \(\mathbf{u}\) to \(\mathbf{v}\). The error vector \(\bigl(4/5,\,-2/5\bigr)\) is perpendicular to \(\mathbf{u}\) and measures how far \(\mathbf{v}\) is from that line.
Answer: \(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}11/5\\22/5\end{pmatrix}\); error: \(\begin{pmatrix}4/5\\-2/5\end{pmatrix}\)
Diagram
Draw the line through the origin in the direction of \((1,2)\). Draw \(\mathbf{v}=(3,4)\) as an arrow from the origin. Drop a perpendicular from the tip of \(\mathbf{v}\) to the line. Example 11.8.2
Projection in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)
  • ProblemFind the projection of \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{pmatrix}\) onto \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\0\\-1\end{pmatrix}\).
  • Calculate$$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u} = 2+0-1=1, \quad \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 = 4+0+1=5$$ $$\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v} = \tfrac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\\-1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2/5\\0\\-1/5\end{pmatrix}$$
  • Check\(\mathbf{z} = \begin{pmatrix}3/5\\1\\6/5\end{pmatrix}\); then \(\mathbf{z}\cdot\mathbf{u} = \tfrac{6}{5}+0-\tfrac{6}{5}=0\) ✓
Answer: \(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}2/5\\0\\-1/5\end{pmatrix}\)

The projection onto the line spanned by \(\mathbf{u}\) can also be written as a matrix transformation. The projection matrix onto the line is:

Projection Matrix onto a Line

$$P = \frac{\mathbf{u}\mathbf{u}^T}{\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{u}}$$

where \(\mathbf{u}\mathbf{u}^T\) is the outer product (an \(n\times n\) matrix) and \(\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{u}\) is a scalar. Then \(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v} = P\mathbf{v}\).

Key properties: \(P^2 = P\) (idempotent — projecting twice gives the same result as projecting once); \(P^T = P\) (symmetric); eigenvalues of \(P\) are 0s and 1s.

Section 11.9

Projections onto Subspaces

The projection formula from Section 11.8 handles lines. Now we extend to projecting onto a subspace of dimension greater than one. Let \(W\) be a subspace with basis \(\{\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_k\}\). Form the matrix \(A\) whose columns are these basis vectors. The projection of any vector \(\mathbf{b}\) onto \(W\) is:

Projection onto a General Subspace

$$\hat{\mathbf{b}} = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T \mathbf{b}$$

The matrix \(P = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T\) is the projection matrix onto \(W\). The error vector \(\mathbf{b}-\hat{\mathbf{b}}\) is always orthogonal to every vector in \(W\).

When the columns of \(A\) are already orthogonal, \(A^TA\) becomes diagonal and the formula simplifies to a sum of scalar projections — one per basis vector.

Section 11.10

Least Squares as an Algebraic Introduction

Consider trying to solve \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) where \(\mathbf{b}\) does not lie in the column space of \(A\) — the system is inconsistent. No exact solution exists. This is the common situation in data science and statistics: you have more equations (data points) than unknowns (model parameters).

The least squares approach asks: find \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) that minimizes the size of the error \(\|A\hat{\mathbf{x}} - \mathbf{b}\|\). This is asking for the vector \(A\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) in the column space of \(A\) that is closest to \(\mathbf{b}\) — which is a projection.

The Normal Equations

The least squares solution \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) satisfies:

$$A^T A\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T \mathbf{b}$$

When the columns of \(A\) are linearly independent, \(A^TA\) is invertible and the unique solution is \(\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (A^TA)^{-1}A^T\mathbf{b}\).

The word "normal" means perpendicular: the residual \(\mathbf{b}-A\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) is perpendicular (normal) to every column of \(A\). This geometric condition is what the normal equations encode.

Why No Calculus?

Even though least squares is often derived by minimizing a sum of squares in a calculus course, we can understand and apply it entirely through projection. The residual being orthogonal to the column space is the geometric condition, and geometry is enough to derive and apply the normal equations.

Least Squares for Line Fitting

One common application is fitting a line \(y = mx + b\) to data points \((x_1,y_1), \ldots, (x_n,y_n)\). Write this as \(A\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{y}\) where:

Line Fitting Setup

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}x_1 & 1\\x_2 & 1\\\vdots & \vdots\\x_n & 1\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{c} = \begin{pmatrix}m\\b\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{y} = \begin{pmatrix}y_1\\y_2\\\vdots\\y_n\end{pmatrix}$$

Solve the normal equations \(A^TA\hat{\mathbf{c}} = A^T\mathbf{y}\) to find the slope \(m\) and intercept \(b\) of the best-fit line.

Example 11.10.1
Least squares line through three points
  • ProblemFind the best-fit line \(y = mx + b\) through \((0,1)\), \((1,3)\), \((2,4)\).

Section 11.11

Gram-Schmidt Process as an Introduction

Given an arbitrary linearly independent set of vectors, how do you convert it into an orthogonal basis for the same subspace? The Gram-Schmidt process answers this question.

The Gram-Schmidt Algorithm

Given a linearly independent set \(\{\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_k\}\), produce an orthogonal set \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}\) spanning the same subspace:

Step 1: Set \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{x}_1\).

Step 2: \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{x}_2 - \dfrac{\mathbf{x}_2\cdot\mathbf{v}_1}{\|\mathbf{v}_1\|^2}\mathbf{v}_1\)

Step 3: \(\mathbf{v}_3 = \mathbf{x}_3 - \dfrac{\mathbf{x}_3\cdot\mathbf{v}_1}{\|\mathbf{v}_1\|^2}\mathbf{v}_1 - \dfrac{\mathbf{x}_3\cdot\mathbf{v}_2}{\|\mathbf{v}_2\|^2}\mathbf{v}_2\)

General step: \(\mathbf{v}_j = \mathbf{x}_j - \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{j-1} \dfrac{\mathbf{x}_j\cdot\mathbf{v}_i}{\|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2}\mathbf{v}_i\)

To obtain an orthonormal basis, normalize: \(\mathbf{q}_j = \dfrac{\mathbf{v}_j}{\|\mathbf{v}_j\|}\).

Each step removes the components of the new vector that lie along previously processed directions. What remains is exactly the part of \(\mathbf{x}_j\) perpendicular to all earlier directions. The result is orthogonal by construction.

Example 11.11.1
Gram-Schmidt in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)
  • ProblemApply Gram-Schmidt to \(\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{x}_3 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix}\).
  • Step 1$$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{pmatrix}$$
  • Step 2$$\mathbf{x}_2\cdot\mathbf{v}_1 = 2, \quad \|\mathbf{v}_1\|^2 = 3$$ $$\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix} - \tfrac{2}{3}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1/3\\1/3\\-2/3\end{pmatrix}$$
  • Step 3$$\mathbf{x}_3\cdot\mathbf{v}_1 = 1, \quad \mathbf{x}_3\cdot\mathbf{v}_2 = \tfrac{1}{3}, \quad \|\mathbf{v}_2\|^2 = \tfrac{2}{3}$$ $$\mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix} - \tfrac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{pmatrix} - \tfrac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1/3\\1/3\\-2/3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1/2\\-1/2\\0\end{pmatrix}$$
  • Check\(\mathbf{v}_1\cdot\mathbf{v}_2 = \tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{1}{3}-\tfrac{2}{3} = 0\) ✓; \(\mathbf{v}_1\cdot\mathbf{v}_3 = \tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{1}{2}+0=0\) ✓; \(\mathbf{v}_2\cdot\mathbf{v}_3 = \tfrac{1}{6}-\tfrac{1}{6}+0=0\) ✓

Section 11.13

Common Orthogonality Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Orthogonality and Linear Independence

Orthogonal nonzero vectors are always linearly independent, but independent vectors are not necessarily orthogonal. The vectors \(\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}\) are independent but not orthogonal (their dot product is 1, not 0). Do not assume that any independent set is orthogonal.

Mistake 2 — Projecting onto a Vector Instead of a Subspace (Wrong Formula)

When projecting \(\mathbf{b}\) onto a subspace \(W\) spanned by two or more vectors, you cannot use the single-vector formula unless the basis vectors are already orthogonal. If the basis is not orthogonal, use the matrix formula \(P = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T\) or first apply Gram-Schmidt to get an orthogonal basis.

Mistake 3 — Forgetting to Subtract the Projection in Gram-Schmidt

The orthogonal vector is \(\mathbf{v}_j = \mathbf{x}_j - \text{(projections)}\), not just the projections. A common error is computing the projection terms but forgetting to subtract them from \(\mathbf{x}_j\). Always check by computing dot products to confirm the result is orthogonal.

Mistake 4 — Confusing the Scalar Component with the Vector Projection
scalar component = vector projection

The scalar component of \(\mathbf{v}\) along \(\mathbf{u}\) is the number \(\dfrac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|}\). The vector projection is \(\dfrac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|^2}\mathbf{u}\) — note the different denominator and the factor of \(\mathbf{u}\). These are related but distinct results.

Mistake 5 — Expecting the Projection to Equal the Original Vector

The projection of \(\mathbf{v}\) onto a subspace equals \(\mathbf{v}\) itself only when \(\mathbf{v}\) already lies in that subspace. If \(\mathbf{v}\) is not in the subspace, the projection is strictly "shorter" in a sense and the error vector is nonzero.

Mistake 6 — Wrong Setup of Normal Equations
\(AA^T\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}\)

The normal equations are \(A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}\). Students sometimes write \(AA^T\) instead of \(A^TA\), which is a completely different (and wrong) matrix. Carefully track the order and placement of transposes.

Mistake 7 — Assuming Normal Equations Always Have a Unique Solution

If the columns of \(A\) are linearly dependent, then \(A^TA\) is not invertible and the normal equations have infinitely many solutions. Every solution minimizes the same residual, but there is no unique least squares solution. This happens when model variables are redundant.

Mistake 8 — Confusing Orthogonal Complement with the Null Space

The null space of \(A\) equals the orthogonal complement of the row space of \(A\) — not the orthogonal complement of the column space. These are different subspaces of different ambient spaces. Confusion between them leads to incorrect dimension counts and structural conclusions.

Section 11.14

Preparing for Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

This chapter has developed the geometric language of perpendicularity, projection, and orthogonal structure. Chapter 12 will use some of this language as you study eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors describe directions that remain on their own line under a matrix transformation — directions of pure stretching or compression. Orthogonality will reappear when you study symmetric matrices, which always have orthogonal eigenvectors. This result, called the Spectral Theorem, means that symmetric matrices can always be diagonalized using an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors.

Least squares is also connected to eigenvalues: the principal components in data analysis are eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix related to the covariance of the data. As you move into Chapter 12, bring these tools: the dot product and orthogonality condition; the projection formula; the understanding that a subspace can be described by an orthogonal basis; and the connection between symmetric matrices and clean geometric properties.

Key Terms Review

dot product
The scalar \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = u_1v_1 + \cdots + u_nv_n\). Also written \(\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v}\). Measures angle and perpendicularity.
inner product
A generalization of the dot product to abstract vector spaces. The dot product on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the standard example.
norm (length)
\(\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}\). Extends the Pythagorean distance formula into any dimension.
unit vector
A vector of length 1. Obtained by normalizing: \(\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{v}/\|\mathbf{v}\|\).
normalizing
Dividing a nonzero vector by its length to obtain a unit vector in the same direction.
distance
\(d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|\). The distance between the tips of two vectors.
orthogonal
Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero — perpendicular in the sense of the inner product.
orthogonal set
A set where every pair of distinct vectors has zero dot product. Automatically linearly independent.
orthonormal set
An orthogonal set where every vector also has length 1.
orthogonal basis
A basis that is also an orthogonal set. Coordinates computed by dot products.
orthogonal complement
\(W^\perp\): all vectors orthogonal to every vector in \(W\). Satisfies \(\dim(W)+\dim(W^\perp)=n\).
projection
The closest vector in a given direction or subspace to a given vector; found via the projection formula.
scalar component
The number \(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}/\|\mathbf{u}\|\). The signed length of the projection of \(\mathbf{v}\) along \(\mathbf{u}\).
projection matrix
The matrix \(P\) such that \(P\mathbf{b} = \hat{\mathbf{b}}\). Satisfies \(P^2=P\) (idempotent) and \(P^T=P\) (symmetric).
residual (error vector)
\(\mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}\). Always orthogonal to the column space of \(A\) in a least squares problem.
least squares solution
Minimizes \(\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|\). Satisfies the normal equations when the original system is inconsistent.
normal equations
\(A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}\). The system whose solution gives the least squares approximation.
Gram-Schmidt process
Algorithm to convert a linearly independent set into an orthogonal (or orthonormal) basis for the same subspace.
QR decomposition
Factorization \(A = QR\) where \(Q\) has orthonormal columns and \(R\) is upper triangular; produced by Gram-Schmidt.
idempotent matrix
A matrix \(P\) satisfying \(P^2=P\). Projection matrices are idempotent because projecting twice gives the same result as projecting once.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 12: Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Chapter 11 gave you the geometric language of perpendicularity and projection. Chapter 12 introduces eigenvalues and eigenvectors — the directions in space that a linear transformation leaves unchanged in direction, only scaling them. The dot product and orthogonality will return when you study symmetric matrices, which have a beautiful property: their eigenvectors are always orthogonal. This result, the Spectral Theorem, connects everything you learned in Chapter 11 to the deepest structure of square matrices. Bring the projection formula and orthogonality condition forward — you will use them.

Bridge to Calculus, Multivariable Mathematics, and Advanced Fields

Multivariable Calculus. Dot products and projections appear throughout. The gradient vector is orthogonal to level curves and level surfaces. Finding the component of a gradient in a particular direction — the directional derivative — is a projection. The tangent plane to a surface uses normal vectors, which are defined by orthogonality.

Differential Equations. Many solution methods rely on orthogonal function spaces. Fourier series decompose functions using orthogonal sine and cosine functions — the analogue of projecting a vector onto an orthogonal basis.

Physics. Work is the dot product of force and displacement. The normal force on an inclined plane is the projection of gravity perpendicular to the surface. Stress tensors and angular momentum use orthogonal decompositions.

Data Science and Machine Learning. Least squares is the foundation of linear regression. Principal Component Analysis uses orthogonal projections to find the most informative directions in high-dimensional data. Orthogonality is central to virtually every major machine learning algorithm.

Statistics. The geometry of linear regression is exactly the projection of a response vector onto the column space of the predictor matrix. Variance decomposition is an orthogonal decomposition of the response vector.

Computer Graphics and Robotics. Orthonormal frames, rotation matrices, and projection operators are used constantly. Camera matrices, lighting calculations, and robot joint coordinates all rely on the ideas of this chapter.

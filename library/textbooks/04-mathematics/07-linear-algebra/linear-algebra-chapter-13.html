Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter brings together eigenvalues, eigenvectors, and matrix structure to address one of the most powerful ideas in linear algebra: diagonalization. When a matrix can be diagonalized, repeated matrix operations become dramatically simpler. Instead of computing large matrix products, diagonalization reduces the repeated action to simple scaling in each eigenvector direction.

The chapter then applies this idea to discrete dynamical systems — mathematical models that describe how a state evolves from one step to the next. These include population models, economic models, network propagation models, and Markov chains. Each of these systems can be analyzed with eigenvalues and diagonalization, revealing long-term behavior without computing hundreds of matrix powers by hand.

This chapter is the culmination of Part IV. It draws on eigenvalues and eigenvectors from Chapter 12, on vector spaces and bases from Chapters 8 and 9, on matrix operations from Chapter 4, and on the invertibility ideas from Chapter 5. Students who understand diagonalization will be well prepared for differential equations, numerical methods, data science, machine learning, and advanced applied mathematics.

Opening Question

A wildlife biologist is studying a population of rabbits and foxes. Each year, the rabbit population and the fox population influence each other in ways that can be described by a system of linear equations. The biologist wants to know: after 50 years, what will the population look like? After 100 years? Will the populations grow, shrink, or stabilize?

Computing A⁵⁰ or A¹⁰⁰ directly would require an enormous amount of work. But if the biologist knows the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of A, she can find A⁵⁰ and A¹⁰⁰ with only a few steps — and understand the long-term behavior without computing a single large matrix product. That is what diagonalization makes possible.

Section 13.1

Why Diagonalization Matters

The Problem of Repeated Multiplication

Suppose you have a matrix A and you want to compute A¹⁰, meaning the result of multiplying A by itself ten times. You could compute A² = A · A, then A³ = A² · A, and continue. For a 2 × 2 matrix this is tedious but manageable. For a 10 × 10 matrix, computing A¹⁰⁰ this way is essentially impossible by hand.

The situation becomes even more interesting when the repeated multiplication represents something real. If A is the matrix that describes how a population changes from one year to the next, then A⁵⁰ describes the population after 50 years. If A is a transition matrix for a network, then Ak describes how information propagates after k steps. Understanding Ak for large k is often the central question.

The Key Insight: Eigenvectors as a Basis

In Chapter 12 you learned that some vectors are special: eigenvectors. If Av = λv, then applying A to v just scales it by λ. The vector keeps its direction; only its length changes. This means that applying A twice to v gives:

A²v = A(Av) = A(λv) = λ(Av) = λ(λv) = λ²v

And in general, for any positive integer k:

Core Eigenvector-Power Formula
Akv = λkv
On an eigenvector, repeated application of A is just repeated scaling by λ. Powers of a number are easy; powers of a matrix are hard.

If you can express any starting vector as a combination of eigenvectors, you can reduce Ak to a set of scalar power computations. Diagonalization is the formal version of this idea.

Diagonalization: The Big Picture

Diagonalization rewrites a matrix A as A = PDP⁻¹, where D is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues and P is a matrix whose columns are the corresponding eigenvectors. This turns the hard problem of computing Ak into the easy problem:

Ak = PDkP⁻¹    Since D is diagonal, Dk is trivial: raise each diagonal entry to the kth power.

Section 13.2

Similar Matrices as an Introduction

Two square matrices A and B are called similar if there exists an invertible matrix P such that B = P⁻¹AP. The matrix P is called the change-of-basis matrix. Similar matrices represent the same linear transformation expressed in different coordinate systems.

PropertyPreserved Under Similarity?Reason
EigenvaluesYesSame characteristic polynomial
DeterminantYesdet(P⁻¹AP) = det(A)
RankYesSame null space dimension
TraceYesSum of eigenvalues is the same
Specific entriesNoCoordinates change with basis
EigenvectorsNot in generalExpressed in different bases
Diagonalization as Special Similarity

Diagonalization is the special case where B = D is a diagonal matrix. The question becomes: can we find an invertible P such that P⁻¹AP = D is diagonal? The diagonal entries of D will be the eigenvalues of A.

Section 13.3

Diagonal Matrices

A diagonal matrix is a square matrix in which every entry off the main diagonal is zero. Diagonal matrices are the simplest matrices to work with because multiplication, powers, inverses, and determinants all reduce to operations on individual diagonal entries.

OperationFor Diagonal Matrix D = diag(d₁, d₂, …, d_n)
Power Dkdiag(d₁k, d₂k, …, d_nk)
Inverse D⁻¹diag(1/d₁, 1/d₂, …, 1/d_n) — requires all d_i ≠ 0
Determinantd₁ · d₂ · ⋯ · d_n
Product D₁D₂diag(d₁e₁, d₂e₂, …, d_ne_n) — entry-by-entry
Example 13.3.1
Power of a diagonal matrix and long-term behavior
  • ProblemLet D = diag(3, −1). Compute D⁴ and D⁵. Then describe what happens as k → ∞.
  • Calculate D⁴ = diag(3⁴, (−1)⁴) = diag(81, 1) D⁵ = diag(3⁵, (−1)⁵) = diag(243, −1)
  • InterpretThe first component scales by 3k, growing rapidly since |3| > 1. The second component alternates between +1 and −1 since |(−1)| = 1. For large k, the behavior is dominated entirely by the first component. The eigenvalue with the largest absolute value controls the long-term picture.
Answer: D⁴ = diag(81, 1), D⁵ = diag(243, −1). First component grows; second oscillates.
Section 13.4

Diagonalizing a Matrix

The Diagonalization Theorem

An n × n matrix A is diagonalizable if and only if A has n linearly independent eigenvectors.

If A has n linearly independent eigenvectors v₁, v₂, …, vₙ with corresponding eigenvalues λ₁, λ₂, …, λₙ, form:

P = [v₁ | v₂ | ⋯ | vₙ]    (columns are the eigenvectors)

D = diag(λ₁, λ₂, …, λₙ)    (eigenvalues on the diagonal, same order)

Then A = PDP⁻¹. The columns of P must be in the same order as the diagonal entries of D.

Why the Formula Works

To verify A = PDP⁻¹, multiply both sides on the right by P to get AP = PD. The jth column of AP is Avⱼ. The jth column of PD is λⱼvⱼ (scaling the jth column of P by λⱼ). Since Avⱼ = λⱼvⱼ by definition of eigenvector, the equation AP = PD holds. Since P is invertible (its columns are linearly independent), we write A = PDP⁻¹.

The Diagonalization Process — Five Steps

StepAction
1Find all eigenvalues by solving det(A − λI) = 0
2For each eigenvalue, find a basis for the eigenspace by solving (A − λI)x = 0
3Count the total eigenvectors. If that number equals n, the matrix is diagonalizable
4Form P with eigenvectors as columns; form D with eigenvalues on the diagonal (matching order)
5Verify: check that AP = PD column by column
Example 13.4.1
Diagonalizing a 2 × 2 matrix
  • ProblemDiagonalize A = [[4, 1], [2, 3]].
  • ThinkFind eigenvalues, then eigenvectors, then assemble P and D.
  • Step 1 det(A − λI) = (4−λ)(3−λ) − 2 = λ² − 7λ + 10 = (λ−5)(λ−2) Eigenvalues: λ₁ = 5, λ₂ = 2
  • Step 2 For λ₁ = 5: A − 5I = [[ −1, 1], [ 2, −2]] Row reduce → [[1, −1], [0, 0]] x₂ = t, x₁ = t → v₁ = (1, 1)ᵀ For λ₂ = 2: A − 2I = [[ 2, 1], [ 2, 1]] Row reduce → [[2, 1], [0, 0]] x₂ = t, x₁ = −t/2. Choose t = −2 → v₂ = (1, −2)ᵀ
  • Step 4 P = [[1, 1], D = [[5, 0], [1, −2]] [0, 2]]
  • Verify AP = [[4,1],[2,3]] · [[1,1],[1,−2]] = [[5, 2],[5, −4]] PD = [[1,1],[1,−2]] · [[5,0],[0,2]] = [[5, 2],[5, −4]] ✓
  • InterpretThe matrix A acts like simple scaling by 5 and 2 when the state is expressed in the eigenvector basis. The column v₁ = (1,1)ᵀ is stretched by factor 5; the column v₂ = (1,−2)ᵀ is stretched by factor 2.
Answer: P = [[1,1],[1,−2]], D = [[5,0],[0,2]], and A = PDP⁻¹.
Section 13.5

When a Matrix Is Diagonalizable

The most convenient sufficient condition is: if an n × n matrix A has n distinct eigenvalues, then A is diagonalizable. Eigenvectors from distinct eigenvalues are always linearly independent. This is sufficient but not necessary — a matrix can be diagonalizable even when eigenvalues repeat, as long as each repeated eigenvalue has enough independent eigenvectors.

Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity

When an eigenvalue λ appears as a repeated root of the characteristic polynomial, its algebraic multiplicity is the number of times it appears as a root. The geometric multiplicity is the dimension of the eigenspace for λ — the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for that eigenvalue.

The geometric multiplicity is always at most the algebraic multiplicity. For A to be diagonalizable, every eigenvalue must have geometric multiplicity equal to its algebraic multiplicity.

Example 13.5.1
A matrix that is NOT diagonalizable
  • ProblemDetermine whether A = [[2, 1], [0, 2]] is diagonalizable.
  • Eigenvaluesdet(A − λI) = (2−λ)² = 0 → λ = 2, algebraic multiplicity 2
  • Eigenspace A − 2I = [[0, 1], Row reduce → [[0, 1], [0, 0]] [0, 0]] Only x₂ = 0, x₁ free. One independent eigenvector: v = (1, 0)ᵀ. Geometric multiplicity = 1. Algebraic multiplicity = 2. MISMATCH.
  • InterpretSince geometric multiplicity (1) is less than algebraic multiplicity (2), this matrix is defective — not diagonalizable. There is no basis of eigenvectors for ℝ².
Answer: Not diagonalizable. The eigenspace for λ = 2 has dimension 1, but algebraic multiplicity is 2.
Student Warning — Not Every Matrix Is Diagonalizable

Never assume a matrix is diagonalizable without checking. A matrix with a repeated eigenvalue may or may not be diagonalizable. Always check whether the geometric multiplicity of each eigenvalue equals its algebraic multiplicity. A single defective eigenvalue makes the entire matrix non-diagonalizable.

Section 13.6

Powers of Diagonal Matrices

For any diagonal matrix D = diag(d₁, d₂, …, d_n) and any non-negative integer k:

Power of a Diagonal Matrix
Dk = diag(d₁k, d₂k, …, d_nk)
Raise each diagonal entry to the kth power independently. The long-term behavior depends entirely on which entries have |d_i| > 1, = 1, or < 1.
Example 13.6.1
Long-term behavior from diagonal entries
  • ProblemDescribe the long-term behavior of D = diag(0.9, 1.5) as k → ∞.
  • Analyze (0.9)ᵏ → 0 because |0.9| < 1 → this component SHRINKS (1.5)ᵏ → ∞ because |1.5| > 1 → this component GROWS without bound
  • InterpretFor large k, Dᵏ ≈ diag(0, (1.5)ᵏ). The long-term behavior is completely governed by the component with the eigenvalue larger than 1 in absolute value — the dominant eigenvalue.
Answer: First component decays to 0; second component grows without bound. Dominant eigenvalue is 1.5.
Section 13.7

Powers of Diagonalizable Matrices

If A = PDP⁻¹, then A² = (PDP⁻¹)(PDP⁻¹) = PD(P⁻¹P)DP⁻¹ = PD²P⁻¹. By induction the pattern extends to all positive integer powers.

Main Formula — Matrix Powers via Diagonalization
Ak = PDkP⁻¹
Steps: (1) Find P and D from the eigenvalue decomposition. (2) Compute Dk by raising each eigenvalue to the kth power. (3) Multiply PDkP⁻¹.
Example 13.7.1
Computing A¹⁰ using diagonalization
  • ProblemUse the diagonalization from Example 13.4.1 to compute A¹⁰ where A = [[4,1],[2,3]].
  • SetupFrom Example 13.4.1: P = [[1,1],[1,−2]], D = [[5,0],[0,2]]. Find P⁻¹ first.
  • P⁻¹ det(P) = (1)(−2) − (1)(1) = −3 P⁻¹ = (1/−3) · [[−2, −1], = [[ 2/3, 1/3], [−1, 1]] [ 1/3, −1/3]]
  • D¹⁰ D¹⁰ = [[5¹⁰, 0 ], = [[9 765 625, 0], [ 0, 2¹⁰]] [ 0, 1 024]]
  • Multiply A¹⁰ = P · D¹⁰ · P⁻¹ PD¹⁰ = [[9 765 625, 1 024], [9 765 625, −2 048]] A¹⁰ entries involve (2·5¹⁰ ± 2¹⁰)/3 and (5¹⁰ ∓ 2¹⁰)/3 — all integers ✓
  • InterpretComputing A¹⁰ directly would require 9 separate matrix multiplications. Diagonalization reduces this to raising two numbers to the 10th power and multiplying three fixed matrices — a dramatic simplification for large k.
Answer: A¹⁰ = PD¹⁰P⁻¹, computed by raising eigenvalues 5 and 2 to the 10th power.
General Solution Formula for a Diagonalizable System

If x₀ = c₁v₁ + c₂v₂ + ⋯ + c_n v_n (initial vector expanded in the eigenvector basis), then:

Akx₀ = c₁λ₁kv₁ + c₂λ₂kv₂ + ⋯ + c_n λ_nkv_n

Each component evolves independently in its eigenvector direction, scaled by the corresponding eigenvalue raised to the kth power. The coefficients c₁, …, c_n come from solving Pc = x₀.

Section 13.8

Discrete Dynamical Systems

A discrete dynamical system is a model in which a state changes from one step to the next according to a fixed rule. The state might represent a population, an amount of money, proportions in a network, or a physical configuration. The rule is applied repeatedly, and we want to understand how the state evolves over time.

Discrete means the time steps are separate and countable — year 0, year 1, year 2, etc. — rather than continuous. This contrasts with differential equations, which model continuous change. Since this book does not require calculus, we focus entirely on the discrete case.

Matrix Form of a Discrete Dynamical System

The system is described by x_{k+1} = A x_k, where:

x_k  — the state vector at step k (all quantities in the system)

A  — the transition matrix (how the state changes each step)

Given initial state x₀, the state at step k is: x_k = Ak x₀

If A is diagonalizable: x_k = c₁λ₁kv₁ + c₂λ₂kv₂ + ⋯ where c = P⁻¹x₀.

Example 13.8.1
Setting up a discrete dynamical system — city neighborhoods
  • ProblemA city has two neighborhoods: Downtown and Uptown. Each year, 10% of Downtown residents move to Uptown and 20% of Uptown residents move to Downtown. The rest stay. Set up the dynamical system if Downtown starts at 8000 residents and Uptown at 6000.
  • RepresentLet x_k = (d_k, u_k)ᵀ be the population vector at year k. The transition rules: Next Downtown = 0.90 · d_k + 0.20 · u_k Next Uptown = 0.10 · d_k + 0.80 · u_k
  • Matrix form A = [[0.90, 0.20], x₀ = [[8000], [0.10, 0.80]] [6000]] x_{k+1} = A x_k
  • CheckEach column of A sums to 1 (90% + 10% = 1; 20% + 80% = 1). Total population is preserved. ✓
  • InterpretWe will find the eigenvalues of A in Section 13.11 to determine the long-term population split. The column sum = 1 property will appear again in Markov chains.
Answer: Transition matrix A = [[0.90, 0.20], [0.10, 0.80]], initial state x₀ = (8000, 6000)ᵀ.
Geometric Description — Eigenvector Decomposition of a State

Draw a timeline of vectors in ℝ². The initial vector x₀ is shown as a combination of v₁ and v₂. At each step, the v₁ component is multiplied by λ₁ and the v₂ component is multiplied by λ₂. If |λ₁| > |λ₂|, then for large k the vector x_k points increasingly in the v₁ direction — the direction of the dominant eigenvector.

Section 13.9

Recurrence Systems

A recurrence relation defines each term in a sequence in terms of previous terms. The second-order linear recurrence a_{k+2} = p · a_{k+1} + q · a_k can be converted to a matrix system by introducing the state vector x_k = (a_{k+1}, a_k)ᵀ. Then x_{k+1} = A x_k with the companion matrix:

A = [[p, q], (companion matrix of the recurrence) [1, 0]]

Diagonalizing the companion matrix yields a closed-form solution for a_k.

Example 13.9.1
The Fibonacci sequence as a matrix system
  • ProblemThe Fibonacci recurrence: F₀ = 0, F₁ = 1, F_{k+2} = F_{k+1} + F_k. Find the companion matrix and describe the long-term growth rate.
  • Represent x_k = (F_{k+1}, F_k)ᵀ, A = [[1, 1], [1, 0]]
  • Eigenvalues det(A − λI) = (1−λ)(−λ) − 1 = λ² − λ − 1 = 0 λ = (1 ± √5) / 2 φ = (1 + √5)/2 ≈ 1.618 (the golden ratio) φ̂ = (1 − √5)/2 ≈ −0.618
  • ResultTwo distinct eigenvalues → A is diagonalizable. Diagonalizing yields Binet's formula: F_k = (φᵏ − φ̂ᵏ) / √5
  • InterpretSince |φ̂| < 1, the term φ̂ᵏ → 0, so for large k, F_k ≈ φᵏ/√5. Each Fibonacci number is approximately φ ≈ 1.618 times the previous one. The golden ratio is the dominant eigenvalue of the Fibonacci companion matrix.
Answer: Companion matrix A = [[1,1],[1,0]] has eigenvalues φ and φ̂. Dominant eigenvalue φ = (1+√5)/2 controls long-term growth.
Tip — Every Linear Recurrence Has a Matrix Form

Any second-order linear recurrence a_{k+2} = p · a_{k+1} + q · a_k becomes the matrix system x_{k+1} = [[p,q],[1,0]] · x_k. The characteristic polynomial of the recurrence and the characteristic polynomial of the companion matrix are the same. The eigenvalues of the companion matrix are exactly the roots of the recurrence's characteristic equation.

Section 13.10

Markov Chains as an Introduction

A Markov chain is a special discrete dynamical system in which the state vector represents probabilities. Each entry x_i of the state vector gives the probability of being in state i. The transition matrix describes the probability of moving from one state to another at each step.

ComponentDefinitionRequired Property
State vector x_kColumn vector of probabilities at time kAll entries ≥ 0; entries sum to 1
Stochastic matrix ATransition matrix encoding movement probabilitiesAll entries ≥ 0; each column sums to 1
Steady-state vector qLong-term probability distributionAq = q; entries ≥ 0, sum to 1
Example 13.10.1
A weather Markov chain
  • ProblemWeather is either Sunny (S) or Cloudy (C). If today is Sunny, tomorrow is Sunny with probability 0.7 and Cloudy with probability 0.3. If today is Cloudy, tomorrow is Sunny with probability 0.4 and Cloudy with probability 0.6. Set up the Markov chain and compute the first few state vectors.
  • Matrix A = [[ 0.7, 0.4], Columns: current state (S or C) [ 0.3, 0.6]] Rows: next state (S or C) Each column sums to 1. ✓
  • Iterations x₀ = (1, 0)ᵀ (start: Sunny) x₁ = A x₀ = (0.7, 0.3)ᵀ x₂ = A x₁ = [[0.7,0.4],[0.3,0.6]] · (0.7, 0.3)ᵀ = (0.61, 0.39)ᵀ
  • InterpretAfter two days starting from Sunny, there is a 61% chance of Sunny and 39% chance of Cloudy. The distribution is already shifting toward a long-term balance.
Answer: Transition matrix A = [[0.7, 0.4], [0.3, 0.6]]. After two days: (0.61, 0.39)ᵀ.

Finding the Steady-State Vector

The steady-state vector q satisfies Aq = q, which means (A − I)q = 0. This makes q an eigenvector of A with eigenvalue λ = 1. For any regular stochastic matrix, one eigenvalue is always exactly 1, and all other eigenvalues satisfy |λ| < 1. The steady state is the eigenvector for λ = 1, normalized so its entries sum to 1.

Example 13.10.2
Finding the steady-state distribution
  • ProblemFind the steady-state vector for the weather Markov chain in Example 13.10.1.
  • Solve A − I = [[ −0.3, 0.4], [ 0.3, −0.4]] Row reduce: → [[ 0.3, −0.4], [ 0, 0]] 0.3 q₁ = 0.4 q₂ → q₁ = (4/3) q₂
  • Normalize q₁ + q₂ = 1 (4/3)q₂ + q₂ = 1 → (7/3)q₂ = 1 → q₂ = 3/7, q₁ = 4/7 q = (4/7, 3/7)ᵀ ≈ (0.571, 0.429)ᵀ
  • InterpretIn the long run, about 57.1% of days will be Sunny and 42.9% will be Cloudy — regardless of today's weather. This equilibrium is the eigenvector for λ = 1, scaled to sum to 1.
Answer: Steady-state vector q = (4/7, 3/7)ᵀ ≈ (0.571, 0.429)ᵀ.
Student Warning — Normalize Markov Steady-State Vectors

The eigenvector for λ = 1 gives the correct direction but must be scaled so all entries are non-negative and sum to 1 to be a valid probability distribution. Simply reporting the eigenvector without normalizing gives the wrong answer in any probability context.

Section 13.11

Long-Term Behavior in Step-by-Step Models

When A is diagonalizable and x₀ = c₁v₁ + c₂v₂ + ⋯ + c_n v_n, the state at step k is:

x_k = c₁λ₁ᵏv₁ + c₂λ₂ᵏv₂ + ⋯ + c_n λ_nᵏ v_n

If |λ₁| > |λ₂| ≥ ⋯ ≥ |λ_n|, then for large k the term c₁λ₁ᵏv₁ dominates all others. The eigenvalue λ₁ with the largest absolute value is the dominant eigenvalue, and its eigenvector v₁ gives the long-term direction of the state vector.

Dominant Eigenvalue |λ₁|Long-Term BehaviorExample
|λ₁| > 1State grows without bound (explosive growth)Growing population, runaway system
|λ₁| = 1 and λ₁ = +1State converges to a stable equilibriumMarkov chain steady state, balanced population
|λ₁| = 1 and λ₁ = −1State oscillates with constant magnitudeAlternating-sign behavior
|λ₁| < 1State decays to 0 (stable decay)Declining population, dying-out process
Example 13.11.1
Long-term behavior of the city neighborhood model
  • ProblemFind the eigenvalues of A = [[0.90, 0.20], [0.10, 0.80]] from Example 13.8.1 and determine the long-term population split.
  • Eigenvalues det(A − λI) = λ² − 1.7λ + 0.70 = 0 λ = (1.7 ± √(2.89 − 2.80))/2 = (1.7 ± 0.3)/2 λ₁ = 1, λ₂ = 0.7
  • Analyzeλ₁ = 1 is the dominant eigenvalue (|λ₂| = 0.7 < 1, so that component decays). The total population is preserved (columns sum to 1). Find the eigenvector for λ₁ = 1: A − I = [[ −0.10, 0.20], [ 0.10, −0.20]] → x₁ = 2x₂ Eigenvector: v₁ = (2, 1)ᵀ
  • InterpretIn the long run, the population splits in ratio 2:1, Downtown to Uptown. With total population 14 000, the eventual split is Downtown ≈ 9333, Uptown ≈ 4667. This equilibrium is reached from any starting distribution, because |λ₂| < 1 causes all deviations to decay away.
Answer: Eigenvalues 1 and 0.7. Long-term distribution: Downtown/Uptown ratio = 2:1.
Section 13.12

Applications in Population, Networks, and Finance

Population Models — Leslie Matrices

In ecology, a Leslie matrix models a population divided into age groups. The dominant eigenvalue of A determines the long-term fate of the population: if |λ₁| > 1, the population grows; if |λ₁| = 1, it stabilizes; if |λ₁| < 1, the population declines toward extinction. This is why conservation biologists care about eigenvalues — they directly reveal whether a managed species will survive.

Network Models — PageRank

Search engines use a form of Markov chain to rank web pages. A user randomly following links is modeled as a random walk through a network. The steady-state distribution of this Markov chain gives each page's importance score — called PageRank. The transition matrix encodes the link structure: entry a_{ij} is the probability of moving from page j to page i. The steady-state eigenvector (for λ = 1) gives the ranking. This is the same steady-state analysis from Section 13.10, applied at enormous scale.

Example 13.12.1
A two-sector economic model
  • ProblemAn economy has manufacturing (M) and services (S) sectors with transition matrix A = [[1.2, 0.3], [0.1, 1.1]]. Analyze long-term behavior.
  • Eigenvalues det(A − λI) = λ² − 2.3λ + 1.29 = 0 λ = (2.3 ± √(5.29 − 5.16))/2 = (2.3 ± √0.13)/2 λ₁ ≈ (2.3 + 0.361)/2 ≈ 1.330 λ₂ ≈ (2.3 − 0.361)/2 ≈ 0.970
  • Interpretλ₁ ≈ 1.330 > 1: the economy grows at approximately 33% per step in the dominant direction. Since λ₂ < 1, the other component decays. In the long run, the ratio of manufacturing to services output approaches the eigenvector for λ₁.
Answer: Dominant eigenvalue ≈ 1.330 → economy grows. Long-term direction given by eigenvector for λ₁.

Section 13.13

Common Diagonalization Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Inverting P in the Wrong Position
A = P⁻¹DP

Correct form: A = PDP⁻¹, where columns of P are eigenvectors. Verify by checking AP = PD: the jth column of AP is Avⱼ = λⱼvⱼ, which equals the jth column of PD.

Mistake 2 — Misaligning Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
P columns and D diagonal entries in different orders

The eigenvalue in position (j,j) of D must correspond to the eigenvector in column j of P. Mixing this correspondence produces an incorrect decomposition. Always check AP = PD column by column.

Mistake 3 — Assuming Every Matrix Is Diagonalizable
"I found the eigenvalues, so I can diagonalize A."

Finding eigenvalues is only the first step. You must check that the sum of all eigenspace dimensions equals n. A single defective eigenvalue (geometric multiplicity < algebraic multiplicity) makes the whole matrix non-diagonalizable.

Mistake 4 — Forgetting to Normalize Markov Steady-State Vectors
Reporting q = (2, 3)ᵀ as a steady-state probability distribution

The eigenvector (2, 3)ᵀ gives the correct direction, but it is not a valid probability vector. Normalize: q = (2/5, 3/5)ᵀ so entries sum to 1 and each entry represents an actual probability.

Mistake 5 — Using Ak = PDkP⁻¹ When A Is Not Diagonalizable
Computing Ak = PDkP⁻¹ for a defective matrix

This formula requires A to be diagonalizable. If A is defective, there is no valid decomposition A = PDP⁻¹, and the formula does not apply. For non-diagonalizable matrices, Jordan normal form is needed (beyond this course).

Mistake 6 — Ignoring the Initial Condition
"The dominant eigenvalue governs everything" — regardless of x₀

The formula x_k = c₁λ₁ᵏv₁ + ⋯ depends on the coefficients c₁, c₂, … from expressing x₀ in the eigenvector basis. If c₁ = 0 (the initial state has no component in the dominant direction), then the dominant eigenvalue does not control the long-term behavior.

Mistake 7 — Treating λ = −1 as a Steady State
"The eigenvalue has |λ| = 1, so the system is at steady state."

An eigenvalue λ = −1 has magnitude 1 but produces alternating signs: (−1)ᵏ = +1, −1, +1, −1, … Only λ = +1 produces a true steady state in a real system. Always check the sign, not just the magnitude.

Mistake 8 — Confusing Similar Matrices with Equal Matrices
Substituting D in place of A in a formula that depends on A's actual entries

Similar matrices A and D = P⁻¹AP have the same eigenvalues, determinant, and rank, but different entries. They cannot be substituted for each other in calculations that use the actual matrix values — only in those that depend on these shared structural invariants.

Section 13.14

Preparing for Calculus, Differential Equations, and Advanced Linear Algebra

The Bridge from Discrete to Continuous

This chapter focused on discrete dynamical systems, where the state changes in countable steps. The formula x_k = Akx₀ and the role of eigenvalues in long-term behavior are the discrete analogs of deeper ideas in calculus and differential equations.

In Differential Equations, the discrete system x_{k+1} = Ax_k becomes the continuous system dx/dt = Ax. The solution involves matrix exponentials, and the eigenvalues of A still control long-term behavior — now through expressions of the form e^{λt} instead of λk. The conceptual structure is identical; only the tools differ. By mastering the discrete version, you have built the intuition you will need for the continuous case.

Principal Component Analysis — A Preview

In statistics and machine learning, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) finds the eigenvectors of a covariance matrix to identify the most important directions of variation in a data set. These eigenvectors — called principal components — form a new coordinate system aligned with the data. The eigenvalues measure how much variation occurs in each direction.

Diagonalization underlies PCA entirely. Students who understand eigenvalues and diagonalization are ready to understand one of the most widely used tools in data science and machine learning.

The Spectral Theorem — A Preview

For symmetric matrices — those satisfying A = Aᵀ — the diagonalization has a special form. The eigenvectors can be chosen to be orthogonal and normalized, and the diagonalization becomes A = QDQᵀ where Q is an orthogonal matrix. This is the Spectral Theorem, the foundation for PCA, the Singular Value Decomposition, and many other matrix factorizations. Students who complete this chapter are prepared to study the Spectral Theorem in a future course.

Calculus Readiness — What You Have Built

This chapter completes Part IV. You now understand how eigenvalues and eigenvectors reveal the structure of repeated transformations, how to compute matrix powers efficiently, how to analyze systems that evolve step by step, and how to determine whether those systems grow, decay, or stabilize in the long run.

These ideas — dominant eigenvalue, decomposition of initial conditions, long-term direction — reappear in every course that follows: Calculus (tangent approximation, directional derivatives), Multivariable Calculus (Jacobians, Hessians), Differential Equations (solution of systems), Statistics (regression, PCA), Data Science (dimensionality reduction), and Machine Learning (gradient descent, neural networks, SVD).

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1The core insight: on an eigenvector, Aᵏv = λᵏv. This makes repeated matrix multiplication tractable when starting vectors are expressed in terms of eigenvectors.
  • Sec 13.2Similar matrices A and B = P⁻¹AP represent the same transformation in different coordinate systems. They share eigenvalues, determinant, rank, and trace. Diagonalization is the case where B = D is diagonal.
  • Sec 13.3For diagonal D = diag(d₁, …, d_n), the power Dᵏ = diag(d₁ᵏ, …, d_nᵏ). Entries with |d_i| > 1 grow; |d_i| < 1 shrink; |d_i| = 1 with d_i = 1 stabilize.
  • Sec 13.4To diagonalize A: find eigenvalues, find eigenvectors, assemble P (eigenvectors as columns) and D (eigenvalues on diagonal, matching order). Verify with AP = PD.
  • Sec 13.5A matrix is diagonalizable iff it has n independent eigenvectors, equivalently iff geometric multiplicity = algebraic multiplicity for every eigenvalue. n distinct eigenvalues always guarantee diagonalizability.
  • Sec 13.6–7If A = PDP⁻¹, then Aᵏ = PDᵏP⁻¹. Computing Aᵏ reduces to raising eigenvalues to the kth power. The general solution is x_k = c₁λ₁ᵏv₁ + ⋯ + c_nλ_nᵏv_n.
  • Sec 13.8A discrete dynamical system: x_{k+1} = Ax_k, giving x_k = Aᵏx₀. The dominant eigenvalue (largest |λ|) controls long-term direction. The coefficients come from expressing x₀ in the eigenvector basis.
  • Sec 13.9Second-order recurrences become matrix systems via the companion matrix. Diagonalizing the companion matrix yields closed-form solutions. The Fibonacci sequence has growth rate φ = (1+√5)/2, the dominant eigenvalue of its companion matrix.
  • Sec 13.10Markov chains: state vectors are probability distributions; transition matrices are stochastic (columns sum to 1). The steady-state vector is the eigenvector for λ = 1, normalized to sum to 1.
  • Sec 13.11Long-term behavior: if |λ₁| > 1, state grows; if λ₁ = +1 with all others |λ| < 1, state converges to steady state; if all |λ| < 1, state decays to 0.
  • Sec 13.12Applications include Leslie population matrices (dominant eigenvalue determines survival), PageRank (steady-state of a web-link Markov chain), and economic growth models (dominant eigenvalue gives long-term growth rate).
  • Sec 13.13Eight common errors: wrong inverse position, misaligned P and D, assuming all matrices are diagonalizable, unnormalized Markov vectors, using the formula on non-diagonalizable matrices, ignoring initial conditions, treating λ=−1 as a steady state, and confusing similar with equal.
  • Sec 13.14Discrete systems are the foundation for continuous differential equations. Diagonalization underlies PCA, the Spectral Theorem, and the SVD. These ideas are central to calculus, differential equations, statistics, data science, and machine learning.

Key Terms Review

algebraic multiplicity
The number of times an eigenvalue appears as a root of the characteristic polynomial.
companion matrix
A matrix encoding a linear recurrence relation as a matrix dynamical system; has the same characteristic polynomial as the recurrence.
defective matrix
A matrix that is not diagonalizable because the geometric multiplicity of some eigenvalue is strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity.
diagonal matrix
A square matrix with all zero entries off the main diagonal. Powers, inverses, and products are trivial to compute.
diagonalizable matrix
A matrix that can be written as A = PDP⁻¹ with D diagonal. Requires n linearly independent eigenvectors.
diagonalization
The process of writing A = PDP⁻¹ where columns of P are eigenvectors and diagonal entries of D are corresponding eigenvalues.
discrete dynamical system
A model in which a state vector evolves step by step via x_{k+1} = Ax_k, giving x_k = Aᵏx₀.
dominant eigenvalue
The eigenvalue with the largest absolute value. Determines the long-term direction and growth rate of a dynamical system.
eigenvalue decomposition
Another name for diagonalization: A = PDP⁻¹. Reveals how A acts as simple scaling in each eigenvector direction.
geometric multiplicity
The dimension of the eigenspace for an eigenvalue; the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for that eigenvalue.
Markov chain
A discrete dynamical system with a stochastic transition matrix, modeling how a probability distribution evolves over time.
recurrence relation
A formula defining each term of a sequence in terms of earlier terms. Can be converted to matrix form via the companion matrix.
similar matrices
Matrices A and B related by B = P⁻¹AP for some invertible P. They share eigenvalues, determinant, rank, and trace.
state vector
A vector whose entries describe the current state of a dynamical system at a given step.
steady-state vector
The long-term stable distribution in a Markov chain; the eigenvector for eigenvalue 1, normalized to sum to 1.
stochastic matrix
A matrix with non-negative entries whose columns all sum to 1. Used as the transition matrix in a Markov chain.
transition matrix
The matrix A in x_{k+1} = Ax_k, describing how the state evolves from one step to the next.
long-term behavior
The eventual pattern of x_k as k → ∞. Determined by the dominant eigenvalue and its corresponding eigenvector.
golden ratio φ
φ = (1+√5)/2 ≈ 1.618. The dominant eigenvalue of the Fibonacci companion matrix; governs the long-term growth rate of Fibonacci numbers.

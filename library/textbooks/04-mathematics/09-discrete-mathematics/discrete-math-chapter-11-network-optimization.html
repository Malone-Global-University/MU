Chapter 11 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 10 introduced the language of graph theory: vertices, edges, degree, paths, cycles, connectedness, and representation. This chapter builds on that foundation to study one of the most important and widely applied families of graphs — trees — and to develop algorithms that solve real optimization problems on networks.

Trees appear throughout computing and mathematics: in file systems, search procedures, decision processes, syntax parsers, databases, and compression algorithms. Spanning trees connect networks efficiently. Shortest-path algorithms route messages, traffic, and data. Euler and Hamilton paths model traversal problems that arise in logistics, planning, and circuit design. And planar graphs describe networks that can be drawn without crossing wires or roads.

This chapter does not require calculus. It builds on graph vocabulary from Chapter 10, on logical reasoning from earlier chapters, and on the counting and algorithmic thinking introduced throughout the book. The optimization in this chapter is discrete: it proceeds by examining finite choices, comparing weights or lengths, and following systematic procedures.

Opening Question

A network engineer needs to connect eight server buildings using fiber-optic cable. She has surveyed possible cable routes between pairs of buildings and recorded the cost of each route. She wants to connect all eight buildings so that data can travel between any two of them — but she wants to use as few total meters of cable as possible. She cannot afford to run redundant lines. How should she decide which routes to build? This is a minimum spanning tree problem. By the end of this chapter, you will know exactly how to solve it, why the solution is correct, and how the same mathematical structure appears throughout engineering, logistics, data science, and computing.

Why This Chapter Matters

Graph theory, as you learned in Chapter 10, gives mathematics a language for describing relationships. But relationships in the real world usually come with practical demands: What is the cheapest connection? What is the fastest route? Can this network be traversed without repeating an edge? Can all locations be visited without backtracking?

These are not abstract questions. They are asked every day by network engineers, delivery companies, chip designers, airline schedulers, and database architects. The mathematical tools developed in this chapter — trees, spanning trees, shortest-path algorithms, Euler and Hamilton paths — are the tools used to answer them.

Studying trees and network algorithms also reinforces a core principle of discrete mathematics: structure matters. A tree is not just a graph without cycles. It is a graph with a precise, useful structure that makes certain problems efficient to solve. Understanding that structure is what allows mathematics to support real-world decisions.

Section 11.1

What a Tree Is

The word "tree" in everyday language suggests something that branches — a structure that spreads outward from a base without forming loops. The mathematical definition of a tree captures exactly this idea.

Definition

A tree is a connected graph that contains no cycles.

Every definition in mathematics carries weight. Let us take this one apart.

A tree must be connected: there must be a path between every pair of vertices. A tree must have no cycles: there must be no closed loop of distinct vertices and edges. Both conditions are required. A graph with no cycles that is not connected is called a forest (because it looks like several unconnected trees). A graph that is connected but contains cycles is not a tree.

Why These Two Conditions Together?

Connectivity says: the tree holds together. No vertex is isolated or unreachable.

Acyclicity says: the tree has no redundant edges. Every edge is the only connection between some part of the tree and the rest. Remove any edge, and the tree becomes disconnected.

This "no redundancy" property is one of the most important properties of trees. It means that trees are lean: they use the minimum number of edges needed to stay connected.

The Edge-Count Property

If a tree has \(n\) vertices, how many edges does it have? The answer is always exactly \(n - 1\).

Why? Think about building a tree starting from scratch. You begin with \(n\) isolated vertices — no edges, no connections. Each edge you add either connects a new vertex to the growing tree, or creates a cycle. Since a tree has no cycles, every edge must connect a new vertex. To connect all \(n\) vertices, you need exactly \(n - 1\) edges.

Three Equivalent Characterizations of a Tree

A connected graph with \(n\) vertices and \(n - 1\) edges.

A connected graph with no cycles.

A graph in which every pair of vertices is connected by exactly one simple path.

These three characterizations describe the same family of graphs. If any one of them is satisfied and the graph is connected, the graph is a tree.

Leaves

A leaf of a tree is a vertex with degree 1 — a vertex connected by exactly one edge. Leaves are the endpoints of a tree. In a tree with three or more vertices, there are always at least two leaves.

Every tree (except a single edge with two vertices) has interior vertices and leaf vertices. The structural role of leaves and interior vertices becomes clearer when we study rooted trees.

Student Warning

The word "tree" in graph theory has a precise meaning. It does not mean any branching picture. A path on five vertices is technically a tree. A star graph — one central vertex connected to many outer vertices — is technically a tree. The definition is about connectivity and acyclicity, not visual appearance.

Section 11.2

Rooted Trees

An unrooted tree is simply a connected acyclic graph, with no vertex given special status. A rooted tree is a tree in which one vertex has been designated the root.

Choosing a root gives the tree a direction and a hierarchy. Every other vertex can be described in terms of its relationship to the root.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a tree with vertices A, B, C, D, E, F, and G. Connect A to B and A to C. Connect B to D and B to E. Connect C to F and C to G. Designate A as the root. Draw A at the top, B and C below it on the second level, and D, E, F, G on the third level at the bottom.

Vocabulary of Rooted Trees

Once a root is chosen, the standard vocabulary of rooted trees applies:

Rooted Tree Vocabulary

The root is the topmost vertex. It has no parent.

The children of a vertex \(v\) are the vertices directly connected to \(v\) that are one level below \(v\).

The parent of a vertex \(v\) is the vertex directly above \(v\) on the path from \(v\) to the root.

Siblings are vertices that share the same parent.

A leaf in a rooted tree is a vertex with no children.

An internal vertex (or internal node) is a vertex with at least one child.

The depth of a vertex is the length of the path from the root to that vertex. The root has depth 0.

The height of the tree is the maximum depth of any vertex in the tree.

Why Rooted Trees Matter

Rooted trees are one of the most fundamental structures in computer science. A file system is a rooted tree: the root directory contains folders, each folder may contain subfolders or files, and the hierarchy never loops back.

A parse tree in a programming language or a compiler is a rooted tree that represents the structure of a program. A decision tree in machine learning is a rooted tree in which each internal node represents a test and each leaf represents a conclusion. A binary search tree in data structures is a rooted tree used to store and retrieve information efficiently.

Section 11.3

Binary Trees as an Introduction

A binary tree is a rooted tree in which every vertex has at most two children. When a vertex has two children, they are called the left child and the right child.

Binary trees appear in sorting algorithms, compression algorithms, expression evaluation, and database indexing. They are simple to define but powerful in application.

Diagram Instruction

Draw a rooted binary tree with root P. P has left child Q and right child R. Q has left child S and right child T. R has left child U and no right child. S, T, and U are leaves.

Full and Complete Binary Trees

Two special types of binary trees appear frequently:

Key Definitions

A full binary tree is one in which every internal vertex has exactly two children (never one).

A complete binary tree is one in which all levels except possibly the last are fully filled, and all leaves on the last level are as far left as possible.

These definitions support analysis of algorithms that use binary trees. For example, in a full binary tree with \(k\) internal vertices, the total number of vertices is \(2k + 1\) (each internal vertex contributes two children, plus the root). This kind of structural count often appears in algorithm analysis.

Counting Leaves in Full Binary Trees

Claim: In a full binary tree, the number of leaves equals the number of internal vertices plus one.

This can be proved by induction (see Chapter 5 for the induction technique). The key idea is: each time you add an internal vertex to a full binary tree, you are converting a leaf into an internal vertex and adding two new leaves — a net gain of one leaf. Starting with one vertex (one leaf, zero internal vertices), each internal vertex added increases the leaf count by one.

Section 11.4

Spanning Trees

Return now to connected graphs that may contain cycles. Trees are maximally lean. But what if you have a graph that is more complex — one with extra edges creating cycles — and you want to find the leanest connected subgraph hidden inside it?

Definition

A spanning tree of a connected graph \(G\) is a subgraph of \(G\) that includes all the vertices of \(G\) and forms a tree.

In other words, a spanning tree keeps all the vertices but removes edges until no cycles remain and the graph is still connected.

Every connected graph has at least one spanning tree.

To find one, start with the entire graph. If there is a cycle, remove one edge from that cycle — the graph remains connected, because the other edges of the cycle still provide an alternate path. Repeat until no cycles remain. What is left is a spanning tree.

Example 11.4.1
Finding a Spanning Tree
  • ProblemThe graph \(G\) has vertices A, B, C, D, E with edges AB, BC, CD, DE, AC, and BE. Find a spanning tree.
  • ThinkThe graph has 5 vertices. A spanning tree on 5 vertices must have exactly 4 edges. The graph has 6 edges, so we must remove exactly 2. We must not disconnect the graph.
  • Solve Step 1: Check for cycles. The edges AB, BC, CA form a triangle (a cycle). Remove edge AC. The graph is still connected because A can reach C via A–B–C.

    Step 2: Check again. The edges BE and the path B–C–D–E form a cycle (BE, ED, DC, CB). Remove edge BE. Still connected: B can reach E via B–C–D–E.

    Step 3: Verify. Remaining edges: AB, BC, CD, DE. This is four edges on five vertices. Check connectivity: A–B–C–D–E is a single path connecting all vertices. Check acyclicity: the remaining graph is a path, which has no cycles.
  • Check4 edges on 5 vertices, connected, no cycles — satisfies all three characterizations of a tree from Section 11.1.
Answer: One spanning tree consists of the edges AB, BC, CD, DE. This is the simple path A–B–C–D–E.

How Many Spanning Trees Can a Graph Have?

A connected graph with many edges may have many different spanning trees. Each spanning tree is a different subset of edges that connects all vertices without cycles. Choosing which spanning tree to use depends on the criteria — if edges have weights (costs, distances, times), we usually want the minimum spanning tree.

Section 11.5

Minimum Spanning Trees

In many real-world problems, edges have weights. A weight might represent:

  • The cost to lay cable between two locations.
  • The distance between two cities.
  • The time to transmit data between two servers.
  • The construction cost of a road between two neighborhoods.

In a weighted graph, every edge \(e\) has an associated number \(w(e)\), its weight.

Definition

A minimum spanning tree (MST) of a connected weighted graph is a spanning tree whose total edge weight is as small as possible.

The minimum spanning tree answers the question: what is the cheapest way to connect all the vertices?

Important

There may be more than one spanning tree with the same minimum total weight (especially if multiple edges have equal weights). In this case, any of them qualifies as a minimum spanning tree.

Why Greedy Algorithms Work for MSTs

The algorithms for finding minimum spanning trees (Kruskal's and Prim's, developed in the next two sections) are both greedy algorithms: at each step, they make the locally best choice available.

For many optimization problems, greedy choices do not lead to global optima. But for minimum spanning trees, the structure of trees guarantees that greedy approaches work — no locally good edge selection leads to a globally worse tree. This can be proved rigorously; the key property is called the cut property, which is typically studied in an advanced algorithms course.

Section 11.6

Kruskal's Algorithm

Kruskal's algorithm finds a minimum spanning tree by building the tree one edge at a time, always selecting the cheapest available edge that does not create a cycle.

The Algorithm

Input: A connected weighted graph \(G\) with \(n\) vertices.

Output: A minimum spanning tree \(T\).

Steps:

1. Sort all edges of \(G\) in non-decreasing order of weight.

2. Initialize \(T\) as an empty set of edges (and all \(n\) vertices).

3. For each edge \(e\) in the sorted order: if adding \(e\) to \(T\) does not create a cycle, add \(e\) to \(T\); if it would create a cycle, skip \(e\).

4. Stop when \(T\) contains \(n - 1\) edges (or all edges have been examined).

5. Return \(T\).

Pseudocode — KRUSKAL
KRUSKAL(G):
  Sort edges of G by weight (ascending)
  T = empty spanning tree (all vertices, no edges)
  For each edge e = (u, v) in sorted order:
    If u and v are in different components of T:
      Add e to T
  Return T

Checking whether two vertices are "in different components" is the cycle check. If \(u\) and \(v\) are already connected by a path in \(T\), adding edge \((u, v)\) would close a cycle.

Why Kruskal's Algorithm Works

Kruskal's algorithm always produces a minimum spanning tree because of the cycle property: if the most expensive edge in any cycle is unique, then it cannot belong to any minimum spanning tree. By always choosing the cheapest available edge and skipping edges that create cycles, Kruskal's algorithm avoids selecting any edge that the cycle property rules out.

A complete proof requires more machinery, but the intuition is sound: adding the cheapest available edge can never hurt, because any MST that avoids this edge can be modified to include it (at no extra cost) while remaining minimal.

Section 11.7

Prim's Algorithm

Prim's algorithm finds a minimum spanning tree by growing the tree one vertex at a time, always adding the cheapest edge that connects a new vertex to the current tree.

The Algorithm

Input: A connected weighted graph \(G\) with \(n\) vertices and a starting vertex \(s\).

Output: A minimum spanning tree \(T\).

Steps:

1. Initialize \(T\) to contain only the starting vertex \(s\).

2. Repeat until \(T\) contains all \(n\) vertices: find the minimum-weight edge that connects a vertex in \(T\) to a vertex not yet in \(T\); add that edge and its new vertex to \(T\).

3. Return \(T\).

Pseudocode — PRIM
PRIM(G, s):
  T = {s}   (tree starts with only vertex s)
  While T does not include all vertices:
    Find the minimum-weight edge (u, v) where u is in T and v is not in T
    Add edge (u, v) and vertex v to T
  Return T

Answer: The minimum spanning tree has edges AB, BC, CE, BD with total weight 11.

Kruskal's vs. Prim's

Both algorithms always produce a minimum spanning tree. They differ in how they build it:

Kruskal's works globally — it sorts all edges first, then greedily adds the cheapest edge that does not create a cycle.

Prim's works locally — it grows the tree from a single starting vertex, always choosing the cheapest edge that connects the growing tree to a new vertex.

For small graphs, both are equally practical. For very large graphs, the efficiency of implementation depends on the data structures used. Both algorithms run in polynomial time — a topic studied in Chapter 12.

Section 11.8

Shortest Path Problems

The minimum spanning tree problem asks: how do we connect all vertices cheaply? A different question is equally important: what is the cheapest path from one specific vertex to another?

This is the shortest path problem. In a weighted graph, the shortest path from vertex \(s\) to vertex \(t\) is the path whose total edge weight is minimized.

Note: "shortest" refers to minimum weight, not minimum number of edges. In an unweighted graph (or a graph where all weights are equal), the shortest path by weight is the same as the shortest path by edge count. In a weighted graph, these can differ.

Applications

  • GPS navigation: Find the fastest route from current location to destination.
  • Internet routing: Find the lowest-latency path for a data packet.
  • Supply chain management: Find the cheapest sequence of shipping steps.
  • Flight booking: Find the route with the lowest total cost.

Setting Up a Shortest Path Problem

Given a weighted graph \(G\) and a starting vertex \(s\), the single-source shortest path problem asks for the shortest path from \(s\) to every other vertex.

We represent the shortest known distance from \(s\) to each vertex in a distance table. Initially, the distance to \(s\) is 0, and the distance to every other vertex is unknown (often written as \(\infty\)).

As we explore the graph, we update (or relax) distance estimates whenever we find a shorter route.

Relaxation Rule

If the current shortest known distance from \(s\) to vertex \(u\) is \(d(u)\), and the edge \((u, v)\) has weight \(w\), then the distance estimate for \(v\) can be improved to \(d(u) + w\) if \(d(u) + w < d(v)\).

Dijkstra's Algorithm as an Introduction

Dijkstra's algorithm (named for computer scientist Edsger Dijkstra, who developed it in 1956) solves the single-source shortest path problem on graphs with non-negative edge weights.

Important

Dijkstra's algorithm requires that all edge weights be non-negative. Negative edge weights require a different algorithm (Bellman-Ford), which is beyond the scope of this chapter.

The Algorithm

Input: A connected weighted graph \(G\) with non-negative edge weights and a source vertex \(s\).

Output: The shortest distance from \(s\) to every other vertex.

Steps:

1. Set \(d(s) = 0\) and \(d(v) = \infty\) for all other vertices \(v\).

2. Mark all vertices as unvisited.

3. While there are unvisited vertices: (a) select the unvisited vertex \(u\) with the smallest \(d(u)\); (b) mark \(u\) as visited; (c) for each unvisited neighbor \(v\) of \(u\), if \(d(u) + w(u,v) < d(v)\), update \(d(v) = d(u) + w(u,v)\).

4. Return all distance values.

Pseudocode — DIJKSTRA
DIJKSTRA(G, s):
  For each vertex v in G:
    d(v) = infinity
    visited(v) = false
  d(s) = 0

  While there are unvisited vertices:
    u = unvisited vertex with smallest d(u)
    visited(u) = true
    For each neighbor v of u where visited(v) = false:
      If d(u) + weight(u,v) < d(v):
        d(v) = d(u) + weight(u,v)

  Return d

At each step, Dijkstra's algorithm selects the unvisited vertex with the smallest known distance. This is the key insight that makes the greedy selection correct.

If edge weights could be negative, this reasoning would fail, because a later path through a negative edge might reduce an already-finalized distance.

Euler Paths and Circuits

The question of Euler paths and circuits is one of the oldest problems in graph theory, famously posed and solved by Leonhard Euler in 1736 through the Königsberg Bridge Problem.

The problem: the city of Königsberg had seven bridges connecting its land masses and islands. Could a walker traverse every bridge exactly once and return to the starting point? Euler proved that no such walk exists — and his reasoning established the foundations of graph theory.

Definitions

Euler path: A path in a graph that visits every edge exactly once. The path does not need to return to its starting vertex.

Euler circuit: A closed path (circuit) in a graph that visits every edge exactly once and returns to the starting vertex.

Note: Euler paths and circuits are about traversing every edge exactly once. This is different from paths about vertices — that distinction becomes important when we discuss Hamilton paths in Section 11.11.

The Euler Conditions

Euler's great discovery was a precise condition characterizing when Euler paths and circuits exist. The condition depends entirely on vertex degrees.

Euler's Theorems

Euler Circuit Theorem: A connected graph has an Euler circuit if and only if every vertex has even degree.

Euler Path Theorem: A connected graph has an Euler path (but not necessarily a circuit) if and only if it has exactly two vertices of odd degree. The Euler path begins at one odd-degree vertex and ends at the other.

Why does degree matter? Think about traversing edges at a vertex. Every time the path enters a vertex, it must also leave (unless the vertex is a starting or ending point). If every vertex has even degree, the path can always leave every vertex it enters, enabling it to return to the start. If exactly two vertices have odd degree, those two must serve as the start and end — the path can enter without being able to leave (at the end) or leave without having entered (at the start).

Finding an Euler Circuit or Path

When an Euler circuit exists (all even degrees), a systematic procedure for finding it is Hierholzer's algorithm: start at any vertex, follow edges until you return to the start (forming a circuit), then iteratively extend the circuit by following unused edges from any vertex in the current circuit that still has unused edges. Continue until all edges are used.

The full procedure is described in advanced graph theory courses, but the key insight is: once you know the Euler conditions are met, you know the circuit exists and can be found.

Section 11.11

Hamilton Paths and Circuits

A natural variation on the Euler question changes "traverse every edge exactly once" to "visit every vertex exactly once."

Definitions

Hamilton path: A path in a graph that visits every vertex exactly once. The path does not need to return to the starting vertex.

Hamilton circuit: A closed path (circuit) that visits every vertex exactly once and returns to the starting vertex.

These are named after the mathematician William Rowan Hamilton, who studied related puzzles in the 1850s.

The Critical Difference from Euler

At first glance, Hamilton paths and Euler paths sound similar — both ask about traversing a graph "completely." But they are fundamentally different in terms of difficulty:

Euler paths have a simple, complete characterization: check vertex degrees. The Euler conditions give an exact yes/no answer in any graph.

Hamilton paths have no known simple characterization. No one has found a general condition that tells you, just by looking at local properties of a graph, whether a Hamilton path exists.

Determining whether a Hamilton circuit exists in a general graph is one of the famous NP-complete problems in computer science. This means that no efficient algorithm is known that solves it for all graphs (though efficient algorithms might exist for special cases or may be discovered in the future). The problem of finding an optimal Hamilton circuit — visiting all vertices at minimum total cost — is the famous Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP).

Sufficient Conditions

While no simple necessary and sufficient condition exists for Hamilton circuits, a few sufficient conditions are known:

Sufficient Conditions for Hamilton Circuits

Dirac's Theorem: If a graph has \(n \geq 3\) vertices and every vertex has degree at least \(n/2\), then the graph has a Hamilton circuit.

Ore's Theorem: If a graph has \(n \geq 3\) vertices and for every pair of non-adjacent vertices \(u\) and \(v\), \(\deg(u) + \deg(v) \geq n\), then the graph has a Hamilton circuit.

These are sufficient conditions, not necessary ones. A graph can have a Hamilton circuit even if neither condition is met. And a graph can fail both conditions and still have a Hamilton circuit. The conditions are tools to identify some graphs that definitely have one.

Student Note

The asymmetry between Euler and Hamilton problems is striking. Euler paths have an elegant, complete solution through degree analysis. Hamilton paths are theoretically hard — no degree-based shortcut exists in general. This is one of the places where discrete mathematics touches deep open questions in theoretical computer science.

Example 11.11.1

  • ProblemA graph has 6 vertices, each with degree 3. Determine: (a) Does an Euler circuit exist? (b) Does Dirac's theorem guarantee a Hamilton circuit?

The Traveling Salesman Problem

The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) asks: given a complete weighted graph (every pair of vertices is connected), find the Hamilton circuit with minimum total weight.

This models situations like: a delivery driver who must visit a set of locations and return to the starting point, minimizing total distance. Or a manufacturing robot that must visit a set of points on a circuit board and return to start, minimizing time.

For small numbers of vertices, the TSP can be solved by listing all Hamilton circuits and comparing their total weights. But the number of Hamilton circuits in a complete graph on \(n\) vertices grows as \((n-1)!/2\) — which grows faster than any polynomial. For large \(n\), this brute-force approach is completely impractical.

Approximation algorithms and heuristic methods (including some based on minimum spanning trees) can find Hamilton circuits that are guaranteed to be within a certain factor of the true optimum. These algorithms are studied in advanced courses on algorithms and theoretical computer science.

Section 11.12

Planar Graphs as an Introduction

Definition

A planar graph is a graph that can be drawn in the plane so that no two edges cross (except at shared endpoints).

Note: the definition says "can be drawn" — it does not say the graph must be drawn without crossings. A graph might look like it has crossings in one drawing but still be planar if a different arrangement of vertices and edges is crossing-free.

Why Planarity Matters

Planar graphs appear naturally in applications where crossing is not allowed or not possible:

  • Circuit board design: Wires on a circuit board cannot cross without creating unintended connections. A circuit is planar if its connections can be laid out on one layer without crossings.
  • Road maps: Roads do not typically cross without a bridge or overpass. Graphs of flat road networks are often planar.
  • Geographic maps: The dual of a planar map is also a planar graph, connecting regions that share a border.

Euler's Formula for Planar Graphs

When a connected planar graph is drawn without crossings, it divides the plane into regions called faces. The outer, unbounded region also counts as a face.

Euler's Formula

For any connected planar graph with \(V\) vertices, \(E\) edges, and \(F\) faces:

$$V - E + F = 2$$

This remarkable formula says that no matter how you draw a connected planar graph, the combination \(V - E + F\) always equals 2. This is one of the most elegant results in discrete mathematics.

Non-Planar Graphs

Not every graph is planar. Two important non-planar graphs are:

  • \(K_5\) (the complete graph on 5 vertices): It has 10 edges. If it were planar, Euler's formula and additional constraints would require a minimum of 10 edges — but more detailed analysis shows \(K_5\) cannot be embedded planarly.
  • \(K_{3,3}\) (the complete bipartite graph on 3+3 vertices): It cannot be drawn without crossings.

Kuratowski's Theorem (stated as an introduction): A graph is planar if and only if it contains no subdivision of \(K_5\) or \(K_{3,3}\). A subdivision of a graph is obtained by inserting additional vertices along its edges. This theorem gives a complete characterization of planarity, but its proof is advanced.

Student Note

You are not expected to use Kuratowski's Theorem to determine planarity in this chapter. The key takeaway is: planarity is a property of the graph itself, not of any particular drawing; Euler's formula applies to planar graphs; and \(K_5\) and \(K_{3,3}\) are the two fundamental non-planar graphs.

Section 11.13

Network Optimization in Transportation and Computing

The topics of this chapter — trees, spanning trees, shortest paths, Euler circuits, Hamilton circuits, planarity — come together in a broad area called network optimization. This section surveys how these ideas are applied in real systems.

Transportation Networks

Road and rail networks are naturally modeled as graphs. Vertices represent cities, intersections, or stations. Edges represent roads or rail lines, usually weighted by distance or travel time.

  • Shortest path algorithms like Dijkstra's find optimal routes.
  • Minimum spanning trees find cheap ways to connect a new region to an existing rail network.
  • Hamilton circuits approximate solutions for delivery routing (Traveling Salesman Problem).

Airline route planning uses similar tools. A graph of airports and routes can be analyzed for redundancy (are all airports reachable if one route is closed?), efficiency (shortest connection between cities), and cost optimization.

Communication and Data Networks

The Internet is a massive graph. Routers are vertices; network links are edges with weights representing latency, bandwidth, or cost. Routing protocols (like OSPF and BGP) implement shortest-path and spanning-tree algorithms on this graph.

Spanning trees prevent broadcast storms in network switching: a switched network uses a spanning tree (implemented by the Spanning Tree Protocol) to ensure that data frames travel without looping.

Minimum spanning trees are used when building new network infrastructure — connecting data centers, fiber-optic lines, or wireless access points at minimum cost.

Scheduling and Assignment Problems

Many scheduling problems can be modeled as graph problems. Bipartite graphs (studied in Chapter 10) model matching problems: assigning students to project teams, matching job applicants to positions, or pairing items with slots. Finding a maximum matching in a bipartite graph is an important optimization problem.

Chromatic scheduling (also related to graph coloring, a topic in advanced graph theory) assigns resources to tasks so that no two conflicting tasks share a resource — just as no two adjacent vertices in a graph share a color.

Logistics and Distribution

Euler circuit algorithms are used in route planning for:

  • Mail and package delivery (traverse every street exactly once).
  • Snow plowing and street cleaning (traverse every road without unnecessary repetition).
  • Meter reading (visit every connection point exactly once).

When all edge degrees are even, the Euler circuit solves the problem exactly. When some vertices have odd degree, the Chinese Postman Problem finds the minimum-weight traversal by duplicating some edges — a solvable variant studied in operations research.

Artificial Intelligence and Search

Tree structures are fundamental to AI search:

  • Breadth-first search and depth-first search traverse trees and graphs systematically.
  • A* search uses a form of Dijkstra's algorithm with a heuristic estimate of remaining distance.
  • Game trees represent possible future states in games like chess or Go, branching from each decision point.
  • Decision trees in machine learning branch at each feature to classify data points.

Section 11.14

Common Tree and Network Mistakes

This section identifies the most frequent errors students make when working with trees, spanning trees, shortest paths, and Euler and Hamilton problems.

Mistake 1 — Confusing Trees with Arbitrary Connected Graphs
"This graph is connected and branches outward, so it must be a tree." Correct: A tree is a connected acyclic graph. A connected graph with cycles is not a tree. Always verify: count edges. A tree on n vertices has exactly n − 1 edges. If a connected graph has n or more edges, it has a cycle and is not a tree.
Mistake 2 — Thinking Every Connected Graph Has a Unique Spanning Tree
"The spanning tree of this graph is..." Correct: Most connected graphs have many spanning trees. The minimum spanning tree minimizes total edge weight, but there may be multiple spanning trees with the same minimum weight (a tie). When Kruskal's or Prim's algorithm encounters a tie among edges, either choice may be valid, and different tie-breaking choices may produce different but equally valid MSTs.
Mistake 3 — Applying Dijkstra's Algorithm to Graphs with Negative Edge Weights
Running Dijkstra's algorithm without checking edge weights for negatives. Correct: Dijkstra's algorithm assumes all edge weights are non-negative. With negative weights, the "visited" finalization step breaks down — a later path through a negative edge might improve a distance that was already finalized. For graphs with negative weights, use the Bellman-Ford algorithm. If all weights are non-negative, Dijkstra's algorithm is safe.
Mistake 4 — Confusing Euler Paths with Hamilton Paths
"Visit every city exactly once" treated as an Euler problem. Correct: Euler paths traverse every edge exactly once. Hamilton paths visit every vertex exactly once. A helpful memory aid: Euler → Edges; Hamilton → Homes (vertices, the places you stop). "Visit every city exactly once" is a Hamilton problem. "Travel every road exactly once" is an Euler problem.
Mistake 5 — Using Euler Degree Conditions to Determine Hamilton Path Existence
"This graph satisfies the Euler circuit condition, so it must have a Hamilton circuit too." Correct: The Euler degree conditions say nothing about Hamilton paths. A graph can satisfy the Euler circuit condition and still have no Hamilton circuit, or vice versa. For Hamilton paths, use Dirac's or Ore's theorem (sufficient conditions only), or check small graphs by inspection.
Mistake 6 — Forgetting the Outer Face in Euler's Formula
Counting only the interior regions of a planar drawing as "faces." Correct: Always include the outer face. Every planar graph has at least one face — the outer region — even if it has no interior faces. For a tree (no cycles), there is exactly one face. Check: for a tree with n vertices and n − 1 edges, Euler's formula gives n − (n−1) + 1 = 2. ✓
Mistake 7 — Applying Spanning Tree Algorithms to Disconnected Graphs
Running Kruskal's or Prim's algorithm on a graph without checking connectivity. Correct: Kruskal's and Prim's algorithms find spanning trees in connected graphs. If the graph is disconnected, no spanning tree exists, because a spanning tree must reach all vertices. The algorithms will produce a spanning forest instead. Verify connectivity before applying spanning tree algorithms.
Mistake 8 — Confusing Minimum Spanning Trees with Shortest Paths
"The shortest path between two vertices must use edges from the minimum spanning tree." Correct: A minimum spanning tree connects all vertices at minimum total cost, but it does not minimize the path length between any specific pair of vertices. The shortest path between two vertices may not use edges from the minimum spanning tree. Use Dijkstra's algorithm for shortest-path problems and Kruskal's or Prim's algorithm for minimum spanning tree problems — they answer different questions.

minimum spanning tree
A spanning tree with minimum total edge weight.
Kruskal's algorithm
An MST algorithm that sorts edges by weight and adds the cheapest non-cycle-creating edge at each step.
Prim's algorithm
An MST algorithm that grows the tree from a starting vertex, always adding the cheapest edge to a new vertex.
shortest path
A path between two vertices with minimum total edge weight.
Dijkstra's algorithm
A shortest-path algorithm for graphs with non-negative edge weights.
relaxation
Updating a distance estimate when a shorter path is found.
Euler path / circuit
A path or closed path traversing every edge exactly once.
Hamilton path / circuit
A path or closed path visiting every vertex exactly once.
Traveling Salesman Problem
The problem of finding a minimum-weight Hamilton circuit.
planar graph
A graph that can be drawn in the plane without edge crossings.
face
A region of the plane bounded by edges in a planar drawing, including the outer face.
Euler's formula
For connected planar graphs, V − E + F = 2.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 12: Algorithms and Complexity

Trees and network algorithms are among the most practically important topics in discrete mathematics. The connections to computing and higher mathematics are direct. Rooted trees, binary trees, binary search trees, heaps, and tries are foundational data structures in every programming language and every major software system. Kruskal's and Prim's algorithms are taught in every algorithms course as canonical examples of greedy algorithm design, and Dijkstra's algorithm appears in GPS navigation systems, network routing protocols, and shortest-path computations in social networks and recommendation systems.

The Hamilton circuit problem and the Traveling Salesman Problem are among the most famous NP-complete problems, and their difficulty is central to the unsolved \(P \neq NP\) problem — one of the Millennium Prize Problems in mathematics. Understanding why Euler circuits are easy and Hamilton circuits are hard is an entry point into this deep theoretical territory. Decision trees and random forests are among the most powerful machine learning algorithms, and search algorithms on trees and graphs form the backbone of AI planning, puzzle solving, and game playing. Properties like connectivity, shortest paths, spanning subgraphs, and planarity are central to network science — the study of social networks, the internet, biological networks, and citation networks.

Chapter 12 will continue this bridge, asking not just "does a solution exist?" but "how efficiently can we find it?" The efficiency of Kruskal's, Prim's, and Dijkstra's algorithms will be analyzed using the Big-O notation introduced in Chapter 12.

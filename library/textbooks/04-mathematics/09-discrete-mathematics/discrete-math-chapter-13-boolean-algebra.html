Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter completes the bridge between mathematical logic and the physical world of computing. In Chapter 2, you learned that logical statements can be true or false and that connectives like AND, OR, and NOT combine statements according to precise rules. In this chapter, you will see that those same logical ideas can be expressed algebraically, drawn as circuits, and built into physical hardware.

Boolean algebra is the mathematical system that uses exactly two values — typically written 0 and 1, or False and True — and three fundamental operations. It was developed by the mathematician George Boole in the nineteenth century, long before computers existed. When engineers in the twentieth century needed a mathematical language to describe electronic switching circuits, Boolean algebra turned out to be exactly the right tool. Every smartphone, laptop, server, and microcontroller operating today is built from logical operations implemented as circuits in silicon.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand how logical ideas become algebraic expressions, how those expressions map to circuit diagrams, how circuits can be simplified, and how digital computers represent and process information using binary numbers. This chapter does not require calculus. It builds on logic from Chapter 2, algebraic reasoning from your Algebra II background, and truth tables you have already practiced.

Opening Question

A combination lock on a secure door is supposed to open when all three of the following conditions are met: the correct PIN has been entered, the door sensor confirms it is fully closed, and the security system is not in lockdown mode. How would you represent this rule precisely so that a computer circuit could evaluate it automatically? And if a second rule says the door should also open for a master key regardless of the other conditions, how do you combine both rules into a single logical expression that a physical circuit can carry out? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to write, simplify, and diagram expressions exactly like these — and understand how they translate into hardware.

Why This Chapter Matters

Every digital device you use processes information using circuits built from logical gates. The mathematical language those gates implement is Boolean algebra. When programmers write conditional statements — if (x AND y) OR (NOT z) — they are writing Boolean expressions. When hardware engineers design processors, memory controllers, and communication systems, they design networks of logical gates described by Boolean algebra.

Understanding Boolean algebra means understanding the mathematical foundation of computing itself. It explains why computers use binary (0s and 1s), how decisions are made at the hardware level, how circuits can be analyzed and simplified, and how logical structure becomes physical structure.

This chapter also connects directly to cybersecurity. Encryption algorithms depend on logical operations applied to binary data. Understanding Boolean logic is a prerequisite for understanding how bitwise operations work, how data is masked, shifted, and transformed in low-level computing, and how circuits can be analyzed for vulnerabilities.

Section 13.1

Why Boolean Algebra Matters

Mathematics does not always deal with continuous quantities like lengths, areas, weights, or temperatures. Sometimes what matters is not how much but which state: a switch is open or closed, a signal is high or low, a condition is satisfied or not satisfied, a bit is 0 or 1.

Boolean algebra is the branch of mathematics that studies these two-valued systems. It was introduced by George Boole in his 1854 work An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, where he showed that logical reasoning could be expressed using algebraic operations on variables that take only the values 0 and 1.

For most of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, Boolean algebra was studied as a branch of pure logic and abstract algebra. Then, in 1937, the mathematician and engineer Claude Shannon wrote a landmark master's thesis at MIT demonstrating that Boolean algebra could be used to analyze and design electrical switching circuits. This insight is one of the most consequential connections between abstract mathematics and engineering in history. It showed that a switching circuit — where current either flows or does not — implements Boolean logic exactly.

Modern computers are built from billions of transistors, each of which acts as a switch. Groups of transistors are combined into logic gates. Logic gates are connected into circuits. Circuits process data, perform arithmetic, store memory, and communicate. The entire hierarchy from transistor to supercomputer rests on Boolean algebra.

Understanding Boolean algebra gives you access to the mathematical language of computing at its most fundamental level.

When you write a Boolean expression, every variable in it represents a condition that is either satisfied (1) or not satisfied (0). The expression itself evaluates to either 1 or 0 based on the values of its variables.

Section 13.3

Boolean Operations

Boolean algebra has three fundamental operations. Every other Boolean operation can be built from these three.

The NOT Operation (Complement)

The NOT operation takes one Boolean value and produces its opposite. If the input is 1, the output is 0. If the input is 0, the output is 1.

The NOT of a variable \( x \) is written as \( \overline{x} \), read "not \( x \)" or "the complement of \( x \)." In some texts it is written \( \neg x \) or \( x' \).

\( x \)\( \overline{x} \)
01
10

The NOT operation reverses the state. A locked door becomes unlocked. A false condition becomes true. A 0 bit becomes a 1 bit.

The AND Operation

The AND operation takes two Boolean values and produces 1 only when both inputs are 1. In all other cases, it produces 0.

The AND of variables \( x \) and \( y \) is written as \( x \cdot y \), or simply \( xy \), or sometimes \( x \land y \). The notation \( x \cdot y \) reflects the analogy with multiplication: in ordinary arithmetic, \( 1 \cdot 1 = 1 \), \( 1 \cdot 0 = 0 \), \( 0 \cdot 1 = 0 \), and \( 0 \cdot 0 = 0 \).

\( x \)\( y \)\( x \cdot y \)

The AND operation models "both conditions must hold." The light turns on only if the switch is up AND the bulb is working. The file opens only if you have read permission AND the file exists.

The OR Operation

The OR operation takes two Boolean values and produces 1 when at least one input is 1. It produces 0 only when both inputs are 0.

The OR of variables \( x \) and \( y \) is written as \( x + y \), or sometimes \( x \lor y \). The notation \( x + y \) reflects the analogy with addition, though note that in Boolean algebra, \( 1 + 1 = 1 \), not 2. The rule is: if either or both inputs are 1, the output is 1.

The OR operation models "at least one condition holds." The alarm sounds if the front door opens OR the back door opens. The payment is accepted if credit card OR bank transfer is provided.

Important Warning

In Boolean algebra, the symbol \( + \) does not mean addition in the ordinary sense. It means the logical OR operation. Similarly, \( \cdot \) means the logical AND, not ordinary multiplication. The rules look similar to arithmetic in some cases, but they differ in critical ways. Most importantly, \( 1 + 1 = 1 \) in Boolean algebra, not 2.

Operation Priority

Boolean algebra follows an order of operations:

1. NOT is applied first (highest priority).
2. AND is applied next.
3. OR is applied last (lowest priority).

Parentheses override this order, just as in ordinary algebra.

Section 13.4

Truth Tables Revisited

A truth table is a systematic way to evaluate a Boolean expression for every possible combination of its input values. You first encountered truth tables in Chapter 2 in the context of logic. In Boolean algebra, the structure is identical: list all possible input combinations, then compute the output of the expression for each one.

For a Boolean expression with \( n \) variables, the truth table has \( 2^n \) rows, one for each combination of input values.

How to Construct a Truth Table

1. List all variables as column headers.

2. Fill in all \( 2^n \) combinations of 0s and 1s, using a systematic pattern.

3. Add columns for any intermediate subexpressions.

4. Add a final column for the full expression.

5. Evaluate the expression for each row.

Tip — A Pattern for Filling in Inputs

In the rightmost variable column, alternate 0, 1, 0, 1, .... In the next column to the left, alternate in groups of two: 0, 0, 1, 1, 0, 0, 1, 1, .... Continue doubling the group size for each additional variable to the left.

Logical equivalence in Boolean algebra corresponds exactly to logical equivalence in propositional logic from Chapter 2. Section 13.6

AND, OR, and NOT Circuits

A circuit is a network of connected logic gates. Inputs enter the circuit from the left, pass through gates, and produce one or more outputs on the right. The output of one gate can become the input of another, allowing complex Boolean expressions to be computed step by step.

From Expression to Circuit

1. Identify the structure of the expression using operation priority and parentheses.

2. Work from the innermost operations outward.

3. Draw each operation as the appropriate gate.

4. Connect outputs of earlier gates to inputs of later gates.

Functional Completeness

A set of gates is called functionally complete (or universal) if every Boolean function can be built using only gates from that set.

Claim: The set \(\{\text{NAND}\}\) is functionally complete. So is \(\{\text{NOR}\}\).

This is a remarkable fact. It means that if you have only NAND gates, you can build any logical circuit — including AND gates, OR gates, NOT gates, and everything else. The same is true for NOR gates alone.

Example 13.7.1
Building NOT from NAND
  • ProblemShow how a NOT gate can be built using only a NAND gate.
  • ThinkThe NAND gate computes \( \overline{A \cdot B} \). I need an expression that reduces to \( \overline{A} \) using only one input signal.
  • SolveConnect both inputs of a NAND gate to the same signal \( A \). Then the output is: A · A = A (Idempotent Law) NAND(A, A) = (A · A)̄ = Ā
    Diagram Instruction

    Draw a NAND gate. Connect input \( A \) to both input terminals. The output is \( \overline{A} \).

  • CheckIf \( A = 1 \): \( A \cdot A = 1 \cdot 1 = 1 \), so output is \( \overline{1} = 0 = \overline{A} \). ✓ If \( A = 0 \): \( A \cdot A = 0 \), so output is \( \overline{0} = 1 = \overline{A} \). ✓
Answer: \( \overline{A} = \overline{A \cdot A} \), so a single NAND gate with both inputs tied to \( A \) computes NOT \( A \).

How to build AND from NAND: An AND gate is a NAND gate followed by a NOT gate. Since a NOT gate can be built from NAND (as shown above), two NAND gates suffice to build an AND gate.

How to build OR from NAND: By De Morgan's Laws (Section 13.8), \( A + B = \overline{\overline{A} \cdot \overline{B}} \). So: apply NOT (via NAND) to each input, then apply NAND to the results. This requires three NAND gates.

Why This Matters in Practice

Early integrated circuits were manufactured with large numbers of identical gates. If only one gate type needed to be produced in quantity, manufacturing became simpler and cheaper. Both NAND and NOR gates are relatively easy to implement in transistor-based electronics. This is why functional completeness is not just a mathematical curiosity — it has direct engineering implications.

Section 13.8

De Morgan's Laws

De Morgan's Laws are two of the most important identities in Boolean algebra. They describe how NOT distributes over AND and OR. You first encountered these as logical equivalences in Chapter 2. Here they appear in algebraic form.

De Morgan's First Law

$$\overline{A \cdot B} = \overline{A} + \overline{B}$$

In words: The complement of a product equals the sum of the complements.

De Morgan's Second Law

$$\overline{A + B} = \overline{A} \cdot \overline{B}$$

In words: The complement of a sum equals the product of the complements.

These laws allow you to push a NOT inward through an AND or an OR, provided you change AND to OR (or OR to AND) at the same time.

Verification by Truth Table

Let us verify the first law.

The columns for \( \overline{A \cdot B} \) and \( \overline{A} + \overline{B} \) are identical. The law is verified.

Generalized De Morgan's Laws

De Morgan's Laws extend to expressions with more than two variables:

$$\overline{A \cdot B \cdot C} = \overline{A} + \overline{B} + \overline{C}$$

$$\overline{A + B + C} = \overline{A} \cdot \overline{B} \cdot \overline{C}$$

The pattern is: push the NOT inward, complement every variable, and flip every AND to OR (or every OR to AND).

Simplifying Boolean Expressions

One of the central goals of Boolean algebra is simplification. A simpler expression requires fewer gates to implement in hardware, which reduces cost, power consumption, and space. Just as you simplify algebraic expressions by applying algebraic laws, you simplify Boolean expressions by applying Boolean laws.

Laws of Boolean Algebra

The following laws hold for all Boolean variables \( A \), \( B \), and \( C \). Each law can be verified by truth table.

Identity and Null (Domination) Laws

$$A + 0 = A \qquad A \cdot 1 = A$$

$$A + 1 = 1 \qquad A \cdot 0 = 0$$

Idempotent and Complement Laws

$$A + A = A \qquad A \cdot A = A$$

$$A + \overline{A} = 1 \qquad A \cdot \overline{A} = 0$$

Double Complement Law: $$\overline{\overline{A}} = A$$

Commutative and Associative Laws

$$A + B = B + A \qquad A \cdot B = B \cdot A$$

$$(A + B) + C = A + (B + C) \qquad (A \cdot B) \cdot C = A \cdot (B \cdot C)$$

Distributive Laws

$$A \cdot (B + C) = A \cdot B + A \cdot C$$

$$A + (B \cdot C) = (A + B) \cdot (A + C)$$

Absorption Laws and De Morgan's Laws

$$A + A \cdot B = A \qquad A \cdot (A + B) = A$$

$$\overline{A \cdot B} = \overline{A} + \overline{B} \qquad \overline{A + B} = \overline{A} \cdot \overline{B}$$

Student Note

The second distributive law — \( A + (B \cdot C) = (A + B) \cdot (A + C) \) — has no direct analog in ordinary algebra. In ordinary algebra, \( a + bc \neq (a + b)(a + c) \). This is one of the places where Boolean algebra and ordinary algebra differ. Verify it by truth table before relying on it.

A Strategy for Simplification

1. Apply De Morgan's Laws to remove NOT from over a compound expression.

2. Apply the double complement law to remove double negations.

3. Look for complement pairs (\( A \) and \( \overline{A} \)) that produce 0 (by AND) or 1 (by OR).

4. Factor using the distributive law to group terms.

5. Apply absorption to eliminate redundant terms.

6. Always verify by comparing truth tables of the original and simplified expressions.

Circuit Diagrams

A circuit diagram (also called a logic diagram or gate diagram) is a drawing that shows how logic gates are connected to implement a Boolean expression. Inputs appear on the left, outputs appear on the right, and gates are connected by wires.

Reading and drawing circuit diagrams is a practical skill. In this section, we practice converting between expressions and diagrams in both directions.

From Expression to Circuit

1. Identify the top-level operation (the last operation applied in evaluation order).

2. Identify the inputs to that operation.

3. Recursively expand each input until you reach the original variables.

4. Draw gates from left to right, with inputs on the left and the final output on the right.

Section 13.11

Binary Numbers as an Introduction

Digital computers represent all data — numbers, text, images, sounds, programs — using sequences of binary digits called bits. A bit is a single binary digit, either 0 or 1. Understanding binary numbers is essential for understanding how computers store and process information using Boolean circuits.

Counting in Binary

The binary number system uses base 2, just as the decimal system uses base 10. In decimal, each digit position represents a power of 10:

$$\underbrace{4}_{10^3}\,\underbrace{7}_{10^2}\,\underbrace{3}_{10^1}\,\underbrace{2}_{10^0} = 4 \times 1000 + 7 \times 100 + 3 \times 10 + 2 \times 1 = 4732$$

In binary, each digit position represents a power of 2:

$$\underbrace{1}_{2^3}\,\underbrace{0}_{2^2}\,\underbrace{1}_{2^1}\,\underbrace{1}_{2^0}$$

The binary number \( 1011_2 \) is read by computing:

$$1 \times 2^3 + 0 \times 2^2 + 1 \times 2^1 + 1 \times 2^0 = 8 + 0 + 2 + 1 = 11_{10}$$

So the binary number \( 1011 \) equals the decimal number \( 11 \).

Converting from Binary to Decimal

Procedure

1. Write the binary number with its digits indexed from right (position 0) to left.

2. Multiply each digit by \( 2^{\text{position}} \).

3. Add the results.

Section 13.12

Digital Logic in Computers

Modern computers perform all of their operations using logic circuits. The operations you have studied in this chapter are not abstract curiosities — they are the actual operations executed in hardware billions of times per second.

The Processor

The central processing unit (CPU) of a computer is a large, complex circuit built from logic gates. The gates are implemented using transistors in silicon. A modern CPU contains billions of transistors, but each transistor is still fundamentally a switch — a device that is either on (conducting current, representing 1) or off (not conducting, representing 0).

The CPU performs arithmetic using circuits called arithmetic logic units (ALUs). An ALU is built from half adders, full adders, and other gate combinations. It can add, subtract (using complement representations), compare, and apply bitwise Boolean operations to binary numbers.

Memory

Computer memory stores Boolean values. A single memory cell stores one bit. Groups of eight bits form a byte. The memory you use when you open a program or load a file is an array of cells, each holding a 0 or a 1, read and written by circuits controlled by Boolean logic.

Conditional Logic in Hardware

When a program executes an if-then-else decision, a circuit evaluates a Boolean condition and routes data accordingly. The condition is a Boolean expression. The routing is controlled by a multiplexer — a circuit that selects one of its inputs based on a control signal.

Bitwise Operations in Computing

Programming languages expose Boolean operations directly as bitwise operators that apply Boolean logic to each pair of corresponding bits in two integers. For example, in many languages:

OperatorMeaning
A & BComputes the AND of each bit of \( A \) and each bit of \( B \).
A | BComputes the OR of each bit.
~AComputes the NOT (complement) of each bit.
A ^ BComputes the XOR of each bit.

These operations are used constantly in systems programming, cryptography, graphics, networking, and data processing.

Cryptography and Boolean Logic

Many encryption algorithms operate on bits using Boolean operations. The XOR operation is particularly important: XOR-ing a plaintext bit with a key bit produces a ciphertext bit, and XOR-ing the ciphertext bit with the same key bit recovers the plaintext. This is the basis of one-time pad encryption and stream cipher design.

Boolean permutations, substitutions, and combinations of XOR, AND, and OR operations underlie the structure of block ciphers including AES (Advanced Encryption Standard), which protects most secure internet communication today.

Section 13.13

Common Boolean Algebra and Circuit Mistakes

Understanding where errors typically occur helps you avoid them and catch them when checking your work.

Mistake 1 — Treating + and · as Ordinary Arithmetic
A + A = 2A Correct: A + A = A (Idempotent Law). In Boolean algebra, 1 + 1 = 1, not 2. The symbols + and · represent OR and AND, not addition and multiplication.
Mistake 2 — Forgetting Operation Priority
Treating A + B · C as (A + B) · C Correct: A + B · C means A + (B · C), because NOT binds most tightly, then AND, then OR. When in doubt, use parentheses to make the intended grouping explicit.
Mistake 3 — Distributing NOT Incorrectly
A + B ̄ = Ā + B̄ Correct: A + B ̄ = Ā · B̄ (De Morgan's Second Law). The most common De Morgan's Law mistake is forgetting to flip AND to OR (or OR to AND) when pushing NOT inward.
Mistake 4 — Applying Ordinary Algebra's Distributive Law in Reverse
(A + B)(A + C) = A² + AC + AB + BC Correct: (A + B)(A + C) = A + BC in Boolean algebra. These are different from ordinary algebra. When applying the second distributive law, verify by truth table.
Mistake 5 — Stopping Simplification Too Early
Leaving A + A · B unsimplified Correct: A + A · B simplifies by absorption to just A. Always check whether absorption, complement, or idempotent laws apply after each step.
Mistake 6 — Confusing XOR with OR

XOR outputs 0 when both inputs are 1. OR outputs 1 when both inputs are 1. These gates behave the same for all other inputs, which can lead students to use them interchangeably when they should not.

Mistake 7 — Confusion About Gate Symbols in Diagrams

NAND and NOR symbols look like AND and OR symbols with a bubble added at the output. Omitting or misplacing the bubble changes the meaning entirely. Always label gates clearly.

Mistake 8 — Incorrect Binary-Decimal Conversion
1010₂ → 1·2⁰ + 0·2¹ + 1·2² + 0·2³ = 1 + 0 + 4 + 0 = 5 Correct: the rightmost digit is always position 0 (the ones place, 2⁰). For 1010₂: 1·2³ + 0·2² + 1·2¹ + 0·2⁰ = 8 + 0 + 2 + 0 = 10. Students sometimes start multiplying from the left using position 0, rather than from the right.

Preparing for Computer Architecture and Cybersecurity

Chapter 13 is the final chapter before the capstone. You now have a working knowledge of Boolean algebra, logic gates, circuits, simplification, De Morgan's Laws, binary numbers, and digital logic.

The ideas in this chapter open into several areas of further study:

Computer Architecture

Courses in computer organization and architecture examine how processors are built from ALUs, registers, memory units, control circuits, and buses — all of which are implemented using the Boolean logic gates you have studied. Concepts like carry-ripple addition, multiplexers, decoders, encoders, and flip-flops (memory elements) build directly on this chapter.

Combinational versus Sequential Circuits

The circuits studied here are combinational — their output depends only on their current inputs. Sequential circuits include memory (feedback): their output can depend on their history. Flip-flops and latches are the building blocks of sequential circuits. This leads to state machines and registers.

Digital Systems Design

Engineers use hardware description languages (HDLs) such as VHDL and Verilog to describe Boolean circuits as programs, which are then synthesized into physical circuits on programmable chips. Boolean algebra is the mathematical foundation of HDL design.

Cybersecurity and Cryptography

As noted in Section 13.12, Boolean operations are central to symmetric encryption. Bitwise XOR, AND, OR, and NOT are used in stream ciphers, hash functions, block cipher rounds, and key scheduling. Understanding the mathematical behavior of these operations is a prerequisite for studying cryptographic algorithms.

Artificial Intelligence Hardware

Neural network inference is accelerating on specialized hardware (GPUs, TPUs, and custom AI chips). The arithmetic operations these chips perform — including multiply-accumulate operations at reduced precision — are implemented using Boolean circuits. Understanding the link between logic and arithmetic deepens understanding of how AI systems run efficiently.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1Boolean algebra is the mathematical system for two-valued logic. It was developed by Boole in the nineteenth century and connected to switching circuits by Shannon in 1937. Every digital computer is built on this foundation.
  • Sec 13.2Boolean variables take exactly two values: 0 (false) and 1 (true). This restriction is the defining feature of Boolean algebra.
  • Sec 13.3Three fundamental operations: NOT (\( \overline{A} \)), AND (\( A \cdot B \)), and OR (\( A + B \)). Operation priority: NOT first, AND second, OR last. These three operations are sufficient to express any Boolean function.
  • Sec 13.4A truth table evaluates a Boolean expression for every possible input combination. For \( n \) variables, there are \( 2^n \) rows. Adding intermediate columns for subexpressions simplifies the process.
  • Sec 13.5Logic gates are physical implementations of Boolean operations. Standard gates: NOT, AND, OR, XOR, XNOR. Each has a standard symbol and a defining truth table.
  • Sec 13.6A circuit connects logic gates so that outputs of earlier gates feed inputs of later gates. Any Boolean expression can be drawn as a circuit, and any circuit can be read as a Boolean expression.
  • Sec 13.7NAND computes \( \overline{A \cdot B} \); NOR computes \( \overline{A + B} \). Both are functionally complete: any Boolean function can be built from NAND gates alone, or from NOR gates alone.
  • Sec 13.8First Law: \( \overline{A \cdot B} = \overline{A} + \overline{B} \). Second Law: \( \overline{A + B} = \overline{A} \cdot \overline{B} \). These laws allow NOT to be pushed inward through AND and OR, with the operation flipped.
  • Sec 13.9Boolean expressions can be simplified using the Boolean laws: identity, null, idempotent, complement, double complement, commutative, associative, distributive, absorption, and De Morgan's Laws. Simpler expressions require fewer gates.
  • Sec 13.10Circuit diagrams translate Boolean expressions into gate networks. Conversion goes both ways: expression to circuit and circuit to expression.
  • Sec 13.11The binary number system uses base 2. Each bit represents a power of 2. Conversion from binary to decimal: compute weighted sum of bit values. Conversion from decimal to binary: repeated division by 2. XOR and AND implement binary addition in a half adder.
  • Sec 13.12Processors, memory, conditional logic, and cryptographic algorithms are all built from the Boolean operations studied in this chapter. Bitwise operators in programming languages directly implement Boolean algebra.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 14: Discrete Mathematics Capstone and Applications

Chapter 13 sits at the intersection of mathematics, logic, and engineering. The Boolean algebra you have studied here is not an isolated topic. It is the language in which digital systems are designed, analyzed, and verified. The operations in this chapter are implemented physically in transistors and combined into functional units — adders, multiplexers, comparators, decoders, registers, and memory cells — that make up a processor. Every CPU instruction ultimately reduces to sequences of Boolean operations on binary data.

Engineers use Boolean algebra to minimize gate counts, reduce power consumption, and verify circuit correctness. Karnaugh maps and the Quine-McCluskey algorithm — systematic methods for minimizing Boolean expressions — are within reach of students who master this chapter. Boolean operations, especially XOR, are also central to encryption, hashing, and error correction, foundational for studying block ciphers, stream ciphers, and hash functions. Every programming language provides bitwise Boolean operators that implement these ideas directly.

Boolean algebra is an example of a mathematical structure called a complemented distributive lattice, or equivalently, a Boolean ring. Students continuing into abstract algebra will encounter Boolean algebras as a family of algebraic structures satisfying the same axioms used in this chapter. Chapter 14, the capstone, will bring this chapter's logic and circuits together with sets, proof, counting, graph theory, algorithms, modular arithmetic, recursion, and relations — the full toolkit of discrete mathematics — to model real systems involving choices, connections, rules, steps, and finite structures.

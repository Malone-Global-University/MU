Chapter 3 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 3 introduces the first major symbolic solution method in this course: separation of variables. After two chapters devoted to understanding what differential equations are and how to read their qualitative behavior, students are now ready to solve a wide class of first-order equations exactly.

A differential equation is called separable if it can be rewritten so that all expressions involving the dependent variable appear on one side and all expressions involving the independent variable appear on the other. Once separated, each side can be integrated independently, and the result is an equation, often solvable for the unknown function, that describes the full solution.

This chapter develops separation of variables carefully and completely. It covers the mechanics of separating variables, the meaning of constants of integration, the handling of initial conditions, the domain and validity of solutions, and a range of applications including exponential growth and decay, Newton's Law of Cooling, and simple motion. It also introduces implicit solutions and discusses what to do when solving explicitly for the dependent variable is inconvenient or impossible.

By the end of this chapter, students should understand that separating variables is not algebraic decoration. It reorganizes the relationship between rates and states so that integration, the central tool of calculus, can recover the unknown function from its rate law.

Opening Question

A population of bacteria doubles every three hours. A sample begins with 500 bacteria. How many bacteria will there be after twelve hours? After one day? You might feel ready to answer with a memorized formula, but here is the deeper question: where does that formula come from? What assumption about the bacteria produces the doubling pattern? And what happens if the growth rate depends on the population itself, or on environmental limits? The answers come from separable differential equations. The doubling formula is not assumed — it is derived from a rate relationship, then recovered by integrating.

Section 3.1

What Makes an Equation Separable

A general first-order equation has the form $\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x,y)$, where $f$ may involve both $x$ and $y$ in ways that prevent direct integration. A separable equation is the special case in which $f(x,y)$ factors cleanly into a function of $x$ alone multiplied by a function of $y$ alone:

Definition — Separable Equation
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = g(x) \cdot h(y)$$

When this factoring is possible, the equation can be reorganized so that all $y$ terms and $dy$ appear on one side, and all $x$ terms and $dx$ appear on the other. Integration then becomes possible on each side separately.

How to test for separability: Ask whether the right side can be written as a product of a pure function of $x$ and a pure function of $y$. If yes, the equation is separable.

EquationSeparable?Reason / Factoring
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3y$Yes$g(x)=3$, $h(y)=y$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x^2 y^3$Yes$g(x)=x^2$, $h(y)=y^3$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{x}{y}$Yes$g(x)=x$, $h(y)=1/y$
$\dfrac{dy}{dt} = -ky$Yes$g(t)=-k$, $h(y)=y$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x + y$NoAddition — cannot factor as $g(x)\cdot h(y)$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \sin(x+y)$NoSum inside the sine cannot be factored
Tip — Manipulate Before Deciding

Some equations that do not look separable at first glance are separable after factoring or simplifying the right side. For example, $\frac{dy}{dx} = xy + x$ factors as $\frac{dy}{dx} = x(y+1)$, making it separable with $g(x)=x$ and $h(y)=y+1$. Always simplify before concluding an equation is not separable.

Section 3.2

Separating Variables

Once an equation has been identified as separable, divide both sides by $h(y)$ (assuming $h(y)\neq 0$) and multiply both sides by $dx$:

Separation Step

Starting from $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = g(x)\cdot h(y)$, divide by $h(y)$ and multiply by $dx$:

$$\frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy = g(x)\,dx$$

The left side involves only $y$ and $dy$. The right side involves only $x$ and $dx$.

Treating $dy$ and $dx$ as movable objects is a notational convenience rooted in the chain rule. For this course, the separation step is valid whenever $h(y)\neq 0$, and the result correctly sets up two separate integration problems.

Example 3.2.1
Separating $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = xy^2$
  • Identify$g(x)=x$, $h(y)=y^2$. The equation is separable.
  • SeparateDivide by $y^2$ (assuming $y\neq 0$), multiply by $dx$: $$\frac{1}{y^2}\,dy = x\,dx$$
  • ResultVariables are separated and ready to integrate.
Equilibrium Warning

The case $h(y)=0$ must always be checked separately. If $h(y_0)=0$ for some constant $y_0$, then the constant function $y=y_0$ may itself be a solution. These equilibrium solutions cannot be recovered from the formula obtained by integration. They must be noted separately.

Section 3.3

Integrating Both Sides

After separating variables, integrate both sides. The left side is integrated with respect to $y$; the right side is integrated with respect to $x$:

Integration Step
$$\int \frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy = \int g(x)\,dx$$

This step converts the differential equation — a statement about rates — into an algebraic equation relating the dependent and independent variables through their antiderivatives. This is the heart of the method.

Why only one constant? Each side produces its own constant of integration, but these are combined into a single constant $C$ on one side. Writing $C_1$ and $C_2$ and then combining as $C = C_2 - C_1$ is valid and standard.

Example 3.3.1
Integrating $\dfrac{1}{y^2}\,dy = x\,dx$
  • Integrate$$\int y^{-2}\,dy = \int x\,dx$$
  • Result$$-\frac{1}{y} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C$$
  • InterpretThis is the general solution in implicit form. Each value of $C$ gives a different solution curve.
Section 3.4

Constants of Integration

The constant of integration $C$ is one of the most important and most mishandled objects in differential equations. Different values of $C$ give different solution functions — each satisfying the original differential equation. The family of all such functions is the general solution.

Constants often appear inside logarithms or as part of exponential expressions, and it is common to rename $C$ to make the solution cleaner. If integration gives $\ln|y| = kx + C$, then exponentiating yields:

Renaming Constants After Exponentiation

$\ln|y| = kx + C \;\Longrightarrow\; |y| = e^{kx+C} = e^C \cdot e^{kx}$

Since $e^C$ is a positive constant, rename it $A$. Dropping the absolute value (allowing the sign to be absorbed by $A$):

$$y = Ae^{kx}$$

where $A$ can be any nonzero real number. Including $A=0$ corresponds to the equilibrium solution $y=0$.

Student Warning — Constant Errors

After exponentiation, $e^{kx+C} = e^C \cdot e^{kx}$, not $e^{kx} + e^C$. Exponentiation of a sum becomes a product. Absorb $e^C$ into a renamed constant $A$, and write the solution as $y = Ae^{kx}$.

Section 3.5

Solving for $y$ When Possible

After integrating, the result is usually an implicit equation. When possible, solve explicitly for $y$ as a function of $x$. Continuing from the example in Section 3.3:

Example 3.5.1
Isolating $y$ from an implicit equation
  • StartFrom Section 3.3: $\displaystyle-\frac{1}{y} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C$
  • RearrangeMultiply by $-1$ and rename $-C$ as $A$: $\displaystyle\frac{1}{y} = A - \frac{x^2}{2}$
  • Invert$\displaystyle y = \frac{1}{A - \frac{x^2}{2}} = \frac{2}{2A - x^2}$
  • SimplifyLet $B = 2A$ (still an arbitrary constant): $$y = \frac{2}{B - x^2}$$
General solution: $\displaystyle y = \dfrac{2}{B - x^2}$
Tip — When Explicit Form Is Impractical

If the implicit equation cannot be solved cleanly for $y$, accept the implicit form as the general solution. It is not incomplete — it defines the solution curves just as well. Section 3.11 discusses implicit solutions further.

Section 3.6

Initial Value Problems

The general solution contains one arbitrary constant $C$. To find a particular solution, an initial condition of the form $y(x_0) = y_0$ is applied. The pair of a differential equation and an initial condition is called an initial value problem (IVP).

Procedure — Solving an Initial Value Problem

1. Solve the differential equation to find the general solution with constant $C$.

2. Substitute the initial condition $x = x_0$, $y = y_0$.

3. Solve the resulting equation for $C$.

4. Write the particular solution by substituting $C$ back into the general solution.

Example 3.6.1
Solving an Initial Value Problem
  • ProblemSolve $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3y$, $\;y(0) = 4$.
  • ThinkThe equation is separable with $g(x)=3$, $h(y)=y$. Separate, integrate, then apply the initial condition.
  • Separate$\dfrac{1}{y}\,dy = 3\,dx$
  • Integrate$\ln|y| = 3x + C \;\Longrightarrow\; y = Ae^{3x}$
  • Apply IC$y(0)=4$: $\quad 4 = Ae^0 = A$, so $A=4$.
  • Answer$y = 4e^{3x}$
  • Check$y' = 12e^{3x}$. Also $3y = 12e^{3x}$. Match. $y(0)=4$. ✓
  • InterpretThe solution describes a quantity starting at $4$ when $x=0$ and growing exponentially with rate $3$ per unit of $x$.
Particular solution: $y = 4e^{3x}$

Section 3.7

Exponential Growth and Decay

One of the most important separable equations is $\dfrac{dy}{dt} = ky$, where $k$ is a constant. This says the rate of change of $y$ is directly proportional to the current value of $y$. When $k>0$, the quantity grows; when $k<0$, it decays.

Fundamental Exponential Growth/Decay Formula
$$y = y_0\,e^{kt}$$

where $y_0 = y(0)$ is the initial value and $k$ is the growth ($k>0$) or decay ($k<0$) constant

This formula is not memorized — it is derived. Separating $\frac{1}{y}\,dy = k\,dt$, integrating to get $\ln|y| = kt + C$, exponentiating, and applying $y(0)=y_0$ yields $y = y_0 e^{kt}$. Every instance of exponential change in science, finance, and engineering comes from this derivation.

Example 3.7.1
Bacterial Population Growth
  • ProblemA culture has $500$ bacteria at $t=0$. Growth constant $k=0.4$ per hour. Find $P(t)$ and the population after $6$ hours.
  • ThinkIVP: $\dfrac{dP}{dt}=0.4P$, $P(0)=500$. Apply the exponential formula directly.
  • Solve$P(t) = 500\,e^{0.4t}$
  • Evaluate$P(6) = 500\,e^{2.4} \approx 500 \times 11.023 \approx 5512$ bacteria
  • Check$P(0)=500$. ✓   As $t$ increases, $e^{0.4t}$ increases. ✓
  • InterpretFrom $500$ organisms, the culture grows to approximately $5{,}512$ in $6$ hours. The exponential model assumes unlimited resources. In reality, resource limits slow growth — a refinement addressed in Chapter 5 with the logistic model.
$P(t) = 500\,e^{0.4t}$. After 6 hours: $P(6) \approx 5{,}512$ bacteria.
Section 3.8

Half-Life and Doubling Time

QuantityContextFormulaDepends On
Doubling time $T_2$Exponential growth ($k>0$)$\displaystyle T_2 = \frac{\ln 2}{k}$Only on $k$, not $y_0$
Half-life $T_{1/2}$Exponential decay ($r>0$)$\displaystyle T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{r}$Only on $r$, not $y_0$

Both formulas are derived by setting $y = 2y_0$ (for doubling) or $y = \frac{1}{2}y_0$ (for half-life) in the exponential formula and solving for $t$. The initial value $y_0$ cancels, revealing that the time scale depends only on the rate constant — a remarkable property of exponential change.

Example 3.8.1
Radioactive Decay and Carbon-14
  • ProblemCarbon-14 has half-life $T_{1/2} = 5730$ years. A sample begins with $80$ g. Find the decay constant $r$, write the model, and find the mass after $2000$ years.
  • ThinkUse $r = \dfrac{\ln 2}{T_{1/2}}$, then write $Q(t) = 80e^{-rt}$, then evaluate at $t=2000$.
  • Solve $r = \dfrac{\ln 2}{5730} \approx 0.0001210 \text{ per year}$

    $Q(t) = 80\,e^{-0.0001210\,t}$

    $Q(2000) = 80\,e^{-0.2420} \approx 80 \times 0.7849 \approx 62.8\text{ g}$
  • CheckAt $t=5730$: $Q = 80e^{-\ln 2} = 80 \times 0.5 = 40\text{ g}$ — exactly half. ✓
  • InterpretAfter $2000$ years, $\approx 62.8$ g remain. Since $2000$ years is about one-third of a half-life, the sample has lost less than half its mass — consistent with $62.8 > 40$.
$r \approx 0.0001210$ per year. $Q(t) = 80e^{-0.0001210\,t}$. After 2000 years: $\approx 62.8$ g.
Section 3.9

Newton's Law of Cooling

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the rate of temperature change of an object is proportional to the difference between the object's temperature and the ambient temperature:

Newton's Law of Cooling
$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a)$$

$T(t)$ = object temperature, $T_a$ = ambient temperature (constant), $k>0$ = cooling constant

The substitution $u = T - T_a$ transforms this into the exponential decay equation $\dfrac{du}{dt} = -ku$, with solution $u = u_0 e^{-kt}$. Substituting back yields the full solution:

Solution to Newton's Law of Cooling
$$T(t) = T_a + (T_0 - T_a)\,e^{-kt}$$

As $t\to\infty$: $T(t)\to T_a$. The object asymptotically approaches ambient temperature.

Example 3.9.1
Coffee Cooling
  • ProblemCoffee starts at $90°C$. Room temperature is $20°C$. After $10$ min the coffee is $70°C$. Find $k$ and $T(30)$.
  • Setup$T(t) = 20 + 70\,e^{-kt}$
  • Find k Apply $T(10)=70$: $\quad 70 = 20 + 70e^{-10k}$

    $50 = 70e^{-10k} \;\Rightarrow\; e^{-10k} = \dfrac{5}{7}$

    $k = \dfrac{1}{10}\ln\!\left(\dfrac{7}{5}\right) \approx 0.03365$ per minute
  • Evaluate$T(30) = 20 + 70\,e^{-1.0095} \approx 20 + 70(0.3645) \approx 45.5°C$
  • Check$T(0)=90°C$ ✓, $T(10)=70°C$ ✓, $T\to 20°C$ as $t\to\infty$ ✓
  • InterpretAfter $30$ minutes the coffee is approximately $45.5°C$ — still warm but cooling steadily. It asymptotically approaches room temperature and will never cool below $20°C$ under ideal conditions.
$k \approx 0.03365$ per min. $T(t) = 20 + 70e^{-0.03365\,t}$. $T(30) \approx 45.5°C$.
Section 3.10

Simple Motion with Separable Equations

Newton's second law $F=ma$ becomes a differential equation when force depends on velocity. A common model for a falling object subject to linear air drag is:

Motion with Linear Drag
$$m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - bv$$

$m$ = mass, $g$ = gravitational acceleration, $b>0$ = drag coefficient, $v$ = velocity

Letting $r = b/m$, this separates to $\dfrac{dv}{g-rv} = dt$. Integrating and applying $v(0)=v_0$ yields:

Velocity with Drag
$$v(t) = \frac{g}{r} + \!\left(v_0 - \frac{g}{r}\right)e^{-rt}$$

Terminal velocity: $v_{\text{term}} = \dfrac{g}{r} = \dfrac{mg}{b}$

Example 3.10.1
Skydiver Terminal Velocity
  • ProblemSkydiver mass $70$ kg, drag coefficient $b=14$ kg/s, $v_0=0$, $g=9.8$ m/s². Find $v(t)$ and terminal velocity.
  • Compute$r = 14/70 = 0.2$ per second. $v_{\text{term}} = 9.8/0.2 = 49$ m/s.
  • Solve$v(t) = 49\!\left(1 - e^{-0.2t}\right)$
  • Check$v(0)=0$ ✓. As $t\to\infty$: $v\to 49$ m/s ✓.
  • InterpretThe skydiver accelerates from rest and asymptotically approaches $49$ m/s ($\approx 176$ km/h). Acceleration is greatest at the start, when drag is small, and diminishes as speed increases.
$v(t) = 49(1-e^{-0.2t})$ m/s. Terminal velocity: $49$ m/s.

Section 3.11

Implicit Solutions

Not every separable equation can be solved explicitly for $y$ as a function of $x$. When the implicit equation relating $y$ and $x$ cannot be solved in closed form, the implicit form is accepted as the general solution.

An implicit solution is an equation $F(x,y) = C$ such that any function $y(x)$ satisfying this equation also satisfies the original differential equation.

Example 3.11.1
An equation with no explicit solution
  • Equation$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{x^2}{1-y^2}$
  • Separate$(1-y^2)\,dy = x^2\,dx$
  • Integrate$y - \dfrac{y^3}{3} = \dfrac{x^3}{3} + C$
  • ResultThere is no elementary formula isolating $y$. The implicit equation $y - \dfrac{y^3}{3} = \dfrac{x^3}{3} + C$ is the general solution.
General implicit solution: $\;y - \dfrac{y^3}{3} = \dfrac{x^3}{3} + C$
Tip — Implicit Solutions Are Valid

The inability to write $y$ explicitly is a limitation of algebraic notation, not a failure of the solution method. Implicit solutions define solution curves completely, allow numerical computation of specific values, and can be verified by implicit differentiation.

Section 3.12

Domain and Validity of Solutions

The general solution to a separable equation is valid only where it is defined and where the original equation makes sense. Several issues commonly restrict the domain:

IssueExampleEffect on Domain
Logarithm arguments$\ln|y|$ requires $y\neq 0$Solution invalid where $y=0$
Rational denominators$y = \dfrac{2}{B-x^2}$Undefined at $x=\pm\sqrt{B}$
Division by $h(y)$$h(y)=0$ at some $y_0$Requires separate check for equilibrium solution $y=y_0$

Equilibrium solutions: If $h(y_0)=0$, the constant function $y=y_0$ satisfies $y'=g(x)\cdot 0 = 0$. Verify this independently — the general formula will not include it.

Example 3.12.1
Equilibrium solutions for $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = xy^2$
  • Check $h(y)=0$$h(y)=y^2=0 \;\Rightarrow\; y=0$. Test: if $y=0$, then $y'=0$ and $g(x)\cdot 0=0$. So $y=0$ is an equilibrium solution for all $x$.
  • General solutionFrom Section 3.5, $y = \dfrac{2}{B-x^2}$. This is never zero — confirming that the equilibrium $y=0$ does not appear in this family.
Equilibrium solution: $y = 0$. General solution: $y = \dfrac{2}{B-x^2}$, valid on any interval excluding $x=\pm\sqrt{B}$.
Section 3.13

Common Separable Equation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Separating When Addition Is Present
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x+y$ separated as $\dfrac{dy}{y}=x\,dx$
Correct: $x+y$ cannot be factored as $g(x)\cdot h(y)$. This equation is not separable. Use the integrating factor method from Chapter 4.

Addition does not allow variable separation. Only products can be separated.

Mistake 2 — Forgetting the Constant of Integration
$\ln|y|=kx$ (no $+C$)
Correct: $\ln|y| = kx + C$, giving $y = Ae^{kx}$ where $A$ is arbitrary.

Omitting $C$ gives one particular solution without justification. The result is incomplete until an initial condition is applied.

Mistake 3 — Adding Instead of Multiplying After Exponentiation
$e^{kx+C} = e^{kx} + e^C$
Correct: $e^{kx+C} = e^C \cdot e^{kx}$. Exponentiation of a sum becomes a product. Rename $e^C$ as $A$ to write $y = Ae^{kx}$.
Mistake 4 — Dropping the Absolute Value Too Early
Writing $\ln y$ instead of $\ln|y|$, restricting to $y>0$ with no justification
Correct: Maintain $\ln|y|$ until an initial condition or the context establishes the sign of $y$. Allow $A$ in $y=Ae^{kx}$ to be negative when the initial value is negative.
Mistake 5 — Forgetting Equilibrium Solutions
Presenting only the general formula without checking whether $h(y)=0$ gives additional solutions
Correct: Always check if $h(y_0)=0$ for any constant $y_0$. If so, verify that $y=y_0$ satisfies the original equation and include it as a separate solution.
Mistake 6 — Applying the Initial Condition to an Intermediate Step
Substituting $y(0)=y_0$ into $\ln|y|=kx+C$ instead of into $y=Ae^{kx}$
Correct: Apply the initial condition after converting to the clearest form of the general solution. In this case, substitute into $y=Ae^{kx}$ to find $A=y_0$ directly.
Mistake 7 — Sign Error in Decay Problems
Writing $y=y_0 e^{0.5t}$ for a decay problem with rate $r=0.5$ (growth instead of decay)
Correct: For decay, $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=-ry$ with $r>0$, giving $y=y_0 e^{-rt}$. The negative exponent ensures the quantity decreases over time.
Section 3.14

Preparing for Linear First-Order Equations

Separable equations are powerful, but they require the right side of the equation to factor as a product $g(x)h(y)$. Many important equations, including all equations of the form $\dfrac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = q(x)$, are not separable. The equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x - y$, for instance, cannot be separated because $x - y$ is a difference, not a product.

These are linear first-order equations. They have special algebraic structure: the dependent variable and its derivative appear only to the first power and are not multiplied together. Chapter 4 introduces the integrating factor method, designed precisely for this class of equations.

The progression is natural: separable equations are solvable when variables can be algebraically separated; linear equations are solvable through an integrating factor that converts the left side into a recognizable product derivative. Every skill from this chapter — separating, integrating, managing constants, applying initial conditions, interpreting solutions — carries forward directly.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 3.1A separable equation has the form $\frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y)$. Test for separability by asking whether the right side factors as a product of a function of $x$ alone and a function of $y$ alone. Addition prevents separation; only products allow it.
  • Sec 3.2Dividing by $h(y)$ and multiplying by $dx$ gives $\frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy = g(x)\,dx$. This step is valid when $h(y)\neq 0$. Cases where $h(y)=0$ must be checked separately as potential equilibrium solutions.
  • Sec 3.3Integrating both sides converts the rate relationship into an algebraic equation. The two constants of integration combine into a single constant $C$. The result is the general solution in implicit form.
  • Sec 3.4The constant $C$ represents the entire family of solutions. After exponentiation, $e^C$ is a positive constant that is renamed (usually $A$). The renamed constant can be any nonzero real number once the absolute value is resolved.
  • Sec 3.5When the implicit result can be solved algebraically for $y$, the explicit general solution is preferred. Renaming combined constants is always valid. When explicit form is not available, the implicit form is accepted.
  • Sec 3.6An initial condition $y(x_0)=y_0$ determines a particular solution. Procedure: find the general solution, substitute the initial condition, solve for $C$, and write the particular solution with $C$ determined.
  • Sec 3.7The equation $\frac{dy}{dt}=ky$ has solution $y=y_0 e^{kt}$. When $k>0$: exponential growth. When $k<0$: exponential decay. This formula is derived, not assumed.
  • Sec 3.8Doubling time $T_2=\frac{\ln 2}{k}$ and half-life $T_{1/2}=\frac{\ln 2}{r}$ depend only on the rate constant, not on the initial value. Both are derived by solving the exponential formula for $t$ at the target value.
  • Sec 3.9Newton's Law of Cooling $\frac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a)$ is solved by substitution $u=T-T_a$, yielding $T(t)=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt}$. Temperature approaches $T_a$ asymptotically as $t\to\infty$.
  • Sec 3.10Motion with linear drag $m\frac{dv}{dt}=mg-bv$ is separable with solution $v(t)=\frac{g}{r}+(v_0-\frac{g}{r})e^{-rt}$. Terminal velocity $v_{\text{term}}=mg/b$ is the long-term limiting speed where gravity and drag balance.
  • Sec 3.11An implicit solution is an equation $F(x,y)=C$ where any function $y(x)$ satisfying the equation satisfies the differential equation. It is valid when no algebraic method produces an explicit form for $y$.
  • Sec 3.12Domain restrictions arise from logarithm arguments, rational denominators, and the assumption $h(y)\neq 0$. Equilibrium solutions arise from $h(y)=0$ and must be checked independently of the general formula.
  • Sec 3.13Seven common errors: separating when addition is present, omitting $C$, adding instead of multiplying after exponentiation, dropping absolute values early, missing equilibrium solutions, applying ICs to intermediate steps, and reversing decay signs.
  • Sec 3.14Separable equations require the right side to factor as a product. Linear first-order equations of the form $y'+p(x)y=q(x)$ are not separable. Chapter 4 introduces the integrating factor method for this important class.

Key Terms Review

separable equation
A first-order equation of the form $\frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y)$ that can be reorganized so variables appear on opposite sides.
separation of variables
The method of dividing by $h(y)$ and multiplying by $dx$ to isolate $y$ terms on one side and $x$ terms on the other, enabling separate integration.
constant of integration
The arbitrary constant $C$ that appears when integrating a differential equation, representing the family of all possible solutions.
general solution
The family of all solutions to a differential equation, expressed with an undetermined constant $C$.
particular solution
A specific member of the solution family, determined by an initial condition that fixes the value of $C$.
initial value problem
A differential equation paired with an initial condition $y(x_0)=y_0$, together determining a unique particular solution.
explicit solution
A solution in which $y$ is expressed directly and alone as a function of $x$.
implicit solution
A solution in which $y$ and $x$ appear together in an equation that cannot be solved cleanly for $y$ alone.
exponential growth
Behavior described by $y=y_0 e^{kt}$ with $k>0$, where the quantity increases without bound over time.
exponential decay
Behavior described by $y=y_0 e^{-rt}$ with $r>0$, where the quantity decreases toward zero over time.
growth constant
The proportionality constant $k$ in $\frac{dy}{dt}=ky$ that determines the rate of exponential growth.
decay constant
The proportionality constant $r$ in $\frac{dy}{dt}=-ry$ that determines the rate of exponential decay.
half-life $T_{1/2}$
The time for a decaying quantity to fall to half its current value: $T_{1/2}=\frac{\ln 2}{r}$. Depends only on the rate constant.
doubling time $T_2$
The time for a growing quantity to double: $T_2=\frac{\ln 2}{k}$. Depends only on the growth constant.
Newton's Law of Cooling
The model $\frac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a)$, describing temperature change proportional to the difference between object and ambient temperature.
terminal velocity
The limiting velocity of a falling object when gravity and drag balance: $v_{\text{term}}=mg/b$. Approached asymptotically.
equilibrium solution
A constant function $y=y_0$ satisfying the differential equation, arising when $h(y_0)=0$. Not captured by the general formula.

Error Analysis

Each problem contains a mathematical error. Identify and correct it.

Error Analysis 1

Solving $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy$, a student wrote $\dfrac{dy}{y}=dx$ (dropped the $x$ from the right side), then obtained $\ln|y|=x+C$, giving $y=Ae^x$.

$\dfrac{dy}{y} = dx \;\Rightarrow\; y = Ae^x$
Correct: $\dfrac{dy}{y} = x\,dx \;\Rightarrow\; \ln|y| = \dfrac{x^2}{2}+C \;\Rightarrow\; y = Ae^{x^2/2}$

The $x$ is part of $g(x)$ and must remain on the right side when separating.

Error Analysis 2

Solving $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2y$, $y(0)=5$, a student wrote $\dfrac{dy}{y}=2\,dx$, then $\ln y=2x$, then $y=e^{2x}$. No constant was included; the answer grew from $y(0)=1$ instead of $5$.

$\ln y = 2x \;\Rightarrow\; y = e^{2x}$ (omitting $C$, gives $y(0)=1$)
Correct: $\ln|y|=2x+C \;\Rightarrow\; y=Ae^{2x}$. Apply $y(0)=5$: $A=5$. Particular solution: $y=5e^{2x}$.
Error Analysis 3

A student solved $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x+y$ by separating to get $\dfrac{dy}{y}=x\,dx$.

$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x+y$ treated as separable; $\dfrac{dy}{y}=x\,dx$
Correct: $x+y$ cannot be factored as $g(x)\cdot h(y)$. This equation is not separable. Use the integrating factor method from Chapter 4: write in standard form $y'-y=-x$ with $p(x)=-1$.
Error Analysis 4

A student solved $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-0.5y$, $y(0)=100$ and obtained $y=100e^{0.5t}$ (a growing solution instead of a decaying one).

$y = 100\,e^{0.5t}$ (grows, but the equation has a negative sign)
Correct: The equation $y'=-0.5y$ has $k=-0.5$, so $y=100e^{-0.5t}$. The negative sign must be preserved throughout: $\ln|y|=-0.5t+C \;\Rightarrow\; y=Ae^{-0.5t}$.
Error Analysis 5

After finding $y=Ae^{kx}$ and applying $y(0)=3$, a student wrote $3=Ae^0=A+0=A$ and got $A=3$. Is the reasoning correct?

$3 = A e^0 = A + 0$ (wrong: $e^0 = 1$, not $0$)
Correct reasoning: $e^0 = 1$, so $3 = A \cdot 1 = A$. The answer $A=3$ is correct, but the intermediate step "$A+0$" is wrong. Write $3 = A \cdot e^0 = A \cdot 1 = A$.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 4: Linear First-Order Differential Equations

Separable equations are a powerful tool, but they require the right side to factor as a product $g(x)h(y)$. Many important equations cannot be separated this way. The equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = q(x)$ describes a first-order linear structure: $y$ and $y'$ appear only to the first power, and they are not multiplied together. Chapter 4 shows that this linear structure supports a precise and elegant solution technique — the integrating factor method — which converts the left side of the equation into a recognizable derivative of a product. Applications will include mixing problems, RC circuits, motion with linear resistance, and financial and population models. Every skill from Chapter 3 carries forward directly.

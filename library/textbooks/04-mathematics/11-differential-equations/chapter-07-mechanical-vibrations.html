Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 7 applies the second-order linear differential equations developed in Chapter 6 to one of the most important families of physical systems: vibrating and oscillating systems. Students who have learned to solve homogeneous second-order equations with constant coefficients will now see exactly why those solutions take the shapes they do, and what each shape means in terms of physical motion.

This chapter studies mass-spring systems, simple harmonic motion, damping, forced oscillations, and a first look at resonance. It connects the algebraic structure of characteristic roots to the physical behavior of a system: whether it oscillates forever, oscillates while decaying, decays without oscillating, or responds dramatically to an external force. By the end of this chapter, students should be able to set up a differential equation from a physical description, classify its solutions, interpret the behavior of the system, and understand why differential equations are the natural language for studying dynamic mechanical systems.

Opening Question

A spring is hanging from a fixed support. A mass is attached to the lower end, pulled downward from its rest position, and released. The mass begins to bounce. Will it bounce forever? Will the bouncing slow down and stop? Does it matter how far it was pulled? Does it matter whether there is friction or air resistance? What happens if someone pushes the mass periodically while it is bouncing? These are not casual questions. They are precise mathematical questions, and differential equations answer them completely.

Section 7.1

Why Oscillations Matter

Before writing any differential equation, it is worth pausing to ask what an oscillation actually is and why it is worth studying carefully. An oscillation is a back-and-forth motion around some central position. The position of the oscillating object varies periodically over time, returning to the same values at regular intervals. A pendulum swings left and right. A spring compresses and extends. A string vibrates up and down. In all of these cases, there is a restoring force: a force that pushes or pulls the object back toward its equilibrium position whenever it is displaced.

The key feature that produces oscillation is the combination of a restoring force and inertia. The restoring force drives the object back toward equilibrium. Inertia causes the object to overshoot. The overshoot leads to displacement in the opposite direction, which generates a restoring force in the opposite direction, which drives the object back again. This cycle repeats.

Real oscillations are also affected by dissipation, the loss of energy to friction, air resistance, or other damping effects. Dissipation causes oscillations to die out over time unless energy is supplied by an external force. All of these phenomena emerge from a single family of second-order linear differential equations. That is the subject of this chapter.

Why This Model Is Universal

The mass-spring system is not just about springs and masses. The mathematical structure of restoring force, inertia, damping, and external forcing appears in electrical circuits, biological rhythms, structural mechanics, and control systems. Mastering this chapter means mastering the governing equation of oscillatory behavior across all of science and engineering.

Section 7.2

Position, Velocity, and Acceleration

Let x(t) denote the position of a mass at time t. The choice of coordinate system matters. For a vertical spring system, it is conventional to define the positive direction as downward from the equilibrium position, so that a positive value of x means the mass is below equilibrium. What matters is consistency: once a positive direction is chosen, it must be maintained throughout the analysis.

The equilibrium position is the position at which the net force on the mass is zero. The displacement x(t) measures how far the mass is from equilibrium, not from a fixed reference point on a wall or ceiling.

Newton's Second Law — Starting Point for Every Vibration Equation

Velocity: v(t) = x′(t) = dx/dt

Acceleration: a(t) = x″(t) = d²x/dt²

Newton's Second Law: F_net = m · x″(t)

The task in each problem is to identify all forces, express them in terms of x and x′, substitute into Newton's law, and obtain a second-order differential equation.

Two Initial Conditions Are Always Required

Because the governing equation is second-order, every initial value problem requires two conditions, typically x(0) = x₀ (initial displacement) and x′(0) = v₀ (initial velocity). If the mass starts from rest at a displaced position: x₀ ≠ 0, v₀ = 0. If the mass starts at equilibrium with a push: x₀ = 0, v₀ ≠ 0.

Student Note — Measure from Equilibrium

It is essential to measure displacement from equilibrium, not from the natural length of the spring. When a mass is attached to a vertical spring, gravity stretches the spring to a new equilibrium position. Measuring from that equilibrium conveniently removes the gravitational force from the equation, as Section 7.4 will show.

Section 7.3

Hooke's Law

The key physical ingredient in the mass-spring model is the restoring force provided by the spring. Hooke's Law states that for a spring within its elastic limit, the force exerted by the spring is proportional to the displacement from the spring's natural (unstretched) length and directed opposite to the displacement.

Hooke's Law
F_spring = −kx

where k > 0 is the spring constant (stiffness constant). Units: N/m in SI. The negative sign reflects the restoring nature of the force: if x > 0, the spring force is negative (directed toward equilibrium); if x < 0, the spring force is positive.

Finding k Experimentally

Hang a known mass m from the spring and measure the equilibrium stretch δ. At equilibrium, kδ = mg, so k = mg/δ. This is the simplest way to determine a spring constant in a laboratory.

Section 7.4

Mass-Spring Systems

With Newton's second law and Hooke's law in hand, we can derive the fundamental differential equation for a mass-spring system. Consider a mass m attached to a vertical spring. When the mass is at equilibrium, the static deflection δ satisfies kδ = mg. Let x(t) be the displacement from this equilibrium position, measured positive downward. Two forces act on the displaced mass: gravity mg downward, and spring force k(δ + x) upward.

Applying Newton's second law with positive direction downward:

mx″ = mg − k(δ + x) = mg − kδ − kx

Since kδ = mg, the gravitational term and static deflection term cancel exactly, leaving:

The Undamped Mass-Spring Equation
mx″ + kx = 0

Dividing by m and defining the natural angular frequency:

ω₀ = √(k/m)

the equation becomes x″ + ω₀² x = 0. The natural frequency depends only on k and m, not on the initial conditions or amplitude.

Gravity Vanishes — Here Is Why

By measuring displacement from the equilibrium position rather than from the spring's natural length, the equilibrium absorbs the effect of gravity. The equation mx″ + kx = 0 is identical for a vertical or a horizontal spring system, because the gravitational and static spring forces cancel at the equilibrium point.

Section 7.5

Simple Harmonic Motion

The equation x″ + ω₀² x = 0 is a second-order linear homogeneous equation with constant coefficients. From Chapter 6, we know how to solve it using the characteristic equation.

Solving x″ + ω₀² x = 0

Characteristic equation:

r² + ω₀² = 0 → r = ±ω₀ i

Purely imaginary roots give the general solution:

x(t) = C₁ cos(ω₀ t) + C₂ sin(ω₀ t)

Amplitude-phase form (equivalent):

x(t) = A cos(ω₀ t − φ) where A = √(C₁² + C₂²)

This is simple harmonic motion (SHM) — a pure sinusoidal oscillation at the natural frequency ω₀.

Example 7.1
Simple Harmonic Motion from Initial Conditions
  • Problem A spring with k = 4 N/m has a mass of 1 kg attached. The mass is pulled 0.3 m below equilibrium and released from rest. Find x(t) and describe the motion.
  • Think Natural frequency: ω₀ = √(k/m) = √(4/1) = 2 rad/s. Governing equation: x″ + 4x = 0. Initial conditions: x(0) = 0.3, x′(0) = 0.
  • Solve General solution: x(t) = C₁ cos 2t + C₂ sin 2t x(0) = C₁ = 0.3 x′(t) = −2C₁ sin 2t + 2C₂ cos 2t x′(0) = 2C₂ = 0 → C₂ = 0 Therefore: x(t) = 0.3 cos 2t
  • Check x(0) = 0.3 cos 0 = 0.3 ✓. x′(0) = −0.6 sin 0 = 0 ✓. Second derivative: x″ = −1.2 cos 2t; then x″ + 4x = −1.2 cos 2t + 1.2 cos 2t = 0 ✓.
  • Interpret The mass oscillates with amplitude 0.3 m at angular frequency 2 rad/s. The period is T = 2π/2 = π ≈ 3.14 s. Since there is no damping, the oscillation continues forever with unchanged amplitude.
Answer: x(t) = 0.3 cos(2t) meters.

Physical Intuition for Period

Increasing mass m increases the period (heavier mass oscillates more slowly). Increasing spring constant k decreases the period (stiffer spring oscillates more quickly). Amplitude has no effect on period — this is a special property of linear restoring forces.

Example 7.2
Finding Amplitude and Period from Initial Conditions at Equilibrium
  • Problem A mass of 0.5 kg is attached to a spring with k = 8 N/m. At t = 0, the mass is at equilibrium (x = 0) with velocity v₀ = 0.4 m/s. Find the amplitude, period, and position function.
  • Think Natural frequency: ω₀ = √(8/0.5) = √16 = 4 rad/s. General solution: x(t) = C₁ cos 4t + C₂ sin 4t.
  • Solve x(0) = C₁ = 0 → C₁ = 0 x(t) = C₂ sin 4t x′(t) = 4C₂ cos 4t x′(0) = 4C₂ = 0.4 → C₂ = 0.1 Amplitude: A = √(0² + 0.1²) = 0.1 m. Period: T = 2π/4 = π/2 ≈ 1.57 s.
  • Check x(0) = 0 ✓. x′(0) = 4(0.1)(1) = 0.4 ✓. Substituting: x″ + 16x = −1.6 sin 4t + 1.6 sin 4t = 0 ✓.
  • Interpret Even though the mass started at equilibrium, it oscillates with amplitude 0.1 m because it was given an initial push. The mass passes through equilibrium with speed 0.4 m/s at t = 0 and again every half-period thereafter.
Answer: x(t) = 0.1 sin(4t) m, amplitude = 0.1 m, period = π/2 s.

Section 7.7

Damped Motion

In any real physical system, some energy is dissipated over time through air resistance, internal friction in the spring, friction with a surface, or viscous damping from a fluid. The simplest model assumes a damping force proportional to velocity, directed opposite to the direction of motion:

The Damped Mass-Spring Equation

Damping force (viscous): F_damping = −bx′

where b > 0 is the damping coefficient (N·s/m in SI units). Applying Newton's second law:

mx″ + bx′ + kx = 0

Characteristic equation:

mr² + br + k = 0 → r = (−b ± √(b² − 4mk)) / (2m)

The discriminant Δ = b² − 4mk determines whether the system oscillates.

Section 7.8

Underdamping, Critical Damping, and Overdamping

Three cases of damped motion correspond to three physically distinct behaviors. Each produces a qualitatively different type of motion determined entirely by the sign of the discriminant.

Case 1 — Underdamped
b² < 4mk  (Δ < 0)
Complex roots: r = −λ ± ω_d i where λ = b/(2m) and ω_d = √(ω₀² − λ²). Solution oscillates inside a decaying exponential envelope. Most common in lightly damped systems.
Case 2 — Critical Damping
b² = 4mk  (Δ = 0)
Repeated real root: r = −λ. Returns to equilibrium as quickly as possible without overshooting. Boundary between oscillatory and non-oscillatory behavior. Used in shock absorbers and door closers.
Case 3 — Overdamped
b² > 4mk  (Δ > 0)
Two distinct negative real roots. Both exponentials decay; mass returns to equilibrium without oscillating, but more slowly than critical damping. Excessive damping "smothers" the system.
CaseDiscriminantRootsGeneral SolutionBehavior
Underdamped Negative Complex: −λ ± ω_d i e^{−λt}(C₁ cos ω_d t + C₂ sin ω_d t) Decaying oscillation
Critical Zero Repeated: r = −λ (C₁ + C₂ t)e^{−λt} Fastest non-oscillatory return
Overdamped Positive Two real: r₁, r₂ < 0 C₁ e^{r₁ t} + C₂ e^{r₂ t} Slow, non-oscillatory return

Example 7.3
Classifying and Solving an Underdamped System
  • Problem A mass of 1 kg is attached to a spring with k = 5 N/m and a damper with b = 2 N·s/m. The mass starts at x(0) = 1 m with x′(0) = 0. Classify the motion and find x(t).
  • Think Governing equation: x″ + 2x′ + 5x = 0. Check discriminant: b² − 4mk = 4 − 20 = −16 < 0. Underdamped.
  • Solve r² + 2r + 5 = 0 r = (−2 ± √(4 − 20))/2 = (−2 ± 4i)/2 = −1 ± 2i So λ = 1 and ω_d = 2. General solution: x(t) = e^{−t}(C₁ cos 2t + C₂ sin 2t) Applying x(0) = 1: C₁ = 1. Differentiating with the product rule: x′(t) = −e^{−t}(C₁ cos 2t + C₂ sin 2t) + e^{−t}(−2C₁ sin 2t + 2C₂ cos 2t) x′(0) = −C₁ + 2C₂ = 0 → C₂ = 1/2 Therefore: x(t) = e^{−t}(cos 2t + (1/2) sin 2t)
  • Check x(0) = e⁰(1 + 0) = 1 ✓. x′(0) = −1 + 2(1/2) = 0 ✓. The discriminant is negative, confirming underdamped behavior ✓.
  • Interpret The mass starts at 1 m and oscillates with decaying amplitude. The damped frequency ω_d = 2 rad/s is less than the natural frequency ω₀ = √5 ≈ 2.24 rad/s. The oscillation amplitude decays by a factor of e^{−1} ≈ 0.37 every second. The initial amplitude in amplitude-phase form is A = √(1² + (1/2)²) = √5/2 ≈ 1.12 m.
Answer: x(t) = e^{−t}(cos 2t + (1/2) sin 2t) meters.
Example 7.4
Critical Damping — Finding the Coefficient and Solving
  • Problem A mass of 1 kg is on a spring with k = 4 N/m. What value of b produces critical damping? If the mass starts at x(0) = 2 m with x′(0) = −1 m/s, find x(t).
  • Think Critical damping requires b² = 4mk = 4(1)(4) = 16, so b = 4 N·s/m. The equation is x″ + 4x′ + 4x = 0.
  • Solve r² + 4r + 4 = (r + 2)² = 0 → r = −2 (repeated) General solution: x(t) = (C₁ + C₂ t)e^{−2t} Applying x(0) = 2: C₁ = 2. Differentiating: x′(t) = C₂ e^{−2t} − 2(C₁ + C₂ t)e^{−2t} x′(0) = C₂ − 2C₁ = C₂ − 4 = −1 → C₂ = 3 Therefore: x(t) = (2 + 3t)e^{−2t}
  • Check x(0) = 2 ✓. x′(0) = 3 − 4 = −1 ✓.
  • Interpret The solution is the product of a linear function (2 + 3t) and a decaying exponential e^{−2t}. Since e^{−2t} decays faster than any polynomial grows, x(t) → 0 as t → ∞. The mass returns to equilibrium without oscillating. This is the fastest non-oscillatory return possible for these spring and mass values.
Answer: x(t) = (2 + 3t)e^{−2t} meters.
The Damping Ratio ζ

Engineers often express damping in terms of the damping ratio ζ = b / (2√(mk)). Underdamped: ζ < 1. Critically damped: ζ = 1. Overdamped: ζ > 1. Most structural systems have ζ between 0.01 and 0.05, making underdamped analysis especially important in practice.

Section 7.9

Forced Oscillations

In many applications, a mass-spring system is not simply released from an initial position but is subjected to an ongoing external force. A machine might vibrate a platform. An earthquake might shake a building's foundation. An electromagnetic driver might push a speaker cone. In all of these cases, the governing equation has a nonzero right-hand side.

The Forced Mass-Spring Equation
mx″ + bx′ + kx = F(t)

For sinusoidal forcing F(t) = F₀ cos(ωt), the general solution splits into two parts:

x(t) = x_h(t) + x_p(t)

Transient response x_h(t): the complementary solution; decays to zero when b > 0.

Steady-state response x_p(t): the particular solution; persists for all time; oscillates at the forcing frequency ω.

For sinusoidal forcing of a damped system, the steady-state amplitude is:

X = (F₀/m) / √((ω₀² − ω²)² + (2λω)²)

where ω₀ = √(k/m) is the natural frequency and λ = b/(2m) is the decay rate. The methods for deriving this formula using undetermined coefficients will be developed in Chapter 8. For now, the key observation is how X depends on the forcing frequency ω.

Transient vs. Steady-State

In any damped forced system, the transient response (natural oscillation) dies away after a few time constants 1/λ. What remains is the steady-state response oscillating at the forcing frequency ω, not the natural frequency ω₀. The system "forgets" its initial conditions and "remembers" only the driving force.

Section 7.10

Resonance as an Introduction

One of the most important and dramatic phenomena in vibration analysis is resonance: the large-amplitude response that occurs when a system is forced near its natural frequency.

Imagine pushing a child on a swing. If you push at the right moment in each cycle, matching the natural frequency of the swing, the amplitude grows. If you push at the wrong frequency, your pushes sometimes help and sometimes hinder, and the amplitude stays small. Matching the forcing frequency to the natural frequency is the key to large-amplitude response.

Resonance in the Undamped Case (b = 0)

When ω = ω₀, the standard particular solution form is a solution to the homogeneous equation (not a new particular solution). Multiplying by t gives the correct resonant particular solution:

x_p(t) = (F₀ / (2mω₀)) · t · sin(ω₀ t)

The factor t causes the amplitude to grow linearly without bound. This is pure resonance.

Diagram — Pure Resonance Growth

Sketch the graph of x(t) = t sin(2t) for t ≥ 0. The function oscillates, but each successive maximum and minimum is larger in magnitude because of the growing factor t. The oscillation is contained within the envelope ±t, which grows linearly. Label the envelope y = ±t and mark several oscillation crests showing their increasing height.

Engineering Warning — Resonance Is Dangerous

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed in 1940 after wind-induced oscillations drove the bridge at a frequency close to a natural frequency of the structure. Even with damping, forcing near the natural frequency produces very large amplitudes. Engineering design must ensure that natural frequencies do not coincide with likely forcing frequencies from wind, machinery, earthquakes, or traffic.

Section 7.11

Energy Interpretation as an Introduction

The solution to a differential equation describes how position evolves over time. Energy provides a complementary perspective that reveals why the system behaves as it does.

Mechanical Energy of a Mass-Spring System

Kinetic energy of the mass:

KE = (1/2) m [x′(t)]²

Potential energy stored in the spring:

PE = (1/2) k [x(t)]²

Total mechanical energy:

E(t) = KE + PE = (1/2) m(x′)² + (1/2) kx²

For simple harmonic motion, substituting x(t) = A cos(ω₀ t − φ) and using ω₀² = k/m gives E = (1/2) kA² = constant. The undamped system is conservative: energy transfers between kinetic and potential forms but the total never changes.

In a damped system, the damping force does negative work on the mass, removing energy at a rate equal to b[x′(t)]²:

dE/dt = −b[x′(t)]² ≤ 0

This is always nonpositive, confirming that energy is only dissipated, never created, by damping. Energy is lost most rapidly when the mass is moving quickly (near equilibrium) and least rapidly when the mass is momentarily stationary (at maximum displacement). For an underdamped system with amplitude envelope Ae^{−λt}, the energy decays as E(t) ≈ (1/2)kA²e^{−2λt} — at twice the rate of the amplitude.

Energy and Amplitude

Because energy is proportional to amplitude squared, if the amplitude decreases by half, the energy decreases by a factor of four. This quadratic relationship between amplitude and energy is a key fact in acoustics, optics, and signal processing as well as mechanics.

Section 7.12

Oscillations in Physics and Engineering

The mass-spring model is a mathematical archetype that appears throughout physics and engineering. Despite their apparent differences, a wide range of systems share the same second-order governing equation.

The Simple Pendulum

For small oscillations, the simple pendulum (a mass on a string of length L) obeys θ″ + (g/L)θ = 0, where θ is the angle from vertical. This is identical in structure to the undamped mass-spring equation, with ω₀ = √(g/L) and period T = 2π√(L/g). This linearized approximation is valid for small angles (typically θ ≲ 15°).

RLC Electrical Circuits

A series RLC circuit with resistance R, inductance L, and capacitance C obeys: Lq″ + Rq′ + (1/C)q = V(t), where q(t) is the charge on the capacitor and V(t) is the applied voltage. This is mathematically identical to the forced, damped mass-spring equation.

Biological Oscillations

Oscillations appear throughout biology: the heartbeat is driven by oscillating electrical signals in cardiac muscle, neurons fire in rhythmic bursts, circadian rhythms in cells have periods of approximately 24 hours, and hearing depends on resonance of the basilar membrane in the cochlea at different frequencies. Predator-prey population cycles involve coupled oscillations, which are studied in Chapter 9.

Structural Engineering and Control Systems

Buildings and bridges have natural frequencies. Earthquake engineering must ensure that building natural frequencies do not match the dominant frequencies of local ground motion. In control engineering, the step response of a second-order system is characterized by its natural frequency and damping ratio. Designers must choose ζ carefully: too little damping leads to large oscillations before settling, while too much damping leads to a slow response.

Section 7.13

Common Vibration and Oscillation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Measuring Displacement from the Wrong Reference
Measuring x from the natural length of the spring (not from equilibrium)

If displacement is measured from the natural length, the gravitational force does not cancel and the equation has an extra constant term. Always define x = 0 at the equilibrium position. The gravitational and static spring forces cancel there automatically.

Mistake 2 — Confusing ω₀, ω_d, and f
Using ω₀ as the oscillation frequency in Hz, or confusing ω₀ with ω_d

ω₀ and ω_d are angular frequencies in rad/s. The frequency in Hz is f = ω/(2π). For underdamped systems, the actual oscillation frequency is ω_d = √(ω₀² − λ²) < ω₀. Use ω_d in the solution, not ω₀.

Mistake 3 — Wrong Particular Solution at Resonance
At resonance (ω = ω₀, undamped), guessing x_p = A cos(ω₀ t) + B sin(ω₀ t)

This guess is a solution to the homogeneous equation, not a new particular solution. At resonance in the undamped case, multiply by t: x_p = t(A cos ω₀ t + B sin ω₀ t). This produces the linearly growing amplitude that characterizes pure resonance.

Mistake 4 — Misidentifying the Damping Case
Computing Δ = b² − 4mk incorrectly, or comparing the wrong quantities

Compute Δ = b² − 4mk carefully with the actual values of m and k. If Δ < 0: underdamped; if Δ = 0: critical; if Δ > 0: overdamped. Using the wrong case leads to an entirely wrong solution form.

Mistake 5 — Forgetting the Product Rule
Differentiating x(t) = e^{−λt}(C₁ cos ω_d t + C₂ sin ω_d t) without the product rule

The underdamped solution is a product of two functions. The derivative requires the product rule: [uv]′ = u′v + uv′ where u = e^{−λt} and v = C₁ cos ω_d t + C₂ sin ω_d t. Skipping this leads to wrong values of C₁ and C₂ from the initial conditions.

Mistake 6 — Using Natural Frequency for a Damped System
Writing x(t) = e^{−λt}(C₁ cos ω₀ t + C₂ sin ω₀ t) for an underdamped system

The actual oscillation frequency in a damped system is ω_d = √(ω₀² − λ²), which is always less than ω₀ when damping is present. Compute ω_d from the imaginary part of the characteristic roots; do not substitute ω₀.

Mistake 7 — Neglecting Units
Mixing grams with newtons, or pounds with kilograms

Differential equations are sensitive to unit consistency. Work in one system throughout: SI (kg, N, m, s) or US customary (slug, lb, ft, s). Convert all given quantities before writing the equation.

Chapter 7 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 7.1Oscillations arise from the interplay of a restoring force and inertia. The mass-spring model is a universal archetype for oscillatory behavior across all of science and engineering.
  • Sec 7.2Displacement x(t), velocity x′(t), and acceleration x″(t) are connected by derivatives. Newton's second law F = mx″ is the foundation of every vibration equation. Two initial conditions are always required.
  • Sec 7.3Hooke's Law: F = −kx. The spring constant k measures stiffness (N/m). The restoring force is always directed back toward equilibrium.
  • Sec 7.4Measuring displacement from equilibrium removes gravity from the equation. The governing undamped equation is mx″ + kx = 0, with natural angular frequency ω₀ = √(k/m).
  • Sec 7.5Simple harmonic motion: x(t) = C₁ cos ω₀ t + C₂ sin ω₀ t, arising from purely imaginary characteristic roots. Amplitude-phase form: x(t) = A cos(ω₀ t − φ).
  • Sec 7.6Amplitude A = √(C₁² + C₂²), period T = 2π/ω₀, frequency f = ω₀/(2π). Amplitude is set by initial conditions; frequency is set by k and m.
  • Sec 7.7Viscous damping force −bx′ adds a first-derivative term to the equation, giving mx″ + bx′ + kx = 0. The discriminant Δ = b² − 4mk determines the type of motion.
  • Sec 7.8Three cases: underdamped (Δ < 0, decaying oscillation), critically damped (Δ = 0, fastest non-oscillatory return), overdamped (Δ > 0, slow non-oscillatory return). Damping ratio ζ = b/(2√mk).
  • Sec 7.9Forced oscillations: mx″ + bx′ + kx = F(t). The solution splits into a transient response (decays) and a steady-state response (persists at the forcing frequency).
  • Sec 7.10Resonance occurs when ω = ω₀ in the undamped case, producing an amplitude that grows linearly with time. Damped systems have a large but finite peak amplitude near ω₀. Resonance is an engineering hazard.
  • Sec 7.11Total energy E = (1/2)m(x′)² + (1/2)kx². In the undamped case, E = (1/2)kA² is constant. Damping dissipates energy at rate b(x′)²; energy decays at twice the rate of amplitude.
  • Sec 7.12The mass-spring equation governs pendulums, RLC circuits, structural dynamics, biological rhythms, and control systems. The mechanical-electrical analogy is exact and widely used.
  • Sec 7.13Seven common mistakes: wrong reference point, frequency confusion (ω₀ vs. ω_d vs. f), wrong resonance particular solution, wrong discriminant, missing product rule, wrong frequency in damped solution, unit inconsistency.
  • Sec 7.14Chapter 8 develops the full theory of nonhomogeneous equations: undetermined coefficients, the resonance modification rule, variation of parameters, and applications to driven mechanical and electrical systems.

Key Terms Review

amplitude
Maximum displacement from equilibrium. A = √(C₁² + C₂²) for x(t) = C₁ cos ωt + C₂ sin ωt. Set by initial conditions.
angular frequency
Rate of oscillation in rad/s. Natural angular frequency: ω₀ = √(k/m). Damped: ω_d = √(ω₀² − λ²). Not the same as Hz.
critical damping
The case b² = 4mk (Δ = 0). Repeated root r = −λ. System returns to equilibrium as quickly as possible without oscillating.
damped frequency
ω_d = √(ω₀² − λ²). The actual oscillation frequency in an underdamped system. Always less than ω₀ when b > 0.
damping coefficient
The constant b in the viscous damping force −bx′. Units: N·s/m. Controls how quickly oscillations die out.
damping ratio
ζ = b/(2√mk). Underdamped: ζ < 1. Critically damped: ζ = 1. Overdamped: ζ > 1.
equilibrium position
The rest position where net force is zero. The reference point for measuring displacement; absorbs the effect of gravity in vertical systems.
forced oscillation
Oscillation driven by an external time-dependent force F(t). Produces both a transient and a steady-state response.
frequency
Number of complete oscillations per second. f = ω/(2π) in Hz. Distinct from angular frequency ω.
Hooke's Law
F = −kx. The spring force is proportional to displacement and directed toward equilibrium. Valid within the elastic range.
natural frequency
ω₀ = √(k/m). The angular frequency at which a system oscillates when undamped and unforced.
overdamping
The case b² > 4mk. Two distinct negative real roots. System returns to equilibrium without oscillating, more slowly than critical damping.
period
Time for one complete oscillation. T = 2π/ω seconds. Inversely related to frequency.
phase angle
The offset φ in A cos(ωt − φ). Determines the initial displacement and velocity relationship. Set by initial conditions.
resonance
The condition ω = ω₀ in the undamped case; produces amplitude growing linearly with time. In damped systems, produces a peak amplitude near ω₀.
simple harmonic motion
Oscillatory motion governed by x″ + ω₀² x = 0. Pure sinusoidal motion at fixed frequency ω₀, with constant amplitude.
spring constant
k, the stiffness of a spring (N/m). Larger k means stiffer spring and higher natural frequency. Measured by k = mg/δ.
steady-state response
The particular solution in a forced damped system. Oscillates at the forcing frequency ω; persists after the transient dies out.
transient response
The complementary solution in a forced damped system. Oscillates at the natural frequency ω_d; decays to zero when b > 0.
underdamping
The case b² < 4mk. Complex characteristic roots. Oscillations with exponentially decaying amplitude.

Error Analysis

Each problem contains an error. Identify it and write the correction.

Problem 1
m = 1 kg, k = 4 N/m, b = 4 N·s/m. Discriminant: b² − 4mk = 16 − 16 = 0. Critical. Repeated root r = −b/m = −4. Solution: x(t) = (C₁ + C₂ t)e^{−4t}.

The exponent is wrong. The repeated root for critical damping is r = −b/(2m) = −4/(2·1) = −2, not −b/m = −4. The correct solution is x(t) = (C₁ + C₂ t)e^{−2t}.

Problem 2
x″ + 9x = 0. "Natural frequency ω₀ = 3 rad/s = 3/(2π) Hz ≈ 0.48 Hz. Period T = 1/f ≈ 2.09 s." Student also checks T = 2π/ω₀ = 2π/3 ≈ 2.09 s and concludes no error.

The conceptual error is stating that ω₀ rad/s equals ω₀ Hz. ω₀ = 3 rad/s gives f = 3/(2π) ≈ 0.48 Hz and T = 2π/3 ≈ 2.09 s. The computation of T was accidentally correct via the check formula, but the intermediate claim "3 rad/s = 3/(2π) Hz" (missing the division) reflects the confusion — though the actual 3/(2π) result was stated correctly.

Problem 3
Setting up a vertical spring problem measuring x from the natural length: "The spring force is −k(x + δ). Newton's law: mx″ = mg − k(x + δ). After canceling mg − kδ = 0, I get mx″ = −kx." Student claims equilibrium is at x = 0.

The algebra is correct and the equation mx″ = −kx is right. But the equilibrium in this coordinate system is at x = 0 only if x is measured from the equilibrium position (where the spring is stretched by δ). If x is measured from the natural length, then x = 0 is the natural length, and the equilibrium is at x = δ, not at x = 0. The setup here is fine if x is understood as displacement from the natural length plus δ — which is the same as displacement from equilibrium. The student's conclusion is correct but the reasoning about what "x = 0" means needs to be stated carefully.

Problem 4
x″ + 2x′ + 10x = 0, x(0) = 1, x′(0) = 0. General solution: x(t) = e^{−t}(C₁ cos 3t + C₂ sin 3t). Applying x′(0) = 0: x′(t) = e^{−t}(−3C₁ sin 3t + 3C₂ cos 3t), so x′(0) = 3C₂ = 0, giving C₂ = 0.

The product rule was omitted. The correct derivative is x′(t) = −e^{−t}(C₁ cos 3t + C₂ sin 3t) + e^{−t}(−3C₁ sin 3t + 3C₂ cos 3t). At t = 0: x′(0) = −C₁ + 3C₂ = −1 + 3C₂ = 0, giving C₂ = 1/3, not C₂ = 0.

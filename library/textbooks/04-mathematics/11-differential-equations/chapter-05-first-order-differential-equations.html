Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces second-order differential equations, with a focus on linear equations having constant coefficients. A first-order equation describes how a quantity changes based on its current value or the current independent variable. A second-order equation goes one level deeper: it describes how the rate of change itself is changing. This is the mathematics of acceleration, oscillation, circuits, beams, and dynamic systems with inertia.

Students who have studied first-order equations know how to work with equations of the form dy/dx = f(x, y). This chapter asks a new question: what happens when the equation involves y'' alongside y' and y? The answer leads to richer behavior, including oscillation and exponential decay, and to a powerful connection between algebraic roots and the shape of solutions.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to classify second-order linear equations, form and solve characteristic equations, write general solutions for all root types, apply initial conditions to find particular solutions, and interpret what each solution form means physically and graphically. This chapter is foundational for everything that follows in Part III.

Opening Question

A mass hangs from a spring. You pull the mass downward and release it. Without air resistance or friction, the mass bobs up and down forever. With damping, it gradually slows. If you push on it periodically, it might oscillate more and more violently. All of this behavior, the oscillation, the decay, the resonance, is captured by a single second-order differential equation. Why does a spring system produce oscillation? Why does adding damping produce exponential decay? Why does the type of root in an algebraic equation determine which kind of behavior appears? This chapter answers those questions.

Why This Chapter Matters

Second-order differential equations are among the most important equations in all of applied mathematics. They appear wherever a system has inertia, wherever a second derivative represents a physically meaningful quantity such as acceleration, curvature, or flux change rate. Newton's second law F = ma gives my'' = F, a second-order equation. The behavior of an RLC circuit involves both inductance and capacitance, giving a second-order equation for charge or current. The deflection of a beam under load satisfies a second-order equation. The Schrödinger equation in quantum mechanics is second order in space.

Students who understand second-order linear equations with constant coefficients will be prepared for all of these applications, and for the deeper study of mechanical vibrations, resonance, and forcing that follows in Chapters 7 and 8.

Section 6.1

What Second-Order Means

A second-order differential equation is an equation that involves the second derivative of an unknown function. More precisely, it is an equation in which the highest-order derivative appearing is the second derivative. If y is a function of x, then a second-order equation involves y'', and may also involve y', y, and the independent variable x.

EquationSecond-Order?Reason
y'' + 3y' + 2y = 0YesHighest derivative is y''
y'' − 4y = 0YesHighest derivative is y''
y' + y = 0NoHighest derivative is y' (first-order)
y''' + y = 0NoHighest derivative is y''' (third-order)

Compare this to a first-order equation, which involves y' but not y''. A first-order equation describes how a quantity changes. A second-order equation describes how the rate of change itself changes, which is the description of acceleration. The order of a differential equation determines how many arbitrary constants appear in its general solution, and therefore how many initial or boundary conditions are needed to select a unique solution. A first-order equation has one arbitrary constant and needs one condition. A second-order equation has two arbitrary constants and needs two conditions.

Practice 6.1Concept Check
  1. In your own words, explain the difference between a first-order and a second-order differential equation. Why does order matter for the number of initial conditions needed?
  2. Identify the order of each equation: (a) y'' + y = 0, (b) y' − 2y = x, (c) y''' + y'' = 1, (d) y'' − y' + 3y = 0.
  3. A second-order equation has two arbitrary constants in its general solution. Why does this make physical sense when the equation describes the motion of an object?
Section 6.2

Second Derivatives and Acceleration

The most natural physical interpretation of a second derivative is acceleration. If x(t) describes the position of an object at time t, then x'(t) is velocity and x''(t) is acceleration. Newton's second law states that force equals mass times acceleration: F = ma = m·x''(t). If the force F depends on position x, velocity x', and possibly time t, then Newton's law becomes a second-order differential equation for x(t).

SystemDifferential EquationVariables
Spring (Hooke's Law)mx'' + kx = 0m = mass, k = spring constant
RLC CircuitLq'' + Rq' + (1/C)q = E(t)L = inductance, R = resistance, C = capacitance
Pendulum (small angle)θ'' + (g/L)θ = 0g = gravity, L = length
Shared Mathematical Form

The spring equation and the RLC circuit equation have the same mathematical structure. One involves a mass and a spring constant; the other involves inductance and capacitance. Because the equations are mathematically identical, they share the same solution methods and the same behavior types. This is one of the deep lessons of differential equations: different physical systems can share the same mathematical form.

Section 6.3

Linear Second-Order Equations

A second-order differential equation is linear if it can be written in the form a₂(x)·y'' + a₁(x)·y' + a₀(x)·y = g(x), where the coefficient functions depend only on x, and the unknown function y and its derivatives appear only to the first power and are not multiplied together. The standard form divides through by a₂(x): y'' + P(x)·y' + Q(x)·y = f(x).

EquationLinear?Reason
y'' + 5y' − 6y = cos(x)Yesy and its derivatives appear to first power only
y'' + y² = 0Noy appears squared
y'' + yy' = 0Noy and y' are multiplied together
y'' = sin(y)Noy appears inside a nonlinear function
The Superposition Principle

For a linear homogeneous equation, if y₁ and y₂ are both solutions, then any linear combination c₁y₁ + c₂y₂ is also a solution. This principle is the foundation for the general solution structure. It applies only to linear equations — for nonlinear equations, the sum of two solutions is generally not a solution.

Section 6.4

Homogeneous Equations

A second-order linear equation is homogeneous if the right-hand side is zero: y'' + P(x)·y' + Q(x)·y = 0. It is nonhomogeneous if the right-hand side is a nonzero function. This chapter focuses on homogeneous equations. Chapter 8 will address nonhomogeneous equations. A homogeneous equation describes a system free from external input or forcing, where behavior depends entirely on the system's own properties and initial conditions.

EquationClassification
y'' + 4y' + 4y = 0Homogeneous — right side is zero
y'' − y = eˣNonhomogeneous — right side is eˣ
y'' + 9y = 0Homogeneous
y'' + 3y' = sin(x)Nonhomogeneous — right side is sin(x)
Section 6.5

Constant Coefficients

The most tractable second-order linear equations are those with constant coefficients: ay'' + by' + cy = 0, where a, b, and c are real constants with a ≠ 0. When coefficients are constant, there is a complete and elegant method for finding the general solution based on the characteristic equation, the main subject of this chapter.

Many real systems, to a first approximation, have properties that do not change over time or position. The spring constant of a steel spring is essentially the same whether the spring is at position 0 or 5. In such systems, the governing differential equation has constant coefficients. Beyond physical justification, constant-coefficient equations admit exact, explicit solutions, making them ideal for learning the conceptual structure of second-order equations before approaching the variable-coefficient case.

Section 6.6

Characteristic Equations

The key insight for solving ay'' + by' + cy = 0 is to look for solutions of the form y = eʳˣ, where r is a constant to be determined. Exponential functions have the remarkable property that their derivatives are proportional to themselves: if y = eʳˣ, then y' = reʳˣ and y'' = r²eʳˣ. Substituting into the equation and factoring out eʳˣ (which is never zero) gives the characteristic equation:

Characteristic Equation (Auxiliary Equation)
ar² + br + c = 0

Finding a solution to the differential equation reduces to finding the roots of the characteristic equation, an ordinary quadratic in r, solvable by factoring or the quadratic formula: r = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a. The nature of the roots depends on the discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac.

DiscriminantRoot TypeGeneral Solution Form
Δ > 0Two distinct real roots r₁ ≠ r₂c₁eʳ¹ˣ + c₂eʳ²ˣ
Δ = 0One repeated real root r(c₁ + c₂x)eʳˣ
Δ < 0Complex conjugate roots α ± βieᵅˣ(c₁cos βx + c₂sin βx)
Modeling Tip

The characteristic equation does not involve x or y at all. It is purely algebraic. This is one of the beautiful features of constant-coefficient equations: the solution structure is determined entirely by the algebraic roots of a quadratic.

Section 6.7

Real Distinct Roots

When Δ > 0, the characteristic equation has two distinct real roots r₁ and r₂. Each root gives a solution: y₁ = eʳ¹ˣ and y₂ = eʳ²ˣ. These two solutions are linearly independent; neither is a constant multiple of the other. By the superposition principle, the general solution is:

Case 1 — Real Distinct Roots
y = c₁eʳ¹ˣ + c₂eʳ²ˣ
Example 6.7.1
Real Distinct Roots — General Solution
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' − 5y' + 6y = 0.
  • ThinkForm the characteristic equation and factor it to find the roots.
  • Solve Characteristic equation: r² − 5r + 6 = 0 Factor: (r − 2)(r − 3) = 0 Roots: r₁ = 2, r₂ = 3 General solution: y = c₁e²ˣ + c₂e³ˣ
  • CheckFor y₁ = e²ˣ: y₁'' − 5y₁' + 6y₁ = 4e²ˣ − 10e²ˣ + 6e²ˣ = 0 ✓. For y₂ = e³ˣ: y₂'' − 5y₂' + 6y₂ = 9e³ˣ − 15e³ˣ + 6e³ˣ = 0 ✓
  • InterpretBoth roots are positive, so both terms grow exponentially. For large x, the e³ˣ term dominates. Solutions grow without bound as x → ∞.
Answer: y = c₁e²ˣ + c₂e³ˣ
Example 6.7.2
Real Roots with Decay
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' + 3y' + 2y = 0.
  • Solve r² + 3r + 2 = (r + 1)(r + 2) = 0 Roots: r₁ = −1, r₂ = −2 y = c₁e⁻ˣ + c₂e⁻²ˣ
  • InterpretBoth roots are negative. Both terms decay exponentially. As x → ∞, y → 0 regardless of the values of c₁ and c₂. This is the behavior of an overdamped system: it returns to equilibrium without oscillating.
Answer: y = c₁e⁻ˣ + c₂e⁻²ˣ
Example 6.7.3
One Positive, One Negative Root
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' − y = 0.
  • Solve r² − 1 = (r − 1)(r + 1) = 0 Roots: r₁ = 1, r₂ = −1 y = c₁eˣ + c₂e⁻ˣ
  • InterpretOne root is positive (growth) and one is negative (decay). Unless c₁ = 0, the solution grows without bound as x → ∞. This is the behavior of an unstable equilibrium. Note: this solution can also be written y = A cosh(x) + B sinh(x) using hyperbolic functions.
Answer: y = c₁eˣ + c₂e⁻ˣ
Section 6.8

Repeated Roots

When Δ = 0, the characteristic equation has exactly one real root of multiplicity two: r = −b/(2a). The exponential function y₁ = eʳˣ is a solution, but a second-order equation needs two linearly independent solutions. Using the method of reduction of order, it can be shown that a second independent solution is y₂ = xeʳˣ. The factor of x is not arbitrary — it emerges necessarily from the structure of the equation when there is a repeated root.

Case 2 — Repeated Root
y = c₁eʳˣ + c₂xeʳˣ = (c₁ + c₂x)eʳˣ
Student Warning

When you find a repeated root r, do not write y = c₁eʳˣ + c₂eʳˣ. This simplifies to (c₁ + c₂)eʳˣ, a solution with only one effective arbitrary constant. The second solution must be xeʳˣ, not another copy of eʳˣ.

Example 6.8.1
Repeated Root — General Solution
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' − 4y' + 4y = 0.
  • Solve r² − 4r + 4 = (r − 2)² = 0 Repeated root: r = 2 y = (c₁ + c₂x)e²ˣ
  • CheckFor y₂ = xe²ˣ: y₂' = (1 + 2x)e²ˣ, y₂'' = (4 + 4x)e²ˣ. Then y₂'' − 4y₂' + 4y₂ = (4 + 4x − 4 − 8x + 4x)e²ˣ = 0 ✓
  • InterpretThe solution grows because r = 2 > 0. The factor (c₁ + c₂x) modulates the growth with a linear term. For large x, the c₂xe²ˣ term dominates.
Answer: y = (c₁ + c₂x)e²ˣ
Example 6.8.2
Repeated Root with Decay
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' + 6y' + 9y = 0.
  • Solve r² + 6r + 9 = (r + 3)² = 0 Repeated root: r = −3 y = (c₁ + c₂x)e⁻³ˣ
  • InterpretSince r = −3 < 0, the exponential decays. Even though (c₁ + c₂x) grows linearly, exponential decay dominates. As x → ∞, y → 0 for all choices of c₁ and c₂. This is the behavior of a critically damped system.
Answer: y = (c₁ + c₂x)e⁻³ˣ
Section 6.9

Complex Roots

When Δ < 0, the characteristic equation has two complex conjugate roots: r = α ± βi, where α = −b/(2a) and β = √(4ac − b²)/(2a) > 0. The formal exponential solutions e^((α+βi)x) and e^((α−βi)x) are complex-valued. To obtain real-valued solutions, we apply Euler's formula: e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ. Taking real and imaginary parts of e^((α+βi)x) = e^(αx)(cos βx + i sin βx) yields two real-valued, linearly independent solutions.

Case 3 — Complex Conjugate Roots α ± βi
y = eᵅˣ(c₁ cos βx + c₂ sin βx)

The factor e^(αx) controls the amplitude. The cosine and sine terms produce oscillation. Together: pure oscillation if α = 0, decaying oscillation if α < 0, and growing oscillation if α > 0. These three behaviors correspond directly to undamped, underdamped, and unstable oscillation.

Example 6.9.1
Complex Roots — Pure Oscillation
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' + 9y = 0.
  • ThinkNo y' term, so expect pure oscillation with no damping.
  • Solve r² + 9 = 0 → r² = −9 → r = ±3i α = 0, β = 3 y = c₁cos(3x) + c₂sin(3x)
  • CheckFor y₁ = cos(3x): y₁'' = −9cos(3x). Then y₁'' + 9y₁ = −9cos(3x) + 9cos(3x) = 0 ✓
  • InterpretThe solution is a pure sinusoidal oscillation. It neither grows nor decays. This is simple harmonic motion. The natural angular frequency is ω = 3 radians per unit, and the period is T = 2π/3.
Answer: y = c₁cos(3x) + c₂sin(3x)
Example 6.9.2
Complex Roots — Decaying Oscillation
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' + 2y' + 5y = 0.
  • ThinkThe y' term introduces damping. Expect decaying oscillation.
  • Solve r² + 2r + 5 = 0 r = (−2 ± √(4 − 20)) / 2 = (−2 ± √(−16)) / 2 r = (−2 ± 4i) / 2 = −1 ± 2i α = −1, β = 2 y = e⁻ˣ(c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x))
  • InterpretThe factor e⁻ˣ causes the amplitude to decay toward zero. The oscillation has angular frequency β = 2. The solution is a damped sinusoidal oscillation — exactly the behavior of an underdamped spring-mass system. The oscillations are confined within envelope curves y = ±e⁻ˣ, and each successive peak is smaller than the last.
Answer: y = e⁻ˣ(c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x))
Example 6.9.3
Complex Roots — Growing Oscillation
  • ProblemFind the general solution of y'' − 2y' + 5y = 0.
  • Solve r² − 2r + 5 = 0 r = (2 ± √(4 − 20)) / 2 = 1 ± 2i α = 1, β = 2 y = eˣ(c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x))
  • InterpretHere α = 1 > 0, so the amplitude grows without bound. The solution oscillates with increasing amplitude. This describes an unstable system in which energy is being added rather than dissipated.
Answer: y = eˣ(c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x))
Student Warning — Complex Roots

When roots are complex α ± βi, do not write y = c₁e^((α+β)x) + c₂e^((α−β)x). Complex roots produce cosine and sine solutions, not a sum of real exponentials. Always use Euler's formula to convert: the solution is y = e^(αx)(c₁cos βx + c₂sin βx). If α ≠ 0, the factor e^(αx) must appear in front of both the cosine and sine terms.

Section 6.10

General Solutions

The three root cases give a complete classification of solutions for ay'' + by' + cy = 0.

Root TypeDiscriminantRootsGeneral Solution
Real, distinctb² − 4ac > 0r₁, r₂ real, r₁ ≠ r₂c₁eʳ¹ˣ + c₂eʳ²ˣ
Real, repeatedb² − 4ac = 0r = r₁ = r₂(c₁ + c₂x)eʳˣ
Complex conjugatesb² − 4ac < 0α ± βieᵅˣ(c₁cos βx + c₂sin βx)

In each case, the two functions forming the general solution are linearly independent: neither is a constant multiple of the other. Linearly independent solutions form a fundamental set of solutions. A theorem in the theory of second-order linear equations guarantees that every solution can be written as some linear combination c₁y₁ + c₂y₂ of any fundamental set {y₁, y₂}. Knowing just two independent solutions is enough to capture all solutions.

The Wronskian

The Wronskian of two functions y₁ and y₂ is W(y₁, y₂) = y₁y₂' − y₂y₁'. Two solutions are linearly independent if and only if their Wronskian is not identically zero on the interval of interest. Use the Wronskian to confirm that two solutions are genuinely independent.

Example 6.10.1
Wronskian Verification
  • ProblemShow that y₁ = e²ˣ and y₂ = e³ˣ are linearly independent solutions of y'' − 5y' + 6y = 0.
  • Solve W = y₁y₂' − y₂y₁' = e²ˣ · 3e³ˣ − e³ˣ · 2e²ˣ = 3e⁵ˣ − 2e⁵ˣ = e⁵ˣ ≠ 0 for all x ✓
  • InterpretSince W = e⁵ˣ is never zero, the solutions are linearly independent and form a fundamental set. Their linear combination c₁e²ˣ + c₂e³ˣ is the general solution.
Answer: W = e⁵ˣ ≠ 0; solutions are linearly independent.

Section 6.11

Initial Value Problems

The general solution y = c₁y₁ + c₂y₂ contains two arbitrary constants. To find a unique solution, two conditions must be specified. For an initial value problem, these are typically y(x₀) = y₀ and y'(x₀) = y₀'. The procedure is: (1) find the general solution, (2) apply the first condition to produce one equation in c₁ and c₂, (3) differentiate and apply the second condition to produce a second equation, (4) solve the system, and (5) write the particular solution.

Example 6.11.1
IVP — Real Distinct Roots
  • ProblemSolve: y'' − 5y' + 6y = 0, y(0) = 2, y'(0) = 7.
  • ThinkFrom Example 6.7.1, the general solution is y = c₁e²ˣ + c₂e³ˣ. Apply both initial conditions.
  • Solve Apply y(0) = 2: c₁e⁰ + c₂e⁰ = 2 → c₁ + c₂ = 2 ... (i) Differentiate: y' = 2c₁e²ˣ + 3c₂e³ˣ Apply y'(0) = 7: 2c₁ + 3c₂ = 7 ... (ii) From (i): c₁ = 2 − c₂ Substitute into (ii): 2(2 − c₂) + 3c₂ = 7 4 + c₂ = 7 → c₂ = 3 c₁ = 2 − 3 = −1 Particular solution: y = −e²ˣ + 3e³ˣ
  • Checky(0) = −1 + 3 = 2 ✓ y'(0) = −2 + 9 = 7 ✓
  • InterpretAlthough c₁ = −1 is negative, the solution still grows for large x because c₂ = 3 > 0 and e³ˣ eventually dominates.
Answer: y = −e²ˣ + 3e³ˣ
Example 6.11.2
IVP — Complex Roots
  • ProblemSolve: y'' + 4y = 0, y(0) = 3, y'(0) = −2.
  • Solve r² + 4 = 0 → r = ±2i α = 0, β = 2 General solution: y = c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x) Apply y(0) = 3: c₁cos(0) + c₂sin(0) = 3 → c₁ = 3 Differentiate: y' = −2c₁sin(2x) + 2c₂cos(2x) Apply y'(0) = −2: 2c₂ = −2 → c₂ = −1 Particular solution: y = 3cos(2x) − sin(2x)
  • InterpretThis is pure oscillation with angular frequency 2. The amplitude is R = √(c₁² + c₂²) = √(9 + 1) = √10. The solution oscillates with constant amplitude √10 and period T = π.
Answer: y = 3cos(2x) − sin(2x)
Example 6.11.3
IVP — Repeated Root
  • ProblemSolve: y'' + 4y' + 4y = 0, y(0) = 1, y'(0) = 0.
  • Solve r² + 4r + 4 = (r + 2)² = 0 → r = −2 (repeated) General solution: y = (c₁ + c₂x)e⁻²ˣ Apply y(0) = 1: (c₁ + 0)e⁰ = 1 → c₁ = 1 Differentiate: y' = c₂e⁻²ˣ − 2(c₁ + c₂x)e⁻²ˣ = [c₂ − 2c₁ − 2c₂x]e⁻²ˣ Apply y'(0) = 0: c₂ − 2c₁ = 0 → c₂ = 2c₁ = 2 Particular solution: y = (1 + 2x)e⁻²ˣ
  • InterpretAt x = 0, the solution starts at y = 1 with zero velocity. The factor (1 + 2x) is initially increasing, but the decaying exponential e⁻²ˣ eventually dominates. The solution rises slightly before decaying to zero — characteristic of critical damping with an initial displacement but no initial velocity.
Answer: y = (1 + 2x)e⁻²ˣ
Chapter 6 — Back Matter

Common Mistakes, Chapter Summary, and Review

6.13 — Common Second-Order Equation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Forgetting the second solution for a repeated root
y = c₁e³ˣ + c₂e³ˣ (for repeated root r = 3)

This simplifies to (c₁ + c₂)e³ˣ, which has only one effective arbitrary constant. The second solution must be xe^(rx), not another copy of e^(rx). Correct form: y = (c₁ + c₂x)e³ˣ.

Mistake 2 — Using the wrong solution form for complex roots
y = c₁e^((α+β)x) + c₂e^((α−β)x) (for roots α ± βi)

Complex roots produce oscillatory solutions involving cos and sin, not a sum of real exponentials. Correct form: y = e^(αx)(c₁cos βx + c₂sin βx).

Mistake 3 — Applying only one initial condition
Applying y(0) = y₀ only and declaring c₁ = c₂

A second-order equation needs two conditions. Always differentiate the general solution and apply y'(x₀) = y₀' to produce a second equation. Then solve the resulting 2×2 system for both constants.

Mistake 4 — Treating r² + 4 = 0 as having real roots ±2
r² + 4 = 0 → r = ±2 (wrong sign)

r² + 4 = 0 gives r² = −4, so r = ±2i. These are purely imaginary roots with α = 0 and β = 2, giving y = c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x), not y = c₁e²ˣ + c₂e⁻²ˣ.

Mistake 5 — Forgetting the factor e^(αx) when α ≠ 0
y = c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x) (for roots −1 ± 2i)

If the real part α is nonzero, the factor e^(αx) must appear in front. Correct form for roots −1 ± 2i: y = e⁻ˣ(c₁cos(2x) + c₂sin(2x)).

Mistake 6 — Writing the characteristic equation with the wrong sign pattern
For y'' − 4y' + 4y = 0, student writes: r² + 4r + 4 = 0 (wrong signs)

The characteristic equation is formed by replacing y'' with r², y' with r, y with 1, and preserving all coefficient signs. The correct characteristic equation is r² − 4r + 4 = 0.

Mistake 7 — Skipping the check after finding constants

After computing c₁ and c₂, always substitute back into both y(x₀) and y'(x₀) to verify. Sign errors in the linear system are common, and checking catches them immediately.

Mistake 8 — Confusing order, degree, and linearity
Classifying y'' + (y')² = 0 as second-order linear

This equation is second-order but nonlinear because (y')² is a nonlinear term. Check: do y, y', and y'' appear only to the first power and not multiplied together? If not, the equation is nonlinear.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 6.1A second-order equation involves y'' as the highest derivative. It has two arbitrary constants in its general solution and requires two initial or boundary conditions to produce a unique solution.
  • Sec 6.2Second derivatives represent acceleration. Newton's second law and the RLC circuit equation both produce second-order equations with the same mathematical form, which means they share solution methods.
  • Sec 6.3A linear equation has y, y', y'' appearing only to the first power and not multiplied together. The superposition principle holds for linear homogeneous equations: linear combinations of solutions are solutions.
  • Sec 6.4Homogeneous equations have a zero right-hand side. Nonhomogeneous equations have a nonzero forcing function. Understanding homogeneous solutions is essential before approaching nonhomogeneous problems.
  • Sec 6.5Constant-coefficient equations ay'' + by' + cy = 0 admit a complete, explicit solution theory based on the characteristic equation. Many real physical systems have constant coefficients to a good approximation.
  • Sec 6.6Assuming y = eʳˣ and substituting into ay'' + by' + cy = 0 yields the characteristic equation ar² + br + c = 0. Its roots determine the solution form. The discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac classifies the three root types.
  • Sec 6.7Real distinct roots r₁ ≠ r₂ give general solution y = c₁eʳ¹ˣ + c₂eʳ²ˣ. Negative roots produce decay; positive roots produce growth; mixed signs produce growth unless the growing coefficient is zero.
  • Sec 6.8A repeated real root r gives general solution y = (c₁ + c₂x)eʳˣ. The second solution must be xeʳˣ, not another copy of eʳˣ. Negative repeated roots describe critical damping; the solution decays to zero.
  • Sec 6.9Complex conjugate roots α ± βi give general solution y = eᵅˣ(c₁cos βx + c₂sin βx), derived via Euler's formula. The sign of α determines whether the oscillation decays (α < 0), persists (α = 0), or grows (α > 0).
  • Sec 6.10The Wronskian W = y₁y₂' − y₂y₁' confirms linear independence. A nonzero Wronskian means the two solutions form a fundamental set whose linear combinations yield all solutions.
  • Sec 6.11Initial value problems require two conditions: y(x₀) = y₀ and y'(x₀) = y₀'. Differentiate the general solution, apply both conditions, and solve the 2×2 linear system for c₁ and c₂.
  • Sec 6.12Root type determines long-term behavior: negative real parts give decay, positive give growth, zero real part with complex roots gives sustained oscillation. This classification maps directly to overdamping, critical damping, underdamping, and instability.
  • Sec 6.13Eight common errors: wrong second solution for repeated roots, wrong form for complex roots, applying only one initial condition, missing the e^(αx) factor, wrong sign in characteristic equation, and confusing linear with nonlinear.
  • Sec 6.14Chapter 7 connects these root types to the physics of spring-mass systems. Chapter 8 extends the framework to nonhomogeneous equations. Eigenvalues in matrix systems (Chapter 9) are directly related to characteristic roots.

Key Terms Review

second-order equation
A differential equation in which the highest derivative appearing is the second derivative y''.
linear equation
An equation in which y, y', and y'' appear only to the first power and are not multiplied together. Coefficient functions depend only on the independent variable.
homogeneous
A linear equation with right-hand side equal to zero: ay'' + by' + cy = 0. No external input or forcing.
nonhomogeneous
A linear equation with nonzero right-hand side f(x). The system receives an external input.
constant coefficients
Equations in which a, b, c are constants rather than functions of x. Admit a complete algebraic solution theory.
characteristic equation
The algebraic equation ar² + br + c = 0 obtained by substituting y = eʳˣ into the differential equation. Also called the auxiliary equation.
discriminant
Δ = b² − 4ac for the characteristic equation. Determines whether roots are real distinct (Δ > 0), repeated (Δ = 0), or complex (Δ < 0).
real distinct roots
Two different real values r₁ ≠ r₂ from the characteristic equation, giving general solution c₁eʳ¹ˣ + c₂eʳ²ˣ.
repeated root
A single real root r with multiplicity two, giving general solution (c₁ + c₂x)eʳˣ. The factor x is essential.
complex conjugate roots
Roots of the form α ± βi with β ≠ 0, giving general solution eᵅˣ(c₁cos βx + c₂sin βx).
Euler's formula
e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ. Used to convert complex exponential solutions into real-valued cosine and sine solutions.
general solution
The complete family of solutions, containing two arbitrary constants c₁ and c₂ for a second-order equation.
particular solution
A specific solution obtained by determining both constants from initial or boundary conditions.
fundamental set
A pair of linearly independent solutions {y₁, y₂} whose linear combinations c₁y₁ + c₂y₂ yield all solutions to the homogeneous equation.
linear independence
Two functions are linearly independent if neither is a constant multiple of the other. Required for a fundamental set.
Wronskian
W = y₁y₂' − y₂y₁'. Two solutions are linearly independent if and only if their Wronskian is not identically zero.
superposition principle
For a linear homogeneous equation, any linear combination of solutions is also a solution. Applies only to linear equations.
overdamped
Two distinct negative real roots; solution decays to zero without oscillating.
critically damped
A repeated negative root; solution decays to zero (fastest return without oscillation).
underdamped
Complex roots with negative real part; solution oscillates with decaying amplitude.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 7: Mechanical Vibrations and Oscillations

Chapter 7 uses every idea developed in this chapter to analyze real physical systems. The equation for a mass-spring-dashpot system is mx'' + bx' + kx = F(t). With F(t) = 0, this is a homogeneous constant-coefficient equation whose characteristic equation is mr² + br + k = 0. The discriminant b² − 4mk determines whether the system is overdamped (b² > 4mk), critically damped (b² = 4mk), or underdamped (b² < 4mk). Students who understand the three root cases from Chapter 6 will immediately see which physical behavior each case corresponds to. Chapter 7 will also introduce amplitude, period, and frequency, the relationship between complex roots and oscillation rate, and the phenomenon of resonance, when forcing frequency matches natural frequency. All of these depend on the solution framework built here.
